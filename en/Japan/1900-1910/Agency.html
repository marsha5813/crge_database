Tsuda Umeko was a Meiji-era educator who contributed significantly to girls‚Äô education as a pioneer. She was born as a daughter of Tsuda Sen, a samurai of the Sakura Domain, in 1864. She traveled to the United States as a member of the Iwakura Mission, which was a Japanese diplomatic mission operated by the Meiji government, being its youngest member at just six years old. Tsuda stayed at an American household in Philadelphia for ten years. While she was there, she converted to Christianity and absorbed American culture. Behind Tsuda‚Äôs prolonged stay in the U. Upon her return to Japan in 1882, Tsuda became an English teacher at a school for noblewomen. However, she had been so accustomed to the language and culture in the U. S. that she found it difficult for her to readjust to Japanese society In 1889, she decided to go to the United States for further study once again. Tsuda obtained a degree in biology from Bryn Mawr College in Philadelphia and also attended the Oswego Normal School to study the English teaching methods. She returned to Japan in August 1892 and began teaching English at Meiji Women‚Äôs School and Women‚Äôs Higher Normal School.

In July 1900, Tsuda founded the ‚ÄúJoshi Eigo Gakujuku (an English school for girls, which was currently known as Tsuda University) and became its principal. The establishment of this school was made possible through substantial donations from individuals who supported Tsuda in the United States, known as the American Committee (Kogi, pp. 150-154). After the Meiji era, numerous schools were established in Japan by foreign missionaries. However, this school, founded by a Japanese woman and based on Christian principles, was the first of its kind for Japanese women. This school for girls, which was open to graduates of girls‚Äô high schools and normal schools, educated them in small groups for three years, emphasizing the importance of the English language. The program also aimed to prepare students for the government examination required to obtain an English teaching certificate, which enabled them to teach English beyond the elementary level (Pamonag, 37). From 1905, graduating from this girl‚Äôs school, they obtained an English teacher‚Äôs license without an examination. In addition, a characteristic of the education at the school was that they did not emphasize the ideal of a‚Äùgood wife, wise mother,‚Äù which confined women to domestic roles (Pamonag, 37). Watching the independent lives of women in Americaled Tsuda to shape her educational philosophy that emphasiezed the importance of women‚Äôs independence. This aspect provides insights into her thoughts regarding women‚Äôs education. In its first year, Joshi Eigaku Juku had 15 students enrolled, but the number of applicants seeking admission increased to 100 in 1907. This shows that the demand among women for educational opportunities was high and provides insights into the changing perception of Christianity within the general society since 1900. Tsuda assumed the role of the first president of the Tokyo branch of the YWCA (Young Women‚Äôs Christian Association) when it was established in 1905. Tsuda utilized her position in the YWCA to connect women‚Äôs education in Japan with Christian evangelism (Johnson, 83). During that time, participation in Christian rituals at schools was prohibited by the Japanese Ministry of Education regulations. The establishment of a YWCA chapter by the students of Joshi Eigaku Juku provided Tsuda with the opportunity to propagate Christianity among her students while avoiding the Christian ceremonies that were prohibited by the regulations. (Johnson, 83) In sum, Tsuda Umeko was an important figure in Japanese Christian society in the decade between 1900 and 1910, who provided girls with opportunities to enjoy the Christianity-based education, fostering their independence.

Honda Y≈çichi was a Meiji-era educator and religious leader who united the previously separate Methodist churches as the Methodist Church of Japan. He also became the first Japanese dean of the Christian school named Aoyama Gakuin. Honda was born as a son of a samurai in Hirosaki domain (Aomori prefecture) in 1849, and in 1870 he was sent to Yokohama to learn English by order of the domain. In Yokohama, Honda enrolled in the Ballagh School run by James Hamilton Ballagh (1832-1920), who taught Christianity there. Then he was baptized by Ballagh in 1872. In 1872, Honda became a head of the local T≈çou Gijuku school, where he worked hard to evangelize Christianity in the Aomori area and to campaign Jiy≈´ Minken Und≈ç (Movement for Liberty and People‚Äôs Rights). After serving as pastor of the Sendai Church in 1887, he became a teacher at the T≈çy≈ç Eiwa School (later Aoyama Gakuin), where he devoted himself to Christian education. He also went to the United States for two years, from September 1888, to observe religious education. Honda also spent six months at the Drew Theological Seminary. He returned to Japan and decided to live as a Christian pastor. He devoted himself more to Christian work than to any other activity.

Upon his return to Japan in June 1890, Honda was appointed President of the T≈çy≈ç Eiwa School and pastor of the Mii Church (Methodist church). Honda started concentrating his energy on missionary work. He was often elected as a representative in various organizations. For example, a president of the Fukuin d≈çmei kai (Evangelical Alliance), the largest Protestant denomination formed in 1897, and the president of the Japan Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association (YMCA) during the Russo-Japanese War (Sasaki, 178). Among the most representative positions was the first director of the Methodist Church of Japan, which united in 1907. Until then, three Methodist denominations were active in Japan under the same system as in the United States. However, after the Russo-Japanese War, there was a growing trend to join forces, and concrete plans were put in place due to financial issues. Honda visited the mother churches in the United States and Canada to persuade them to permit the union. As a result, the Methodist Church in Japan was born in 1907 with 12671 members of the three denominations, 90 Japanese pastors, 49 evangelists, and 164 missionaries, with Honda as the first director. During this time, Honda was also busy running Aoyama Gakuin and giving lectures nationwide. However, in 1908 he resigned as headmaster of Aoyama Gakuin to concentrate on church work under the church‚Äôs rule of ‚Äúone and full-time supervision.‚Äù (Sasaki, 212)„ÄÄHonda argued that the way to solve Japan‚Äôs problems was to ‚Äúharmonize‚Äù traditional Japanese ‚ÄúKeishin Shiso‚Äù (piety thought) and Christianity and ‚ÄúK≈çsei‚Äù (reclaim) the country through Christian ‚ÄúShinsei Aikoku shin‚Äù (true patriotism). Honda‚Äôs presence can be considered ‚Äúa mediator and harmonizer‚Äù among unique Christians such as Kanz≈ç Uchimura and Danj≈ç Ebina. In the relationship between Japanese society and Christianity after 1900, Honda played an important role in establishing Christianity in Japan and running the organization.

In 1903, the Student YMCA and the Urban YMCA merged to form the Japanese YMCA. Between 1900 and 1910, the YMCA succeeded in expanding its influence, partly by cooperating with the Japanese in the Russo-Japanese War and by organizing the World‚Äôs Student Christian Federation. The Japanese YMCA‚Äôs comfort services during the Russo-Japanese War changed the Japanese impression of Christianity. Initially, the YMCA was regarded as an enemy of religion. However, thanks to the efforts of Tokyo YMCA director Suroku Ehara and YMCA committee chairman Yoichi Honda, consolation to the troops were realized (Nara, 123). The work included setting up a tent and other comfort sections in Korea and Manchuria, bathing and laundry for soldiers, barbershops, assistance in writing letters to families, entertainment, and evangelization through religious lectures (Nara, 123). Between July 1904 and March 1906, it spent more than 60000yen and provided those opportunities for many soldiers. Inoue Enry≈ç (1858-1919) was a Buddhist philosopher during the Meiji period, who critiqued Christianity from a theoretical standpoint based on Buddhism. Inoue was born in the Nagaoka domain (present-day Nagaoka City) in a J≈çdo Shinsh≈´ Buddhist temple. Throughout his life, Inoue energetically engaged in various activities such as writing, lecturing, giving speeches, and managing schools. His notable works on anti-Christianity were predominantly published in 2-3 years following his graduation from T≈çky≈ç Imperial University (Serikawa, 97) Some of his main works on anti-Christianity include ‚ÄúHaja Shinron‚Äù (Critique of Evil) , ‚ÄúYaso-ky≈ç no Nanmoku‚Äù (Difficulties of Christianity) and ‚ÄúShinri Konshin‚Äù (Golden Needle of Truth) published in 1885. Inoue believed that many anti-Christian arguments from the Buddhist side at that time were more emotional than logical or scholarly. Therefore, Inoue attempted to create a theoretical Christology using the analytical framework of Western philosophy, which developed on the foundation of Christianity (Kasahara, 197). For Inoue, Western philosophy encompassed the philosophical traditions of rationalism, which is rooted in the ideology of natural science, and idealism, represented by Kant‚Äôs philosophy. One of the characteristics of Inoue‚Äôs approach was his attempt to employ these theories of Western philosophy to criticize Christianity. He provided a theoretical critique in line with academic understanding of the time (Hazama, 257-258). From this, it becomes evident that Inoue prefered truth over uncertain things, such as superstition and spells. That is why he criticized ‚Äúuncertain things.‚Äù Additionally, Inoue is well-known as a researcher of yokai (supernatural creatures) and advocated the necessity of yokai criticism to debunk superstitions that mislead people. Kato Hiroyuki was a legal scholar during the Meiji period. He criticized Christianity from an evolutionary standpoint. Kato, born into a samurai family in the Tajima domain (present-day Hyogo Prefecture) in 1836, and he studied at the domain school. At the age of 17, he traveled to Edo and learned Western studies under the supervision of a famous scholar in the field, Sakuma Shozan. From 1860, Kato worked at Bansho shirabesho of Edo Bakufu, which investigated Western books. Through reading foreign books, he developed an interest inWestern philosophy, ethics, and geography (Tabata, 11). In 1861, Kato wrote ‚ÄúTonarigusa,‚Äù which introduced the concept of constitutionalism in Japan and discussed the necessity of the parliamentary system. After the Tokugawa shogunate fell in 1868, Kato continued to publish works on constitutionalism. In 1873, he joined the Meirokusha (the Society of Enlightenment) and continued his works. Meirokusha, aimed to enlighten the people and create a foundation for modern thought. In 1882, Kato wrote ‚ÄúJinken Shinsetsu‚Äô‚Äô (a New Theory of Human Rights), which denied the theory of Tenpujinken-ron (the view of the universal human rights given by heaven) from an evolutionary standpoint and developed a critique against Christianity. Kato was initially drawn to inherent rights despite being born in a strict feudal era with a rigid social hierarchy. However, he later shifted towards a position of social Darwinism and began to attack Christianity (Tabata,18).

After 1900, Kato wrote several articles criticizing Christianity, further intensifying his activities against it. In the 1907 publication ‚ÄúWagakokutai to Kirisutokyo‚Äù (our national polity and Christianity), Kato explained why he believed religion to be harmful, citing Herbert Spencer‚Äôs theory of social Darwinism. According to Kato, all religions lead humans into superstition and hinder scientific progress. He believed that significant progress and development would only be possible if humans free themselves from superstition. He mainly targeted Christianity, considering it to be filled with superstitions, and it was the greatest enemy of Japan (Tabata, 153). Furthermore, Kato argued that if Christianity were to spread and Christ were considered more honorable than the Emperor and the world more important than Japan, it would lead to a decline in loyalty and filial piety values in Japan (Tabata, 155). ‚ÄúWagakokui-tai to Kirisutokyo‚Äù was criticized not only by Christians but also by Buddhist scholars and foreign intellectuals. The Christian leaders, including Ebina Danjo of the Nihon Kumiai Kirisuto Kyokai ( Congregational Church), believed that Kato‚Äôs attack on Christianity did not provide an accurate critique of the religion since he merely attacked Christianity as a body of knowledge, without acknowledging the significance of faith and belief (Tabata, 405). Christians, including Ebina, thought that utilizing Christianity could strengthen the foundation of the imperial system and the national polity in Japan. From these criticisms, Kato selected sixteen arguments and further criticized them in the publication of ‚ÄúMeisou teki uchuu kan (Conflicting Views on the Universe)‚Äù in 1908. Based on the theory of social Darwinism, Kato launched a counterattack against the criticisms (Tabata,157). Furthermore, he wrote ‚ÄúKirisutoky≈çto Ky≈´su (Plight of Christians).‚Äù to counter the complaints from the Christian side, in 1909. Additionally, in 1911, he published ‚ÄúKirisutoky≈ç no Gaidoku (Harmfulness of Christianity),‚Äù which compiled these three books together, emphasizing the harmful effects of Christianity. However, when Kato engaged in the discourse of attacking Christianity, the political landscape had changed since Tetsujiro Inoue wrote ‚ÄúThe Conflict between the State and Christianity‚Äù in 1893. In the 1900s, the government began to acknowledge the contributions of Christianity to the nation, and they sought to utilize religions, including Christianity, to guide the hearts of the people. The conclusion of the Anglo-Japanese Alliance held great significance. However, after the Russo-Japanese War, the Japanese government, wanting to demonstrate a cooperative stance with the West, did not engage in criticism of Christianity. Kato accused Christianity of being superstitious, relying mainly on the logic of evolution that he acquired from Western knowledge. On the other hand, the repeated exchange of criticism through magazines and books between Kato and Ebina provided readers with knowledge about Christianity and evolutionism, giving them opportunities to think more deeply about these subjects.

During the early 1900s, it saw the rise of Anti-Christian movements after the Russo-Japanese War. (Ogawara,161-162) In 5-6 September 1905, a riot broke out due to dissatisfaction with the peace treaty following the Russo-Japanese War. After the congress , some of the participants of the event attacked the Home Ministry residence, the Foreign Ministry, newspaper offices, police stations, churches, and train stations, burning some 360 buildings to the ground. The government deployed the army to suppress the riot by declaring martial law. As a result, 17 people were killed, 2,000 were injured, and more than 2,000 people were detained. Moreover, some Japanese workers mobilized in the anti-Church movement too. Although many Christians, such as the Evangelical Alliance and the Christian Youth Association, had endorsed the Russo-Japanese War, the riot attacked many churches and destroyed the churches properties due to the xenophobic sentiment toward the west and dissatisfaction toward the result of the Russo-Japanese war. In Asakusa Ward alone, the anti-church movement has damaged churches such as the Komagata-cho Evangelical Mission, Sangen-cho Mipu Church, Kurofune-cho Covenant Church, Morishita-cho Salvation Army branch. The pastors made positive comments on the Russo-Japanese Peace Treaty and its subsequent conclusion during their services(Fujino, 45). The crowd had turned into an angry mob. The riot threw stones and wooden sticks to the pastors on the podium. While the pastors fled to the Komagata Evangelical Mission House to avoid the life threatening situation,the angry mob vandalized the Mission house and had thrown torches at the facility. This was partially due to the existing popular antipathy towards the pastor from the previous day‚Äôs and the 5th‚Äôs experiences, which immediately led to violence. Moreover, this was also because some held a misconception about the Christian pastors. It is not too difficult to speculate that local vendors and small business owners would express their resentment toward the Christian‚Äôs denouncement of Buddhism because their everyday lives were heavily depend on the Asakusa temple in the vicinity (possibly street vendors and expositors). In other words, one of the main reasons why anti-Christianity movement grew so quickly and unfolded in extreme violence can be highlighted as the Christians‚Äô biased view and criticism toward Buddhism as heresy. The local merchants and vendors thought the core religious doctrine of Christianity was at odds with local businesses.
