Japan: Context (1900-1910)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

In this period, there was no religion in Japan that was explicitly designated as the state religion. However, the Meiji government created a new ‚Äúreligion‚Äù called ‚ÄúState Shinto‚Äù in order to create a modern state centering on the emperor. The Meiji government separated the existing Shinto religion into two distinct branches: denominational Shinto, which was responsible for religion and rituals, and shrine Shinto, which served as the nation‚Äôs moral code. Teaching Shinto had its own gods and was considered a ‚Äúreligion‚Äù as a folk belief officially recognized by the Meiji government. Shrine Shinto, on the other hand, was given a special status as an important ritual of the state, and although it was sung as not a ‚Äúreligion,‚Äù it was combined with Emperor Shinto and reorganized as a state Shinto that honored the emperor and his family (Murakami, 57-58). The modern state was then centered on the ideology of the Emperor System, and this system was reinforced by the Imperial Japanese Constitution and the Imperial Rescript on Education. Therefore, even though ‚Äúfreedom of religion‚Äù was recognized in the Constitution of the Empire of Japan, it was limited to ‚Äúfreedom of religion‚Äù under the Emperor‚Äôs rule. With the enactment of the Constitution of the Empire of Japan and administrative measures, new religions and Christianity, which had previously been subject to various restrictions, were allowed to engage in religious activities similar to those of Shintoism and Buddhism. However, in order to prevent religions (especially new religions) from spreading doctrines and ideas that were contrary to the ‚Äúnational spirit,‚Äù restrictions were placed on them through disrespectful crimes and police punishment ordinances. The new penal code issued in April 1907 included ‚Äúcrimes against the imperial family‚Äù and made disrespect for the emperor‚Äôs family and the Jingu shrine a crime. This was the strongest state regulation in terms of doctrine for the new religion, which held a variety of deities as objects of worship (Inoue, 476-477). Article 2 of the decree stipulated that any person who spread falsehoods or false rumors, or who practiced witchcraft or prayer on a sick person, or who gave medical treatment to a sick person, and whom the police considered to be ‚Äúlewd and evil‚Äù was subject to punishment, and the expansion of new religions and other organizations was severely restricted (Inoue, 477). The Security Police Law of 1900 banned priests, ministers, monks, and other religious groups from joining political associations. This deprived professional religious people of the right to vote and prohibited them from political activity, thereby almost completely depoliticizing them, but it also created a system in which the government could make the most political use of them. Furthermore, in 1906, shrines throughout Japan were reorganized and consolidated, and in 1907 the Ministry of Home Affairs institutionalized rituals. This led to a series of cases in which rituals that had previously been formed in connection with the culture and customs of each region were modified into state Shinto rituals (Murakami, 86). This is thought to have had the significance of strengthening the influence of state Shinto. The Dajokan-tatsu No. 19, issued in August 1884, ended the traditional direct relationship between the state and religion, in which the state directly selected and appointed religious leaders, and moved Japan‚Äôs relationship between politics and religion into a new phase. However, it is said that this was not an end to the government‚Äôs laissez-faire attitude toward religion, but rather a reflection of the deterioration of the previous relationship between state and religion, and a measure aimed at more effectively using religion to mobilize it for national policy. This continued to constrain religion in modern Japan for the next 60 years until the implementation of the Religious Organizations Law in 1930 (Inoue, 473).

Social Hostility toward Christianity

Between 1900 and 1910, social hostilities towards Christianity decreased compared to the preceding decade. This can be attributed to the increase in young intellectuals and the effects of the Russo-Japanese War. Many university-educated young people, known as the ‚Äúintellectuals(Áü•Ë≠òÂ±§)‚Äù, gathered at the Hongo Church of Ebina Danjo and the nondenominational church of Uchimura Kanzo. Students also flocked to Masahisa Uemura‚Äôs church, and Sunday schools and student youth associations were more active than in the early Meiji period(Ohata, Ikado, 406). In Arimichi Ebisawa and Saburo Ouchi‚Äôs History of Christianity in Japan, a graph from 1928, which shows that the ‚ÄúReport of the Church Survey‚Äù edited by the Japan Christian Federation, shows that the number of Christian believers had increased rapidly from 1902. It shows a rapid increase in congregations since 1902 (Ouchi, 250). Also, during the Russo-Japanese War of 1904, the Christian churches in Japan were divided into two groups: those who cooperated with the war effort and those who were anti-war. The mainstream Christian churches actively cooperated in the war effort. For example, the Japanese government requested Honda Yoichi and Ibuka Kajinosuke to travel to foreign countries and to appeal that the Russo-Japanese war was not aggression. The Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association also went to the front lines with a tent to provide the soldiers with bathing, laundry, hairdressing, and preaching. This tent project by YMCA has significantly corrected the prejudice against Christianity in Japan(Ohata, Ikado, 409). It can be said that the nonpartisan religious meeting was one of the attempts to mobilize religious groups for the sake of the nation-state rather than encourage religious activities (Ebisawa Arimichi, Encyclopedia of the History of Christianity in Japan, 814).
