Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

The 1960s were a time when the Christian community actively participated in social movements, including opposition to the revision of the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty and efforts to establish Yasukuni Shrine as a state religion. It was also a year when Japan resumed exchanges with countries in Asia.

In January 1960, a meeting of Christians protesting the security treaty revision was held in Tokyo, and after the gathering, approximately 1,200 people sang hymns and participated in a street demonstration (Suzuki, 451). The defeat of the 1960 security treaty struggle also impacted Christian churches, forming the Christian Peace Association, Japan Fellowship of Reconciliation(JFOR), the Baptist Church, and the Non-Church Movement. This conference was formed under the influence of the World Christian Peace Conference held in Prague in 1961, which was attended by Christian peace activists worldwide (Suzuki, 451).

Peace movements

Furthermore, in July, the First World Conference on Religions for Peace was held in Kyoto, with participants including the Christian Peace Conference, the Japan Fellowship of Reconciliation, and the Christian Women‚Äôs Temperance Union. In September, the National Council of Churches in Japan issued a statement opposing U.S. nuclear testing. The Council of Religious Leaders for Peace in Japan (Sh≈´ky≈çsha Heiwa Ky≈çgikai ) was formed with representatives from Buddhism, Christianity, Shinto, Tenrikyo, and other new religions in 1962. They sent a statement opposing the Christmas Island nuclear tests to the U.S. president. It reflects the interfaith dialogue that characterized the 1960s, with Christianity transcending religious boundaries to engage in such activities.„ÄÄ

Following 1962, various Christian organizations for peace were established, and meetings were held in several places in Japan. The All Japan Christian Peace Conference(Zennihon Kirisutosha heiwa kaigi ) was held in 1962, and in April 1964, the Japan Christian Peace Association(Nihon kirisutosha heiwa no kai) was formed. In July, the Second World Conference of Religions for Peace was held in Tokyo. In May 1965, the Emergency Conference of Christians for Peace in Vietnam was established.

Anti- Yasukuni movement

In May and June 1960, Christians held rallies opposing the ratification of the new security treaty, but right-wing groups and gangsters sometimes attacked them. In March 1963, the NCC issued a statement opposing the national protection of Yasukuni Shrine, and in March 1969, the Christian Bereaved Families Association issued a statement opposing the Yasukuni Shrine bill. The Japan-U.S. security alliance was strengthened, and Japan‚Äôs rearmament was promoted. In 1965, the Central Council for Education of the Ministry of Education published an interim draft of ‚ÄúThe Ideal Human Being,‚Äù stating that ‚Äúloving one‚Äôs homeland, Japan, is the same as loving the Emperor.‚Äù It was strongly opposed by the Japan Teachers‚Äô Union, the Socialist Party, and the Christian community, who argued that it had ‚Äústrong parliamentary overtones‚Äù (Nakamura, 311)

Amidst such social circumstances, there was a growing movement to place Yasukuni Shrine under state control, and in 1969, the Yasukuni Shrine Bill was submitted to the Diet. This Japanese bill stipulated that Yasukuni Shrine would be placed under state control, that the government would conduct ceremonies and events, that the state would be involved in appointing officials, and that the state would bear part of the expenses and provide subsidies. In response to this move, opposition arguments were put forward, mainly by religious and civic groups, claiming that it was a move to restore the prewar status quo(Nakamura, 311). Other religious groups, including Christianity, opposed the move, arguing that it would give Yasukuni Shrine special status.
