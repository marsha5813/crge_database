Yamamuro Gunpei was the first Japanese Salvation Army officer.When he was young, he came to Tokyo from Okayama to study. In 1887, he attended the evangelical association ‚ÄúTsukiji Evangelical Church‚Äù in Tsukiji, Tokyo, then studied at a seminary (missionary school) and was baptized there. He entered Doshisha in 1889 and got interested in social work. As a Christian, Yamamuro had been interested in social issues, poverty, and the reformed movement since his days at Doshisha. This interest in social issues was also the basis for his enlistment in the Salvation Army and his lifelong thought. (Murota, 40) He could not graduate from Doshisha but worked for social problems and joined the Salvation Army in December 1895. Yamamuro actively addressed social issues in Japan. In 1900, he advocated for the voluntary cessation of prostitution, and in 1906, he established a labor referral center to solve the economic downturn after the Russo-Japanese War. In 1916, he initiated a tuberculosis control effort by founding a sanatorium, and in 1922, he launched a campaign to prevent child abuse. At the time, his activities paved a way to address some of the ongoing social problems such as the prohibition of prostitution, aiding farmers, rescuing and supporting victims of human trafficking after the Great Kanto Earthquake, and safeguarding human rights.Around 1918, the term ‚Äúsocial work (Shakai undo)‚Äù began to be commonly used in place of the word ‚Äúrelief (Kyusai),‚Äùand this period was called the ‚Äúperiod of the establishment of relief work‚Äù in Japan (Murota, 170). The central idea of Yamamuro was based on Christianity and the Bible, and the idea of transforming people at the bottom of society, thereby enabling further social transformation, was carried through. (Murota, 180-181) In contrast, most of the Protestant churches in Japan were not interested in alleviating the existing social problems. Therefore, Yamamuro‚Äôs social activism provided one of the important examples of what Christian social work can do for society (Akimoto, 1444-1445).

The Nihon Kumiai Kirisuto Kyokai (Japan Congregational Church) was founded in 1886, mainly by missionaries from the American Board(Congregational). Doshisha served as a training institute for this denomination, and graduates such as Ebina Danjo, Ozaki Hiromichi, and Miyagawa Keiki later became active as Christian leaders. Their evangelism took place mainly in large cities, and until the 1940s, it was one of the three major Protestant denominations in Japan (Takahashi, 1951-1952). Korean evangelism by Kumiai kyokai had been conducted for the Japanese since before 1910. After Korea was under Japanese rule, the Kumiai kyokai established a Korean mission department and began evangelizing Koreans (Nakamura, 29). The person in charge of the Korean mission of the Kumiai kyokai was Tsunekichi WataseÔºàÊ∏°ÁÄ¨ÊÅíÂêâ), who Danjo Ebina influenced. He had worked at the Gyeongseong School(‰∫¨ÂüéÂ≠¶Â†ÇÔºâ of the Dainippon Overseas Educational Association, an educational institution aimed at reconciliation between Japan and Korea, since 1899. In addition, he had experience working in Korea (Nakamura, 30). In 1913, he published a pamphlet entitled ‚ÄúChosen kyoka no kyumu(„ÄéÊúùÈÆÆÊïôÂåñ„ÅÆÊÄ•Âãô„Äè),‚Äù in which he justified the annexation of Korea and further argued that the Korean people needed not only political and economic support but also spiritual inspiration through religion. Watase's insistence on evangelizing Korea was integrated with the Japanese government's colonial policy (Nakamura, 32). The Kumiai kyokai established churches in Gyeongseong and Pyongyang in 1911 and subsequently showed tremendous growth.

By 1918, the Chosen Kumiai kyokai had grown rapidly to 149 member churches and 13,631 congregations. During this period, the Japanese Kumiai kyokai showed no significant growth, with the number of churches growing from 102 to 113 and the number of congregations from 16,630 to 20,427. Behind this growth in Korea, support from Japanese financial circles and intervention by the Office of the Governor-General of Korea were noted.

In 1914, the Kumiai kyokai‚Äôs Korean mission fund was 38,000 yen, more than double the annual budget of the Kumiai kyokai in Japan (Nakamura, 34). Since 10,000 yen at that time would be worth about 100 million yen today, a considerable amount of money was allocated for the Korean mission. The funds were provided by Shigenobu Okuma and other government officials who lobbied Mitsubishi, Mitsui, and other financial cliques(zaibatsu), and the Office of the Governor-General of Korea also paid a large amount of money anonymously from confidential funds (Nakamura, 34). Furthermore, it is believed that the intervention of the Office of the Governor-General of Korea was behind the large number of people joining the Kumiai churches (Nakamura, 35). It is highly likely that participation in the Japanese Kumiai church, rather than Western Christianity, was being promoted in Korea. In addition, the leap of the union church in Korea was seen as a result of the connection between the Governor-General of Korea and the Kumiai church. In other words, after under the Japan of Korea in 1910, the the Governor-General of Korea, who was struggling with colonial rule, welcomed the mission of the Kumiai churches advocating assimilation policies and expected the mission of the Kumiai churches to the Korean people to play a role as a pacifying agent to facilitate colonial rule. Therefore, the Governor-General and the financial cliques provided tremendous assistance for Kumiai church (Nakamura, 35-36).

Sophia University/‰∏äÊô∫Â§ßÂ≠¶

Sophia University is the first Catholic institution of higher education in Japan. The school was founded by the Jesuits in 1913. The Society of Foreign Missions of Paris mainly conducted Catholic missions in Japan, targeting the lower middle class. However, Catholic missions stagnated from the 1890s and reached an impasse in the early 20th century. They plan to establish Catholic higher education institutions to break through these deadlocks. Catholic leaders thought that (1) the image of the Catholic Church in Japan was not good (2) missionary methods were not adapted to the conditions of Japanese society, and (3) a lack of human and financial resources was behind the missionary slump. Therefore, they decided to make a new effort: the arrival of Jesuits in Japan and establishing an institution of higher learning by them (Miyoshi, 84-88). The papacy changed its policy on the Japanese mission, citing problems with the sole pastoral system by the Paris Foreign Mission and the lack of influence of French missionaries in the upper echelons of society. It was demonstrated through the multinationalization of missionary organizations and the establishment of higher education institutions (Miyoshi, 94). After 1904, various missionary groups came to Japan, including religious orders that focused on educational projects.

In October 1908, three Jesuits arrived in Japan, and Sophia University was established in 1913. At first, even though it was named ‚Äúuniversity,‚Äù it was legally classified as a ‚Äútechnical college. In 1918, the Japanese government issued a ‚ÄúUniversity Ordinance,‚Äù in 1928, Sophia University was officially approved as a university (Miyoshi96). Initially, the university had three departments: Philosophy, German Literature, and Commerce. The fact that foreign languages were English and German only, with no French at all, clearly shows the intent of de-France in university education (Miyoshi96). 16 students were enrolled in the first year in 1913, 91 in 1917, and 146 in 1920, making it a Catholic higher education institution in Japan. In 1917, there were 91 students enrolled, and in 1920, there were 146 students enrolled, making it a Catholic institution of higher education in Japan (‰∏äÊô∫Â§ßÂ≠¶Âè≤Ë≥áÊñôÈõÜÁ∑®Á∫ÇÂßîÂì°‰ºöÁ∑®, 1908, 47). Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

From 1910 to 1920, Japan colonized Korea and entered World War I as a member of the Allied side. Therefore„ÄÅ This period was referred to as the formation of ‚ÄúChristianity in the Japanese Empire („ÇΩ„Éª„Ç∏„Éß„É≥„Éü„É≥). National cooperative evangelism was a joint effort by Japanese denominations from 1914 to 1917. The World Missionary Conference was held in Edinburgh in June 1910, with the participation of Honda Yoichi and others from Japan. This marked the beginning of the ecumenical movement, as the traditional closed evangelism system of individual churches was replaced by cooperative evangelism (Dohi, 240; Ochi, 231; Ochi, 232). In 1913, John R. Mott (1865-1955), president of the World Missionary Council, visited Japan and encouraged the ecumenical movement in Japan (Dohi, 240; Ouchi, 792). With Mott‚Äôs visit to Japan, Japanese Christians organized a committee to continue the movement, with Kajinosuke Ibuka as a chairman, Masahisa Uemura representing the eastern side of Japan, and Kyoki Miyagawa representing the western side.

In this evangelistic movement, various methods were used, such as camp meetings, bicycle meetings, lectures, and conventions for girls students, office workers, educators, etc. The total number of meetings was 4,788, with 777,119 participants, 27,350 seekers, and more than 1 million tracts distributed (Ouchi, 792). Furthermore, due in part to the influence of the Taisho Democracy, the number of churches increased from 586 in 1910, including various denominations, to 1,056 in 1915 and to approximately 1,500 in 1920, tripling in 10 years, and the number of believers increased to 164,500 (Ouchi, 792). The following are three characteristics of this movement.

First, this mission was the first movement of the Christian Church in Japan to work with international organizations overseas. This helped solidify cooperation with foreign missions and shift the focus from independence during the Meiji period to domestic and international cooperation. Second, they conducted activities to use the mass media, especially newspapers. At the time, newspapers were becoming a means of information, and the church used them to evangelize. In addition, they also strengthened their ties with politicians, thus gaining greater access to the ruling class. Third, Masahisa Uemura was in charge of this evangelism. Although he had criticized cooperative evangelism by other denominations during the 20th-century Taikyo evangelism(„Äå20‰∏ñÁ¥ÄÂ§ßÊåô‰ºùÈÅì„Äç), he played a central role at this time (Dohi, 240-241).„ÄÄ
