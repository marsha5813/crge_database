Japanese New Testament scholar, theologian, and religious philosopher

Born: Yokohama, Kanagawa

Born and raised in a non-church movement father, Yagi graduated from the Department of German Language and Literature at the University of Tokyo in 1955, writing his thesis on Kierkegaard. He then attended graduate school and studied at the University of G√∂ttingen in Germany. During his studies abroad, he encountered Buddhism. He began to question whether the underlying principles of Christianity and Buddhism were not the same, leading him to depart from the framework of Christianity (Karatani) radically.

In 1960, he became a full-time lecturer at Kanto Gakuin University. However, due to his critical stance toward New Testament studies, he was asked not to teach in his classes and resigned from the university in defense of academic freedom. He then moved to the Tokyo Institute of Technology and continued his research. Yagi‚Äôs research began with New Testament studies and, through dialogue with Buddhism, sought to explore the essence of religion at the intersection of Christianity and Buddhism. Until the 1980s, he appeared to adopt a position extremely close to Buddhism.

As a result, while maintaining a critical stance toward traditional biblical interpretation, his understanding of Christianity at the intersection with Buddhism was highly radical, often leading to criticism from the church as heretical. (He may not have been a Christian.)

He served as president of the Japanese Society for Christian Studies.

Lee In-ha (Êùé‰ªÅÂ§è, June 15, 1925 ‚Äì June 30, 2008)

a pastor and an advocate for the human rights of foreign residents in Japan.

Born in Yeongdeok County, Gyeongsangbuk-do, Korea, Lee In-ha was born in 1925.

At 15, he moved to Japan to study and enrolled in the second year of Toji Middle School in Kyoto. There, he met Wada Masashi and studied English and the Bible. In 1943, during Easter, he was baptized at the Nishida-cho Church of the Japan Christian Church. After graduating from the Japan Christian Theological Seminary (now Tokyo Theological University) in 1952, he became a missionary at the Tama River Mission in Chofu, Tokyo. After graduation, he served as a missionary for the Korean Christian Church in Japan, working in the Tama River Mission in Chofu. From 1955 to 1957, he studied abroad at the University of Toronto in Canada. During his time abroad, he began to reflect on the connection between racial discrimination in the United States and the history of ethnic discrimination he had experienced in his own life.

In 1959, he returned to Japan and was assigned to Japan‚Äôs Kawasaki Church of the Korean Christian Church.

One of his primary roles was accompanying and serving as interpreter for Isamu Ohmura, who represented the United Church of Christ in Japan and the Japan Christian Council, during his visit to Seoul in September 1965. This visit was realized at the invitation of the Presbyterian Church of Korea and marked the first time that representatives of the postwar Japanese church spoke at the General Assembly. At the assembly, Ohmura apologized for Japan‚Äôs wartime aggression and the sins of the United Church of Christ in Japan.

Another significant role played by Lee was his leadership in the legal battle against employment discrimination against Koreans in Japan at Hitachi in the early 1970s, where he served as a co-convener and co-representative of the ‚ÄúSupport Group for Mr. Park.‚Äù At that time, there was a strong trend toward ‚Äúassimilation or exclusion,‚Äù and many Koreans in Japan were forced to use Japanese names. This movement later led to the establishment of the ‚ÄúCoordinating Council Against Ethnic Discrimination,‚Äù where Lee was appointed as the national representative.

In 1969, a kindergarten was established that accepted children of various nationalities, including Japanese, Koreans, and North Koreans, to break down ethnic barriers.

In 1983, Lee served as the representative in the fingerprint refusal lawsuit. Under the previous ‚ÄúForeign Registration Law,‚Äù foreigners residing in Japan must submit fingerprints for registration certificates. These were deemed discriminatory toward Koreans and Chinese residing in Japan, and complaints began to surface in the early 1980s. In this movement, Lee took the lead in the fight, and in May 1985, it led to the revision of the fingerprinting method for foreigners.

His church served as a refuge for Koreans facing discrimination in Japan and facilitated exchanges between Koreans and Japanese, playing a bridging role between the two countries.

February 1981: Visit of John Paul II to Japan

During his visit to Japan, John Paul II visited churches in Tokyo, Hiroshima, and Nagasaki, celebrated Mass, and appealed for peace. Following the Second Vatican Council, the Catholic Church has actively pursued a course of action aimed at implementing liturgical reforms, religious freedom, ecumenical movements with Protestant churches, interfaith dialogue, world peace, human rights issues, North-South issues, environmental issues, and other social justice concerns. (The Christ Weekly, 107) John Paul II appealed for peace worldwide during his visit to Hiroshima and Nagasaki.
