Japan: Context (1930-1940)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

One of the most representative regulations concerning religion during this period was the enactment of the Religious Organizations Law (Sh≈´ky≈ç Dantai H≈ç). This law was promulgated on April 8, 1939, and came into effect on April 1, 1940. Enacted after the National Spiritual Mobilization Law, its purpose was to bring religious organizations under state control and integrate them into the total war mobilization system.

Attempts to enact a basic law concerning religion had been made repeatedly since the Meiji era (in 1899, 1927, 1929, and 1935), but all failed to pass (Uemura, Kindle 44). In the early Sh≈çwa period, religious affairs were handled by the Ministry of Education‚Äôs Bureau of Religion, while shrine affairs were managed separately by the Ministry of Home Affairs‚Äô Bureau of Shrines. Since the Meiji era, it had been the government‚Äôs policy to treat shrines managed by the state not as religion but as a form of state administration distinct from religious governance (Uemura, Kindle 44). However, from the 1930s onward‚Äîamid events like the Manchurian Incident and the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War‚ÄîJapanese society increasingly moved toward nationalism and a wartime regime (Hara, 19). As a result, the Religious Organizations Law, which had previously failed repeatedly, was enacted with little resistance.

The Religious Organizations Law was a concise piece of legislation consisting of 37 articles, but its enforcement regulations contained 75 articles, and the detailed local implementation rules established by prefectures added another 22 articles. These included extensive paperwork, such as application and reporting forms submitted to the prefectural governors (Kaino, 28).

Under the law, the establishment of a religious organization required the permission of the Minister of Education (Article 3). Moreover, religious activities were to be monitored to ensure they did not disturb public peace or violate the duties of subjects of the Emperor (Hara, 20). Consequently, if a religious group‚Äôs activities were judged to contradict the moral or political order of the imperial system, they could be strictly suppressed.

The law clearly distinguished between religious organizations and religious associations. Religious organizations recognized under the law were granted legal corporate status and certain privileges, while groups not recognized as such were treated as quasi-religious bodies without privileges and remained under government supervision. Recognized religious organizations received tax exemptions (e.g., from income tax) and protections such as immunity from asset seizure. Although the qualifications and personnel decisions of religious teachers were left to the autonomy of each organization, the law also imposed obligations for reporting and required government approval for various matters, giving the state significant means of intervention (Hara, 23).

In June 1940, following the enactment of the law, the Ministry of Education‚Äôs Bureau of Religion presented the conditions and policies for approval of religious organizations at a meeting with religious leaders.
