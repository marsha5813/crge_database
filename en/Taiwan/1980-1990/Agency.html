Taiwan: Agency (1980-1990)

Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

Daniel Dai (Êà¥Áæ©Âã≥, January 1, 1930 ‚Äì April 23, 2022)

Born in Hsinchu, Taiwan, Daniel Dai was a Hakka pastor and the founder and director of the China Christian Prayer Institute. He initially worked as a foreign affairs police officer but later converted to Christianity under the missionary work of Helen Elizabeth Gilkerson. He began participating in Bible study sessions at Gilkerson‚Äôs Bilaka Church in Hsinchu and subsequently enrolled in the Baptist Theological Seminary.

After graduating, he pioneered Miaoli Baptist Church in 1958, the first Hakka-speaking Baptist church in Taiwan. He later worked with Campus Crusade for Christ, promoting the booklet The Four Spiritual Laws as part of Taiwan‚Äôs ‚ÄúEvangelistic Explosion‚Äù movement, which helped him establish connections with numerous churches.

In 1974, Dai attended a large-scale prayer gathering in South Korea, where he witnessed 1.5 million people praying for their nation, which deeply moved him. Inspired by this, he began overnight prayer meetings in 1975, starting with just four people in his home. The gatherings quickly grew, sometimes accommodating 20 to 30 people in a small space, praying continuously day and night. After another visit to Korea in 1978, he envisioned establishing large-scale prayer venues in Taiwan similar to Korea‚Äôs prayer mountains.

In 1981, Yeh Rong-chao, a believer who had accompanied Dai to Korea, responded to this vision by donating land near Tanwen Lake in Zaoqiao Township, Miaoli County, to build a prayer mountain. In June of the same year, the site was officially named the ‚ÄúChina Christian Prayer Institute,‚Äù with Dai serving as its director.

This institution played a crucial role in the development of Protestantism in Taiwan from the 1980s to the 2000s, particularly in advancing the Pentecostal and Charismatic movements. It also significantly influenced indigenous churches. Many pastors and believers from indigenous congregations, such as the Amis Guangfu Church (now Mataian Church), the Bunun Qingshui Church, the Mayuan Church, the Paiwan Caopu Holiness Church, and the Atayal Tianpu Church, participated in gatherings at the prayer mountain. These experiences exposed them to the charismatic dimension of the Holy Spirit‚Äôs work, which subsequently shaped their respective churches. Moreover, various ‚Äúprayer mountains‚Äù and ‚Äúprayer institutes‚Äù across indigenous communities were largely inspired by this movement (Shih 2012a: 120-132; Yeh 2012: 93; Rubinstein 1996: 356-357; Tan n.d.: 2-4; Chang 2009: 548-549).

Chris Su (ËòáÂçóÊ¥≤, October 14, 1953 ‚Äì February 27, 2020)

Born in Taipei, Chris Su was a Christian social activist, the founder and president of Agape Publishing House and The Wilderness magazine. He earned a bachelor‚Äôs degree in architecture from Tunghai University and a master‚Äôs degree from National Taiwan University‚Äôs Graduate Institute of Civil Engineering (now the Graduate Institute of Building and Planning).

During his university years, Su converted to Christianity and extensively engaged with literature, history, and philosophy, which laid the foundation for his intellectual pursuits. He also served as the editor-in-chief of the Christian campus magazine The Vineyard, fostering his deep passion and sense of mission for writing. In 1982, he married Peng Hai-ying, and together they opened their home for fellowship, gathering Christian friends to discuss and reflect on various church and social issues.

In 1987, Su co-founded Agape Publishing House and The Wilderness magazine with Kang Lai-hsin and Peng Hai-ying. Unlike other Christian publications at the time, their works extended beyond devotional literature, Christian living, and church affairs to cover faith-based critical thinking, social engagement, environmental conservation, literature, art, and public theology.

Su was actively involved in social justice issues. In late 1989, he organized the ‚ÄúSupport Liu Xia, Care for the Disabled‚Äù seminar and prayer meeting to advocate for the famous disabled writer Liu Xia, who was barred from running for the Legislative Yuan due to academic qualifications requirements. He also initiated the ‚ÄúFebruary 28 Peace Movement/Worship Service,‚Äù the ‚ÄúAnti-Child Prostitution Manifesto,‚Äù and supported environmental protection and historic preservation efforts.

In 1975, after Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs death, certain ‚ÄúParty-State Christians‚Äù planned to construct the ‚ÄúChiang Kai-shek Memorial Church‚Äù on the site of Jinan Presbyterian Church. By 1989, fundraising for the project was nearly complete. While working on his master‚Äôs thesis in 1988, Su studied the architectural space of Jinan Church and unexpectedly discovered plans for the Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Church. He passed the information to Pastor Ong Siu-kiong, who deemed it a serious matter and informed President Lee Teng-hui. In 1990, Lee ordered the church to be designated a historical site, halting the construction project. Su‚Äôs thesis research indirectly led to the preservation of Jinan Presbyterian Church‚Äôs historic sanctuary.

Su is best known for his advocacy for the families of the victims of the February 28 Incident. Before Taiwan‚Äôs martial law was lifted in the late 1980s, he had already begun researching and supporting these families. Beginning in early 1989, The Wilderness magazine featured discussions on the February 28 Incident in every issue. This effort culminated in the ‚ÄúRespect Human Rights, Commemorate 228‚Äù peace worship service on December 8, 1990, marking the beginning of the ‚ÄúFebruary 28 Peace Movement.‚Äù This movement took a softer, more conciliatory approach centered on reconciliation and care for the suffering, contrasting with the Presbyterian Church‚Äôs stance of ‚Äújustice and peace, with justice preceding peace.‚Äù Later, Su collaborated with Lin Tsung-yi, the son of February 28 victim Lin Mao-sheng, to push for the government‚Äôs public apology, compensation for victims‚Äô families, and the establishment of a memorial monument (Tseng Ching-pao 2016: 339-343; Peng Hai-ying 2020: 1-2; Lin Shu-fen 2024: 55-57; Wang Chao-wen 2010; Liang Ching-yen 2020: 1).

Chiang Peng-chien (Ê±üÈµ¨Â†Ö, April 25, 1940 ‚Äì December 15, 2000)

Born in Taipei, Chiang Peng-chien was a politician, human rights lawyer, and a founding member of the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP), serving as its first party chairperson. He was also a legislator and a member of the Control Yuan.

In 1958, Chiang entered the Department of Law at National Taiwan University. He passed the national bar exam in 1964 and became a practicing lawyer. His political career began in 1980 when he defended Lin Yi-hsiung, one of the main defendants in the Kaohsiung Incident trial, where key figures were charged with sedition in a military court.

In 1983, Chiang was elected as a legislator, vowing not to seek re-election unless martial law was lifted. True to his promise, he declined to run again after his three-year term ended. In September 1986, despite the continued ban on opposition parties, he helped establish the DPP at the Grand Hotel Taipei. On November 10 of the same year, he narrowly defeated Fei Hsi-ping by one vote to become the party‚Äôs first chairperson. The competition between Chiang and Fei arose partly because both were legislators, making them less vulnerable to immediate arrest by the Kuomintang (KMT) government, which indirectly provided a degree of protection for the nascent party.

In 1987, the Legislative Yuan passed the National Security Law, which imposed three restrictions on the DPP: it could not violate the Constitution, advocate communism, or promote national division. Nevertheless, President Chiang Ching-kuo refused to recognize the DPP, and by the end of the year, he instructed Chief of the General Staff Hau Pei-tsun to ensure the military was prepared for potential unrest.

Although Chiang Peng-chien served as DPP chair for only one year, he played a crucial role in shaping the party‚Äôs foundational structure. He established both the central and local party offices and advocated for internal democratic processes. He also created a negotiation mechanism within the party to mediate conflicts between the legislative caucus, party staff, and party leadership, as well as to ease tensions between the parliamentary and grassroots factions.

After stepping down as party chair, Chiang continued to serve as a legislator and later as a member of the Control Yuan. During his tenure in the Control Yuan, he reopened investigations into major cases such as the Lin Family Massacre, the Kaohsiung Incident, and the Su Bing-kun wrongful conviction case (Chen Shun-chen 2007: 133-183).

Although Chiang frequently interacted with members of the Presbyterian Church in his early years, he formally converted to Christianity and was baptized only shortly before his passing.

Lin Tsung-cheng (ÊûóÂÆóÊ≠£, March 4, 1950 ‚Äì )

Born in Tainan, Lin Tsung-cheng is a pastor of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan and a social activist. His father, Lin Chan-ao, was influenced by Tsai Pei-huo and joined the Taiwan Cultural Association, which aimed to resist Japanese rule through cultural activism. He later converted to Christianity.

Lin Tsung-cheng graduated from the graduate program at Tainan Theological College and Seminary in 1979. During his studies, he was influenced by Professor Wang Hsien-chih‚Äôs Contextual Theology and was introduced to the democratic movement by Professor Hsieh Hsiu-hsiung and to Taiwan‚Äôs Urban Rural Mission (URM) by Professor Hsieh Min-chuan. In 1985, he traveled abroad to participate in the fourth session of the Christian Urban Rural Mission (URM) training, a movement that extended beyond evangelism to include the promotion of nonviolent resistance. After returning to Taiwan, he became the chairman of URM, incorporating its principles into his social activism and political resistance.

Since 1980, Lin was actively involved in the democratic movement outside the KMT‚Äôs control, giving speeches and campaigning for the families of those persecuted in the Kaohsiung Incident. He was accused by the Kaohsiung District Prosecutor‚Äôs Office of violating election laws and inciting sedition, but the case was dropped due to insufficient evidence. That same year, he became a minister at the Tainan Presbyterian University Student Center, where he led students in addressing environmental and Indigenous issues. He became increasingly aware that many of the cheap laborers recruited for Taiwan‚Äôs Ten Major Construction Projects in the late 1970s were Indigenous people. Additionally, after the 1984 Haishan Coal Mine explosion, which claimed many Indigenous workers‚Äô lives, he dedicated himself to Indigenous rights advocacy.

One of his early contributions to Indigenous activism occurred in March 1987 when the Nantou County government forcibly exhumed the ancestral graves of the Bunun people in Dongpu Village, citing their obstruction of a planned parking lot for a tourist hotel. The government left the remains exposed, prompting Indigenous protests. Lin helped organize the demonstration, marking the first large-scale Indigenous protest in Taiwan‚Äôs history.

His most well-known activism took place on December 31, 1988, with the Removal of the Wufeng Statue. This protest, planned alongside representatives from eleven Indigenous tribes, members of the Presbyterian Church, and Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) members, aimed to dismantle the statue of Wufeng and challenge the discriminatory Wufeng Myth, which depicted the Tsou people as savages. Lin and other participants were arrested but were later acquitted. During the case proceedings, Ho Kang-mei, a Taiwan independence advocate based at the Catholic University of Leuven in Belgium, informed Pope John Paul II of the event through the Catholic Peace and Justice Committee, prompting the Pope to send a letter to Taiwan‚Äôs Presidential Office. In 1989, the National Institute for Compilation and Translation removed the Wufeng story from school textbooks, and the Ministry of the Interior renamed Wufeng Township as Alishan Township.

On February 25, 1987, Lin participated in Cheng Nan-jung‚Äôs 228 Peace Memorial March, the first public commemoration of the February 28 Incident. That same year, when The Taiwan Church News was confiscated by the Taiwan Garrison Command, Lin organized a protest involving over eighty pastors, which ultimately pressured the authorities to return the publication.

In 1988, when political exile Chen Tsui-yu passed away and a funeral service was held in Taiwan, Lin deliberately led the funeral procession toward the Presidential Office to protest the KMT government‚Äôs past persecution of Chen.

Throughout the 1980s, Lin Tsung-cheng played a crucial role in major protests, making him one of the most representative figures among Presbyterian clergy involved in Taiwan‚Äôs social movements (Chiu Fei-hsien 2012: 122-147).

New Testament Church (Êñ∞Á¥ÑÊïôÊúÉ)

The New Testament Church was founded in Hong Kong in 1963 by former actress Kong Duen-yee (stage name Mui Yee) and her followers, advocating a form of ‚Äúreligious reformation.‚Äù It had Pentecostal roots, emphasizing ‚ÄúHoly Spirit infilling‚Äù while rejecting all denominational organizations, including Pentecostal ones, as corrupt and apostate.

In 1965, Kong Duen-yee established the church in Taiwan during a period of schism within the Local Church movement, attracting many followers. Hong San-chi (later renamed Hong Elijah) emerged as the leader and dominant figure of the church in Taiwan. The church also established congregations in Malaysia and Singapore.

In 1966, Kong prophesied that ‚Äúthe true gospel will spread, a wondrous revival will begin across the world, and the Holy Spirit‚Äôs flow will move from the East, where God has established the New Testament Church, to the West, reaching all nations.‚Äù However, she passed away later that year, and leadership transitioned to her daughter, Ruth Chang, who emphasized the authority of ‚Äúprophets and apostles‚Äù and introduced the concepts of the Foreman and the Vessel of the Age. She claimed that in every biblical era, God appointed a religious leader as the Foreman, the highest-ranking apostle, assisted by co-workers who carried out evangelistic and church-building missions under the Foreman‚Äôs direction. Hong Elijah, claiming divine revelation, declared Ruth Chang as Kong‚Äôs rightful successor.

After 1969, Ruth Chang gradually distanced herself from some of Kong‚Äôs more extreme teachings. In 1976, she formally renounced the Foreman and Vessel of the Age doctrines, a move that displeased the majority of New Testament Church congregations in Taiwan. As a result, the Taiwanese church, under Hong Elijah‚Äôs leadership, became the new global center of the movement, declaring him as God‚Äôs newly anointed ‚Äúspiritual leader‚Äù in Taiwan. He revived and reinforced Kong Duen-yee‚Äôs original doctrines.

During the 1980s, the church was most well-known in Taiwan for establishing a religious utopian community called Mount Zion on a hillside in Namasia District, Kaohsiung (formerly Shuanglian Ku, Jiaxian Township). As early as 1963, Hong Elijah and his followers had begun developing the land. In 1978, he formally declared that God had chosen the site as the present-day Mount Zion. The following year, he decided to permanently settle there, relocating the church‚Äôs headquarters from Nangang, Taipei.

Hong Elijah introduced what he called ‚Äúthe greatest revelation of the age‚Äù: One Man, One Mountain‚Äî‚Äúone man‚Äù referring to himself, and ‚Äúone mountain‚Äù to Mount Zion. He and his followers practiced communal living, sharing all possessions and living in significant isolation from mainstream society.

From the 1970s onward, conflicts between the church and the government over land use escalated. Authorities accused Mount Zion residents of falsifying household registrations, illegally occupying state-owned land, and posing a national security risk due to their communal lifestyle. The church, on the other hand, believed the ‚Äúsecular kingdom‚Äù (the government) was attempting to seize their ‚Äúdivine homeland.‚Äù

Throughout the 1980s, the government launched a series of crackdowns, known as Project Qingyue (Ê∏ÖÂ≤≥Â∞àÊ°à), against Mount Zion residents:

  • January 1980: Police forcibly evicted church members from the mountain.

  • May 1980: Authorities confiscated properties left behind by the evicted believers.

  • 1983: The government demolished houses belonging to church members.

  • 1980‚Äì1983: Evicted members camped in Xiaolin Riverbank in protest, only to have their shelters razed again and be imprisoned.

  • 1985: A confrontation at the airport between church members and police led to multiple arrests.

  • 1986: Hong Elijah and his followers attempted to return to Mount Zion, leading to violent clashes with security forces, during which Hong suffered serious injuries.

The church fiercely resisted government intervention, often using religious rhetoric to condemn and curse state authorities, attributing their misfortunes to divine retribution. When Chiang Ching-kuo passed away in 1988, the church declared that he had died as divine punishment, claiming he ‚Äúbled from all seven orifices‚Äù upon death.

By late 1986, the government allowed Mount Zion residents who had legal household registration to return. After Taiwan‚Äôs democratization, the government took a more passive approach toward the church, but tensions between the two persisted, with street protests continuing until 1991.

Throughout the 1980s, the New Testament Church was one of the most vocal opponents of the Kuomintang (KMT) regime, often surpassing even the Presbyterian Church in its confrontational stance. The church‚Äôs protest banners and literature frequently contained denunciations such as ‚ÄúChiang Ching-kuo is a tyrant‚Äù and ‚ÄúThe KMT is a dictatorship.‚Äù

In 1997, the church escalated its opposition to the state by calling on its members‚Äô children to collectively withdraw from the public school system (which they termed ‚Äúsecular education‚Äù) and instead enroll in ‚ÄúEden Homes,‚Äù church-run institutions across Taiwan providing what they called ‚ÄúGod-centered education.‚Äù

Today, Mount Zion remains in existence and has become a tourist attraction in Kaohsiung. Over 200 residents still live there, and the New Testament Church continues to have a global following exceeding 10,000 members (Peng Fei 1997: 329-341, 371-379; Zhu Zhaohua 2021: 134-139; Shao Huizhe 2016: 32-45; Xue Huayuan 2022: 185, 49-113; Rubinstein 1991: 143-147).

Elim Book Store (‰ª•Áê≥Êõ∏Êàø) and Hosanna Ministries (ÂíåÊííÈÇ£‰∫ãÂ•â‰∏≠ÂøÉ)

Elim Book Store is a Christian publishing house with Pentecostal/Charismatic roots. Its origins trace back to the ministry of Nicholas Krushnisky, a Ukrainian-Canadian missionary from the Christian and Missionary Alliance. In 1974, Krushnisky began promoting Charismatic beliefs and experiences while serving as a counselor for the Huagang Fellowship at Chinese Culture University and at Te Sheng Church. From 1975 onward, he organized winter and summer retreats in Wanli, inviting Pentecostal/Charismatic speakers from various backgrounds. These gatherings significantly influenced many students to pursue Charismatic experiences.

In 1981, Krushnisky established Elim Christian Center. One of his influenced students, Chang Chi-ming, the son of a businessman, founded Elim Book Store on November 25, 1982, followed by the establishment of Hosanna Ministries in 1987. These two platforms were created to promote Pentecostal/Charismatic beliefs.

Elim Book Store systematically translated and published a large number of foreign Pentecostal/Charismatic works. However, due to its association with the Charismatic movement, it faced resistance from the broader Taiwanese Christian community, particularly in its first five years of operation. In 1983, the bookstore launched Spring Rain Magazine, which was later renamed Hosanna Monthly in 1994. The magazine primarily featured translated articles by international Pentecostal/Charismatic leaders, introducing the movement to a Taiwanese audience that largely remained skeptical toward Charismatic teachings. It also addressed theological concerns regarding the Charismatic movement within Taiwanese churches.

The theological foundation of Elim Book Store was closely tied to the Latter Rain Movement of 1948, which differed from traditional Pentecostalism and Charismatic theology in several aspects. It also introduced elements from the New Apostolic Reformation, a more recent movement within Charismatic Christianity.

In 1989, Elim Book Store introduced Praise and Worship music and worship styles from abroad. Initially, they translated and published New Song Hymns, a collection of worship songs from Western traditions. Later, they invited Ha Yong-inn, a Korean worship leader, to conduct worship conferences in Taiwan. This effort gradually led to the acceptance of contemporary worship music and styles in Taiwanese churches.

While Elim Book Store adopted a softer approach to promoting Charismatic beliefs, Hosanna Ministries took a more direct stance, urging churches to acknowledge the Holy Spirit‚Äôs work. In the second half of 1988, Hosanna Ministries organized two Holy Spirit Renewal Seminars, each lasting twelve weeks, strengthening its connection with local Pentecostal/Charismatic groups.

During the 1980s, Elim Book Store/Hosanna Ministries and Prayer Mountain were the two most influential forces in introducing Charismatic practices to Taiwanese churches. Of the two, Elim Book Store had a more enduring impact (Lin 2020: 169-172).

Operation Dawn (Êô®Êõ¶ÊúÉ)

Operation Dawn is Taiwan‚Äôs first institution dedicated to Gospel-based drug rehabilitation. In 1983, Lin Chih-ping, chairman of the Cosmic Light Holistic Care Foundation, became increasingly concerned about Taiwan‚Äôs growing drug problem. He invited Pastor Lau Man-wo, a former drug addict and member of Hong Kong‚Äôs Operation Dawn, to Taiwan to share his testimony on addiction and recovery. In 1984, Paul Chan, the founder of Hong Kong‚Äôs Operation Dawn, sent Lau Man-wo, his newlywed wife, and Pastor Kong Tak-lai‚Äôs family to Taiwan to establish a Gospel-based drug rehabilitation ministry.

In 1989, Operation Dawn Taiwan was officially registered as a Christian foundation. The organization gradually established rehabilitation centers in Miaoli, Taitung, Tainan, and Kaohsiung. Unlike conventional drug rehabilitation programs that rely on sedatives and detox medication, Operation Dawn emphasizes the holistic integration of body, mind, and spirit, based on the Christian belief that humans possess a spiritual nature.

Operation Dawn advocates using Christian teachings as a method to help addicts overcome drug dependency and reintegrate into society. Participants must commit to an 18-month residential rehabilitation program in one of Operation Dawn‚Äôs drug rehabilitation villages. Their Gospel-based therapy, known as spiritual therapy, consists of four stages:

  1. God‚Äôs Calling (spiritual rebirth)

  2. Internalization of Faith (obedience and repentance)

  3. God‚Äôs Abundance (emptying oneself and being filled with the Holy Spirit)

  4. God‚Äôs Commission (responding to the call and dedicating oneself)

The core practices involve prayer, Bible study, counseling, and religious instruction. Graduates who feel a divine calling may enroll in Operation Dawn‚Äôs training center to become counselors and assist other addicts. Those who do not choose this path either reintegrate into society independently or transition to halfway houses.

Other Gospel-based drug rehabilitation programs in Taiwan include House of the Lord‚Äôs Love (established in 1984) and Grace House (established in 1993). However, Operation Dawn remains the most well-known Christian drug rehabilitation institution in Taiwan. To date, it has assisted over 6,000 individuals, with a rehabilitation success rate of approximately 20% (Mo Shao-chen & Hsu Chia-yun 1994: 20-22; Chou Tzu-ching 2006: 21; Wan Shu-min 2022: 34-39).

Wilderness Society (Êõ†ÈáéÁ§æ)

The Wilderness Society was closely linked to The Wilderness magazine, which was first published in 1987. The society was officially founded in January 1988 by a group of interdenominational Christian intellectuals, mostly in their 40s. Su Nan-chou was the key organizer. The initial group consisted of over 30 members, many of whom were second-generation waishengren (Mainlanders who came to Taiwan after 1949) but identified themselves as the ‚Äúfirst generation of native Taiwanese.‚Äù Their church backgrounds were predominantly within the Evangelical or Fundamentalist Chinese-speaking churches.

However, unlike their home churches‚Äîwhich either emphasized spirituality in an apolitical sense or leaned toward the pro-KMT party-state ideology‚Äîmembers of the Wilderness Society embraced local consciousness and engaged deeply in theological and social reflection. From its inception, both The Wilderness magazine and the Wilderness Society expressed sympathy and concern for the families of the victims of the February 28 Incident (228 Incident).

During this period, members studied the theological works of Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906‚Äì1945) and Yang Mu-ku‚Äôs The Weeping Prophet Jeremiah, reinforcing their belief that they should ‚Äúweep with those who weep.‚Äù Starting in early 1989, several issues of The Wilderness magazine focused on the 228 Incident. After the events of June 4, 1989 (Tiananmen Square Massacre), Su Nan-chou and other members felt a heightened responsibility as Christians to confront and process Taiwan‚Äôs historical trauma. This led to their decision to organize the 228 Peace Memorial Service.

With over 60 church leaders collaborating, the 228 Peace Memorial Service was held on December 8, 1990, at Huai-en Church (Êá∑ÊÅ©Â†Ç). The service was attended by key political figures from across the spectrum, including Premier Hau Pei-tsun. The Wilderness Society framed the event as a way to address political issues through religious means. However, it was still widely perceived as a political act.

The event faced criticism from multiple sides:

  • Many members of Mandarin-speaking churches opposed the service, seeing it as overly political and biased toward benshengren (native Taiwanese). Some even sent anonymous letters attacking Pastors Chou Lien-hua and Ong Siu-kiong, accusing them of politicizing religion.

  • Some Presbyterian Church leaders were concerned that the event might be manipulated by the KMT government for its own political ends.

Despite the controversy, the 228 Peace Memorial Service was a milestone event in Taiwan‚Äôs church history, and the Wilderness Society played a pivotal role in making it happen (Wu Kuo-an 2015: 72-74).

However, after its engagement with the 228 Issue, the society drew intense attention from both political factions and became the subject of heavy media coverage, including deliberate efforts by certain media outlets to sow discord and incite conflict. These pressures ultimately led to the dissolution of the Wilderness Society after just 30 months of existence (Su Nan-chou 2016).

Anti-Child Prostitution Movement (ÂèçÈõõÂ¶ìÈÅãÂãï)

In 1985, the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) held a seminar titled Tourism and Prostitution. The Presbyterian Church in Taiwan played a crucial role in the Anti-Child Prostitution Movement, ultimately contributing to the passage of the Child and Youth Sexual Transaction Prevention Act in 1995 (initially called the Child Prostitution Prevention Act). The Confession of Faith of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) (Âè∞ÁÅ£Âü∫Áù£Èï∑ËÄÅÊïôÊúÉ‰ø°‰ª∞ÂëäÁôΩÁôºÂ∏É)

From April 9 to 12, 1985, the 32nd General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) passed the Confession of Faith of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan. On April 11, all assembly members read it aloud sentence by sentence, and after a third reading, it was officially approved. The final text was confirmed on May 9, 1985, by the Committee on Faith and Church Order.

In fact, as early as April 16, 1918, during the 7th Taiwan Assembly, Moderator Tan Chheng-gi proposed drafting a confession of faith, leading to the establishment of a Confession Drafting Committee to prepare a statement for review by the North, Central, and South Presbyteries. However, no conclusion was reached at the time.

By 1978, the PCT reinitiated the drafting process, emphasizing that the confession should be deeply rooted in Taiwan and serve as a public declaration of faith to all Taiwanese people, rather than merely a doctrinal statement for the denomination or the wider Christian community. The adoption of this confession marked the 120th anniversary of the church‚Äôs mission in Taiwan, symbolizing a historic milestone.

The third section of the confession states that the church is a fellowship of God‚Äôs people, called to proclaim the salvation of Jesus Christ, to be agents of reconciliation, universal yet locally rooted, identifying with all inhabitants, becoming a sign of hope through love and suffering. It further declares:

‚ÄúHe will set people free from sin, bring liberation and equality to the oppressed, renew humanity in Christ, and transform the world into His kingdom, filled with justice, peace, and joy.‚Äù (Understanding the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, Editorial Team 2010: 31-32).

This theological stance differs significantly from many other Christian confessions, which primarily emphasize doctrinal orthodoxy and objectivity. Instead, the PCT‚Äôs confession places a strong focus on contemporary social contexts, adopting a contextual theological interpretation that is actively practiced.

Moreover, this confession aligns with the PCT‚Äôs longstanding commitment to holistic faith, which, since the 1970s, has regarded social justice and political engagement as integral to Christian faith.

The 228 Peace Service

On the evening of December 8, 1990, at 7:30 PM, the landmark 228 Peace Service was held at the Wesley Grove Chapel. Prior to the lifting of martial law, the 228 Incident had long been a taboo and sensitive topic in Taiwanese society. However, after martial law was lifted, the atmosphere shifted dramatically, and discussions about the 228 Incident gradually increased across various sectors. Within the church community, some individuals began addressing the event from a perspective of reconciliation. In 1989, Su Nanzhou, then the president of Wilderness Society, conceived the idea of organizing a 228 Peace Service. He gained support from Ong Siu-kiong, the pastor of Jinan Presbyterian Church, who frequently led family worship services for Lee Teng-hui, as well as Chou Lien-hua, the personal pastor of Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs family. Su believed that these two figures could help mediate the differing perspectives between opposing factions.

Serving as the secretary-general of the event, Su and Wilderness Society took charge of the primary preparations. The event was organized under the themes ‚ÄúHealing pain and rebuilding hope‚Äù and ‚ÄúBearing suffering and responsibility, witnessing Christ‚Äôs love, acceptance, and the gospel of forgiveness.‚Äù At the time, Wilderness Society had been established for just over two years, yet it took on the significant task of reaching out to the families of 228 victims. Additionally, over 60 church leaders collaborated in organizing the service. Given the sociopolitical context of the time, planning such an event was not easy, as it involved sensitive issues related to religion and politics as well as the Mandarin-speaking churches and Taiwanese-speaking churches divide.

During the preparation process, some members of Mandarin-speaking churches opposed the Peace Service, even sending anonymous letters attacking Pastors Chou Lien-hua and Ong Siu-kiong. The opposition may have stemmed from concerns that the event was overly political and biased toward native Taiwanese. Conversely, some within the Presbyterian Church worried that the event‚Äôs intentions were not pure and that it might be exploited by the Kuomintang government. At a preparatory meeting on October 19, Su Nanzhou sought the agreement of Pastors Chou and Ong to preach in Taiwanese and Mandarin, respectively, despite their limited proficiency in those languages. This symbolic gesture was meant to demonstrate reconciliation between the Mandarin-speaking and Taiwanese-speaking church communities. Su noted that he drew inspiration for this approach from his architectural background, applying its principles to address structural social challenges.

The service centered on themes of reconciliation and repentance. The program included a moment of silence, a choral performance, a floral tribute to the families of victims, and a collective singing of ‚ÄúO Come, Emmanuel.‚Äù The service concluded with the congregation passing candlelight to one another while singing ‚ÄúSilent Night.‚Äù Notably, several prominent political figures attended voluntarily, including Premier Hau Pei-tsun, Minister of the Interior Hsu Shui-teh, Presidential Deputy Secretary-General Chiu Chin-i, former and incumbent Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) chairmen Shih Ming-teh and Hsu Hsin-liang, and human rights activist Dr. Chen Yung-hsing. The event garnered significant attention from both the ruling and opposition parties, marking an important response and contribution by Taiwan‚Äôs church community to the collective trauma of the 228 Incident. Displacement Theology

Displacement Theology is a historical theological perspective that emphasizes a shift from viewing biblical Israel as the central focus of history to recognizing other nations as integral components of God‚Äôs historical construction. This concept was proposed by Shoki Coe (ÂÆãÊ≥âÁõõ) and builds upon his earlier contextual theology discussions, which emphasized the concept of incarnation. In the 1980s, he further argued that the Word of God must take on an Asian flesh and that God‚Äôs redemption must hold specific significance for the Asian people. This perspective seeks to explore the concrete meaning of the redemptive history embodied by ancient Israel and the Western Christian Church for the peoples of Asia.

Coe advocated for narrative theology as a means to translate the Christian traditions, theological expressions, and complex ideas of the Western world into a language that Asian peoples could understand and integrate into their own cultural contexts. He pointed out that the history of the Israelites serves as a symbol indicating that God also engages with other nations in a redemptive manner. Furthermore, he suggested that other nations should recognize their own Exodus experiences from the Israelites‚Äô unique journey out of Egypt, thereby finding their rightful place alongside Israel in God‚Äôs redemptive plan.

This new historical-theological framework decentralizes Israel‚Äôs role in salvation history, asserting that every nation‚Äôs narrative and history possess unique value and can be connected to God‚Äôs redemption. Coe argued that, under the dominance of centrism, Christian theology has historically created points of conflict between the history of Israel and the Church and the histories of other nations and peoples. He believed that only by removing the obstacles formed by these conflicts could humanity engage in an exchange of love beyond distinctions of race, religion, and nationality.

Thus, in contrast to Deuteronomic Theology, which centers on Israel, Coe favored Second Isaiah Theology, recognizing within it a theological vision of global scope. He saw a decisive theological breakthrough in the biblical passages where God calls Cyrus His shepherd and anoints him to rule over nations. Coe also introduced a new understanding of the concept of election, linking divine selection to displacement, thereby suggesting the possibility of multiple forms of election, leading to various displacements (Zhuo Xinping 2007: 227-228; Olli Rin 2007: 51-52; Song 1982: 16-17).

Ren-Hou Tsai (1930 ‚Äì June 4, 2019)

Born in Zishan Town, Yudu County, Jiangxi Province, Ren-Hou Tsai was a representative figure of the third generation of New Confucianism and a disciple of Mou Zongsan. He studied at Guangzhou University and later served as a professor in the Department of Philosophy at both Chinese Culture University and Tunghai University, where he taught courses such as Neo-Confucianism of the Song and Ming Dynasties, Lu-Wang Philosophy, Special Topics on Wang Yangming, and History of Chinese Philosophy. In 2004, he was appointed as the first Honorary Professor at Tunghai University.

During the 1970s, the Catholic Church, centered around Fu Jen Catholic University, initiated efforts to interpret Chinese culture through two approaches: Thomistic philosophy and Chinese philosophy. In this context, a Catholic priest argued in a paper that non-Christian religions were merely ‚Äúnatural religions‚Äù, serving as precursors to the Christian gospel, and that once they encountered Christianity, their mission would be completed and should give way to Christianity. This claim sparked criticism from Tsai.

In 1980, at the International Philosophy Conference hosted by Fu Jen Catholic University, Tsai presented a paper titled Confucian Spirit and Moral Religion, in which he raised six critiques and challenges against the indigenization movement of Christianity in China:

  1. Can everyone become Christ?

  2. Is Jesus human, divine, or both divine and human?

  3. Besides accepting Jesus as the Savior, does humanity have the possibility of self-salvation?

  4. From the perspective of religious syncretism, should Jesus be uniquely revered, or should he be honored alongside Confucius and the Buddha?

  5. Do non-Christian religions merely have a legitimate status as preparatory steps toward Christianity, after which they must yield once their mission is fulfilled? Or do non-Christian religions possess their own eternal and independent standing?

  6. In the integration of religions, should Christianity be Sinicized, or should Chinese culture be Christianized?

Tsai argued that the introduction of foreign religions must respect the primacy of Confucianism within Chinese culture and should not displace the host with the guest or replace pillars with beams. He asserted that Confucianism is not merely one among the many schools of Chinese thought but rather represents the unifying cultural tradition of the Chinese nation.

The first four questions explored fundamental differences between Christianity and Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism, expressing skepticism toward the syncretic approach of Christian indigenization. The fifth critique targeted Christianity‚Äôs exclusive and dominant stance toward other religions and cultures. The sixth issue was central to his argument, as he believed Christianity should integrate into Chinese culture rather than transform Chinese culture into Christianity. His stance, while rooted in New Confucianism, also reflected a Chinese-centered perspective, emphasizing that being Chinese should be primary, while being a religious adherent should be secondary. Although the two identities may sometimes merge or separate, their order, he insisted, must never be reversed.

Tsai‚Äôs critiques gained widespread attention in academic circles, and his paper was later published in Philosophy and Culture, Goose Lake Monthly, and Chinese Culture Monthly. Within the Christian community, the Catholic Church, which traditionally values interfaith dialogue, did not directly respond. Instead, the Protestant pastor Chou Lien-hua addressed Tsai‚Äôs arguments. In 1982, Lin Chih-ping of Cosmic Light Magazine invited Chou to publish an article titled A Dialogue Between New Confucianism and Christianity, in which he systematically responded to Tsai‚Äôs challenges. However, rather than adopting an apologetic stance, Chou engaged in a dialogical approach. Tsai, unsatisfied with Chou‚Äôs response, issued a further reply.

Their exchange was later compiled into the book Integration and Transformation: A Dialogue Between Christianity and New Confucianism, published by Cosmic Light Magazine in 1985. Although the dialogue was often seen as talking past each other rather than achieving true integration and transformation, it marked the first formal dialogue between Protestant Christianity and New Confucianism in Taiwan (Lin Jinshui 2003: 423-425; Liang Yancheng 2014: 80-93).

Republic of China (ROC) Regime

During the 1980s, Taiwan was still under martial law, and religious groups remained under the control of the authoritarian regime. The Kuomintang (KMT) government continued to employ its existing intelligence network to tightly monitor and manage religious organizations under the direction of security agencies. The primary concern was the potential infiltration of these groups by ‚ÄúCommunist spies‚Äù. In particular, religious organizations that the government did not fully understand or could not control‚Äîsuch as I-Kuan Tao, Jehovah‚Äôs Witnesses, and the New Testament Church‚Äîwere frequently labeled as ‚Äúcults‚Äù, ‚Äúillegal organizations‚Äù, or even accused of harboring ‚ÄúCommunist spies‚Äù. These groups faced suppression, including bans, administrative penalties, and legal prosecution.

At the time, the KMT government employed two main strategies for managing religious organizations. Traditional indigenous religions were regulated individually under the Temple Supervision Ordinance. Meanwhile, institutionalized religions such as Buddhism, Daoism, and Christianity were collectively incorporated under the People‚Äôs Organization Law and the Civil Code. For religious groups that were difficult to effectively monitor or control, the government resorted to various political measures. For example, in the case of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT)‚Äîwhich had international connections‚Äîthe KMT government adopted policies of suppression and surveillance, even infiltrating its operations (Hsueh Hua-yuan 2022: 29-30).

One such infiltration effort was the Dao Cong Project, initiated by security agencies. This project began in 1983, just before the 30th General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church, and continued until after martial law was lifted, concluding with the 39th General Assembly in 1992 when the Garrison Command was reorganized. The strategy involved manipulating the annual election for vice secretary of the General Assembly in order to reshape the Presbyterian Church to align with the government‚Äôs interests (Huang Kexian 2021: 43).

The Dao Cong Project was also applied to the New Testament Church, which, like other marginal and difficult-to-control religious groups, was accused of being ‚Äúheretical‚Äù and ‚ÄúCommunist-infiltrated‚Äù. Declassified documents confirm that during the 1980s, the party-state apparatus deliberately subjected these two Christian groups to distinct forms of control: infiltration for the Presbyterian Church and severe crackdowns for the New Testament Church. The government used administrative excuses‚Äîsuch as household registration regulations and mountain area control policies‚Äîto expel believers and demolish their residences, effectively carrying out a policy-driven purge.

Additionally, the government repeatedly employed physical violence against the New Testament Church‚Äôs followers. Notable incidents include the 1982 and 1983 forced demolitions at Xiaolin Riverside and the 1985 Taoyuan Airport Incident, where state authorities carried out particularly severe crackdowns on the group.
