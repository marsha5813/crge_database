Taiwan: Context (1980-1990)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Draft Law on Religious Protection (ÂÆóÊïô‰øùË≠∑Ê≥ïËçâÊ°à)

In 1979, the government proposed the Draft Regulations on Temples and Churches („ÄàÂØ∫ÂªüÊïôÂ†ÇÊ¢ù‰æãËçâÊ°à„Äâ), which was met with opposition from various religious groups due to its perceived inconsistency with legal principles and its unconstitutional nature. As a result, the government abandoned the proposal. However, in 1983, the Ministry of the Interior introduced the Draft Law on Religious Protection („ÄàÂÆóÊïô‰øùË≠∑Ê≥ïËçâÊ°à„Äâ). Minister of the Interior Lin Yang-kang, in response to inquiries, stated that the Regulations on the Supervision of Temples („ÄàÁõ£Áù£ÂØ∫ÂªüÊ¢ù‰æã„Äâ), promulgated by the government in 1929, contained only 13 articles and applied solely to Taoism and Buddhism, excluding other religious denominations. The Ministry of the Interior believed that the existing regulations were insufficient for contemporary circumstances and thus began drafting new religious legislation.

Lin emphasized that the new law would adhere to constitutional principles, including:

  1. The guarantee of freedom of proselytization and religious equality,

  2. Protection taking precedence over regulation,

  3. Guidance taking precedence over supervision, and

  4. Autonomy taking precedence over restriction.

He assured that the law would not interfere in the internal affairs and organization of religious groups. However, many religious organizations, including churches, opposed the draft, arguing that it contained numerous problematic provisions and infringed on religious freedom under the principle of separation of church and state.

In defining religion, Article 2 of the Draft Law on Religious Protection recognized religious organizations as public legal entities. While this appeared to be an improvement over the Draft Regulations on Temples and Churches, which had previously defined temples and churches (as physical structures) as legal entities, the new draft still equated religious groups with their buildings. The underlying logic remained that managing religious buildings meant managing religious organizations, effectively extending control over religion itself.

Furthermore, Article 3 categorized churches into different levels: local, county/city, provincial/municipal, regional, and central. Article 8, Clause 9 required ‚Äúlocal churches to submit an approval document from their affiliated churches,‚Äù which did not align with the organizational structures of certain denominations and was even less applicable to temples. Article 11 stipulated that foreign religious organizations ‚Äúmust provide proof of registration from their home government,‚Äù a requirement that was rare in global legal frameworks.

Regarding organizational and property matters, the draft abandoned the Draft Regulations on Temples and Churches‚Äôs requirement that each temple or church establish a foundation. Instead, it stipulated that ‚Äúreligious organizations should manage their internal affairs according to their own constitutions.‚Äù However, Article 19 required that ‚Äúlocal churches must appoint a leader, who must be a missionary‚Ä¶ If a church does not have a missionary, its leader must be selected by its affiliated church, and the list must be submitted to the Ministry of the Interior for record-keeping.‚Äù This provision failed to accommodate the structures of certain churches, particularly folk religious temples.

In terms of administrative intervention, the draft contained even stronger governmental oversight than its predecessor. Articles 11 to 15, 17, 18, and 21 to 26 all exhibited clear administrative intervention. For example, Article 13 stated:

‚ÄúMissionaries, as defined by this law, refer to individuals who engage in proselytization as their profession. Missionaries must be reviewed and registered by their respective religious organizations, and their information must be submitted to the competent authorities for record-keeping. Those who are not officially registered cannot engage in proselytization.‚Äù

The Presbyterian Church viewed this provision as a violation of religious freedom and the right to proselytize. It also contradicted the church‚Äôs doctrine that ‚Äúeveryone should spread the gospel,‚Äù thereby infringing upon the principles of religious autonomy and separation of church and state.

Similarly, Article 14 stated:

‚ÄúReligious proselytization activities must align with the nation‚Äôs fundamental policies, must not endanger national security, must fulfill civic obligations, must adhere to public order, must promote traditional moral values, must uphold good customs, and must maintain harmonious relations with other religious organizations.‚Äù

This provision was seen as contradicting the principle of separation of church and state.

On June 3 of the same year, the Ministry of the Interior convened a symposium with experts, scholars, and religious representatives to discuss the draft. However, the Presbyterian Church was not invited. Concerned about the implications of the Draft Law on Religious Protection, the Presbyterian Church, along with some Buddhist and Catholic organizations, urgently convened a symposium, inviting legal experts, academics, religious leaders, and politicians to express their views and provide critiques. A consensus emerged that the draft violated Article 13 of the Constitution, which guarantees religious freedom.

On August 9 of the same year, the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church issued a Petition Against the ‚ÄúReligious Protection Law‚Äù („ÄàÂèçÂ∞ç„ÄåÂÆóÊïô‰øùË≠∑Ê≥ï„ÄçË´ãÈ°òÊõ∏„Äâ), criticizing the unconstitutional provisions in the draft and urging the Legislative Yuan to halt its enactment. They warned that implementing such a law would infringe on the religious freedom of citizens, heighten tensions between the people and the government, and damage Taiwan‚Äôs democratic image.

Ultimately, due to opposition from religious organizations, the draft did not progress further. (Chu Hai-yuan, 2006: 246-251; Anonymous, 1983).

Social Hostility toward Christianity

Conflicts Between Pentecostal/Charismatic and Non-Pentecostal/Charismatic Churches

In the 1980s, in addition to ideological and political divisions within the church, Pentecostal/Charismatic beliefs spread in Taiwan through the rise of institutions such as the China Evangelical Prayer House (‰∏≠ËèØÂü∫Áù£ÂæíÁ•àÁ¶±Èô¢), Elim Bookstore (‰ª•Áê≥Êõ∏Êàø), and Hosanna Ministry Center (ÂíåÊííÈÇ£‰∫ãÂ•â‰∏≠ÂøÉ). However, this trend was not well received by certain groups within the Christian community, including conservative fundamentalists, intellectuals, and churches focused on social justice.

Research suggests that the opposition to the Charismatic movement within Taiwanese churches was not primarily based on a denial of supernatural phenomena in contemporary times but stemmed from three main concerns:

  1. Theological Misinterpretations

  2. Deviation from Traditional Practices

  3. Imbalanced Preaching Messages

For instance, in 1987, Shen Jieshan (Ê≤à‰ªãÂ±±), a professor at China Evangelical Seminary, wrote an article criticizing the ‚Äúprosperity theology‚Äù promoted by the movement. His article was published in Campus Magazine („ÄäÊ†°ÂúíÈõúË™å„Äã), a prominent publication within the Mandarin-speaking Christian community. She also warned that memory healing, a key emphasis of the movement, could lead to negative psychological effects.

Christian leaders such as Rao Xiaojie (È•íÂ≠ùÊ•´) and Lee Hsiu-chuan (ÊùéÁßÄÂÖ®) expressed particular concerns over the movement‚Äôs emphasis on miraculous healing, arguing that an excessive focus on supernatural healing practices had led to theological imbalance. Additionally, some churches disapproved of the adoption of foreign worship and praise styles introduced by the Charismatic movement in the 1980s. Critics considered this form of worship shallow and believed it neglected the rich spiritual heritage found in traditional sacred music.

Studies on Campus Magazine from 1970 to 1995 highlight a consistent concern among non-Charismatic Mandarin-speaking churches, particularly regarding the practical aspects of the movement. Within the Presbyterian Church, the rise of Charismatic practices led to various disputes, especially within indigenous churches. Yushan Theological Seminary (ÁéâÂ±±Á•ûÂ≠∏Èô¢) actively engaged in discussions and responses to these issues. Unlike Mandarin-speaking churches, which focused on practical critiques, Presbyterian leaders were more concerned about the theological differences between the Charismatic movement and Reformed Presbyterian traditions.

In 1987, Yushan Theological Seminary compiled these discussions into a book titled A Study on the Charismatic Movement: Perspectives from Taiwan‚Äôs Indigenous Churches and the Global Church („ÄäÈùàÊÅ©ÈÅãÂãï‰πãÁ†îÁ©∂ÔºöÂè∞ÁÅ£Â±±Âú∞ÊïôÊúÉÂíåÊôÆ‰∏ñÊïôÊúÉÁöÑ‰∏Ä‰∫õËßÄÈªû„Äã). The book incorporated both local and international analyses, offering a more comprehensive and objective evaluation of the Charismatic movement within a global context. However, some contributors expressed negative views of the movement, and the New Testament Church (Êñ∞Á¥ÑÊïôÊúÉ) was also criticized due to its recruitment of indigenous Presbyterian believers and leaders in mountain areas.

Additionally, some indigenous ‚Äúprophets‚Äù incorporated strong ideological influences from the ruling party, using prophecy as religious capital and rhetoric to oppose Presbyterian ministers who were engaged in political and social justice activism (Chen Nanzhou, 1987: 9-51). Since many of the discussions at the time were centered on addressing disruptions caused by Pentecostal/Charismatic beliefs within indigenous churches, these debates left a lasting impression on the Presbyterian Church‚Äôs perception of the movement.

Throughout the 1980s, the non-Pentecostal/Charismatic sector of Taiwan‚Äôs Christian community maintained a high degree of skepticism and negative perceptions toward the Pentecostal/Charismatic movement. It was not until the 1990s that tensions between the two camps began to ease (Lin 2021: 245).

When the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC) established diplomatic relations with the United States, it issued the Message to Compatriots in Taiwan („ÄàÂëäÂè∞ÁÅ£ÂêåËÉûÊõ∏„Äâ), calling for the implementation of the Three Links (postal, transport, and trade connections) and the Four Exchanges (academic, cultural, sports, and artistic exchanges) to ease long-standing cross-strait isolation. As cross-strait exchanges began in the late 1980s, religious groups, including Christian organizations, also initiated interactions. In 1988, the General Secretary of the Taiwan YMCA led a delegation of four regional secretaries to visit the Shanghai YMCA. Since the Shanghai YMCA had a relatively weak religious identity and was less politically sensitive, it was easier for such exchanges to occur legally. However, due to China‚Äôs religious policies, missionary activities remained strictly restricted. Compared to other religions, Christian exchanges across the Taiwan Strait were more restrained due to ideological complexities. Several key factors contributed to this tension:

  1. The PRC government‚Äôs entrenched view of Christianity as historically linked to imperialism.

  2. Taiwanese churches‚Äô skepticism toward the Three-Self Patriotic Movement (‰∏âËá™ÊÑõÂúãÈÅãÂãï) and the Two Associations (ÂÖ©ÊúÉ, referring to the China Christian Council and the Three-Self Patriotic Movement Committee).

Since obtaining formal approval or an invitation from the Two Associations was difficult, and many Taiwanese churches rejected these organizations for their perceived alignment with the Communist Party, they instead focused on house churches as their primary partners, using these networks to expand missionary work.

One such organization was the Chinese Gospel Mission (‰∏≠ÂúãÁ¶èÈü≥ÊúÉ), which established a Taiwan branch in 1986. Once cross-strait travel was permitted, the mission immediately launched its outreach efforts. Visits to Korea for church observation increased significantly, and institutions such as the China Evangelical Prayer House (‰∏≠ËèØÂü∫Áù£Á•àÁ¶±Èô¢, also known as Prayer Mountain) were established in Taiwan, inspired by the Korean Prayer Mountain Movement and the Osanri Choi Ja-shil Memorial Fasting Prayer Mountain (Ê¢ßÂ±±ÈáåÁ•àÁ¶±Èô¢).

Starting in 1986, Korea‚Äôs Yoido Full Gospel Church (Á¥îÁ¶èÈü≥ÊïôÊúÉ) began hosting the annual Asia Saints Pilgrimage to Korea (‰∫ûÁ¥∞‰∫ûËÅñÂæíË®™ÈüìËÅñÊúÉ), with Taiwanese attendees forming the largest group of participants. However, as East Asian geopolitics shifted in the late 1980s, Korea began engaging with China, leading to a gradual weakening of diplomatic ties with Taiwan.

Taiwan-U.S. Relations, Human Rights, and Taiwan-Vatican Diplomacy in the 1980s

Starting in 1980, Taiwan‚Äôs relationship with the United States was maintained through the Taiwan Relations Act (TRA). Under this framework, the U.S. established the American Institute in Taiwan (AIT) in Taipei, while Taiwan set up the Coordination Council for North American Affairs (CCNAA) in Washington, D.C., which was renamed the Taipei Economic and Cultural Representative Office (TECRO) in 1994. One of the fundamental requirements of the TRA was the protection of human rights for the people of Taiwan, leading the U.S. to oppose various human rights violations under Taiwan‚Äôs martial law regime.

For instance, after the Chen Wen-chen Incident in July 1981, in which Taiwanese political dissident and Carnegie Mellon professor Chen Wen-chen was found dead under suspicious circumstances, the U.S. State Department expressed concern through AIT and CCNAA and temporarily suspended riot control equipment sales to Taiwan. In October of the same year, the U.S. House Foreign Affairs Subcommittees on Asia-Pacific Affairs and Human Rights held a secret hearing on Taiwan intelligence agents‚Äô illegal activities in the U.S. In December, the U.S. Congress passed the Amendment of the Arms Export Control Act, with Clause 6 prohibiting the U.S. government from providing military aid or selling weapons to countries that engaged in systematic intimidation or harassment of individuals. Following the Chen Wen-chen Incident, the U.S. Congress increasingly linked Taiwan‚Äôs human rights record to arms sales.

Another high-profile case was the Henry Liu Assassination (Ê±üÂçóÊ°à) in 1984. Henry Liu (pen name: Jiang Nan), a Taiwanese-American journalist and writer, was assassinated at his home in San Francisco. The FBI determined that the mastermind, Chen Chi-li (Èô≥ÂïüÁ¶Æ), had been commissioned by Taiwan‚Äôs Military Intelligence Bureau. The U.S. State Department expressed strong dissatisfaction over Taiwan‚Äôs involvement in the murder of a U.S. citizen, exposing the intelligence agency‚Äôs ties to organized crime in suppressing political dissent.

In response to these concerns, overseas Taiwanese activists founded the Formosan Association for Public Affairs (FAPA) in February 1982. FAPA initiated congressional lobbying efforts, advocating for lifting martial law, releasing political prisoners from the Kaohsiung Incident (ÁæéÈ∫óÂ≥∂‰∫ã‰ª∂), and securing Taiwan‚Äôs right to self-determination. They also worked closely with U.S. lawmakers to keep human rights issues under scrutiny and pressured the Kuomintang (KMT) government, particularly regarding calls for the end of martial law (Chen Cuilian, 2021: 338-352). Through legislative actions and sanctions under the TRA‚Äôs human rights provisions, the U.S. Congress and Taiwanese advocacy groups exerted significant pressure on Taiwan‚Äôs authorities, forcing them to tread cautiously.

Additionally, Taiwanese expatriates in the U.S. actively supported Congressman Stephen J. Solarz, who championed human rights in Taiwan, by backing his reelection campaigns and raising political donations. As a result, in December 1982, Solarz and Senator Edward Kennedy successfully pushed for an amendment to the U.S. Immigration Act, which separated Taiwan from China‚Äôs immigration quota, allowing the U.S. to accept 20,000 Taiwanese immigrants annually. This policy led to a significant increase in Taiwanese migration to the U.S., driven by six key factors:

  1. Quality of life

  2. Political instability

  3. Educational opportunities

  4. Public safety concerns

  5. Business and investment prospects

  6. Social welfare benefits (Chen Cuilian, 2021: 349; Hong Yuru, 2019: 389, 394-396).

Taiwan-Vatican Relations

On another diplomatic front, Taiwan‚Äôs relationship with the Vatican experienced turbulence in the 1980s. Pope John Paul II sought to foster engagement with China, raising concerns in Taiwan. In 1983, Taiwanese bishops met with the Secretary-General of the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples to express their dissatisfaction with the Pope‚Äôs overtures toward China. In response, the Vatican invited seven Taiwanese bishops to a meeting the following year, during which they warned Church officials that a potential Taiwan-Vatican diplomatic rupture could have significant consequences.

However, in November 1987, CCP General Secretary Zhao Ziyang met with Cardinal Jaime Sin from Manila in Beijing, and both agreed to initiate diplomatic negotiations, once again reviving the possibility of China-Vatican relations. Despite these efforts, progress stalled due to unresolved technical issues, particularly the power struggle between the state-controlled Chinese Catholic Patriotic Association and the Vatican (Zhao Zhongwei, Liang Jiefen, Huang Yiying, 2020: 147-148).

Although Taiwan-Vatican relations faced diplomatic challenges in the 1980s, formal ties ultimately remained intact.

The Rise of Taiwanese Nationalism and Ethnic Movements in the 1980s

Taiwanese nationalism, which originated under Japanese colonial rule, experienced a resurgence in the 1980s, increasingly diverging from the dominant Chinese nationalism that had long been mainstream in Taiwan‚Äôs political discourse. Following the diplomatic crises of the 1970s and the rise of opposition movements, localist narratives gained prominence. The Bensheng intellectuals (Êú¨ÁúÅÁ±çÁü•Ë≠ò‰ªΩÂ≠ê) who played a leading role in the opposition camp gradually moved away from the state-sanctioned Chinese nationalist ideology, developing their own visions of national identity and sovereignty. By the early 1980s, Taiwan‚Äôs future should be determined by its residents (Âè∞ÁÅ£ÂâçÈÄî‰ΩèÊ∞ëËá™Ê±∫) became the consensus among opposition intellectuals, and Taiwanese nationalism emerged as the core ideology of the pro-democracy movement.

As Taiwan transitioned toward democratization, political divisions in the emerging party system largely revolved around the ideological struggle between Taiwanese nationalism and Chinese nationalism. In the process of reimagining and constructing a new national identity, multiculturalist national integration policies gradually gained consensus across political factions.

Ethnic Reconciliation: Addressing the Legacy of Authoritarian Oppression

Ethnic reconciliation efforts sought to address the historical divisions and oppression under authoritarian rule, particularly those stemming from two major events:

  1. The February 28 Incident (1947)

  2. The White Terror (1949 and beyond)

The February 28 Incident silenced Taiwanese society for decades, instilling fear and political apathy. It also became the historical root of ethnic tensions between the Benshengren (local Taiwanese) and Waishengren (Mainlanders). According to demographic estimates from the Executive Yuan‚Äôs 228 Incident Research Task Force (formed during Lee Teng-hui‚Äôs presidency), the number of deaths was between 18,000 and 28,000.

Conclusion: The Rise of a Multicultural National Identity

The rise of Indigenous and Hakka identity movements was closely linked to the emerging discourse on ethnicity (ÊóèÁæ§). The 1980s revival of Taiwanese nationalism was no longer framed within a singular ethnic identity but instead grounded in multiculturalism and pluralistic ethnic relations. This contrasted sharply with the KMT‚Äôs China-centric nationalism, which emphasized a unified Han Chinese identity.

Even some KMT-affiliated politicians and institutions gradually began accepting the ‚Äúethnic group‚Äù framework, reflecting a broader shift in Taiwan‚Äôs national and social identity during democratization.
