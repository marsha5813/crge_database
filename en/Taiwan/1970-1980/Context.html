Draft of the Temple and Church Ordinance (1978-1979)

In 1978, Chiang Ching-kuo assumed the presidency and formulated the Draft of the Temple and Church Ordinance, intending to extend state control over churches based on the pre-existing Temple Supervision Ordinance and Temple Management Ordinance. The draft, prepared by the Ministry of the Interior, was approved by the Executive Yuan on June 14, 1979. However, it faced widespread opposition from religious circles and was never submitted to the Legislative Yuan for review.

Scholars analyzing this controversy identify four main objections raised by critics, primarily from religious groups:

  1. Fundamental Legislative Principles

    Critics argued that the draft ordinance violated the principle of religious freedom, emphasizing ‚Äúmanagement‚Äù and ‚Äúsupervision‚Äù rather than the ‚Äúguidance‚Äù and ‚Äúprotection‚Äù expected in a democratic nation. The legislative rationale stated that the increasing number of religious activities, new religious sects, and foreign Protestant denominations required legal regulations. It also claimed that the current establishment of churches lacked prior approval and registration, allowing ‚Äúunscrupulous individuals to exploit religion for financial gain or to corrupt public morals, deceiving the public and endangering genuine religious faith.‚Äù This reasoning was criticized as overly subjective and particularly targeting Christianity. The draft was also believed to have borrowed elements from Japan‚Äôs abolished Religious Organizations Law, violating the spirit of Japan‚Äôs Religious Juridical Persons Act by granting excessive discretionary power to regulatory authorities. Unlike Japan, where such measures required judicial review, the draft ordinance in Taiwan allowed administrative authorities to impose penalties directly.

  2. Definition Issues

    Critics pointed out that the draft failed to clearly define religion. The ordinance attempted to define temples and churches based on their physical structures while simultaneously classifying related individuals and activities under the same definition. Some religious scholars suggested incorporating definitions used by Western academics to explicitly state the criteria for what constitutes a religion. However, other scholars warned that strict definitions could lead to the exclusion and potential prohibition of groups that failed to meet these criteria.

  3. Organizational and Property Issues

    The ordinance sought to grant legal status to temples and churches as religious corporations, requiring them to draft bylaws, submit an inventory of assets, and register a board of directors with at least five members, half of whom had to be clergy. These regulations posed practical difficulties for many religious institutions. Article 19 gave regulatory authorities the power to reorganize or dissolve temples and churches based on vague criteria such as ‚Äúviolating the founding purpose‚Äù or ‚Äúharming public welfare.‚Äù Additionally, Article 17 stated that upon dissolution, religious assets would be transferred to local governments, raising concerns about government interference and threats to religious property rights.

  4. Administrative Interference

    The draft ordinance was designed to subject religious organizations to administrative control. The Ministry of the Interior defined the scope of religious activities (Article 4), determined who qualified as a missionary (Article 5), and set criteria for what constituted a temple or church (Article 3). More concerning were provisions such as Article 19, which allowed authorities to impose administrative penalties, including dissolution, and Article 20, which granted the government the power to dismiss church board members, leaders, and missionaries. These measures were criticized as excessive expansions of executive power, violating the principles of religious freedom and the separation of church and state. Consequently, the ordinance was ultimately abandoned (Chu Hai-yuan, 2006: 240-246).

Opposition from the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan

Upon learning of the draft, the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) submitted a petition titled ‚ÄúOpposition to the Temple and Church Ordinance‚Äù, arguing that aside from its stated purpose in Article 1, the ordinance entirely lacked provisions to protect religious freedom or safeguard the rights of religious organizations. Instead, it threatened religious faith, disrupted the structure, doctrine, and administration of religious groups, and violated their autonomy. The PCT particularly objected to Article 7, which required missionary work to be conducted publicly and in the Chinese language, allowing translation only if the missionary was not proficient in Chinese. This provision was seen as an additional obstacle to evangelism. The church argued that language is an essential tool for religious practice and that freedom of faith is tied to the ability to use local languages. The ordinance‚Äôs restrictions, they claimed, violated constitutional rights to linguistic freedom and religious practice.

Furthermore, the PCT strategically warned the government that passing the ordinance would ‚Äújeopardize Taiwan‚Äôs international reputation among democratic nations and allies, potentially playing into the hands of communist propaganda.‚Äù (Anonymous, 1979). The church viewed the draft ordinance as a continuation of previous government restrictions, such as the confiscation of Romanized Bibles in 1975 and the broader suppression of native languages under the Mandarin Promotion Movement.

Kao Chun-ming and Others Case (Sheltering Shih Ming-teh Case)

Also Known as the ‚ÄúKaohsiung Incident - Kao Chun-ming and Others Case‚Äù

Three days after the Kaohsiung Incident on December 10, 1979, the Kuomintang (KMT) government launched a large-scale crackdown. The main commander, Shih Ming-teh, escaped and remained in hiding for 26 days before being captured on January 8, 1980. During his time in hiding, Shih received assistance from several individuals, many of whom were Christians, particularly those affiliated with the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT). Key figures who helped him included:

  • Chao Chen-erh, Publication Officer of the Bible Society

  • Wu Wen, Pastor of the China Evangelical Fellowship

  • Lin Wen-chen, President of Taiwan Women‚Äôs Theological Seminary

  • Huang Chao-hui, a PCT member

  • Kao Chun-ming, Secretary-General of the PCT General Assembly

  • Shih Jui-yun, Kao Chun-ming‚Äôs secretary

On January 8, 1980, Shih Ming-teh was arrested at the residence of Hsu Ching-fu, a friend of his brother Shih Ming-cheng. Over the following months, several individuals who had aided him, including Shih Jui-yun, Lin Wen-chen, Chao Chen-erh, and Huang Chao-hui, were also arrested. Kao Chun-ming was detained later, on April 24, 1980.

The Garrison Command sentenced Chao Chen-erh, Huang Chao-hui, and Shih Jui-yun to two years in prison under Article 9 of the Act for the Control and Punishment of Rebellion During the Period of National Mobilization for Suppression of Communist Rebellion, which criminalized ‚Äúknowingly failing to report communist spies.‚Äù However, all three were given a three-year suspended sentence.

Among those convicted, Kao Chun-ming received the heaviest sentence. Under Article 4, Section 1, Clause 7 of the Punishment of Rebellion Act, he was found guilty of ‚Äújointly harboring a traitor‚Äù and sentenced to seven years in prison, with a five-year deprivation of civil rights. Additionally, all his assets were confiscated, except for a portion allocated for his family‚Äôs basic living expenses. He was released early in 1984.

Lin Wen-chen was sentenced to five years in prison, deprived of civil rights for two years, and had all her property confiscated except for a portion reserved for family necessities.

Wu Wen was convicted under the same statute for ‚Äúrepeatedly and jointly harboring a traitor‚Äù and sentenced to two years in prison with two years of civil rights deprivation.

The legal basis for the case included:

  • Article 173 (first paragraph) of the Military Trials Act

  • Article 8, Section 2 of the Martial Law Act

  • Articles 10 (last section), 8(1), 9(1)(1), and 12 of the Punishment of Rebellion Act

  • Articles 28, 30(1), 56, 59, 66, 38(1)(2), and 38(3) of the Criminal Code

All individuals convicted in this case were later exonerated and compensated under Taiwan‚Äôs Act on Promoting Transitional Justice and the Compensation Act for Improper Trials of Sedition and Espionage Cases During the Martial Law Period (Anonymous, 1980; Chen Yi-shen, date unknown; various anonymous sources, date unknown).

Social Hostility toward Christianity

Conflict Between the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) and the ‚ÄúMandarin Churches‚Äù

During the 1970s, tensions between the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) and the Mandarin-speaking churches established by Chinese mainlanders who retreated to Taiwan became increasingly apparent. These tensions stemmed from the anti-communist religious movement of the 1960s, its associated theological positions, and the Three Declarations, which highlighted political differences between the two groups.

The ‚ÄúMandarin Churches‚Äù could be broadly categorized into two groups:

  1. Traditional Denominations, such as members of the Taiwan Church Cooperation Association (TCCA) excluding the PCT, including the Taiwan Episcopal Church, Taiwan Lutheran Church, Methodist Church in the Republic of China, and the Catholic Bishops‚Äô Conference of Taiwan. Their primary disagreement with the PCT was political.

  2. Evangelical or Fundamentalist Churches, which were outside the ecumenical tradition. Their differences with the PCT were not only political but also theological.

During this period, international anti-communist Christian organizations and party-state-affiliated Christians in Taiwan collaborated to portray liberal and ecumenical groups as politically pro-communist and theologically ‚Äúimpure.‚Äù Since the PCT was a member of the World Council of Churches (WCC), it faced significant pressure from both the government and other Christian groups. In 1970, the PCT was forced to establish the Anti-Communist Promotion Committee and organize a series of anti-communist lectures to demonstrate its anti-communist stance. However, later that year, the PCT was still pressured to withdraw from the WCC.

In 1971, the Draft of the National Affairs Statement was originally co-authored by Zhou Lianhua (Baptist Church), T. Otto Nall (Methodist Church), and Daniel Beeby (PCT), with Zhang Qitang (Lutheran Church) later joining. Kao Chun-ming served as the convenor, and the statement was initially intended to be released under the Church Cooperation Committee. However, other denominations refused to sign the statement, leading the PCT to revise and publish it independently. In protest against these modifications, Zhou Lianhua withdrew from the Church Cooperation Association (Wu Changxing, 2008: 284; Zeng Qingbao, 2016: 214-219).

Following the PCT‚Äôs publication of the 1977 Human Rights Declaration, a splinter group, the Presbyterian Church in the Republic of China (PCCROC), expressed strong disapproval. In an official letter, Xinyoutang Church (a PCCROC-affiliated church) declared:

  • ‚ÄúWe live in a nation that honors Jehovah, values human rights, and upholds freedom.‚Äù

  • ‚ÄúThere is only one legitimate government representing the 800 million Chinese people‚Äîthe Republic of China, founded on the Three Principles of the People.‚Äù

Xinyoutang supported the government‚Äôs ‚ÄúHan-Traitor Dichotomy‚Äù policy and Taiwan‚Äôs withdrawal from the United Nations, citing 1 Peter 2:13 to urge the PCT to ‚Äúobey the government‚Äôs leadership.‚Äù

In response, the PCT General Assembly rebuked Xinyoutang for misinterpreting the Human Rights Declaration and ignoring Taiwan‚Äôs urgent political situation. The PCT criticized Xinyoutang for behaving like ‚Äúfalse prophets‚Äù who preach ‚Äúpeace‚Äù even in times of crisis.

The Catholic Church in Taiwan also rejected the PCT‚Äôs Human Rights Declaration, stating:

  • ‚ÄúThis statement represents only a small faction and cannot speak for the 17 million Chinese people in Taiwan.‚Äù

  • ‚ÄúIts arguments are not only naive but also contradictory.‚Äù

  • ‚ÄúIt violates fundamental national policies and does not align with the aspirations of 17 million free Chinese citizens. It will inevitably cause division within the PCT and have a negative impact on its development.‚Äù

Additionally, starting from February 12, 1978 (Issue No. 900), The Gospel News published a series of articles attacking the PCT and its leaders, accusing them of interfering in politics. Some articles even condemned seminaries and churches that used the Taiwanese language, claiming that doing so violated government policy, was ‚Äúdemonic possession,‚Äù and was ‚Äúa shameful act.‚Äù (Chen Nanzhou, 2000: 16).

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

Taiwan‚Äôs Diplomatic Crisis and Its Impact on Christianity in the 1970s

During the 1970s, Taiwan faced a major diplomatic crisis, primarily due to its relationship with China. While cross-strait artillery exchanges continued until 1979, Taiwan and China experienced shifts in legitimacy over international representation. Key events included:

  • 1971: The People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC) replaced the Republic of China (ROC) as the official representative of China in the United Nations (UN).

  • 1979: The United States established diplomatic relations with the PRC and severed official ties with Taiwan.

These developments had a profound impact on Christianity in Taiwan, particularly on the Catholic Church.

Since the Second Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945), the Kuomintang (KMT) government had maintained a close relationship with the Catholic Church. After the KMT retreated to Taiwan, the Catholic Church in Taiwan continued to align itself with the government, closely monitoring the Vatican‚Äôs interactions with Beijing. Both the Taiwanese government and the Catholic Church feared that the Vatican might establish diplomatic relations with the PRC, abandoning Taiwan‚Äôs government and its local Catholic community.

In fact, since the 1960s, Cardinal Agostino Casaroli, the Vatican‚Äôs Secretary of State, had advocated for a policy of reconciliation with Eastern European communist states and the Soviet Union, shifting from confrontation to adaptation. The Vatican was also preparing to engage in dialogue with Beijing, a stance that became even more pronounced in the 1970s. Notably, when the PRC released Bishop James Edward Walsh, M.M., an American Catholic missionary, the move was widely interpreted as a step toward diplomatic normalization with the Vatican.

On the day the ROC withdrew from the UN, the Vatican‚Äôs Apostolic Nuncio to Taiwan, Archbishop Edward Idris Cassidy, was recalled to Rome. For several months, the Vatican‚Äôs embassy in Taipei remained vacant until Francesco Colasuonno was temporarily appointed as charg√© d‚Äôaffaires. Cardinal Casaroli had already intended to abandon Taiwan, but Archbishop Lo Kuang actively lobbied the Vatican to maintain its presence. As a result, the Vatican continued to station representatives in Taiwan, but from that point onward, the Apostolic Nunciature in Taipei was downgraded to the level of a charg√© d‚Äôaffaires, a status that remains unchanged to this day.

In 1978, as Deng Xiaoping implemented China‚Äôs economic reform and opening-up policy, Vatican-Beijing negotiations became more likely. In 1980, the PRC released Bishop Dominic Tang Yi-ming, who had been imprisoned for 22 years, in an attempt to test international reactions to its open-door policy.

Throughout this period, Taiwan closely monitored Vatican-China relations. Any move by the Vatican toward diplomatic engagement with Beijing was met with careful observation and analysis by the Taiwanese government (Zhao Zhongwei, Liang Jiefen, Huang Yiying, 2020: 143-147).

After the establishment of the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC) in 1949, the governments of Taiwan (ROC) and China (PRC) began competing over China‚Äôs representation in the United Nations (UN). Due to the U.S. strategy of supporting Chiang Kai-shek and opposing communism, the United States repeatedly deferred discussions on China‚Äôs UN representation during the 1950s, using the justification of ‚Äúpostponing consideration in this General Assembly session.‚Äù In the 1960s, the U.S. changed its approach, designating the issue of China‚Äôs representation as an ‚Äúimportant question‚Äù that required a two-thirds majority vote in the UN General Assembly.

However, by the 1970s, the United States reassessed the global situation. The PRC had solidified its leadership position within the Third World, and in 1970, both Canada and Italy established diplomatic relations with Beijing. That same year, during the 25th UN General Assembly vote on China‚Äôs representation, the PRC received more votes than Taiwan. Additionally, as U.S.-Soviet military power became more balanced, and China-Soviet relations deteriorated, the United States adjusted its strategy, shifting from ‚Äúsupporting Chiang and opposing communism‚Äù to ‚Äúaligning with China to counter the Soviet Union.‚Äù

During the 26th UN General Assembly in 1971, the United States officially endorsed the PRC‚Äôs entry into the UN. After the vote, the PRC was admitted to the UN, taking over China‚Äôs seat and its permanent membership in the Security Council. This decision was formalized through Resolution 2758, which explicitly expelled ‚ÄúChiang Kai-shek‚Äôs representatives‚Äù from the UN.

In 1972, U.S. President Richard Nixon visited China and, alongside Zhou Enlai, issued the Shanghai Communiqu√©, committing the U.S. to sever diplomatic ties with Taiwan, terminate treaties, and withdraw military forces. According to scholar Masataka Wakabayashi, this communiqu√© served as a ‚Äúde facto letter of intent‚Äù for the U.S.-China diplomatic normalization process.

In early 1973, U.S. National Security Advisor Henry Kissinger visited Beijing and issued the U.S.-China Joint Communiqu√©, announcing the establishment of ‚Äúliaison offices‚Äù in Beijing and Washington, which functioned similar to embassies.

After Jimmy Carter became U.S. president in 1976, he accelerated efforts to normalize relations with China. However, Congress remained supportive of Taiwan, delaying official recognition of Beijing until December 16, 1978. The White House maintained secrecy and did not consult Congress beforehand. Additionally, the U.S.-ROC Mutual Defense Treaty was unilaterally nullified, and the U.S.-China Joint Communiqu√© made no mention of Taiwan‚Äôs security, which angered the U.S. Congress and prompted legislative action to address the situation.

In February 1979, the Senate Foreign Relations Committee held hearings on the Taiwan Relations Act (TRA), which was passed in March 1979. The TRA established the American Institute in Taiwan (AIT) as a de facto embassy, maintaining unofficial U.S.-Taiwan relations. The law also committed the U.S. to providing Taiwan with defensive weapons. Additionally, it included human rights provisions, highlighting concerns over Taiwan‚Äôs human rights situation during martial law, particularly regarding political prisoners.

The Kuomintang (KMT) government viewed the human rights provisions as interference in domestic affairs. It also protested that these provisions were influenced by Taiwanese independence activists abroad, but these objections had little effect. As a result, Taiwan‚Äôs human rights issues became a frequent topic in U.S. congressional discussions on arms sales to Taiwan. In 1980, 14 members of the U.S. House of Representatives introduced Proposal 603, which criticized Taiwan‚Äôs poor human rights record and called for a suspension of arms sales until improvements were made (Chen Tsui-lian, 2021: 330-338; Masataka Wakabayashi, 2014: 138-142; Chen Shih-chang, 2015: 213-220).

Another Christian nation with strong ties to Taiwan was the Vatican, which had a direct connection to the Taiwanese Catholic Church and its followers. Initially, the Holy See recognized the ROC government in Taiwan as the legitimate representative of China, largely due to its anti-communist stance and condemnation of religious persecution in China.

However, during the papacy of Pope John XXIII, the Vatican adopted a more open attitude toward socialism. Under Pope Paul VI, the Vatican expressed goodwill toward Beijing and, in a UN speech, suggested that the PRC should be admitted to the UN. Despite these shifts, Taiwan-Vatican relations remained stable throughout the 1970s (Zhao Zhongwei, Liang Jiefen, Huang Yiying, 2020: 141-145).

On December 16, 1978, the Carter administration announced that the United States would establish diplomatic relations with the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC) on January 1, 1979, and would simultaneously terminate diplomatic ties with Taiwan (ROC). The U.S.-ROC Mutual Defense Treaty was set to be annulled one year later. Upon receiving the news, Chiang Ching-kuo was furious and delivered an impromptu speech, condemning the U.S. for betrayal and abandoning its ally.

In the 1970s, Taiwan was already facing growing international isolation, and the U.S.-Taiwan diplomatic rupture only exacerbated this crisis, triggering widespread existential fears across the island. Consequently, Taiwan witnessed its second wave of anti-American protests since the 1957 Liu Ziran Incident. On the same day, angry demonstrators gathered at the Yuanshan U.S. Military Club, smashing cars and breaking windows. Thousands of people also marched to the U.S. embassy in protest.

On December 27, 1978, U.S. Deputy Secretary of State Warren Minor Christopher led a delegation to Taiwan for negotiations regarding post-diplomatic relations. Upon arrival, the delegation was met by Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs Chien Fu, who issued a 1,500-character statement, harshly criticizing the U.S. for not only destroying U.S.-Taiwan friendship but also undermining peace in the Western Pacific. As the delegation‚Äôs motorcade left the airport, it was surrounded by furious demonstrators, some of whom threw eggs and stones at the vehicles. Over the two-day negotiations, multiple U.S. embassy vehicles were vandalized.

During this period, Taiwanese citizens and overseas Chinese who supported the ROC initiated numerous pro-government actions, including blood donation drives and the ‚ÄúNational Defense Contribution Movement.‚Äù Seizing the moment, the Executive Yuan established the ‚ÄúSelf-Strengthening National Salvation Fund,‚Äù enlisting entertainers to promote patriotism and redirect public sentiment into a nationalistic movement.

For the Kuomintang (KMT) regime, the U.S.-Taiwan diplomatic break was both a crisis and an opportunity. Although it threatened Taiwan‚Äôs international standing, it also strengthened nationalist sentiments and reinforced loyalty to the state. To maintain social stability, Chiang Ching-kuo invoked emergency powers under the Temporary Provisions and issued an ‚ÄúEmergency Order,‚Äù suspending the upcoming legislative elections for additional central representatives and postponing all campaign activities. This move aimed to prevent the KMT from suffering electoral defeats that could destabilize the regime. Some candidates strongly supported the decision, displaying patriotic slogans on campaign vehicles to express their backing for the government.

However, for non-KMT political figures, who relied on elections as their only avenue for political participation, the postponement effectively shut them out of the political arena. As a result, they shifted their efforts toward mass protests and political movements.

On January 22, 1979, opposition figures staged the ‚ÄúQiaotou Incident‚Äù in Kaohsiung to support former mayor Yu Teng-fa and his son, who were accused of ‚Äúrebellion.‚Äù This marked the first political demonstration since martial law was imposed in 1949. Later that year, on December 10, 1979, opposition activists organized a Human Rights Day march in Kaohsiung. When authorities denied their application for a permit, the protest escalated into violent clashes between demonstrators and military police, leading to the Kaohsiung Incident (Formosa Incident). Several opposition leaders were arrested and tried by military courts.

Meanwhile, Taiwanese pro-democracy activists and Taiwan independence advocates abroad took action upon learning of the impending U.S.-Taiwan diplomatic break. They lobbied the U.S. Congress in advance, hoping to mitigate the impact. At the time, Guo Yuxin‚Äôs ‚ÄúTaiwan Majority Political Advancement Association,‚Äù Peng Ming-min‚Äôs ‚ÄúTaiwanese People‚Äôs Self-Rescue Movement,‚Äù and Huang Zhanghui‚Äôs ‚ÄúTaiwanese People‚Äôs Self-Determination Movement‚Äù joined forces to establish the ‚ÄúTaiwan Democracy Movement Overseas Alliance.‚Äù This organization actively lobbied during the Taiwan Relations Act hearings, advocating for continued U.S. support for Taiwan.

During and after the U.S.-Taiwan diplomatic break, a sense of anxiety permeated Taiwanese society. This tense atmosphere may have served as a catalyst for Taiwan‚Äôs democratization movement in the 1980s (Chen Shih-chang, 2015: 218-220; Chen Tsui-lian, 2021: 334-335; Guan Lijia, 1979; Anonymous, 2022; Anonymous, 2022; Anonymous, 2018).
