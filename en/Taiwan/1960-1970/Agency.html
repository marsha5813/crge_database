Ë¨ùÁ∑Ø (Hsieh Wei)

Hsieh Wei (March 2, 1916 ‚Äì June 17, 1970) was born into a medical family in Nantou Town, Nantou District, Nantou Hall during the Japanese rule of Taiwan (now Nantou City, Nantou County). His maternal grandfather was the missionary Wu Ge. In 1934, he enrolled at Tainan Theological College, and four years later, he entered Tokyo Medical College. After graduating from medical school in 1942, he worked in Sendai City, where he experienced American air raids.

After the war, on November 17, 1945, he married his cousin Yang Ching-ying, who was also studying medicine in Japan. The following year, he returned to Taiwan to take over the Nantou Datong Hospital left by his father and served as a voluntary preacher at Chishui Church in Mingjian for three years. In 1949, he was ordained as a pastor of the Nantou Presbyterian Church. Influenced by Sun Lillian, the founder of the Mustard Seed Mission, he joined the Mennonite medical team from 1950 to 1951, providing medical services in mountain indigenous villages.

In 1951, Hsieh Wei went to the United States for further studies, undergoing general surgical training for three years. In 1955, Sun Lillian invited him to serve as the first director of the Christian Mountain Center Clinic (the predecessor of Puli Christian Hospital), where he was responsible for surgical work and also taught at the nursing school. The following year, he established a tuberculosis sanatorium at Liyutan, Puli, to treat indigenous patients. In 1965, he traveled to Japan for several months to study new thoracic surgery techniques for treating tuberculosis patients.

In 1960, at Sun Lillian‚Äôs invitation, he also served as a volunteer physician at the Beimen Blackfoot Disease Free Clinic, participating in the treatment of Blackfoot disease patients in Beimen Islet, Tainan County (now Beimen District, Tainan City) alongside Wang Chin-ho and Sun Lillian. Every Thursday, he led a team of medical personnel from Nantou Datong Hospital, personally covering their travel expenses, to Beimen, working until late at night before returning to Nantou‚Äîa practice he maintained for over a decade. In 1964, he became a physician at Erlin Christian Hospital (now Erlin Branch of Changhua Christian Hospital), dedicating himself to coastal area medical services and missionary work. (Chen Jin-xing, 2010, pp. 116‚Äì126)

In 1968, he was elected as the 15th Vice Moderator of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan. That same year, local political figures and the Nantou County branch of the Kuomintang attempted to recruit him to run for Nantou County Magistrate, but he declined. In 1969, he became the 16th Moderator of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan. The Kuomintang government pressured the church to withdraw from the World Council of Churches, and Hsieh Wei engaged in prolonged negotiations with the government to protect the church‚Äôs membership in the organization, ultimately succeeding in maintaining its position. (Chen Jin-xing, 2024, pp. 211‚Äì216)

On June 17, 1970, Hsieh Wei led morning prayers at his hospital, then went to work at Puli Christian Hospital. He returned to Nantou at noon, rested briefly after lunch, and prepared to head to Erlin for medical consultations. His wife advised him to rest a little longer, but he replied, ‚ÄúIf I arrive at the hospital just one minute earlier, a patient may suffer one minute less of pain, or perhaps a life could be saved.‚Äù With that, he left by car. Around 1 p.m., while driving through Mingjian Township, he accidentally crashed into a roadside tree and passed away while en route to Erlin Christian Hospital. He was called to the Lord at 1:55 p.m. at the age of 55. On June 22 at 1 p.m., the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan held a ‚ÄúGeneral Assembly Funeral‚Äù at Nantou Presbyterian Church.

However, to many, the circumstances surrounding Hsieh Wei‚Äôs death were not seen as a simple accident but rather a case with unresolved suspicions. Some believe that because he had refused the Kuomintang‚Äôs recruitment to run for Nantou County Magistrate and, as the Moderator of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, resisted the government‚Äôs demand to withdraw from the World Council of Churches, he was targeted by Kuomintang secret agents and assassinated. (Chen Jin-xing, 2015)

In 1992, Hsieh Wei was posthumously awarded the Medical Contribution Award, which was accepted on his behalf by his wife.

Chen Xi-Zhen

Pastor Chen Xi-Zhen (November 14, 1895 ‚Äì February 15, 1990) was born in Keelung as the eldest son of Elder Chen Liang-Gu and Lin Lai-Hao. His father, Chen Yu-Gu, was baptized at Dr. Mackay‚Äôs mission early in life and was a devout Christian. He served as a deacon at Dadaocheng Church and was elected as an elder of the newly established Taipei Shuanglian Church in 1918, a year after its completion. Encouraged by his father, Chen Xi-Zhen pursued a path in ministry. In 1916, he graduated from Tamsui Oxford College (the predecessor of Taipei Theological Seminary). During his studies, he excelled academically and was sent by missionaries to further his theological education at Doshisha University in Kyoto, Japan. Two years later, he transferred to Tokyo Theological Seminary to continue his studies in biblical and theological research. In 1919, after completing his studies, he returned to Taiwan and first served as a preacher at Yilan Church. In April 1921, he was assigned to Taipei Shuanglian Church, beginning a pastoral career that lasted fifty-five years. (Su Hui-Ya, 1990, pp. 14‚Äì15)

Later, Pastor Chen negotiated a land exchange with Mackay Memorial Hospital, enabling the successful construction of a second church building for Shuanglian Church in 1926. This two-story structure featured a worship hall on the upper floor, while the lower floor housed a Sunday school chapel, offices, and other facilities. The church was completed in 1927 and was considered one of the finest churches of its time. Following its expansion, the congregation grew rapidly, prompting the construction of Shuanglian Tower on Zhongshan North Road in Taipei, which became the largest church building in Taiwan at the time and one of the most representative large-scale churches. In 1928, he also helped establish Shuanglian Kindergarten, bringing attention to early childhood education within the church and pioneering church-run preschool education efforts.

Beyond his pastoral duties at Shuanglian Church, he played a significant role in the development of Christianity in northern Taiwan. He supported various church organizations, assisted in relocating Taiwan Theological Seminary to Lingtou, Yangmingshan, and helped Pastor Sun Ya-Ko establish Yushan Theological Seminary. He also held numerous key positions, including Moderator of the Northern Synod of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, Moderator of the Northern General Assembly, Vice Moderator and Moderator of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, Chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Bible Society of the Republic of China, President of the National Christian Council, Chairman of Billy Graham‚Äôs Taiwan Crusade, and Chairman and Honorary Chairman of the Bible Society of the Republic of China, among other roles. His efforts were entirely dedicated to advancing Christianity in Taiwan. (Kang Jun-Bi, 1994, pp. 251‚Äì253)

In 1940, while serving as Moderator of the Taipei Synod, he strongly advocated for the establishment of the Northern General Assembly to unify various synods. After the Northern General Assembly was established, under his leadership, the so-called ‚ÄúNewcomer Movement‚Äù was launched, pushing for church reform and greater church independence. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2015, pp. 18‚Äì19) During his tenure, he also proposed founding Tamsui Industrial and Commercial Junior College to cultivate professionals for the business sector.

The conflicts between the northern and southern churches could be seen as a reflection of the discord between Chen Xi-Zhen and Huang Wu-Dong. Despite holding many significant positions, Chen was never able to attain the highest position in the General Assembly, which was a disappointment compared to his other achievements. However, Chen Xi-Zhen was highly active in politics. He served as a committee member of the ‚ÄúThree Principles of the People for Unified China Alliance,‚Äù a director of the Asian Christian Apologetics and Anti-Communism Conference, and was one of the key figures responsible for building the ‚ÄúChiang Kai-shek Memorial Church.‚Äù His involvement in these roles was particularly notable compared to other Christian figures aligned with the ruling party. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2015, pp. 18‚Äì19)

His wife, Hsiao Mei-Chu (1898‚Äì1991), was the eldest daughter of Pastor Hsiao An-Ju and Chen Chen-Jen (the eldest daughter of Pastor Chen Rong-Hui). The couple had four sons and one daughter. In 1971, Faith Theological Seminary in the United States, associated with the fundamentalist Christian movement and Pastor McIntire, awarded him an honorary Doctor of Theology degree. (Lai Jun-Ming, 1994, p. 43) He retired from Shuanglian Church in 1976. On February 15, 1990, he passed away due to illness at the age of 95 and was buried in the Chen family cemetery in Liuzhangli, Taipei.

Dr. Marcy Leroy Ditmanson

Dr. Marcy Leroy Ditmanson, M.D. (1919‚Äì2000), was an American surgeon and the founder of Chiayi Christian Hospital. He was the recipient of the 6th Medical Contribution Award in Taiwan. Born to American Lutheran missionaries in China, he did not return to the United States until the age of 17, where he pursued a degree in history. After graduating in 1940, he returned to China. During the Second Sino-Japanese War, he was captured and became a prisoner of war under the Japanese military. While in prison in 1941, he fell in love with Joyce Stranks, the daughter of an Australian missionary. After the war, he earned a Master‚Äôs degree in history from Yenching University. Witnessing the suffering of refugees while working for the United Nations Relief and Rehabilitation Administration (UNRRA), he decided to pursue a career in medicine. In 1948, he was briefly detained by the Chinese Communist Party for a month before being released. That same year, he married Joyce Stranks in Australia and returned to the U.S. to study medicine at the University of Minnesota. (Planning Office, 2019, p. 5)

After completing his residency in 1957, Dr. Ditmanson came to Taiwan and worked as a surgeon at Mennonite Christian Hospital in Hualien for a year. He then conducted a solo motorcycle tour around the island to assess the state of medical services, discovering a severe shortage of healthcare in the Yunlin-Chiayi-Tainan region. In August 1958, he began providing medical services from his home in Chiayi. In 1962, with funds raised overseas, he purchased land on Zhongxiao Road in Chiayi City and officially established Chiayi Christian Hospital. Despite serving as the hospital‚Äôs director, he barely took a salary. (Chou Tien-Hung, 2024, p. 61)

In 1968, due to the increasing number of polio and scoliosis patients, Dr. Ditmanson returned to the U.S. for four years to specialize in orthopedics. He later spent two years practicing medicine in Bangladesh before returning to Taiwan in 1974. (Chou Tien-Hung, 2024, p. 63) After his return, he spent three days a week at Chiayi Christian Hospital performing consultations and surgeries and another three days at Pingtung Christian Hospital overseeing orthopedic treatments. In 1981, he retired and returned to the U.S. The hospital‚Äôs board of directors offered him a retirement fund of one million NTD, but having witnessed two elderly employees spend their final days in intensive care due to terminal cancer, he decided to establish the ‚ÄúDitmanson Fund‚Äù to train professional palliative care teams. (Chiayi Christian Hospital website)

In December 1993, the Ditmanson Memorial Building at Chiayi Christian Hospital was inaugurated, and Dr. Ditmanson and his wife returned to Taiwan for the ribbon-cutting ceremony. In 1996, he was awarded Taiwan‚Äôs 6th Medical Contribution Award and returned to Taiwan to accept it. In 1997, during the 35th anniversary of Chiayi Christian Hospital, over twenty members of the Ditmanson family traveled to Taiwan to participate in the celebrations. In his later years, Dr. Ditmanson suffered from Alzheimer‚Äôs disease and Parkinson‚Äôs disease and settled in Green Valley, Pima County, Arizona. He passed away on June 27, 2000. His wife, Joyce Stranks, later passed away in Arizona in 2018. (Planning Office, 2019, p. 6)

Ecumenical Consultative Committee (ECC) / The National Council of Churches of Taiwan (NCCT)

The ecumenical movement in Taiwan, like in other parts of the world, was initiated by Protestant churches. Under the advocacy of figures such as Huang Wu-Dong from the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT), the predecessor of the ‚ÄúEcumenical Consultative Committee‚Äù (ECC) was established. The PCT joined the World Council of Churches (WCC) in 1952. From 1959 to 1962, three ‚ÄúFaith and Order‚Äù conferences were convened under the WCC, bringing together over ten denominations. (Fang Zhi-Rong, 1992, p. 198)

One key event took place on October 20, 1960, when four denominations‚Äîthe Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, the Taiwan Episcopal Church, the Taiwan Lutheran Church, and the Methodist Church in the Republic of China‚Äîjointly organized the ‚ÄúChristian Church Work Seminar‚Äù at Tunghai University in Taichung. Several subsequent meetings were held to plan closer collaboration in evangelism and missionary activities. (NCCT website) The participating denominations recognized this effort as a blessing from God. During the fourth ‚ÄúFaith and Order‚Äù conference held in Taichung on April 15-16, 1962, representatives from the Catholic Church were also invited to join, alongside delegates from the Episcopal Church, Baptist Church, Apostolic Church, Methodist Church, and Mennonite Church, with over twenty participants attending.

In 1963, the Presbyterian Church, the Episcopal Church, the Methodist Church, and the Lutheran Church formally established the ‚ÄúEcumenical Consultative Committee‚Äù (ECC), which the Catholic Church referred to as the ‚ÄúCatholic Ecumenical Consultative Committee.‚Äù (Fang Zhi-Rong, 1992, p. 198) The first chairman was Catholic Bishop Stanislaus Lo Kuang, with Pastor Yang Qi-Shou, Pastor Shang Zheng-Zong, Bishop Yu Ji-Bin, and Zhang Xing-Zhu serving as the first executive committee members. The committee aimed to strengthen interchurch relations, overcome denominational barriers, promote cooperation in church affairs, engage in social welfare, charity, and educational work, facilitate communication, inform church leaders about current events and needs, and collaborate on initiatives for the well-being of the people in Taiwan. It also sought to participate in global missionary work to ‚Äúachieve unity in Christ.‚Äù However, during the government registration process, the Ministry of the Interior preemptively established the ‚ÄúChinese Christian Council,‚Äù appointing Chen Wei-Ping as its chairman and requiring other Christian denominations to join. It was not until January 24, 1991, that the organization was officially registered with the Ministry of the Interior as the ‚ÄúNational Council of Churches in the Republic of China‚Äù (NCC). (Lin Chun-Jie, 2012)

At the end of 1966, the ECC invited the Catholic Church to become a formal member. Bishop Wang Chang-Ling of the Episcopal Church wrote to Archbishop Lo Kuang of Taipei, stating, ‚ÄúWe hope you will accept this invitation and participate in our meetings. In addition to yourself, two other Catholic representatives are also welcome.‚Äù On January 9, 1967, Catholic representatives attended an ECC meeting and formally became members. (Fang Zhi-Rong, 1992, p. 198)

On November 19, 1971, a meeting was held at Tunghai University to discuss the future of Taiwan following the expulsion of the ‚ÄúRepublic of China‚Äù from the United Nations. The participating representatives included the Presbyterian Church, the Episcopal Church, the Lutheran Church, the Methodist Church, the Baptist Church, the Mennonite Church, the Catholic Church, Tunghai University, and other Christian organizations. During this meeting, Daniel Beeby, the Vice President of Tainan Theological College, proposed that in these turbulent times, churches should speak with conscience based on Christian faith and not remain silent. His proposal was supported by Baptist Pastor Chou Lian-Hua and Methodist Bishop Warner.

The meeting concluded that a statement should be issued under the name of the ‚ÄúEcumenical Consultative Committee of the Republic of China‚Äù and designated a drafting committee. This group included Baptist Pastor Chou Lian-Hua, Methodist Bishop Roy E. Brown, and Daniel Beeby. Later, Lutheran Bishop Chang Qi-Tang joined, with Presbyterian Pastor Kao Chun-Ming serving as the convener. The statement urged the government to implement comprehensive political reforms and respect the sovereignty and dignity of the 15 million people of Taiwan, allowing them to determine Taiwan‚Äôs future based on the principle of ‚Äúself-determination.‚Äù However, due to the sensitivity of the statement‚Äôs content, only the Presbyterian Church was willing to sign and publish it, while other denominations refused. As a result, the statement, titled A Declaration and Suggestions on National Affairs, was issued solely by the Presbyterian Church in December 1971, causing a major international reaction. (Lu Jun-Yi, 2017)

In 1991, the organization held its inaugural meeting at the Taipei YMCA City Center and was formally registered as the ‚ÄúNational Council of Churches in the Republic of China‚Äù (NCC), becoming a legally recognized organization. Today, the National Council of Churches of Taiwan (NCCT) consists of 14 church groups and institutions. Its mission remains to strengthen interchurch relations, promote cooperative efforts, engage in social welfare, charity, and education, convey timely messages, and participate in global missionary work to achieve unity in Christ. The organization hosts various activities, including joint worship services, the World Day of Prayer, the Week of Prayer for Christian Unity, the Asia Remembrance Service, and the Christian Sports Festival.

Christian Children‚Äôs Fund (Now Taiwan Fund for Children and Families, TFCF)

The Christian Children‚Äôs Fund (CCF), originally named China‚Äôs Children Fund, was founded by American Presbyterian missionary J. Calvitt Clarke. In 1937, Clarke began rescuing children orphaned by war in the battle zones of northern China, making the relief of wounded, starving, and displaced orphans his primary mission. On October 6, 1938, he established an orphanage to aid children displaced by the Second Sino-Japanese War. The following year, Clarke‚Äôs humanitarian efforts inspired many American church leaders, leading to the formal establishment of China‚Äôs Children Fund (CCF) in Richmond, Virginia, USA. Around the same time, the first official orphanage was founded in Guangdong, China, and subsequently, CCF established 42 orphanages across China, providing care for countless orphaned children. (Hsiao Tsung-Chi, 2005, pp. 317‚Äì318)

In 1950, after the Chinese Communist Party took control of mainland China, foreign missionaries were expelled, forcing Christian Children‚Äôs Fund to relocate its operations. Reverend Verent Mills, who was overseeing CCF‚Äôs work in China, moved to Hong Kong to continue its mission. This later became the foundation for the Hong Kong Youth Services Office, which was officially established on August 1, 1978.

Meanwhile, in August 1950, CCF launched its child welfare services in Taiwan, establishing the first family-style orphanage, Guangyin Orphanage, in Taichung. As Taiwan‚Äôs economy improved, CCF became financially independent from the United States in 1985 and rebranded itself as the Taiwan Fund for Children and Families (TFCF). In June 2002, TFCF joined ChildFund Alliance, a global network of 12 international child welfare organizations.

Lifeline

Lifeline is an international telephone counseling organization dedicated to suicide prevention through 24-hour crisis hotline services. The world‚Äôs first Lifeline center was established in 1963 by Rev. Dr. Sir Alan Walker, a Methodist missionary in Sydney, Australia. The center was founded at the Wesley Mission‚Äôs central district to provide telephone-based support for individuals experiencing loneliness, despair, distress, and emotional pain. (Lifeline Australia)

During the 1960s, Taiwan underwent rapid urbanization and industrial development, leading to an increase in social problems, including a rising suicide rate. For example, in 1963, the ‚ÄúTaipei U.S. Military Club‚Äù was established at the intersection of Zhongshan North Road and Minzu West Road, spurring the growth of the entertainment industry in the area. As a result, Mackay Memorial Hospital‚Äôs emergency room began receiving an increasing number of female entertainers attempting suicide by consuming sleeping pills. In 1965, Dr. Samuel Noordhoff, the director of Mackay Memorial Hospital, attended a medical conference in Tokyo, where he learned about Australia‚Äôs Lifeline initiative. On March 14, 1969, Mackay Memorial Hospital officially established a Family Counseling Clinic, and Dr. Noordhoff facilitated collaboration between Mackay Hospital and National Taiwan University Hospital‚Äôs Department of Psychiatry. On July 1, Mackay Lifeline was officially launched under the leadership of Dr. Samuel Noordhoff, Vice Director Dr. Chang Jin-Wen, several professors from National Taiwan University Hospital, and Pastor Cheng Lien-Te. This organization led the coordination of Lifeline services across different cities and counties in Taiwan. Dr. Bright‚Äôs vision was to ‚Äúwin the world for Christ in his lifetime.‚Äù This personal mission led to the launch of the organization‚Äôs global evangelism movement. In 1952, Bill Bright and his team faced difficulties in sharing the gospel, prompting them to collaborate with salesman Bob Ringer to develop the Four Spiritual Laws, a structured approach to evangelistic conversations. Ashram (ÊÑõ‰øÆÊúÉ), sometimes translated as ‚Äú‰∫û‰øÆÊ¨Ñ,‚Äù is originally a term from the Indian language, composed of two words: A meaning ‚Äúfrom‚Äù and shram meaning ‚Äúhard work.‚Äù In essence, it signifies ‚Äúwithdrawing from daily life for spiritual and physical training,‚Äù which is why it is often translated as ‚Äúretreat.‚Äù In India, such retreats are common and not limited to religious gatherings.

Participants are divided into small groups called ‚ÄúHoly Families,‚Äù where they pray together, share their faith, and engage in deep spiritual reflection. During ‚Äúopen-heart sessions,‚Äù participants courageously confess their struggles, seek forgiveness, and rededicate themselves to God through commitments, intercessory prayer, and offerings. The central theme of Ashram is based on 1 Timothy 4:7‚Äî‚ÄúTrain yourself in godliness‚Äù‚Äîstressing how faith should be practiced in daily life.

This movement, known for its transformative impact on individual spirituality and church renewal, was greatly expanded in Japan by Pastor Enomoto Yasuroh (Ê¶éÊú¨‰øùÈÉé, 1925‚Äì1977) of the Congregational Church, who boldly adopted and promoted Dr. Stanley Jones‚Äôs Ashram model, leading to a significant revival of the movement in Japan.

In 1970, Pastor Su Tian-Ming, then serving as chairman of the Overseas Mission Committee of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, invited Pastor Enomoto Yasuroh to Taiwan to introduce the Ashram movement. Since then, Taiwan Ashram has continued to hold nationwide gatherings every February and March, with additional retreats organized in cities like Kaohsiung during the summer.

Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

Indigenous Peoples and the ‚ÄúMiracle of the 20th Century‚Äù

The introduction of religious missionary activities in Taiwan dates back to the late 19th century when Dutch and Spanish missionaries evangelized among the Plains Indigenous groups in the western regions. However, modern Indigenous evangelism began during the late Japanese colonial period. Despite the imposition of a prohibition on religious activities under the colonial ‚ÄúIndigenous Management Policy,‚Äù missionary efforts remained active. Two missionaries, Japanese independent evangelist Inoue Inosuke and Canadian Anglican missionary Narcissus Peter Yates, defied the ban and entered Indigenous villages, leaving behind a legacy of evangelism in the eastern and central-northern Indigenous communities. (Cheng Yang-En, 2014)

A true breakthrough came when the Taroko Indigenous evangelists Ciwang Iwal and Wilan-Takoh, supported by missionary James I. Dickson and his wife, spread Christianity among their people despite facing severe hardships and persecution. (Suning Siwa, 2024)

After the Nationalist government took control of Taiwan following World War II, it initially imposed strict regulations on Indigenous areas. However, due to the leadership‚Äôs own religious inclinations, Christian organizations received preferential treatment. Many missionaries from Europe, the United States, and Taiwan carried out evangelistic work in Indigenous communities. Taiwanese pastors such as Luo Xian-Chun, Hu Wen-Chi, Hsu You-Tsai, Chuang Sheng-Mao, Lin Peter, and Chen Yao-Zong were actively engaged in Indigenous missions with the tacit approval of the government. Foreign missionaries like James I. Dickson returned to Taiwan in 1946, expanding Indigenous mission work alongside theological education. They also supported local pastors in their missionary efforts. (Luo Wei-Ren & Luo Wei-Dao, 2016, pp. 52‚Äì95)

In 1946, Dickson collaborated with Hakka pastor Wen Rong-Chun and others to establish the Taiwan Bible Institute in Hualien, the predecessor of Yushan Theological Seminary, which trained many Indigenous church and community leaders.

The number of Indigenous Christian believers in Taiwan grew from a few thousand to 100,000 by 1965, with churches flourishing across Indigenous communities. The widespread acceptance of Christianity among Taiwan‚Äôs Indigenous peoples was so remarkable that it became known as the ‚ÄúMiracle of the 20th Century.‚Äù (Cheng Yang-En, 2014)

During the 1970s, many Indigenous churches experienced spiritual revival movements, leading some congregations to engage in overseas missionary work, a unique phenomenon among Indigenous Christian communities. After martial law was lifted, government restrictions on Indigenous areas were gradually eased, fostering closer interaction between Indigenous churches and those in urban areas.

Apologetics and Anti-Communism / Patriotic Christian Defense

During the 1950s, the world was entrenched in the Cold War, and within Western Christianity, the ecumenical movement led to the establishment of the World Council of Churches (WCC) in 1948. The WCC adopted a stance of accepting socialist countries and later advocated for the recognition of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, which brought it into ideological conflict with the Kuomintang (KMT) regime in Taiwan. (Lin Jin-Shui, 2003, p. 345)

Under the party-state system during Taiwan‚Äôs Cold War era, the political influence on all aspects of society was pervasive, and Christianity in Taiwan inevitably became involved in the Apologetics and Anti-Communism / Patriotic Christian Defense movement. This period marked a significant turning point in the history of Christianity in Taiwan. KMT official Zhang Jing-Yu followed the lead of American fundamentalist pastor Carl McIntire, who criticized the WCC. In 1960, Zhang launched the Anti-Communist Christian Defense Movement, rallying Mandarin-speaking churches to denounce the WCC, accusing it of compromising Christian faith and violating biblical teachings. (Huang Ke-Xian, 2021, p. 23)

In 1962, Zhang founded the Christian Apologetics and Anti-Communism United Association as a counterforce against the WCC. (Huang Ke-Xian, 2021, p. 23) In 1965, he established the Asian Christian Anti-Communism Association (ACAA) with the intent of weakening the influence of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT), which was under suspicion for alleged pro-communist sympathies during the height of martial law. In 1969, the World Christian Anti-Communism Association (WCAA) was established while the ACAA continued to exist until the two organizations officially merged in 1973. The WCAA explicitly prioritized anti-communism and aimed for the total eradication of communism.

Zhang Jing-Yu and others established fundamentalist Christian groups under the banner of apologetics and anti-communism, not only following McIntire‚Äôs model but also potentially acting on covert directives from the Chiang Kai-Shek regime to mobilize religious organizations for political objectives.

In retrospect, while McIntire‚Äôs fundamentalism stemmed from resistance to liberal theology, the fundamentalist movement in Taiwan under Apologetics and Anti-Communism was primarily driven by party-state control and anti-communist ideology. The opposition between the Anti-Communist Christian Defense Movement and the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT), as well as the WCC, was not a doctrinal dispute within Christianity but rather a struggle over political ideology. This movement ultimately served as a mechanism for the KMT government to monitor and divide churches to maintain political control.

The Ecumenical Bible Translation

On January 27, 1965, a historic meeting was held at the Kowloon Furama Hotel in Hong Kong, bringing together Protestant and Catholic representatives from Taiwan and Hong Kong to discuss a joint Bible translation effort. Participants included Catholic scholars from the Studium Biblicum Franciscanum, such as Fathers Gabriele Maria Allegra, Theobaldus Diederich, Chen Wei-Tong, and Liang Ya-Ming. Representing the Protestant side were Eugene A. Nida, director of the United Bible Societies (UBS) Translation Department; Gu Dun-Fu, a professor at Tunghai University; Pastor Chou Lian-Hua; Vernon Lowis, General Secretary of the Hong Kong Bible Society; and Methodist Pastor Chou Yu-Xi. The initial goal was to produce a common translation that would serve modern readers. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2022)

However, strong opposition arose against the introduction of a new Chinese Bible translation. In 1964, Rev. Lai Bing-Jiong, then director of the Taiwan Bible Society, issued two statements addressing concerns from Taiwanese churches regarding whether the organization intended to retranslate or revise the Bible. Some opponents feared that theological perspectives inconsistent with traditional Christian doctrine might be subtly introduced. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2022) Despite this resistance, increasing numbers of believers expressed the need for a more conversational and accessible Bible translation. Eugene A. Nida, a leading authority on Bible translation, proposed that existing translations be rendered into contemporary language. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2022)

During this same period, the Catholic Church initiated a translation project to meet its evangelistic needs. Two scholars, Gao Ji-Huan and Chen Bang-Zhen, who had studied English in Japan, completed a translation of the Four Gospels in July 1966. At the end of that year, the text was submitted to the United Bible Societies for review by Protestant scholars. In 1968, a Taiwanese Romanized version of the Four Gospels was published, followed by the complete New Testament in 1972. However, this translation faced restrictions in Taiwan, as the government banned it shortly after its publication by the Taiwan Bible Society in October 1973, limiting its circulation. (Tsai Jin-Tu, 2011, p. 33)

By the 1970s, the United Bible Societies formally initiated the Contemporary Chinese Bible (Áèæ‰ª£‰∏≠ÊñáË≠ØÊú¨) translation project, hoping that this version could serve as a reference for future translations into various Chinese dialects. Key figures in the translation discussions included Pastor Chou Lian-Hua, Luo Wei-Ren, and Tsai Ren-Li, with the Taiwan Bible Society officially launching the translation effort following these meetings. The New Testament was published in 1975, followed by a New Testament with Psalms in 1976, and finally, the complete Contemporary Chinese Bible in 1979. A revised edition was later released in 1997.

This translation followed the dynamic equivalence principle developed by Eugene A. Nida in the 1960s, aiming for natural readability in contemporary Chinese. The translation team was led by Xu Mu-Shi (hired by the American Bible Society), alongside Luo Wei-Ren, Chou Lian-Hua, Wang Cheng-Zhang, and Jiao Ming. The team used Today‚Äôs English Version (later renamed the Good News Bible) as a reference. The Old Testament was translated based on the Biblia Hebraica Kittel (BHK) third edition and the Septuagint, while the New Testament was translated from the United Bible Societies‚Äô Greek New Testament third and fourth editions. Notably, three Catholic scholars participated in the review process. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2022)

The Contemporary Chinese Bible featured modern Chinese syntax, making it more fluent than L√º Zhenzhong‚Äôs Translation and more readable than the older Chinese Union Version (CUV). However, despite its release, it never reached the level of widespread acceptance enjoyed by the CUV.

In 1975, the Catholic Church published its New Testament using the Contemporary Chinese Bible translation. Taipei Archbishop Stanislaus Lo Kuang approved the edition and wrote its preface. However, because the Contemporary Chinese Bible did not include the Deuterocanonical books, the Catholic Church only recognized the New Testament portion. This project can be seen as an implicit achievement of the Catholic-Protestant ecumenical movement.

In the 1980s, the Joint Bible Translation Committee was established to facilitate cooperative efforts. Despite years of effort, the committee ultimately only completed The Four Gospels: Common Translation, which was finally published in 2015. (Tseng Ching-Bao, 2022)

Master Sheng Yen

Master Sheng Yen (ÈáãËÅñÂö¥, January 22, 1931 ‚Äì February 3, 2009) was born in Nantong, Jiangsu. He was the founder of Dharma Drum Mountain and Dharma Drum Lineage, as well as the 51st lineage holder of the Caodong School and the 57th lineage holder of the Linji School of Chan Buddhism.

He became a monk in his youth but was forced to return to lay life and join the military due to the civil war in 1949. After arriving in Taiwan with the Nationalist army, he continued his service, working as a communications officer in the logistics division due to his Buddhist precepts against killing.

Early Writings and Response to Christian Criticism

In the 1950s, Buddhism in Taiwan faced increasing challenges. Some monks engaged in misconduct, leading to misunderstandings and criticism from lay believers. At the same time, certain Christian clergy, possibly emboldened by the Kuomintang government‚Äôs preferential treatment of Christianity, launched public critiques of Buddhism. This led to a defensive response from Buddhist leaders, including Master Zhu Yun, who gave public lectures in Tainan Park comparing Buddhism and Christianity, later recorded in A Comparison of Buddhism and Christianity by Li Zhigang. Master Sheng Yen first engaged with Christian discourse when he came across A Refutation of ‚ÄúA Comparison of Buddhism and Christianity‚Äù, written by Wu En-Pu, the editor of Christian Life Bimonthly. In 1959, he further clarified his views in On the Similarities and Differences Between Buddhism and Christianity, addressing misconceptions spread by King Fung, a publication from the Christian Institute for Chinese Religion Studies in Hong Kong. After leaving the military in 1960, Master Sheng Yen re-ordained as a monk. In 1969, he traveled to Japan to pursue further education. In 1975, he completed all academic requirements at Rissho University and obtained his doctoral degree, becoming the first Han Chinese Buddhist monk to earn a doctorate in Japan. (Sheng Yen, 2005, Online Edition)

Returning to Taiwan in 1977, he became the abbot of Nong Chan Monastery and, in 1979, founded the Chan Meditation Center in New York, beginning his regular teaching trips between the United States and Taiwan. In 2005, he transmitted the Dharma Drum lineage to 12 disciples. He passed away from kidney failure on February 3, 2009, while traveling from National Taiwan University Hospital to the Dharma Drum Mountain World Center for Buddhist Education. He was 78 years old.

Anti-Christian Organizations

Christianity Under the Party-State System

From the founding of the Kuomintang (KMT), there were many Christians among its ranks, including Sun Yat-sen, Chen Shao-bai, Zheng Shi-liang, Lu Hao-dong, Soong Yao-ru, and Xu Qian. Chiang Kai-shek also converted to Christianity before marrying Soong Mei-ling at the request of the Soong family in order to secure their political support (Huang Zijin & Pan Guangzhe, 1927, 38). In his 1947 constitutional declaration, Chiang publicly stated: ‚ÄúThe 36th year of the Republic of China is the Christmas of 1947, the year of Jesus‚Äô birth‚Ä¶ The special feature of our new constitution is that it guarantees the fundamental elements of Christian doctrine‚Äînamely, the dignity and freedom of the individual‚Äîto all our compatriots nationwide‚Äù (Chiang Kai-shek, 1947, 1-6). After the KMT‚Äôs retreat to Taiwan in 1949, the high proportion of Christians in its ruling ranks remained unchanged.

Chiang Kai-shek incorporated Christianity into his rule by accusing the Communist Party of atheism and religious persecution, thereby aligning Christian identity with loyalty to his leadership (Wang Zhaowen, 2023). For the KMT regime, religious policy was ultimately a tool to maintain political control‚ÄîChristians had to be both patriotic and devout, and Christianity itself served as a political instrument. The regime further delineated any force that could potentially challenge Chiang‚Äôs leadership.

From their perspective, anything deemed unpatriotic was also considered un-Christian. After 1949, the KMT‚Äôs approach to Christianity in Taiwan was characterized by the principle of ‚Äúprotecting the faith through anti-communism‚Äù and the belief that ‚Äúto protect the faith, one must oppose communism‚Äù (Liu Weikai, 2010, 10). The Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, now recognized for supporting ‚ÄúTaiwan independence,‚Äù was actually encouraged by the KMT in the 1950s due to its alignment with the regime‚Äôs strategy of ‚Äúdefending the faith against communism.‚Äù

Chiang Kai-shek and many other KMT leaders were fundamentalist Christians with strong faith and a fervor for evangelism. They were deeply concerned about the potential negative influence of modernist Christians and communism. Any Christian group or figure that was not explicitly anti-communist, ‚Äúdisobedient,‚Äù or ‚Äúinsufficiently cooperative‚Äù was classified as heretical and unfaithful (Su Muli, 2010, 135). For instance, when the Presbyterian Church clashed with the KMT government over issues such as the World Council of Churches controversy, the establishment of the Taiwan Christian Fellowship, and its insistence on preaching in Hokkien, it faced suppression. Pastor Chou Lien-hua was severely warned by KMT intelligence agencies simply for expressing support for the Presbyterian Church‚Äôs centennial celebration in the 1960s.

By the 1960s, under the pretext of ‚Äúprotecting the faith through anti-communism,‚Äù the KMT government sought to establish the ‚ÄúAsian Christian Defense and Anti-Communist Conference‚Äù within the Christian community. High-ranking KMT officials such as Cheng Wen-xiang and Zhang Jing-yu, who were also church leaders, organized various Christian groups to actively participate. Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs KMT effectively leveraged the interests of American conservative missionaries to further its political agenda.

Buddhism‚Äôs Counterattack Against Christianity

Starting in the 1950s, the environment for Buddhism in Taiwan began to deteriorate. At the same time, under the preferential treatment given to Christianity by the Chiang regime, certain Christian groups leveraged their advantage to launch a series of challenges against Buddhism, which in turn provoked a Buddhist counterattack. In the previous era, Master Zhu Yun had already engaged in efforts to defend Buddhism. He publicly delivered a lecture in Tainan Park comparing Buddhism and Christianity, which was later recorded into a book titled A Comparison Between Buddhism and Christianity by layman Li Zhigang.

Even in the 1960s, some Christian clergy, for reasons unknown, launched a series of attacks against Buddhism, prompting a response from Buddhist writers, including Master Yin Shun. Around Youth Day in 1963, a group of believers from a church on Heping East Road in Taipei visited Huiri Lecture Hall and presented Master Yin Shun with a copy of the Old and New Testaments, urging him to ‚Äústudy it.‚Äù Seeing this as an offensive gesture, he responded by writing two articles, God Loves the World and Between God and Jehovah (Yin Shun, 1994, 160), which were later included in the sixth volume of My View on Religion in The Collection of Wonderful Clouds. Additionally, My View on Religion contained another article titled A Further Discussion on God‚Äôs Love for the World (Shi Shengyan, 1991, 9), making a total of three articles on Christianity published between July 1963 and August 1964.

Meanwhile, Wu Enpu, the chief editor of Christian Life Bimonthly, wrote a rebuttal titled A Refutation of A Comparison Between Buddhism and Christianity in response to Master Zhu Yun‚Äôs views. This article eventually reached Master Sheng Yan before he had re-entered monastic life. After reading it, he found many of its arguments to be biased. As a result, he spent ten days composing A Critique of ‚ÄúA Refutation of A Comparison Between Buddhism and Christianity‚Äù (Master Sheng Yan, 1999, Preface).

In 1959, he further wrote On the Similarities and Differences Between Buddhism and Christianity to clarify the misconceptions spread by Jingfeng, a magazine published by the Christian Chinese Religious Research Society in Dao Feng Shan, Hong Kong. Subsequently, he published A Further Discussion on the Similarities and Differences Between Buddhism and Christianity to set the record straight.

After completing his military service in 1960, Master Sheng Yan re-entered monastic life. Between 1964 and 1966, he continued writing articles discussing Christianity, which were published in Buddhist magazines such as Hai Chao Yin, Jue Shi, and Hong Kong Buddhism. In 1967, these articles were compiled and published as a book titled A Study on Christianity by the Buddhist Cultural Service Office. The book received highly polarized reactions within religious circles, with fundamentalist Christian believers vehemently attacking it (Master Sheng Yan, 1999, Preface).

The Death of Hsieh Wei

In 1968, local figures in Nantou County and the Kuomintang (KMT) Nantou County branch initially intended to support Hsieh Wei in running for Nantou County Magistrate, with the KMT refraining from fielding a competing candidate. However, Hsieh Wei, who had no interest in politics‚Äîincluding church politics‚Äîdeclined the offer (Chen Jin-xing, 2015). Nevertheless, in 1969, despite deliberately absenting himself from the meeting, he was elected as the Moderator of the 16th General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) (Chen Jin-xing, 2010, 168-170).

At that time, the PCT faced intense pressure from the government to withdraw from the World Council of Churches (WCC). Determined to safeguard the sovereignty and dignity of the PCT, Hsieh Wei resisted the KMT government‚Äôs demands and, with steadfast faith, temporarily secured the church‚Äôs seat in the WCC (Chen Jin-xing, 2015).

On the afternoon of June 17, 1970, Hsieh Wei (1916-1970), who was both a pastor and a physician, was driving from Datong Hospital in Nantou Town to Erlin Christian Hospital to perform emergency surgery when he was involved in a fatal car accident in Mingjian Township. Various ‚Äúspeculations‚Äù regarding the cause of his death have circulated (Hsieh Da-li, 2000, 79-81).

The most common explanations attribute the accident to his exhaustion from work. The previous night, he had performed surgery on a blackfoot disease patient in Beimen, returning home to Nantou at 4 a.m. on June 17, leading to fatigue and sleep deprivation while driving. Another theory suggests that Hsieh had preexisting high blood pressure and heart problems, and a sudden myocardial infarction while driving caused the fatal accident (Hsieh Da-li, 2000, 79-81).

More recently, some scholars, based on various clues, have suggested the ‚Äúpossibility‚Äù that Hsieh Wei was the target of a political assassination by the KMT. His refusal to comply with the government‚Äôs demands for the PCT to withdraw from the WCC may have led to his orchestrated death under the guise of a car accident. However, due to the pervasive atmosphere of the ‚ÄúWhite Terror‚Äù at the time, even Hsieh Wei‚Äôs close friends and family members avoided discussing the matter (Chen Jin-xing, 2015).
