Rev. Wudong Huang was born in 1909 in Donghouliao, Yizhu Village, T≈çseki District, Tainan Prefecture (present-day Dongguang Village, Yizhu Township, Chiayi County, Taiwan). Originally surnamed Li, he was adopted by the Huang family and took their surname. His family converted to Christianity and joined Niutiaowan Church when he was a child. After recovering from a severe illness in his youth, his father dedicated him to God, leading him to study at Tainan Theological Seminary. Upon graduating in 1930, he served at churches in Penghu, Shuishang (Chiayi), and Douliu (Yunlin). In 1937, he was ordained at Douliu Church. (Cheng Er-Yu, 1995, p. 46)

In January 1950, Huang was elected as the 10th Moderator of the Southern Synod and, in April of the same year, became the first General Secretary of the Southern Synod. In March 1951, Huang and other church leaders initiated the unification of the northern and southern churches, leading to the establishment of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT), where he served as its first Moderator. After studying in the United Kingdom for two years, he returned to Taiwan in 1953 and helped promote the Multiplication Movement within the Presbyterian Church while also representing the church in the founding of Tunghai University. In February 1957, he was elected as the first General Secretary of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, a position he held until his retirement in 1966.

Even after retirement, Huang remained active in church ministries, serving in various domestic and international Christian organizations. In July 1966, he traveled to the Philippines for short-term pastoral work, and from 1967 to 1972, he served as the director of the Taiwan Christian Welfare Organization.

In 1972, Huang traveled to the United States to visit his children and grandchildren. While in the U.S., he pastored at Grace Reformed Church in New York City and churches in Orange County, California. In July 1980, he returned to New York and continued to support Taiwanese churches and community organizations, leading to the formation of the Formosan Association for Public Affairs (FAPA).

In 1985, his Memoirs were published by the Taiwan Publishing Company in Los Angeles. (Cheng Er-Yu, 1995, pp. 48-49)

In 1987, Huang and Rev. Chang-Hui Huang were awarded honorary Doctor of Theology degrees by Tainan Theological Seminary. Concerned about the aging school buildings and the preservation of historical records, he advocated for the establishment of the Taiwan Church History Museum at Chang Jung High School.

In September 1994, he returned to Taiwan and settled in Changhua. On November 13, 1994, he passed away, and on December 10, a funeral service was held by the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan.

(August 20, 1914 ‚Äì October 28, 1988)

Rev. Shoki Coe, a native of Changhua, Taiwan, was a minister of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) and an influential theologian. He graduated from Westminster College, University of Cambridge in the United Kingdom and served as President of Tainan Theological Seminary as well as General Secretary of the Theological Education Fund (TEF) of the World Council of Churches (WCC) in London. In 1972, along with Rev. Wudong Huang and others, he co-founded the Formosans for Self-Determination Movement in the United States. (Cheng Er-Yu, 1995, pp. 48-49)

Early Life and Education

Growing up in Tainan, Coe was influenced by his father, Rev. Huang Siming, a Presbyterian minister. In 1927, he entered the Presbyterian Church Middle School and later transferred to Aoyama Gakuin High School in Tokyo in 1930. However, due to his father‚Äôs illness, he returned to Taiwan. The following year, he passed a rigorous entrance examination to attend Taipei High School. In 1934, he pursued philosophy at Tokyo Imperial University. In 1938, under the recommendation of Rev. W.E. Montgomery from Tainan Theological Seminary, he received a scholarship to study theology at Westminster College, University of Cambridge, where he completed a three-year program. (Cheng Yang-En, 2001, p. 31)

Pastoral and Theological Leadership in Taiwan

In 1941, Coe was ordained as a minister by the Church of Scotland and returned to Taiwan to teach at Tainan Theological Seminary, where he committed himself to theological education. In 1949, he was ordained as a minister of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan and became the first Taiwanese President of Tainan Theological Seminary, serving until 1965. During his tenure, he actively promoted the unification of the Northern and Southern Synods of the Presbyterian Church, leading to the formation of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan.

At the same time, with support from Dr. Van Dusen, President of Union Theological Seminary in New York, Coe helped establish Tunghai University under the United Board for Christian Higher Education in Asia. Along with Rev. Wudong Huang and Rev. Chen Mingqing, he served as one of the founding board members of Tunghai University. (Leung Bifeng, 2015, pp. 49-66)

Global Theological Influence and Political Advocacy

Starting in 1953, Coe participated in conferences of the World Council of Churches (WCC) and the World Presbyterian Alliance (WPA), where he was exposed to ecumenical theological thought. He persuaded Rev. Wudong Huang, the then General Secretary of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, to develop a post-war missionary strategy for the Taiwanese church, which indirectly led to the launch of the Multiplication Movement (P.K.U.).

In 1965, Coe moved to London to serve as General Secretary of the Theological Education Fund (TEF), becoming the first Taiwanese theologian to hold a position within the WCC. In 1971, he became Director of Theological Education at WCC, marking a significant milestone for Taiwanese representation in international Christian institutions.

While abroad, Coe remained deeply concerned about Taiwan‚Äôs political situation. In March 1972, he co-founded the Formosans for Self-Determination Movement in the U.S. alongside Rev. Wudong Huang, Rev. C.T. Song, and Dr. Lin Tsung-Yi. The movement called for Taiwanese self-determination and supported the three faith statements issued by the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan, leading to Coe‚Äôs placement on the Kuomintang government‚Äôs blacklist.

In 1980, together with Peng Ming-Min and other prominent overseas Taiwanese figures, Coe co-founded the Formosan Association for Public Affairs (FAPA), an organization dedicated to Taiwan‚Äôs international advocacy and diplomatic efforts. (Cheng Er-Yu, 1995, pp. 48-49)

Theological Contributions and Legacy

Coe made significant contributions to theological education and ecumenism, particularly through his pioneering use of the term ‚Äúcontextualization.‚Äù He argued that contextualization better captured the dynamic engagement of Christian faith with contemporary realities, surpassing the earlier concept of ‚Äúindigenization.‚Äù His work earned him recognition as a Taiwanese indigenous theologian committed to the ecumenical movement.

Moreover, his ‚ÄúM√∫-guƒÅn Theology‚Äù (Âë£È°òÁ•ûÂ≠∏) reflected the dual historical experiences of modern Taiwanese people, embodying both a sense of suffering and hope for a better future. (Cheng Yang-En, 2001, p. 33)

In 1987, Coe was awarded an Honorary Doctorate in Theology from Tainan Theological Seminary, alongside Rev. Wudong Huang. Concerned about the preservation of Taiwanese church history, he also helped establish the Taiwan Church History Museum at Chang Jung High School.

On October 28, 1988, Coe passed away in London, England, leaving behind a profound legacy in Taiwanese theological education, global ecumenism, and the Taiwanese democracy movement.

China Inland Mission (CIM) / Overseas Missionary Fellowship (OMF)

The China Inland Mission (CIM) was founded in 1865 by British missionary Hudson Taylor. In 1964, it was renamed the Overseas Missionary Fellowship (OMF), though it continues to be known as the China Inland Mission in Taiwan. It is a Congregationalist Protestant missionary society. (Chang Chen Yi-Ping, Tai Shao-Tseng, et al., 2005, p. 39)

In 1854, Hudson Taylor first arrived in China as a missionary of the Chinese Evangelization Society, founded by Karl Friedrich August G√ºtzlaff (1803‚Äì1851) under the Prussian Missionary Society. He initially preached in Shanghai and Shantou. In 1857, he settled in Ningbo and established the Ningbo Missionary Society.

In 1860, Taylor returned to England, and two years later (1862), he recruited James Joseph Meadows (1835‚Äì1914), the first volunteer for the Ningbo Missionary Society, and sent him to Ningbo. Recognizing the need for missionary work in inland China, Taylor and other early missionaries sought to follow the example of the Apostle Paul, avoiding areas where churches were already established. On June 25, 1865, the Ningbo Missionary Society was renamed the China Inland Mission (CIM). Its mission was to recruit missionaries willing to relocate permanently to inland China to spread Christianity beyond coastal regions. (Chang Chen Yi-Ping, Tai Shao-Tseng, et al., 2005, p. 39)

Growth and Controversy

In its early years, CIM missionaries adopted Chinese attire and hairstyles (shaving their foreheads and keeping queues), which sparked criticism in Britain. However, by 1912, when the Republic of China was established, CIM had over 1,000 missionaries, making it the largest missionary organization in China.

World Council of Churches (WCC)

The World Council of Churches (WCC), also known as the World Christian Council or the World Christian Cooperation Council, is an interdenominational Christian ecumenical organization founded in 1948. Its headquarters is located at the Ecumenical Centre in Geneva, Switzerland. The WCC‚Äôs member churches represent approximately 590 million Christians across about 150 countries (Harmon, Steven R., 2010, p. 97).

The origins of the WCC can be traced back to the 1910 World Missionary Conference, held in Edinburgh from June 14 to 23, 1910, where representatives from 159 Protestant organizations agreed on the importance of mutual respect among Christians and committed to seeking cooperation among different denominations in missionary regions to promote church unity. Church leaders at the conference reached a consensus to establish the WCC in 1937, but due to the outbreak of World War II, its official founding was postponed until August 23, 1948. Representatives from 147 churches convened in Amsterdam, Netherlands, to witness the merger of the Life and Work Movement and the Faith and Order Movement, which together formed the WCC (WCC, 2011).

The WCC‚Äôs member churches include most branches of the Oriental Orthodox Churches, the Assyrian Church of the East, and the Eastern Orthodox Churches. Many Protestant denominations are also members, including the Anglican Communion, Baptist churches, Lutheran churches, Methodist churches, Moravian Church, Mennonite churches, Presbyterian churches, and other Reformed churches, as well as some independent churches, Pentecostal churches, and Old Catholic churches. The Roman Catholic Church participates in WCC meetings as an observer.

Campus Evangelical Fellowship (CEF)

In 1951, after the Chinese government fully took control of Christian churches in China and following the proclamation of the Three-Self Declaration, the China Inland Mission (CIM) began withdrawing missionaries and redeploying them to other regions in East Asia. By 1952, some CIM missionaries had already started student evangelistic work in northern, central, and southern Taiwan, though there was no unified organization at the time.

One key figure in this movement was Cha Yi-Kun (later known as David Cha), who came to Taiwan from China in 1949 to study at the Baptist Theological Seminary. By then, Adeney had joined the International Fellowship of Evangelical Students (IFES), an evangelical student movement founded in 1947, and was responsible for coordinating and promoting student ministry in East Asia. Christian Denominations Resuming Operations in the 1950s

Background

In October 1949, as the Chinese Civil War ended with the Communist Party taking control of China, the Kuomintang (KMT) government retreated to Taiwan. The Communist Party‚Äôs atheist policies led many churches from mainland China to relocate to Taiwan. However, some denominations did not immediately resume operations upon arrival. Instead, they gradually regrouped and reestablished themselves in the 1950s, becoming part of Taiwan‚Äôs diverse religious landscape.

Lutheran Church

During the early missionary efforts of the 1950s, eight mission societies from four countries (the United States, Norway, Denmark, and Finland) arrived in Taiwan. Due to differences in policies, three countries with four mission societies remained under the Taiwan Lutheran Church, while the remaining four mission societies established independent synods.

  5. Republic of China Taiwan Lutheran Church (Established in 1977) ‚Äì Started in Pingtung and later expanded to Kaohsiung, Taipei, and Hsinchu. Methodist Church

Methodist missionary efforts in China date back to 1847, establishing annual conferences in various regions and investing in general and higher education, as well as medical services. After the regime change in 1949, many Methodists were among the large wave of immigrants who moved to Taiwan along with the KMT government.

On March 8, 1953, approximately 93 believers gathered in a classroom on the third floor of the Soochow University School of Law building on Hankou Street, Taipei, marking the official establishment of the Methodist Church in Taiwan.

In January 1953, Bishop Huang An-su and Rev. Edward K. Knettler hosted a tea reception at the Friends of China Club in Taipei to initiate the church‚Äôs outreach efforts. Later that year, the Taipei Methodist Church was officially founded, with additional house church gatherings established in Taichung and Tainan. The Methodist Church also convened a Taiwan-Hong Kong Provisional Annual Conference to resume its pastoral work for congregations in Taiwan and Hong Kong.

Bread of Life Christian Church (Ling Liang Church)

In 1936, Chao Shi-kuang joined the China Foreign Missionary Society and became one of the earliest Chinese missionaries to preach the gospel overseas in Southeast Asia. During World War II, in 1941, Chao launched the Ling Liang Monthly and founded Ling Liang Church through Bible study groups. In 1945, he officially established the China Christian Ling Liang World Evangelism Association.

When the Chinese Communist Party took power in 1949, some members of the Shanghai Ling Liang Church relocated to Taiwan along with the KMT government. In 1954, an informal gathering of believers in Taipei led to the decision to establish a new Ling Liang Church in Taiwan. In July of the same year, Chao Shi-kuang was invited to Taiwan, and by mid-September, the Taipei Bread of Life Christian Church was officially founded.

The 1954 Multiplication Movement

The Multiplication Movement (Poe-ka Un-tong, PKU) was a missionary initiative launched by the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (PCT) between 1954 and 1965, aiming to double the number of churches and believers within a decade.

In August 1954, the Second Assembly of the World Council of Churches (WCC) was held in Evanston, USA, under the theme ‚ÄúChrist‚Äîthe Hope of the World.‚Äù This assembly urged member churches to conduct in-depth research on their local missionary contexts. In response, Rev. T. C. Huang convened a seminar at Tainan Theological Seminary, where Rev. Huang Wu-Tung, then General Secretary of the Southern Synod, presented a study titled ‚ÄúEvangelism in Formosa.‚Äù His report highlighted that 161 out of Taiwan‚Äôs 324 towns and townships had no churches, and Christians made up only 0.72% of the total population. Reflecting on the ninety years of missionary efforts in Taiwan, he called for serious re-evaluation of the church‚Äôs effectiveness (Hsu Chien-Hsin, 1965, p. 342).

At the same time, the Kaohsiung Presbytery proposed a ‚ÄúBring All Taiwan to Christ‚Äù evangelism plan, led by Rev. Su Tian-Min of Kaohsiung Xinxing Church. This proposal laid the groundwork for the Multiplication Movement. Once accepted by the General Assembly, it was officially launched as a ten-year evangelistic campaign, envisioned as a gift to God for the centennial celebration of Christian missions in Taiwan.

Four Key Strategies of the Movement

  1. Believers ‚Äì Encouraging a ‚ÄúThree Offerings‚Äù initiative:

    • Dedication of heart (spiritual commitment)

    • Dedication of wealth (financial contributions)

    • Dedication of service (volunteering for missionary work)

  2. Local Church Leadership ‚Äì Strengthening church gatherings, selecting appropriate locations for weekday meetings, and gradually establishing new churches.

  3. Presbytery Evangelism Department ‚Äì Promoting and explaining the movement to mobilize believers, raising funds, conducting overall planning, and assisting churches in outreach, education, and training.

Impact and Criticism

The Multiplication Movement successfully met its goals of doubling the number of churches, believers, and financial contributions for church buildings. It had a profound impact on the expansion of the Presbyterian Church and its commitment to Taiwan‚Äôs social and religious development (Huang Bo-He, 2023, p. 147).

However, while the PCT celebrated the success of the movement, scholars later critiqued its theological and missionary implications:

  • The emphasis on numerical growth led to concerns about quantity over quality.

  • Evangelism remained individualistic, focusing on personal salvation and the afterlife rather than a holistic understanding of the gospel.

  • The reported growth statistics did not account for Taiwan‚Äôs population and economic growth trends or compare with other denominations.

  • Following the Multiplication Movement, Taiwan entered a period of stagnation in Christian development, making it a subject of continued sociological and theological study (Chu Hai-Yuan, 1982, pp. 494, 501).

Apologetics and Anti-Communism / Patriotic Apologetics

During the 1950s, the global situation was shaped by the Cold War. Under the ecumenical movement within Western Christianity, the World Council of Churches (WCC) was established in 1948. During the Cold War, Taiwan was under a party-state system, where political influence was pervasive. Ecumenical Movement

The ecumenical movement, which emerged in the 19th century and expanded in the 20th century, is a significant topic in modern Christian history. The World Missionary Conference, held from June 14 to 23, 1910 (also known as the Edinburgh World Missionary Conference), marked the beginning of the modern ecumenical movement. At this conference, representatives from 139 different Protestant denominations expressed their desire for unity among Protestant churches while spreading the gospel. Later scholars even referred to the movement initiated by this conference as a ‚ÄúNew Reformation.‚Äù The conference report highlighted the missionary achievements of the British Presbyterian Church in southern Taiwan and the Canadian Presbyterian Church in northern Taiwan: ‚ÄúEverything is favorable for active progress in Taiwan. On a relatively small land, there are so few obstacles, and the conditions are so conducive to rapid and thorough evangelization.‚Äù (Brian Stanley, 2009)

However, as early as 1903, there were calls for a merger between the northern and southern churches in Taiwan. Yet, due to significant disparities in church strength, the merger did not materialize. It was not until 1912, following the World Missionary Conference, that the global trend toward ‚Äúunity‚Äù may have influenced both sides to formally establish the ‚ÄúTaiwan Assembly.‚Äù Before its dissolution in 1944, the Taiwan Assembly held 20 meetings, jointly discussing and planning church regulations, religious education, and other matters. While faith and church governance cooperation progressed, there was a lack of consensus on hospital management and property affairs. The most challenging issue was the unification of theological seminaries, which remained an unresolved regret within the ecumenical movement. (Lu Qiming, 2022, 41-42)

With the merger of the northern and southern assemblies in 1951 to establish the General Assembly, the mission board‚Äôs global perspective continued, leading to Taiwan‚Äôs Presbyterian Church (PCT) joining international church organizations such as the World Council of Churches (WCC), the World Alliance of Reformed Churches (WARC), and the Christian Conference of Asia (CCA). Given the historical context of post-war denominational traditions arriving in Taiwan, the PCT, influenced by the ecumenical movement, actively participated in the ‚ÄúEcumenical Church Council‚Äù (ECC), an organization formed by mainstream churches (including the Catholic Church), promoting church unity within Taiwan. The PCT played a key role in establishing the Sunday School Association, Christian Tribune (with Pastor Huang Wudong as its first president), and Tunghai University (facilitated by Pastor Milo L. Milmine). These efforts stand as significant testimonies in Taiwan‚Äôs Christian history. However, in reality, many of these collaborations and organizational models ultimately failed, highlighting the paradox of Taiwan‚Äôs church unity movement. (Huang Bohe, 2005, 31-32)

The divisions within Taiwan‚Äôs church after the 1960s differed from the denominational separations in Europe and the United States. Unlike Western churches, Taiwanese denominations did not undergo theological disputes related to the Reformation, and doctrinal differences between them were not as significant as often imagined. Most believers inherited their denominational identity rather than consciously choosing it. The failure of the unity movement was instead influenced by historical experiences, political conflicts, ideological struggles, and power structures. (Huang Bohe, 2005, 33)

The Presbyterian Church, which strongly advocated unity, faced an insurmountable barrier in its efforts to unite with Mandarin-speaking churches, which were aligned with the ruling authorities. As a church belonging to a colonized ethnic group, the PCT found itself unable to extend its political, social justice, and identity concerns. Consequently, the two groups developed in entirely different directions. Unlike Mandarin-speaking churches, which were less involved in global ecumenical organizations, the Presbyterian Church, through its long-term participation, was profoundly influenced by the WCC‚Äôs emphasis on faith-based advocacy for human rights, social justice, ethnic harmony, and environmental sustainability.

Carl McIntire

Charles Curtis McIntire Jr. (May 17, 1906 ‚Äì March 19, 2002), also known as Pastor Carl McIntire, was the founder of the Bible Presbyterian Church, the International Council of Christian Churches (ICCC), and its long-term chairman. McIntire emphasized the doctrine of separation, based on 2 Corinthians 6:17, stressing the purity of the church and condemning liberalism as a deviation from Christian orthodoxy. In 1941, he helped establish the American Council of Christian Churches (ACCC) to counter the National Council of Churches (NCC), which he viewed as a liberal organization. Similarly, in 1948, he assisted in founding the International Council of Christian Churches (ICCC) to challenge the World Council of Churches (WCC). He was elected the ICCC‚Äôs first chairman and remained in the position until his death. From the 1940s onward, McIntire frequently traveled the world, encouraging evangelical Christians abroad while expressing his opposition to the WCC. International Council of Christian Churches (ICCC)

The International Council of Christian Churches (ICCC) was founded on August 12, 1948, at the British Reformed Church in Amsterdam. It is a trans-denominational council of churches with strong fundamentalist beliefs, characterized by its militant opposition to the liberal-leaning World Council of Churches (WCC), communism, and what it perceives as deviations from orthodox Christianity. During the Iron Curtain era, ICCC maintained connections with major political decision-makers across four continents and later established an extensive network of informants and collaborators after the Cold War. (Chen Huihong, 2023, 208)

ICCC‚Äôs membership includes national church organizations, local churches, and related institutions (such as missionary societies and Bible alliances). From its founding in 1948 until the establishment of the World Congress of Fundamentalists in 1975, ICCC was the only global fundamentalist or ‚ÄúBible-believing‚Äù Christian church organization that served as a platform for information sharing, coordination, and cooperation.

ICCC‚Äôs missionary wing was called The Associated Missions (TAM), and it also operated an international Christian relief organization. Its youth movement, known as International Christian Youth, provided an alternative ecumenical platform for Christians who did not wish to join the WCC. (McIntire, Carl, retrieved in 2024)

The ICCC was primarily founded by American Presbyterian pastor Carl McIntire, who also led the creation of the Bible Presbyterian Church, Shelton College, and the American Council of Christian Churches (ACCC). The movement‚Äôs main publication was McIntire‚Äôs Christian Beacon, a weekly newspaper headquartered at the Bible Presbyterian Church of Collingswood, New Jersey, where he served as pastor. McIntire was also widely known for his controversial radio program, Twentieth Century Reformation Hour. (Matt Stefon, 2011, 323)

By 1972, the ICCC had 155 Bible-believing Protestant denominations as members, most of which were relatively small. By the mid-1980s, its membership had grown to nearly 400 denominations or churches in Western Europe, the Americas, Southeast Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa, with an estimated 55 million adherents. However, throughout its history, some members left the ICCC due to disagreements with McIntire himself.

ICCC‚Äôs headquarters were located in Amsterdam, Netherlands, with an additional office in Collingswood, New Jersey, USA. The organization maintained national offices in Africa, the Middle East, Latin America, East Asia, Canada, the United States, and Europe. ICCC held regional and national council meetings annually, and its World Congress was deliberately scheduled in the same cities or nearby locations as WCC assemblies. The council published approximately 34 periodicals in 16 languages across 89 countries.

Prohibition of Romanization

Romanization was an excellent tool for church communication, newspapers, the Bible, hymns, and school education, allowing for rapid dissemination and significant influence. In the 1880s, Taiwan had hundreds of thousands of Romanized script readers. However, during Japanese rule, the Governor-General‚Äôs Office banned the use of native languages in church schools, worship services, and Bible classes. After 1931, the authorities further mandated the use of Japanese as the national language and prohibited the use of Romanized script Bibles. In the final years of World War II, the Taiwan Church News was forcibly shut down in 1942. (Chang Lifu, 2005, 23-24)

After the Kuomintang (KMT) arrived in Taiwan, the government continued language assimilation policies. In 1946, it established the Mandarin Promotion Committee under the Taiwan Provincial Executive Office. Over the next 40 years, schools were forbidden from using native languages, including those of Taiwan‚Äôs various ethnic groups. That same year, the committee issued six major guidelines for the Mandarin movement, and in 1947, it was reorganized into the Taiwan Provincial Government Mandarin Promotion Committee.

In 1959, the Provincial Department of Education absorbed the Mandarin Promotion Committee, but similar committees remained active at county and city levels. In 1964, the government issued an official directive prohibiting the use of dialects in workplaces and requiring the exclusive use of Mandarin. In 1970, the Chinese Cultural Renaissance Movement led to the publication of the ‚ÄúMandarin Promotion Guidelines.‚Äù In 1973, the government introduced the ‚ÄúMandarin Promotion Measures for Mountainous Areas,‚Äù indicating that Mandarin was still not widespread in Indigenous regions at the time. This was followed by the ‚ÄúGuidelines for Strengthening Mandarin Education‚Äù in 1974. In 1981, the government re-established the Mandarin Promotion Committee under the Ministry of Education. Over 40 years, the Mandarin policy was highly effective, achieving significant success in linguistic assimilation. However, it also led to the decline and near extinction of local languages, including Indigenous languages. The government‚Äôs suppression of the translation and use of Bibles in local languages was closely linked to the aggressive enforcement of the Mandarin policy, with the Mandarin Promotion Committee playing a crucial role.
