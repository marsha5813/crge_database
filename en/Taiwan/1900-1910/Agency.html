Key Theological Issues

Modern Education during the Japanese Occupation

In the early period of Japanese rule, the Governor‚Äôs Office suppressed the influence of Taiwanese and foreigners in politics, economy, and education, but intervened little in religious beliefs and foreign clergy. At the same time, the Governor‚Äôs Office viewed Christianity as a representative of Western culture, thus maintaining a favorable attitude towards the church, allowing missionary work to progress smoothly. On the other hand, foreign clergy mostly affirmed the governance of the Governor‚Äôs Office, believing that the ‚Äúatmosphere of progress‚Äù greatly benefited the development of the church. As the power transitioned, a new generation of intellectuals grew up in Japanese-ruled Taiwan, not only receiving modern education but also many studying abroad. This so-called ‚ÄúPost-Battle of Yingwei New Generation,‚Äù both in background and ideology, differed from previous and subsequent generations. Christians in this period were no exception; they began to engage with Japanese culture, even being born as Japanese nationals. Church leaders believed that Christians had earlier and greater opportunities to receive education than ordinary Taiwanese, and thus Christians should become good citizens, displaying excellent conduct and adhering to disciplined lifestyles. In the early days of Japanese rule, most Taiwanese were deeply skeptical of Japanese education, fearing that their children would be conscripted after attending school, but many Christians quickly accepted education from the Mandarin Language Schools and aspired to study in Japan. At that time, the total number of Christians was less than one percent of the entire Taiwan population, yet, compared to ordinary Taiwanese, they were quite capable of adapting to and accepting the new Japanese education. For example, in the early medical schools, many students were Christians, accounting for a quarter of the total graduates between 1902 and 1906, a significant ratio. Furthermore, due to the proactive acceptance of Western education by Christians, they initiated the trend of Taiwanese studying overseas. This influence led Christians to long engage in a dilemma between ‚Äúassimilation‚Äù and ‚Äúmodernization,‚Äù resulting in profound reflections on issues of identity and self-recognition.
