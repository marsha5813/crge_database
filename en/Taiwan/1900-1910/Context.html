Taiwan: Context (1900-1910)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Religious Investigation

In the early period of Japanese rule, military governance was implemented, leading to mutual misunderstanding among the government, the church, and the common people, resulting in frequent church incidents and hindering church development. During this period, in order to eliminate opposing forces, the Japanese conducted various surveys in Taiwan, including those related to religion, which can be seen as the origin of subsequent religious investigations. From the records of the clergy association in January 1896, it was learned that the Presbyterian Church in the south hoped to obtain protection for the church through legal procedures. Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

Russo-Japanese War

Russo-Japanese War

From February 8, 1904, to September 5, 1905, for more than a year and a half, Japan and Russia (Japanese: Èú≤Ë•ø‰∫û), two empires, engaged in conflict over their spheres of influence in the Korean Peninsula, Liaodong Peninsula, and the three northeastern provinces of China (also known as Manchuria). The main battlefield included surrounding seas, and the war was fought in a ‚Äútotal war‚Äù manner with the full strength of the nation, not just localized military actions. Both sides mobilized large numbers of troops and required massive supplies, making the war akin to a prelude to World War I. Many new military technologies emerged, such as quick-firing artillery, machine guns, and more accurate carbines, high-explosive ammunition, etc., leading to significant casualties. For example, during the Battle of Port Arthur for Hill 203 and naval supremacy, the Japanese army suffered 15,000 deaths in just five days, a heavy sacrifice that even included instances of suicide attacks.

During the war, Russia suffered consecutive defeats and ultimately signed the Treaty of Portsmouth, mediated by U.S. President Theodore Roosevelt (1858-1919), bringing the war to an end. Following the defeat, Russia lost a significant portion of its regular army‚Äôs strength, leading to domestic turmoil and a series of revolutionary events that resulted in the ‚ÄúBloody Sunday Revolution‚Äù of 1905.

The Russo-Japanese War was the first instance since the 19th century of an Asian country defeating a European power and also the first case of a non-Christian nation defeating a Christian nation. Consequently, Europe changed its perception of newly industrialized Asian countries. Japan‚Äôs international status saw an unprecedented rise, greatly enhancing non-European nations‚Äô national confidence and giving rise to the idea of ‚ÄúËÑ±‰∫ûÂÖ•Ê≠ê‚Äù (leaving Asia for Europe). This war also affected East Asian countries, witnessing Japan surpassing the autocratic Russian Empire in a short time with a constitutional monarchy, prompting the Qing Dynasty to seek self-reform. Due to its defeat in the Far East, Russia inadvertently strengthened its army in Europe and became more focused on economic achievements rather than military ones; meanwhile, militarism within Japan rose.

Chinese Presbyterian Church Joint Proposal

At the end of the Qing Dynasty, the British Presbyterian Church established missionary centers in Xiamen (1849) and Shantou (1858). In 1865, Dr. George Mackay initially went to Xiamen to learn the language, and later accompanied by Wu Wenshui from Quanzhou, crossed the sea to reach Takao (Kaohsiung) to commence medical missionary work, making Taiwan the third missionary center. Pastor Mackay‚Äôs missionary journey also began in Xiamen before he eventually traveled to Tamsui, indicating that the coastal regions of South China were indeed significant bases for the British Presbyterian Church. As a result, Taiwan was able to share experiences and resources from places like Xiamen and Shantou, introducing the ‚Äúmedical evangelism‚Äù model. Additionally, due to the close geographical proximity of Taiwan and Xiamen, both falling under Fujian jurisdiction, Taiwan naturally became a support center for Xiamen, playing a role in language learning, missionary training, and resource provision. This process illustrates how Taiwan served as an extension of European and American churches‚Äô missionary efforts in China. In terms of church organization, prior to the British Presbyterian Church‚Äôs missionary efforts in Taiwan, there was already missionary activity in Quanzhou, Fujian, later joined by American Reformed churches from Zhangzhou and London Missionary Society churches in Xiamen to establish the ‚ÄúMin Nan Conference.‚Äù The relationship between Taiwan and the Min Nan churches was quite close, maintaining a good friendship. (Zhang Miaojun 2001: 479-510)

In 1896, the Tainan Presbyterian Conference was established; a delegate from the Min Nan Conference, Pastor Yang Huaide, attended, and Taiwan sent Liu Maokun to reciprocate. At the same time, the Bibles and hymnals used in Taiwan were imported from across the strait. (Conference Hymn Committee 1937) However, during the Japanese occupation, the church in Taiwan gradually entered an organizational expansion phase, and the location of the missionary center was established. From the periodicals of the British mother church, it can be inferred that the dioceses in Taiwan and China held almost equal importance. Moreover, due to political factors and differing nationalities, interaction between Christians on both sides was not frequent, and church administration saw little communication.

Non-Directive Policies (Patriotic Rituals, Education Systems, and Public Opinion)

During the tenure of the fourth Governor-General of Taiwan, Kodama Gentar≈ç, he appointed Goto Shinpei, a trained physician, as Chief of Civil Affairs. From 1898 to 1906, under Kodama‚Äôs administration, Goto proposed that the foundation of governance must be built on the ‚Äúprinciples of biology,‚Äù stating that ‚Äúflounder cannot be treated the same way as sea bream.‚Äù Therefore, he adopted a policy of ‚Äúnon-directiveism‚Äù or ‚Äúpreserving old customs,‚Äù adapting to the environment and responding flexibly to face the continuous resistance from the Taiwanese populace.

In the early period of Japanese rule, incidents of anti-Christian sentiment, such as the Southern Christian Incident in 1895 and the Eastern and Douliu incidents in 1896, arose from complex factors, and Taiwanese people knew very little about modern laws at the time. European and American powers demanded that the Japanese government implement Western-style laws, but Japan‚Äôs colonies could be exceptions. In other words, the Japanese government did not have to immediately apply modern Western laws, which it was not very familiar with, in Taiwan, but instead used traditional methods to maintain public order and suppress dissidents. Consequently, anti-Christian incidents in the early period of Japanese rule did not receive appropriate investigation and handling. The intersection of church and state relations was limited to consular negotiations, with little substantial meaning beyond mere declarations. The church was left to manage the aftermath and pacify local anti-Christian sentiments.

In short, the ‚Äúnon-directiveism‚Äù policy was based on the principles of gradualism. It did not impose extreme policies or destructivism on the Taiwanese people but instead moderately respected Taiwanese customs and social organizations, even cleverly utilizing them to win over hearts and diminish resistance.

After the Meiji Restoration, Japan actively imitated the West, establishing a unified education system and subsequently instituting a nationalist education policy aimed at educating citizens to uphold Japan‚Äôs language, customs, systems, and national essence. The highest honor and happiness was to serve the ‚ÄúImperial Family for all ages,‚Äù that is, to cultivate citizens‚Äô loyalty to the Emperor and love for their country as the ultimate goal, thereby establishing Japan‚Äôs modern national education system. Through popular elementary education, citizens gained basic literacy, economic knowledge, and political obedience.

Under the impetus of the nationalist education trends and modernization awareness, the Taiwan Governor-General‚Äôs Office had already utilized education as a means of assimilation and enlightenment towards the Taiwanese people, thus promptly establishing elementary educational facilities focusing on the Japanese language. From 1896 to 1897, 16 national language promotion centers and 18 branches were established. In 1898, a six-year public school was established, providing education in Japanese, moral training, and practical skills. The first graduating class entered society in 1904. Additionally, regulations were set to manage traditional study rooms, turning them into auxiliary institutions for public schools. Educational facilities above the secondary level were extremely inadequate and lacked a systematic approach. During this period, education in Taiwan lacked a fixed system and developed according to needs, gradually resulting in differentiated education systems for Taiwanese, indigenous peoples, and Japanese. The primary and secondary education received by Taiwanese people was entirely different from that in the home country, being limited to the Japanese language education and basic technical education required by the Taiwan Governor-General‚Äôs Office for the governance and development of the colony. (Huang Xiuzheng, Zhang Shengyan, Wu Wenxing 2002: 207-212)
