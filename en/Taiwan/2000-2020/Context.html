Taiwan: Context (2000-2020)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

1. Enforcement Statute for Substitute Services, January 15, 2000

‚ÄúSubstitute services as referred to in this Statute means that men of service age perform auxiliary work for the agencies in need, fulfilling government public affairs or other social services.‚Äù (Article 3) ‚ÄúWhen the number of applications for substitute services exceeds the approved quota, a lottery will be used to decide. However, individuals applying for substitute services due to family or religious factors are exempt from participating in the lottery.‚Äù (Article 5, Paragraph 3)

According to this Statute, substitute services are added beyond regular military service and are managed by the Ministry of the Interior. Men of service age are employed in government agencies to perform auxiliary work, fulfilling government public affairs or other social services. The original legislative intent of this Statute was to take into account the gradual increase in the education level of conscripts at the time in Taiwan, combined with the decreasing demand for regular military personnel. To effectively utilize human resources, this law was established to employ surplus manpower in government and social services, aiming to enhance social living standards and government efficiency. However, subsequent provisions included religious factors. This is related to last year's Constitutional Interpretation No. 490 by the Constitutional Court.

Regarding the issue of conscientious objectors caused by the refusal of Jehovah's Witnesses to perform military service, in October 1999, the Constitutional Court made Interpretation No. 490, stating that the Military Service Law does not restrict or promote religious effects. People must fulfill basic obligations and cannot be exempted solely due to religious beliefs. Article 59, Paragraph 2 of the Enforcement Act of the Military Service Law does not violate the principle of no double jeopardy. However, four months after this interpretation was made, the Legislative Yuan amended the Military Service Law and the Enforcement Act of the Military Service Law's penalty time conditions to resolve the issue of believers repeatedly being imprisoned for refusing to serve. In addition, Article 5, Paragraph 3 of the Enforcement Statute for Substitute Services clearly stipulates that individuals with religious factors may apply for substitute services, fundamentally resolving the conscientious objector issue caused by the refusal to serve. (Legislative Yuan Database: Legislative Evolution of the Substitute Services Enforcement Statute)

2. Regulations Governing the Establishment of Religious College, January 19, 2009

‚ÄúThe religious college referred to in Article 8, Paragraph 1 of this Law means a private university or a college under a private university established for the purpose of cultivating clergy and religious talents of specific religions and granting religious degrees, which has been approved by the Ministry of Education (hereinafter referred to as the Ministry).‚Äù (Article 2)

Before formulating these regulations, educational institutions of various religions in Taiwan were all under the management of the Ministry of the Interior. Until 1988, when Fu Jen Catholic University established the Graduate Institute of Religious Studies, marking the beginning of religious studies entering Taiwan‚Äôs higher education system. Subsequently, Fu Jen Catholic University established the undergraduate program and doctoral program in religious studies in 1992 and 2003 respectively, which completed the academic research of religious studies in Taiwan. However, education institutions of individual religions remained excluded from the education system. In 2008, the amendment of the Private School Law allowed school juridical persons and religious juridical persons to apply to the Ministry of Education for the establishment of religious colleges to cultivate clergy and religious talents and grant degrees. This became the basis for the formulation of these regulations. Since then, various religions have been able to establish educational institutions under the supervision of the Ministry of Education, and the degrees conferred have also been recognized by the Ministry. In Christianity, the Taiwan Baptist Theological Seminary of the China Baptist Convention became the first school to be officially registered with the Ministry of Education as per the regulations (2010); thereafter, the Taiwan Theological College of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (2015) and the Tainan Theological College (2017), as well as the inter-denominational China Evangelical Theological Seminary (2020) were successively approved for registration by the Ministry. (National Regulatory Database: Regulations Governing the Establishment of Religious College)

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

  1. From 2000 to 2020, with the liberalization of politics and the rise of subjectivity in Taiwan, the relationships between Taiwan and neighboring countries in the Asia-Pacific region underwent significant changes. After Chen Shui-bian was elected president in 2000, he implemented the ‚ÄúOne Country on Each Side‚Äù policy, emphasizing that Taiwan and China are two different countries, which provoked strong backlash from China and led to increased tensions across the Taiwan Strait. China‚Äôs diplomatic pressure on Taiwan intensified, causing many countries with diplomatic ties to Taiwan to sever relations due to pressure from China. In 2008, after Ma Ying-jeou was elected president, he adopted a friendlier policy towards China, advocating for ‚Äúpeaceful development across the Taiwan Strait.‚Äù During this period, trade and economic exchanges between Taiwan and China significantly increased, and several agreements were signed, such as the Economic Cooperation Framework Agreement (ECFA). However, this policy also faced skepticism from some citizens, who believed that excessive reliance on China could harm Taiwan‚Äôs sovereignty. After Tsai Ing-wen was elected president in 2016, she adopted a ‚Äúpragmatic diplomacy‚Äù policy, emphasizing Taiwan‚Äôs sovereignty and democratic values. By not recognizing the ‚Äú1992 Consensus‚Äù in cross-Strait relations, China‚Äôs diplomatic pressure on Taiwan further escalated. The Tsai administration faced challenges of diplomatic isolation from China, and in this context, Taiwan actively sought cooperation with other countries. Among them, U.S. support for Taiwan continued to strengthen, reaffirming its commitment to Taiwan‚Äôs security on multiple occasions, including arms sales and high-level visits, providing Taiwan with a stronger backing against Chinese pressure. Overall, from 2000 to 2020, Taiwan‚Äôs diplomatic relations in the Asia-Pacific region experienced a series of changes from actively seeking international support to facing challenges of isolation. Adjustments in foreign policy under different administrations, along with China‚Äôs increasing influence, profoundly affected Taiwan‚Äôs diplomatic interactions with neighboring countries in the Asia-Pacific region. (Taiwan‚Äôs China Policy and Peace in the Asia-Pacific Region: A 16-Year Review of Diplomatic, Economic, and Military Changes in U.S.-China-Taiwan Relations)

  2. From 2000 to 2020, influenced by the international political environment, cross-Strait relations, and internal policies in Taiwan, relationships between Taiwan and some countries with a Christian majority changed, particularly with the Vatican, the center of the Catholic Church.

The relationship between the Vatican and Taiwan has always been one of the key points in Taiwan‚Äôs foreign relations. Apart from being Taiwan‚Äôs only diplomatic ally in Europe, its nature as the governing body of the global Catholic Church has drawn significant global attention. The diplomatic relationship between Taiwan and the Vatican originated in 1942 when the Republic of China government, still on the mainland, established diplomatic ties with the Vatican. After the Chinese Communist Party took control of mainland China in 1949, it began expelling Christian missionaries from Europe and America, and in 1951 it expelled the Holy See‚Äôs envoy to China, Li Peili. The following year, at the invitation of Chiang Kai-shek, Li Peili reopened the embassy in Taipei, allowing the diplomatic relationship between the Republic of China and the Vatican to continue in Taiwan. Meanwhile, the Communist Party began the ‚ÄúThree-Self‚Äù movement in Chinese Catholicism and appointed self-selected bishops. The relationship between the People‚Äôs Republic of China and the Vatican did not begin until 1978, when Cardinal Jaime Lachica Sin from the Philippines met with General Secretary Zhao Ziyang of the Communist Party, and both sides agreed to initiate negotiations to restore diplomatic relations. However, the CCP insisted that the Vatican must agree to sever ties with Taiwan and not interfere in China‚Äôs internal affairs (including church matters) before establishing diplomatic relations. As a result, negotiations between the two sides made no progress. However, after 2000, the Vatican gradually began to recognize the bishops elected by the Chinese Catholic Patriotic Association. In 2018, an interim agreement regarding the appointment of bishops was signed between China and the Vatican, granting the Chinese government the right to appoint bishops, which the pope could either accept or reject. The two sides appeared to have made progress by taking steps back, but after the signing of the agreement, incidents of China unilaterally consecrating bishops occurred. Additionally, the CCP required underground church clergy and bishops to comply with this agreement, causing discontent among the underground church. The trilateral relationship between China, Taiwan, and the Vatican remains in a state of stalemate. (2021: Ê¢ÅÊΩîËä¨, Pages 1-20)

In his inaugural speech, Chen proposed the ‚ÄúFour Noes and One Without‚Äù policy, emphasizing that he would not declare Taiwan‚Äôs independence but also calling on the Taiwanese people to consider the necessity of legislation for referendums. Ultimately, the controversy surrounding the Legislative Yuan‚Äôs review of the Cross-Strait Service Trade Agreement became the flashpoint, leading to the outbreak of the ‚ÄúSunflower Student Movement‚Äù in 2014. This movement reflected the dissatisfaction of the younger generation with the government‚Äôs cross-Strait policies and became a catalyst for Taiwanese subjectivity. After Tsai Ing-wen was elected president in 2016, she again emphasized the importance of Taiwan‚Äôs sovereignty.

In addition to political aspects, the reform of educational content also prompted a shift in the national identity of the Taiwanese people. The publication of the ‚ÄúUnderstanding Taiwan‚Äù series of textbooks by the National Translation and Compilation Institute in 1997 marked the beginning of an educational system that recognized Taiwan as the main subject of education. After the implementation of the nine-year curriculum, Taiwanese history, geography, and related social issues formally became the main content of social studies courses. The younger generation who received the new curriculum gradually began to identify primarily as Taiwanese. Public opinion surveys conducted by the Research Center of National Chengchi University also indicated that since the 2000s, ‚ÄúTaiwanese‚Äù has gradually become the mainstream identity among the public.

In conclusion, from 2000 to 2020, Taiwanese nationalism underwent an important phase of rising and consolidation. With the alternation of political parties, reforms in the education system, and the promotion of social movements, the identity of ‚ÄúTaiwanese‚Äù gradually became mainstream in Taiwanese society.
