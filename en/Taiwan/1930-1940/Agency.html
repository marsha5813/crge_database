Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

  1. Northern Church‚Äôs Three-Year Movement

During the Japanese occupation, the Northern Church gradually transitioned from its initial phase of establishment to a period of organizational pastoral care. The missionaries born in this era witnessed significant changes in the Northern Church throughout their growth, education, and service. In the 1930s, young missionaries were greatly influenced by Japanese Christian socialist He Kawaguchi‚Äôs ‚ÄúKingdom Movement,‚Äù and learned from the experience of Chinese Christians who promoted the ‚ÄúFive Year Movement,‚Äù absorbing the idea of ‚Äúrevival evangelism.‚Äù The missionaries observed the practices of Japanese churches, learning about rigorous procedural systems to discuss issues related to revitalizing church growth.

Thus, in 1934, during the 11th Conference of Northern Missionaries held in Tamsui, young missionaries, emphasizing the importance of faith improvement and church development, proposed the concept of the ‚ÄúThree-Year Movement.‚Äù They focused on the responsibility of missions and decided to reorganize the church‚Äôs faith, establishing the essence of Christianity. At the same time, considering that the Canadian mother church had assisted the Taiwanese for over 60 years, Christians were eagerly looking forward to self-governance. In 1935, the Canadian mother church successfully approved this plan, and the Northern Presbytery officially endorsed the three-year movement motion proposed by the missionaries.

In November 1935, the ‚ÄúNorthern Christian Three-Year Movement Department‚Äù was officially established, with missionary Ming Youde serving as the head and Xiao Leshan as the chief officer (secretary-general), positioning the movement as a ‚Äútotal mobilization of the church,‚Äù aiming at ‚Äúreviving faith, enhancing the spirit of Christ, guiding people back to the Lord, and establishing a church with God‚Äôs life,‚Äù clearly indicating the direction of their efforts.

In January 1936, the three-year movement commenced, dividing the work into three parts: the Revival Department aimed at encouraging church members to be zealous and progressive, focusing on internal renewal; the Salvation Department hoped members would actively invite others to believe, emphasizing external outreach; and the Youth Department aimed to inspire Sunday schools and youth groups to become active and progressive, targeting specific age groups to cultivate the most malleable young people to become the foundational pillars of the church.

In 1937, entering the second phase of the three-year movement, after a year of effort, Northern Christians sensed signs of revitalization in the church, hoping for continuous reflection and progress, avoiding jealousy disputes or the devaluation of the Bible. Xiao Leshan further encouraged believers to ‚Äúspeak the truth in love,‚Äù carrying out justice and kindness to avoid falling short of God‚Äôs grace. He believed Christians should train themselves to be faithful to God, ensuring that this ‚ÄúBible movement‚Äù would spread the correct faith throughout Taiwan. He called on Christians to embrace the mission of evangelism, guiding and assisting each other, allowing both missionaries and lay believers to find their roles in contributing.

In 1938, entering the last phase of the three-year movement, Christians still focused more on the internal development of the ‚Äúchurch.‚Äù Xiao Leshan pointed out that although the North had over 70 churches that appeared strong, the actual spirit of evangelism needed strengthening, especially given the looming clouds of international conflict and the financial difficulties faced by the Canadian mother church. Therefore, he encouraged everyone to take on the role of ‚Äúpioneers,‚Äù emphasizing that progress could only be achieved through action, and hoped Christians would love the Lord more, be harmonious, and walk the right path without fear of obstacles, making the three-year movement a model for future generations.

Overall, the implementation of the three-year movement showed that the first year had many activities, such as dedication ceremonies, lectures, and special gatherings, divided into three major goals: revitalization, youth, and salvation, executed in a more systematic manner with active participation. The second and third years shifted mainly towards ‚Äúliterature evangelism.‚Äù Many publications were issued during these three years, but the gathering format leaned more towards static events.

In reality, the ups and downs of the three-year movement were attributed to changes in the political climate. Influenced by World War II, Christians found it difficult to focus on faith revitalization and held few public gatherings. In 1938, Xiao Leshan lamented that many believers were conscripted for ‚Äúhard work‚Äù in constructing defenses, leaving them unable to maintain church life.

Clearly, ‚Äúcurrent circumstances‚Äù restricted large gatherings, and the events held were mostly static lectures and Bible study meetings, similar in nature to regular Sunday services. Christians passively listened and studied, lacking opportunities for active outreach, resulting in fewer encounters with non-Christians and reduced chances for conversion. However, Xiao Leshan consistently hoped that believers would build a stronger foundation in the Bible and enhance their study through newspaper publications, a concept that undoubtedly forms the basis of faith.

When Northern Christians reflected on the three-year movement, they did not discuss it extensively; some former leaders only briefly commented that ‚Äúalthough a few small publications were issued, they did not achieve the expected results due to entering a period of wartime tension.‚Äù In reality, the variety of publications released was considerable, but the practical effects remained to be improved once these materials reached the hands of the believers.However, it is undeniable that under the efforts of Pastor Xiao Leshan and others, the Three-Year Movement did significantly aid the quality and quantity of Christians‚Äô faith identity. Through newspapers and magazines, in-depth discussions on the concepts and practices of faith benefited Christians in self-reflection and encouragement. In 1938, the Taipei Theological College and Tamsui Women‚Äôs Vocational School officially established their boards, further solidifying the educational institutions. The number of believers in 1938 increased to 9,667, which also led to the Northern Presbytery being divided into three presbyteries: Taipei, Hsinchu, and Eastern, laying the groundwork for the elevation of the ‚ÄúGeneral Assembly.‚Äù The direction of the three-year movement holds significant historical meaning for the church in Taiwan.

  1. The Tainan Theological School Closure Incident

At the end of May 1940, shortly after Daichuan Seiji took office as principal in Taipei, the Education Section of Tainan Prefecture required that a Japanese person be appointed as principal for the Tainan Theological School. On June 11, the Clergy Council held a regular meeting, where acting principal Xi Li Zhi reported on the process of meeting with the education section chief. The council disagreed with the official request and decided to abandon the operation of the theological school, with three main points: first, to arrange for male students to transfer to Taipei Theological School; second, for female students to transfer to Tamsui Bible School; third, to notify Taiwanese church leaders of this decision and convey the council‚Äôs views on the united theological school. After reaching a preliminary consensus, the council sought to understand the perspectives of church leaders in the south and sent Yang Shiyang to privately inquire, with many engaging in discussions, often late into the night, regarding the school‚Äôs existence issue, ultimately agreeing. The Taiwanese expressed:

‚ÄúHowever, if the Japanese insist on having a principal, and we close the school, it poses great danger given the current situation. So we must be very cautious.‚Äù Consequently, it was conveyed to Pastor Xi that if the council intended to shut down, sending students to Taipei was something we would agree to.

On June 21, 1940, a Standing Committee meeting was held in the south where it was heard that the Clergy Council would no longer continue operating the theological school. The Taiwanese, feeling reluctant, organized a ten-member group to hold a meeting on ‚ÄúMissionary Training‚Äù to discuss countermeasures. On July 7, Wan Ronghua immediately telegrammed the British mother church stating, ‚ÄúCOLLEGE CLOSED STUDENTS GOING NORTH SEPTEMBER EDWARD BAND.‚Äù On the morning of July 17, the Missionary Training meeting was held at Taipingjing in Tainan City, where both Er Lang and Fan Jiang Tie Xiong encouraged continued operation of the theological school. In the afternoon, the Southern Assembly held a Standing Committee meeting, considering the continuation of the school, but agreed that the annual budget of 10,000 yuan would be divided among the four presbyteries, with a deadline for response. If unable to bear this cost, students would be sent to Taipei, with both schools‚Äô properties to be used for establishing the united theological school.

Looking at the church finances for the 1940 fiscal year, the Kaohsiung Presbytery had total revenue of 5,613.55 yuan and total expenses of 4,219.26 yuan, with only 1,394.29 yuan left; the Chiayi Presbytery had total revenue of 4,695.07 yuan and total expenses of 3,771.80 yuan, leaving only 923.27 yuan. The total budget for the Southern Assembly for two years was only 1,590 yuan. Such financial conditions evidently could not support the expenses of a theological school. Thus, on July 22, the Tainan Presbytery resolved: ‚ÄúThis standing committee cannot bear the 2,500 yuan operating expenses of the theological school, but hopes the united theological school centered on the Taiwan Assembly can be realized.‚Äù On July 23, the Kaohsiung Presbytery resolved: ‚ÄúThis presbytery cannot support this due to heavy financial burdens, and fully agrees with the standing committee‚Äôs resolution.‚Äù Responses from the Taichung and Chiayi presbyteries were similar, indicating none of the four southern presbyteries could bear the large financial burden.

The Taiwanese‚Äôs desire to independently operate the theological school could not be realized, and they could only proceed with closing procedures for faculty and students, arranging for teachers to be reassigned and students to transfer, while applying to the Governor-General‚Äôs Office for the closure. During the closing ceremony, attendees felt the atmosphere was heavy and solemn, with some church elders expressing feelings of ‚Äúspiritual unrest.‚Äù This outcome led to the principal‚Äôs successor, Miyazaki Akira, becoming unemployed, leaving introducer Er Lang very displeased. Public opinion in the community believed that foreign influence had withdrawn, making way for local individuals to rise. The missionaries summarized: ‚ÄúFor a long time, we truly hoped the northern and southern theological schools could unite, but unfortunately, it has not succeeded. This time, the authorities wanted us to replace the principal with a local person; fortunately, Taipei Theological School has already appointed the capable Mr. Daichuan, so it is entirely appropriate for our students to go there. After all, this is the first step towards unification. Moreover, we can save many expenses, as historically, most theological school funding came from British subsidies, but due to the current unusual times, there are financial difficulties, and they fear they may not be able to continue funding each year.‚Äù

Anti-Christian Leaders

<h1>Royal Subjects Public Service Association</h1>
<p>In April 1941, the Taiwan Governor-General's Office, in coordination with the "Greater Japan Imperial Rule Assistance Association" from the Japanese mainland, established the "Royal Subjects Public Service Association." The aim was to cooperate in the war and mobilize all human and material resources. The Governor-General's Office utilized the existing administrative system to establish branch organizations step by step, with officials of the Governor-General's Office concurrently serving as cadres of the association, while also recruiting individuals from various sectors of society to participate. This was intended to summon the entire island of Taiwan to promote the "Royal Subjects Public Service" movement, thus training islanders to increase production for their country, establishing a wartime living system, and turning Taiwan into a base for wartime preparations.</p>

<p>The Royal Subjects Public Service movement emphasized "practicing loyalty," initiated and executed by the Governor-General's Office, highlighting the official-driven character significantly more than the "ambiguous" nature of the assimilation movement.</p>

<p>In accordance with national policy, the Royal Subjects Public Service Association prioritized the promotion of the concepts of "national body principle" and "reverence for gods and ancestors" (where "reverence for gods" refers to honoring Shinto spirits and the "living deity" Emperor). It established departments for general affairs, propaganda, training, culture, everyday life, economy, etc., and organized various affiliated groups such as the Industrial Public Service Corps, the Youth Public Service Corps, the Literature Public Service Association, the Bellflower Club, the Public Service Young Men's Corps, and the Public Service Old Age Service Corps. Through speeches, exhibitions, printed materials, and other propaganda efforts, the association instilled the concept of "fighting for His Majesty the Emperor," covering a wide range of areas such as the publication of a set of "Royal Subjects Public Service Series" in 1941 and 1942, and the issuance of the association's official journal "New Construction" as tools for public propaganda. In other words, the Royal Subjects Public Service Association was an organization mobilizing all of Taiwan, attempting to exert a propaganda effect, and all associations, committees, tribal clubs, etc., were required to adhere to the spirit of reverence for gods and ancestors, with rituals such as remote worship of the imperial palace and service in front of shrines seen as necessary.</p>

<p>The Royal Subjects Public Service Association was integrated closely with the administrative institutions of the Governor-General's Office, with its basic organizational structure largely following the model of the Greater Japan Imperial Rule Assistance Association in Japan. Apart from the "regional organizations" formed according to administrative divisions, the Royal Subjects Public Service Association gradually incorporated various groups into "occupational organizations," including the Taiwan Industrial Public Service Corps, Commercial Public Service Corps, Public Service Young Men's Corps, Taiwan Youth Corps, Bellflower Club, Greater Japan Women's Association, Patriotic Women's Association, Taiwan Drama Association, Taiwan Buddhist Public Service Corps, Literature Public Service Association, Fine Arts Public Service Association, Music Public Service Association, and other cultural and artistic groups. The Christian leader Lin Maosheng was appointed as a cadre in the association.</p>

<p>In early August 1942, the "North China Incident Full Taiwan Christian Public Service Association," established for five years, reorganized in a series to expand its organization in response to the Royal Subjects Public Service Association. Under Japanese convening, more than 1,300 Japanese and Taiwanese Christians held the founding ceremony of the "Taiwan Christian Public Service Association" at the Taipei Church Hall, electing 17 directors, including Shang Yierlang, and local committee members. By the end of August, under the manipulation of the board, it was renamed the "Taiwan Christian Public Service Corps" (hereinafter referred to as the Public Service Corps) and adopted regulations that clearly stated its purpose: "to carry out the sacred mission of supporting the declaration of war against the United States and Britain, to fulfill the duties of the imperial subjects, to assume the Christian responsibility of serving the nation, with the goal of refining the imperial subjects." Its actual business included "organizing courses, lectures, and training sessions aimed at enhancing members' awareness, uplifting national wartime consciousness, guiding national wartime life, assisting in the promotion of the national language for internal unity, and holding prayer meetings for victory in battle."</p>

<p>In December 1942, the Public Service Corps Tainan local division convened 400 believers for an inaugural meeting. In January 1943, the Public Service Corps Kaohsiung local division and Taitung local division were established successively. The former held an inaugural meeting at the Kaohsiung Church Hall, chaired by Shang Yierlang, with 300 believers participating. The latter held its inaugural meeting at the Taitung Street Militia Assembly Hall, also presided over by the pastor, with 200 believers participating. In summary, the Christian Public Service Corps was an external organization of the Royal Subjects Public Service Association, driving churches to cooperate in the war. Although it was not directly governed by the church, it provided space for the Japanese to direct the entire church, uniting Taiwanese Christians into a broad wartime cooperative group.</p>
