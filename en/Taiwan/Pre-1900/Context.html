Taiwan: Context (Pre-1900)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

The Decree from Kabayama Sukenori, Governor of Taiwan, January 18, 1896:

‚ÄúIn the island, the native temples and shrines, although established with distinctions between public and private, are nevertheless the sources of religious faith and reverence. They constitute the foundation of moral and ethical standards, essential for maintaining public order and security. While it may be unavoidable to utilize them for military purposes during times of urgency, it is imperative to avoid causing undue damage to traditional customs, especially the destruction or scattering of sacred images and ritual implements. Such actions must not be tolerated. Henceforth, greater care must be taken for their preservation. If temporarily utilized for military purposes, they must be promptly restored to their original state.‚Äù

This decree elucidates that the actions of the Japanese military in occupying temples and causing damage to sacred images and implements were deemed unacceptable and warranted a direct order from the Governor. However, the issuance of such a directive led to a significant reduction in the occupation of temples and destruction of Buddhist statues during the early period of Japanese rule. Tsai Maotang believes that during the administration of Governor Got≈ç Shinpei, the unique ‚Äúbiological principles‚Äù were employed to understand the customs of Taiwan society, laying the groundwork for governance. Both Kabayama and Got≈ç sought to avoid backlash from the Taiwanese people due to radical cultural and educational policies. Although the Imperial Constitution of Japan guarantees religious freedom, during the early Japanese rule in Taiwan, the ‚ÄúSix Three Law‚Äù was implemented, granting the Governor of Taiwan the authority to promulgate ordinances (legal decrees) within its jurisdiction. Moreover, temporary emergency ordinances could be issued without requiring the approval of the central governing authority and were immediately enforced. Therefore, the decrees, proclamations, and instructions issued by the Governor of Taiwan carried legal validity.

Government of Taiwan Order No. 95, issued on July 21, 1899, Regarding the procedures for the establishment and abolition of traditional shrines and temples in Taiwan:

‚ÄúFor the establishment of temples, prayer halls, as well as deity and ancestral associations in accordance with local customs on this island, permission must be obtained from the local authorities. Similarly, for existing temples, prayer halls, deity, or ancestral associations wishing to be abolished, merged, or undergo changes in name or relocation, the same procedure applies. Regarding previously established shrines and temples, from the date of promulgation of this order, their names and locations must be reported to the respective local authorities within three months. In the implementation of this order, when the need arises for detailed regulations, they shall be determined by the local chief executive and subsequently approved by the Governor of Taiwan.‚Äù

This regulation pertains to the regulation of local religious sites. In the previous issuance of Government Order No. 47, concerning the establishment, abolition, merger, etc., of shrines and temples established according to local customs, appropriate procedures were stipulated, indicating the necessity of application to the Governor of Taiwan. However, for the local people, despite the elaboration of detailed regulations, they were practically challenging to enforce. Therefore, the handling of related procedures was left to the discretion of local authorities (Wen Guoliang 2001: 7-8).

Social Hostility toward Christianity

Violence against Christianity during the Jiawu Anti-Japanese War. During the Japanese invasion of Taiwan in 1895 and the subsequent Jiawu Anti-Japanese War, the Taiwan church suffered violence from two different forces. Firstly, there were attacks and killings of Christians by civilians who suspected them of colluding with the Japanese army. For example, during the August skirmish in Douliu where the militia attempted to block the Japanese army but failed due to inferior weapons, they subsequently blamed Christians, spreading rumors accusing them, including priests Lin Maode and Gao Hengde, of guiding the Japanese army, which enraged the locals and led to the arrest and execution of Christians. Additionally, in the Ma Dou incident on October 14 of the same year, 19 people were killed by the militia. Earlier, Lin Tianding, along with two seekers and a Catholic, was falsely accused of colluding with the Japanese army and was beheaded by villagers.

Secondly, during wartime, the Japanese army distrusted Westerners and missionaries, believing they incited believers to support or participate in the anti-Japanese movement, and that the weapons of the anti-Japanese militia were provided by Westerners. In July 1895, the Japanese army was defeated by the Black Flag Army in Douliu. Father Gao Hengde had been transferred away before the incident, and the Japanese army directly accused him of being aware of the situation. Churches in the eastern and northern parts of Taiwan were also burned down or occupied by the Japanese army, and believers were killed. Throughout the entire period of the anti-Japanese war from 1896 to 1902, many Christians were killed by both the militia and the Japanese army, especially during the ‚Äúclearing the countryside‚Äù campaigns conducted by the Japanese army across Taiwan, which resulted in widespread slaughter of civilians, including many Christians.

During the Jiawu Anti-Japanese War, Christians found themselves in a dilemma. Being a minority group on the margins, they were perceived as easy targets for manipulation and incitement, leading to suspicions of collusion with external forces. The attacks against Christians by the Taiwanese stemmed from nationalistic and xenophobic sentiments, while those by the Japanese army were driven by political factors and mistrust of Western religions. However, the common motivation between the two was to choose specific groups as ‚Äúscapegoats‚Äù in order to pacify the crisis and achieve social stability. According to Rene Girard‚Äôs perspective, when society faces sudden or overwhelming crises, there emerges a ‚Äúsacred collective violence‚Äù aimed at purifying the community of its impure elements.

Arrival of Japanese Church Leaders in Taiwan. On September 28, 1895, the Japanese church dispatched three delegates under the guise of comforting soldiers: Ryu Hosokawa (Japan Christian Church), Kame Shikawa (Baptist Church), and Yoshisaburo Takeda (YMCA), to Taiwan. Amid the turmoil of the Jiawu War at the time, these three individuals made efforts to liaise with the military headquarters and assist the Taiwan church in various ways, including attending church services to offer solace to the congregation. Ryu Hosokawa initially visited the Makung Church in Penghu, then, on November 12, accompanied by the Barkleys, traveled north from Tainan to visit various churches in the south. Subsequently, they proceeded to Taipei, where on the evening of December 4, they visited the Mengjia Church to meet with George Leslie Mackay, holding a joint worship service attended by approximately 140 people, including 13 to 14 Japanese individuals. During the service, Hosokawa and Mackay delivered sermons in Japanese and Taiwanese respectively, with Mackay also speaking in English, translated into Japanese by Hosokawa and into Taiwanese by Mackay, facilitating a rare exchange between Taiwanese and Japanese Christians.

On October 8, 1896, the Japan Christian Church dispatched Ichiro Daigi as a comforter and missionary inspector to the Taiwan church. Daigi visited Mackay, Campbell Naismith Moody, and various churches in both northern and southern Taiwan, holding meetings with church representatives.

Amidst the inception of Japanese colonial rule and the turmoil of the Jiawu War, the visit of Japanese church leaders provided solace to Taiwanese Christians who were facing hostility and attacks from both Japanese and Taiwanese militia. Although the power dynamics between the colonial motherland and its colony could not be ignored entirely, the visit held significant meaning in terms of church unity. Inadvertently, it also fostered a form of religious unity, adding to the goodwill of the colonial regime among the people of the colony.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

  1. In the midst of the Sino-Japanese War of 1894, China suffered defeat, leading to negotiations at Maguan for peace. Initially, Japan demanded that China cede territories including Andong, Kuanhe, Fenghuang City, Xiuyan, Taiwan, and the Penghu Islands. However, when Li Hongzhang was injured in an assassination attempt, sparking international outrage, Japan, fearing unfavorable international repercussions, allowed Britain, Russia, and France to mediate. On April 17, 1895, the Treaty of Shimonoseki was signed, ceding Taiwan and the Penghu Islands to Japan. However, there were domestic voices against the cession of Taiwan, with some advocating for seeking external assistance to preserve Taiwan. During the negotiations, Zhang Zhidong and others proposed borrowing two or three million taels from Britain, using Taiwan as collateral, believing that with the loan secured, Britain would defend Taiwan with troops. Additionally, they suggested allowing Britain to mine in Taiwan for twenty years. However, Britain refused; negotiations with France by Wang Zhichun and Gong Zhaoyuan initially met with ambiguity, ultimately rejecting the Qing court‚Äôs demands, citing changing circumstances. They then turned to Germany, which not only refused to help but also accused Qing officials in Tianjin of secretly ordering Taiwanese resistance against Japan, breaching agreements.

As external aid was not forthcoming, the ‚ÄúRepublic of Formosa‚Äù was established in May 1895, with Tang Jingsong as President, Liu Yongfu as Commander-in-Chief, and Qiu Fengjia as Drillmaster, and notifications were sent to foreign consuls. However, due to fear of the advancing Japanese forces, officials, including Tang Jingsong and Liu Yongfu, fled to the mainland. The ‚ÄúRepublic of Formosa‚Äù collapsed after 148 days. However, starting in May, Taiwanese organized guerrilla forces to resist the Japanese occupation, known as the ‚ÄúJiawu War.‚Äù From May to October 1895, more than 14,000 Taiwanese perished in the anti-Japanese struggle, while Japanese casualties were also significant, with a total of 32,135 casualties including disease-related deaths until the capture of Anping on December 25 (Jian Houcong 2002: 397-400; Shi Ming 2014: 265-271).

During the Japanese invasion and the ‚ÄúJiawu War,‚Äù incidents occurred where Christians were attacked and killed by local people who considered them spies, while the Japanese army accused Christians of inciting mobs to resist, leading to the execution of believers and the occupation of churches. These events were primarily caused by the Sino-Japanese War. Once Japan formally established its rule and restored order, the new regime generally maintained good relations with the church and even viewed Christians favorably.

  1. After 1895, Taiwan became a Japanese colony, and besides defining borders with Qing China, it also needed to change and confirm boundaries with other countries. On July 19, 1895, the Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs issued the Taiwan and Penghu Islands Possession Rights Declaration, notifying the Russian, German, and French legations in Japan that the Taiwan Strait was a public waterway and Taiwan and the Penghu Islands would not be ceded to other countries. Additionally, a diplomatic memorandum was exchanged with Spain to define the boundary with Spanish-ruled Philippines, using the latitude and longitude of the central navigable area in the Bashi Channel as the reference point. Governor Kabayama Sukenori reported to Acting Foreign Minister Nishi no Maseki on November 30 of the same year the jurisdiction areas of consuls stationed in Taiwan: W.S. Ayrtom, the British consul, entrusted with protecting Austro-Hungarian and Spanish nationals, stationed in Danshui Port, responsible for Danshui and Keelung; R.W. Hursts, the British consul, entrusted with protecting German, Austro-Hungarian, and Spanish nationals, stationed in Anping Port, responsible for Anping and Dagou; Allan Bain, the Dutch consul, stationed in Anping Port, responsible for Taiwan; Del. Kenper, the American consul in Xiamen, responsible for American expatriates in Taiwan (Wang Shiqing 1960: 238-239).

On February 23, 1896, Acting Foreign Minister Nishi no Maseki issued orders to Japanese envoys abroad regarding the Treaty of Commerce and Navigation signed with Japan, allowing nationals and ships of signatory countries to enjoy the freedom to anchor, operate, and reside in Keelung, Danshui, Anping, and Dagou ports. In addition, investigations into the number of expatriates and leased lands were conducted. Subsequently, France (1896) and Spain (1897) also established vice-consulates and consulates in Taiwan (Mo Guangxin 2012: 83-84; Wang Shiqing 1960: 238-241, 245). In the early days of Japanese rule in Taiwan, despite sporadic trade disputes and minor criminal cases arising from Taiwanese xenophobia, overall relations with predominantly Christian Western countries were good. These countries were eager to engage with Japan after the establishment of Japanese rule and the restoration of order, adopting a more positive attitude compared to dealings with the Qing court. For example, after returning to Taiwan in November 1895, George Leslie Mackay met with Governor Nogi Maresuke and received his apology and consolation, evidently leaving a positive impression on him. Even during the early turmoil of Japanese rule, some Taiwanese attended church gatherings for refuge, as the Japanese army did not persecute Christians after the establishment of governance (Taiwan Presbyterian Church General Assembly History Committee 1965: 96, 112). Until the 1930s, Japan implemented a policy of religious freedom in Taiwan, and relations with the church remained relatively harmonious.

3)In 1895, Taiwan was ceded to Japan according to the Treaty of Shimonoseki. There was widespread dissatisfaction in Taiwanese society. Scholars, led by Chiu Feng-chia, submitted petitions to Tang Ching-sung expressing their discontent and established the ‚ÄúRepublic of Formosa‚Äù with the national title ‚ÄúYongqing,‚Äù meaning they did not forget the Qing dynasty and were willing to ‚Äúfollow the orthodox, acting as a protective barrier‚Äù (Wang Shilang 1979: 73-75). Meanwhile, the public launched the ‚ÄúDefense of Taiwan War of 1895.‚Äù At that time, anti-Japanese and anti-foreign sentiments in Taiwan were extremely high, especially after Japanese military units entered Taiwan, burning villages and wantonly killing people, which ignited an armed resistance against Japan and led to the formation of guerrilla forces across various regions.

From the perspective of historian Shi Ming‚Äôs class analysis, the gentry class in Taiwan during the Qing dynasty was nurtured by the Qing court as compradors to govern Taiwan. He believed that during the Japanese invasion, the ‚ÄúRepublic of Formosa‚Äù revered by these scholars symbolized collusion with the remnants of the Qing dynasty. In contrast, the majority of the local population, mainly composed of peasants and urban poor, formed the backbone of the anti-Japanese forces, driven by local consciousness as their spiritual foundation. However, some leaders of the anti-Japanese forces also came from the gentry class (Shi Ming 2014: 266-267).

However, although the ‚ÄúRepublic of Formosa‚Äù and the anti-Japanese forces had differences, they were not clearly distinct. Both were results and manifestations of heightened national sentiments at the time, marking the beginning of the formation of a Taiwanese collective consciousness. However, this collective consciousness was essentially latent. It meant transforming Chinese consciousness into Taiwanese consciousness temporarily, seeking independence and self-preservation first, and then waiting for the opportunity to return to China (Yu Yihui 2004: 65-83). After the cession of Taiwan in the Treaty of Shimonoseki, armed resistance by the Taiwanese population intermittently continued for about ten years before coming to a conclusion.
