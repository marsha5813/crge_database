Hong Kong: Agency (1910-1920)

Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

  1. Kwan King Leung (ÈóúÊôØËâØ)

References: Zhou Jiayong (2016: 84-87); Ding Xinbao (2012: 60); Kwan Siu-Cheuk and Wing-Yu Yung (1997)

Kwan King Leung (1869-1945), courtesy name Xinyan, was a native of Panyu, Guangdong. His father, Kwan Yuan Cheung, was Hong Kong‚Äôs first registered dentist and an elder at the To Tsai Church. Kwan completed his secondary education at the Diocesan Boys‚Äô School and entered the Hong Kong College of Medicine in 1887, where he became a classmate and roommate of Sun Yat-sen, forging a close friendship. His mother, surnamed Lai, was the head nurse at the same medical college. Although his mother opposed direct involvement in revolutionary activities, Kwan maintained good relations with other revolutionaries like Sun Yat-sen and his associates. After graduating in 1893, he worked at Nethersole Hospital. In 1904, Zhang Zhidong invited him to serve as the chief medical officer for Jiangyin coastal forts due to his renowned medical skills. Returning to Hong Kong in 1908, Kwan started his private medical practice. In 1910, he initiated the ‚ÄúHair-Cutting but Clothing-Preserving Society,‚Äù advocating for cutting off the Qing dynasty-style queue but retaining traditional Chinese attire. He also founded the Chinese Medical Association. Kwan passed away in 1945.

References: Cheung Kat Shing (1951); Ying Fuk-Tsang (2014a: 355-358)

Cheung Kat Shing (1880-1963) was a renowned Christian businessman from Sha Gang Village, Kaiping. In 1911, he founded the Amoy Garden Restaurant, embodying the spirit of ‚Äúbusiness as mission.‚Äù As a child, he learned to read through church literacy classes. On February 12, 1901, he was baptized at the Dan Shui Kou Methodist Church in Kaiping. After moving to Hong Kong in 1901, he joined the Methodist congregation on Wellington Street, which later moved to Aberdeen Street in 1906. Cheung became a volunteer preacher in 1909 and the district treasurer for missionary funds in 1921.

Cheung‚Äôs Amoy Garden business is legendary in Hong Kong‚Äôs Christian history. Despite initial financial struggles, Cheung devoted his success to evangelism, using biscuit packaging to print verses such as John 3:16. Post-war, Cheung incorporated biblical verses into restaurant signage and used company vehicles for outdoor evangelism. He passed away in 1963 at the age of 84, remembered as a true Christian entrepreneur who spread the gospel through business.

  1. Sir Kai Ho (‰ΩïÂïü)

References: Lee Kam Keung (2014: 329-334); Choa (1981)

Sir Kai Ho (1859-1914), from Nanhai, Guangdong, was the son of Ho Fuk-tong, the first ordained Chinese pastor in Hong Kong under the London Missionary Society. Educated in the UK, Ho obtained degrees in medicine and law. He returned to Hong Kong in 1882 as a barrister and actively engaged in public service, becoming a Justice of the Peace in 1882 and serving in the Legislative Council from 1890 to 1914.

In 1887, Ho funded the establishment of Alice Memorial Hospital in memory of his late wife. He also helped found St. Stephen‚Äôs College and supported the creation of the University of Hong Kong. Later in life, Ho supported the revolutionary cause and served as an advisor to the Guangzhou military government after the 1911 Revolution. He was knighted in 1912, becoming the first Chinese knight in Hong Kong. Ho passed away in 1914.

References: Cheung Chuk Ling (1922); Au Bing-Kwong (1975); Chan Chi-Hang (2021)

Cheung Chuk Ling (1877-1961), born in Dongguan, Guangdong, was the eldest son of Pastor Cheung Shing-Wo of the Basel Mission. He led To Tsai Church in Hong Kong, revitalizing its ministry by establishing Bible study groups, Sunday schools, and outreach initiatives. Under his leadership, the church was reconstructed in 1926 and renamed Hop Yat Church.

Cheung actively supported the Chinese Christian movement and the Hong Kong Council of the Church of Christ in China. During the Japanese occupation, Cheung was arrested and tortured but continued his ministry. After retiring in 1950, he remained an advisor to Hop Yat Church until his passing in 1961.

Cheung Kaam-ue (1871-1931), from Ping Le, Guangxi, was an influential Christian apologist. Initially alienated from his family due to his conversion, Cheung pursued his faith through writing and editing for Christian publications, including The True Light and The Great Light. He used his platform to address controversial topics like Confucianism, ancestor worship, and atheism, defending Christianity through theological arguments.

Cheung‚Äôs legacy is marked by his intellectual contributions to Chinese Christianity. He passed away in 1931.

So Pui Kow (1868-1951) was among the earliest members of the Methodist Church in Hong Kong. After serving as a missionary in Vancouver for a decade, he returned to Guangzhou and later to Hong Kong to lead congregations. So was instrumental in establishing the first Chinese church-sponsored Boy Scout troop in 1921. He passed away in Hong Kong in 1951.

Lee Kau Yan (1882-1962), from Xiangshan, Guangdong, served as a pioneering Anglican pastor in Hong Kong. He contributed significantly to education, establishing schools for underprivileged children. Lee chaired the Hong Kong Christian Council for 33 years and published the weekly Ling Lok, documenting church activities and theological discussions.

Tsang Kei-Ngok (1890-1980) served as an Anglican priest in Hong Kong and Macau. He established schools and churches and was deeply involved in church rebuilding efforts during the Japanese occupation. Tsang retired in 1953 and passed away in 1980.

Christian or Pro-Christian Organizations

Christian Population in Hong Kong

References: Ying Fuk-Tsang (2004: 4); Hong Kong Government (1910a, 1910b, 1911, 1919a, 1919b)

According to the Hong Kong Blue Book (Ecclesiastical Return), the number of attendees at Chinese Christian worship services grew steadily from 2,140 in 1910 to 3,120 in 1919. During the same period, the number of attendees at non-Chinese churches increased from 1,480 to 2,125, with the growth rate for Chinese churches (45.8%) slightly surpassing that of non-Chinese churches (43.6%). For baptism numbers, Chinese churches recorded 219 baptisms in 1910, significantly higher than the 84 recorded by Western churches.

However, the Blue Book only included statistics from major Chinese denominations such as the Anglican Church, the London Missionary Society, the Basel Mission, the Wesleyan Missionary Society, and the Rhenish Mission.

Census data, which was conducted every ten years since the establishment of Hong Kong, included religious affiliation statistics only in 1881 and 1911. In 1911, the census listed Chinese adherents to the ‚ÄúChurch of England‚Äù at 3,292. Yet, the Blue Book for the same year recorded Chinese church attendance at only 2,270, suggesting that the census category ‚ÄúChurch of England‚Äù might have included other Protestant denominations.

By 1919, Hong Kong‚Äôs population totaled 501,584, with 487,380 Chinese and 13,600 non-Chinese. Among Chinese Christians, the proportion attending worship services was approximately 0.64%, slightly lower than the 0.67% recorded in 1909. This decline was primarily due to the rapid increase in the Chinese population, which grew by 58.5% over the decade, outpacing the growth of the Christian community.

  1. St. Paul‚Äôs Church, 1911

References: Zhou Jiayong and Huang Wenjiang (2013); Liu Yuet-sheng (1996: 87-96)

Victoria City was the center of Hong Kong‚Äôs colonial political, economic, and religious institutions. Early missionary societies established their headquarters and churches in Central, Sheung Wan, and Sai Ying Pun. In 1865, the Anglican Church built St. Stephen‚Äôs Church on Hollywood Road as the first Chinese church. After its relocation to Pok Fu Lam in 1888, no Chinese Anglican churches remained in Central.

In 1909, several local leaders, including Lam Woo, Wong Mau-lam, and Arthur D. Stewart (Principal of St. Paul‚Äôs College), proposed establishing a Chinese Anglican church in Central. Initially, services were held at the Bishop‚Äôs Chapel, but as the congregation grew, they collaborated with St. Paul‚Äôs College to build a church named ‚ÄúSt. Paul‚Äôs Church.‚Äù The foundation stone was laid in March 1911, with Governor Frederick Lugard officiating. The church opened in October 1911, and Reverend Arthur D. Stewart, fluent in Cantonese, served as its first pastor until 1919.

By the 1910s, the ‚ÄúSeven Major Christian Denominations‚Äù had established churches in the Central and Western Districts, including the Basel Mission‚Äôs Kau Yan Church, the London Missionary Society‚Äôs To Tsai Church, the Baptist Independent Church, the American Board Church, the Wesleyan Missionary Society, St. Paul‚Äôs Church, and the Rhenish Mission Church.

  1. Hong Kong Harbour Mission Church, 1912

References: Peng Shumin (2023: 35-38); Liu Yuet-sheng (1996: 99-103)

Fishing was a traditional industry in Hong Kong, with over 2,000 boat people living on fishing vessels in the early colonial period. In 1912, the Hong Kong Harbour Mission Church was established specifically to evangelize fishermen. Initiated by Ho Lai-chun, the General Secretary of the Chinese YMCA, and supported by Elder Mak Hoi-sang, the mission aimed to bring fishermen to Christ and provide civic education.

Initially managed by the YMCA‚Äôs ethics department, the mission launched its first gospel boat in 1913, which traveled between fishing ports on Hong Kong Island, Kowloon, and the New Territories. By 1917, a second boat was in operation. The mission also established schools, including a free school in Ap Lei Chau in 1920, providing literacy education for fishermen‚Äôs children.

In 1936, the mission became autonomous and was renamed the Chinese Christian Harbour Mission Church. A new church building was constructed in Ap Lei Chau in 1939. Over the years, the mission expanded its educational efforts, operating the Harbour Mission Fishermen‚Äôs School, the Harbour Mission Kindergarten, and the Yan Oi Kindergarten.

  1. China Peniel Missionary Society, 1914

References: Tan Pingbin (1965: 12-23); Liu Yuet-sheng (1996: 103-104)

The China Peniel Missionary Society was founded by Pastor A.K. Reiton in 1914. After arriving in Hong Kong in 1909, Reiton initially worked in Guangdong but later returned to Hong Kong to establish the mission. The society focused on ministering to fishermen and operated in various locations, including Yau Ma Tei, Kowloon City, and Sham Shui Po.

  1. Hong Kong Christian Churches Union, 1915

References: Liu Yuet-sheng (1996: 169-181); Ng Si-yuan and Lee Kam-keung (2018)

The Hong Kong Christian Churches Union was established in 1915 following a joint fundraising effort for flood relief in Guangdong. Its founding mission was to unite Chinese Christian churches in Hong Kong for shared ministries and mutual support. The Union managed cemeteries, organized city and rural evangelism, and supported education and charitable activities.

By 1941, the Union had grown from 7 to 34 member churches. During the Japanese occupation, the Union ceased operations but resumed post-war. Today, it is the oldest ecumenical Christian organization in Hong Kong, with over 400 member churches as of 2024.

  1. The Hair-Cutting but Clothing-Preserving Movement, 1910

References: Zhou Jiayong (2016: 84-101)

In 1910, the ‚ÄúHair-Cutting but Clothing-Preserving Movement‚Äù emerged in Hong Kong, primarily advocated by Chinese Christians. On October 2, members of the To Tsai Church, including Ho Lok-kam, Li Yuk-tong, Wong Hin-tong, and Tam Kau-ng, founded the ‚ÄúChinese Clothing and Haircutting Association.‚Äù In November, Kwan King Leung, also from the To Tsai Church, initiated the ‚ÄúHair-Cutting but Clothing-Preserving Association,‚Äù which held an inauguration ceremony attended by the prominent ‚ÄúSix Elders of Victoria Harbour.‚Äù Five of the six elders were affiliated with churches, representing the London Missionary Society, Rhenish Mission, and Congregational Church.

The movement‚Äôs manifesto emphasized the symbolic significance of retaining traditional Chinese attire while cutting off queues, which represented Qing rule. This movement carried strong anti-Qing sentiment and resonated with broader reformist trends in China, where cutting queues and adopting Western clothing were seen as signs of modernization. The movement gained widespread support across various sectors, including business, education, and labor, spreading to cities like Guangzhou, Zhongshan, Foshan, and Macau.

While preserving traditional clothing suggested a tendency to maintain heritage, cutting queues symbolized a rejection of Qing rule and aligned with the reformist agenda. This movement illustrated the active role of Hong Kong‚Äôs Christian elite in advocating societal reform within a colonial context.

  1. Founding of The Great Light Daily, 1913

References: Ying Fuk-Tsang (2014c: 235-237); Liu Shaolin (2003: 198-201)

On February 8, 1913, The Great Light Daily was established, becoming the first Christian-backed daily newspaper in modern China. Its creation was closely tied to Dr. Yan Wen-kai, a member of the To Tsai Church, who envisioned a Christian daily that would serve as a moral and social guide. Yan emphasized the importance of newspapers in shaping public opinion and contributing to social development. Inspired by the role of the press in revolutionary movements, he proposed the paper as a medium for promoting justice, equality, and virtue through Christian principles.

Yan‚Äôs proposal received support from prominent church members, including Au Fung-chee, Cheung Chuk-ling, Mak Mui-sang, and Wong Kwok-shuen. Funds were raised by forming a limited company with 6,000 shares priced at HKD 5 each. The newspaper‚Äôs name, The Great Light Daily, was inspired by Jesus‚Äô declaration: ‚ÄúI am the light of the world‚Äù (John 8:12), symbolizing the church‚Äôs mission to guide the nation‚Äôs progress.

Sun Yat-sen, learning of the initiative, purchased shares and inscribed the phrase ‚ÄúTogether with the Nation‚Äôs Spring‚Äù to encourage the newspaper‚Äôs role in strengthening the Republic. In 1920, he contributed an inscription to commemorate the paper‚Äôs anniversary.

  1. George S. Eddy‚Äôs Hong Kong Evangelism, 1914

References: Dai Weiliang (1915: 124-125); Ye Channing (2014: 89); Ying Fuk-Tsang (2006: 85-86)

George S. Eddy, who served as the East Asia Secretary of the North American YMCA, conducted nine missionary tours to China between 1907 and 1934. His 1914 visit included stops in 12 cities, including Hong Kong, where he preached on November 8 and 9 at the New Theatre in Kau U Fong. His topics, ‚ÄúTrue Patriotism‚Äù and ‚ÄúThe Causes and Consequences of National Decline,‚Äù drew large crowds, requiring multiple sessions to accommodate attendees. Nearly 4,000 people attended each night, with close to 1,000 expressing interest in Christianity.

Eddy‚Äôs success was attributed to his deep understanding of China‚Äôs political and social context, delivering messages that resonated with intellectuals and youth. He framed Christianity as a solution to national salvation, combining faith with patriotism in a rational and compelling manner.

The Hong Kong campaign was organized by the YMCA and Chinese churches, marking a milestone in interchurch cooperation. Preparations included scientific lectures to attract young people and pre-evangelism prayer meetings. Eddy‚Äôs visit also spurred the formation of the Hong Kong Christian Churches Union in 1915.

  1. Christian Weeklies Tan Tin (1916) and The Great Light Weekly (1917)

References: Ying Fuk-Tsang (2014c: 237-238)

In the 1910s, two Christian weeklies were launched in Hong Kong, both associated with Zhang Yijing. In 1916, Zhang and Yan Wen-kai founded Tan Tin Weekly, which was published by The Great Light Daily every Sunday. In its manifesto, the weekly emphasized inclusivity, integrating insights from Confucianism, Daoism, Buddhism, and Western philosophy alongside Christianity.

Following Tan Tin, Zhang established The Great Light Weekly in 1917, aimed at promoting Christian values and addressing social and spiritual needs. Its content focused solely on religion, avoiding general news, mundane church events, and secular political commentary. Zhang‚Äôs editorial vision emphasized spreading ‚Äúthe great light‚Äù of the gospel to bring enlightenment to those in darkness.

In 1918, Zhang left Hong Kong to resume his role at The True Light Daily in Shantou. Without its chief editor, The Great Light Weekly ceased publication the same year.

Key Theological Issues

  1. Christianity and the Founding Vision of the University of Hong Kong

References: Bishop of Victoria (1912: 154-158); Marshall (1996: 201); Mellor, Bernar (1980: 28-30)

Since the modern era, Western missionary societies had been actively involved in establishing Christian universities in China. In colonial Hong Kong, the founding of its first institution of higher education, the University of Hong Kong (HKU), in March 1912 also spurred discussions about its educational philosophy and its relationship with Christianity.

Governor Frederick Lugard, the primary advocate for establishing HKU, envisioned it as ‚Äúa British University in Hong Kong for China,‚Äù aspiring to make it the ‚ÄúOxford and Cambridge of the Far East.‚Äù With significant efforts, enough funding was secured to establish the university, including a donation of nearly ¬£18,000 from businessman Hormusjee Mody for building its campus. However, during the planning stages, Christian leaders in both Britain and Hong Kong debated the university‚Äôs value from a Christian perspective.

In Britain, the Bishop of Exeter, Lord William G. Cecil, emphasized in his 1911 book Changing China the crucial role Christian universities played in promoting reform and fostering the vision of a ‚ÄúChristianized China.‚Äù He argued that HKU should be a Christian university rather than a ‚Äúmerely utilitarian university‚Äù guided by practical concerns. Lugard, however, saw the role of missionary societies as providing moral oversight in student hostels but resisted framing the university as a Christian institution. Cecil countered that the rise of science would erode traditional Chinese religious values, posing a significant risk if no higher ideals replaced them.

The Bishop of Victoria, Gerald H. Lander, also expressed reservations about Lugard‚Äôs vision, noting that the university‚Äôs Christian character would depend on whether its appointed principal, professors, and lecturers held firm Christian beliefs and demonstrated personal piety. Nevertheless, Lander supported the involvement of missionary societies in overseeing HKU‚Äôs student hostels.

As a result, several hostels with Christian affiliations were established: St. John‚Äôs College (Anglican), St. Stephen‚Äôs Hall (London Missionary Society), Morrison Hall, and Ricci Hall (Jesuit). Post-war, St. John‚Äôs College and St. Stephen‚Äôs Hall merged into the current St. John‚Äôs College.

Ultimately, the colonial government chose not to adopt Christianity as HKU‚Äôs founding ethos. It was not until 1951, with the establishment of Chung Chi College by Christian leaders, that Hong Kong saw the realization of a Christian university.

Anti-Christian Events/Movements

  1. Responses to the Confucian National Religion Proposal

References: Wu Changxing (2015); Zhu Xiulian (2004); Goossaert & Palmer (2011: 43-66)

On February 8, 1917, the National Affairs Report (Guo Shi Bao), published by the Confucian Society of Hong Kong, strongly advocated for Confucianism to be established as the national religion and criticized Christianity. Members of Hong Kong‚Äôs Christian community urged Zhang Yijing to refute these claims in The Great Light Daily. Zhang temporarily postponed the preparation of The Great Light Weekly to engage in a written debate, publishing a series of articles countering the National Affairs Report. This debate lasted over a month, concluding on March 14. The articles were later compiled into a collection titled Great Light Breaks the Darkness, published by The Great Light Daily in 1919.

The title Great Light Breaks the Darkness reflected Zhang‚Äôs perspective: the ‚Äúgreat light‚Äù symbolized the enlightenment brought to people living in darkness, while ‚Äúbreaking the darkness‚Äù referred to exposing the fallacies of the national religion argument.

The debate centered on the Confucian Society‚Äôs proposal to establish Confucianism as the national religion, an idea initially suggested by Kang Youwei in 1898. After the establishment of the Republic of China, Chen Huanzhang and others in Shanghai founded the Confucian Society to reignite this movement, receiving support from Yuan Shikai, who sought to restore the monarchy. Even after Yuan‚Äôs death, the campaign continued, targeting Christianity as a major obstacle to Confucianism‚Äôs national prominence. The society argued that every new Christian in China signaled a step toward national decline and claimed that only by adopting Confucianism as the national religion could China achieve prosperity.

In his rebuttal, Zhang Yijing emphasized the principle of religious freedom, asserting that a state religion violated constitutional guarantees. He clarified that opposing a state religion was not equivalent to opposing Confucianism itself. Zhang further argued that promoting one religion above all others would insult other faiths and could lead to the eventual eradication of Christianity. He warned that a national religion would result in the merging of religion and politics, undermining the principle of separation between church and state.

On March 13, the National Affairs Report published a critique of Zhang Yijing‚Äôs views, to which Zhang responded the following day. The Confucian Society did not issue further rebuttals, marking the end of the month-long debate.

In May 1917, the National Assembly convened a constitutional meeting, affirming the principle that ‚Äúcitizens of the Republic of China have the freedom to revere Confucius and practice religion, subject to no restrictions except by law.‚Äù The proposal to establish Confucianism as the national religion failed to secure the required two-thirds majority in the legislature, bringing the controversy to a close.

Although Hong Kong was a British colony, Zhang Yijing‚Äôs defense of Christianity during this anti-national religion movement demonstrated the significant role Hong Kong Christians played in shaping the broader debate.
