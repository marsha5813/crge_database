Hong Kong: Context (1910-1920)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

  1. German Missions and Missionaries Before and After World War I (Leung 2020: 165-169; Leung Chui Wah 2020: 117-119)

After the outbreak of World War I, the British Foreign Office instructed colonial governments to implement a series of regulations. These included detaining and expelling nationals of enemy countries (Germany and Austria-Hungary), seizing related businesses, properties, and assets, and restricting local residents from conducting transactions with enemy states. Most German missionaries in Hong Kong were expelled; they either returned to Germany or relocated to mission stations in China. The Basel Mission initially avoided being classified as an enemy organization due to its registration in Basel, Switzerland. However, its strong German affiliation eventually led to its inclusion. Many Basel missionaries had entrusted their personal savings to a German company in Hong Kong for management and investment. These funds were confiscated by the British government during the war as reparations, wiping out their retirement savings.

In 1916, the British government began discussing how to handle the German missions and related commercial assets in imperial territories after the war. In March 1916, Governor Francis May supported a policy to reject the return of German missions but highlighted the challenges of confiscating their assets. Due to illness, May retired early in 1918, and Colonial Secretary Claud Severn acted as governor. In March 1919, Severn transferred the German mission‚Äôs work to the Anglican Church and expelled several German female missionaries in April.

  1. Pastor Conrad Bitzer of the Basel Mission Arrested by British Military Court in 1914 (Leung 2020: 166; Leung Chui Wah 2020: 117-118)

In September 1914, Pastor Conrad Bitzer, the treasurer of the Basel Mission, was arrested by a British military court. He was accused of remitting funds to German missionaries in China and engaging in anti-British propaganda among the Chinese population. This occurred during World War I when Britain and Germany were still at war. The military court found Bitzer guilty and sentenced him to four and a half months in prison. However, he was released after five days due to health reasons. At the time, Governor Francis May had written to General F. M. Kelly, the British military commander in China, noting that Bitzer‚Äôs remittance had been approved by the Hong Kong government. May also sought legal counsel, questioning the legality of trying and imprisoning a civilian in a military court.

  1. Trading with the Enemy Amendment Ordinance, 1919 (Leung 2020: 170-172; Leung Chui Wah 2020: 121-122)

On June 21, 1919, the Legislative Council passed the Trading with the Enemy Amendment Ordinance. This law placed the movable and immovable assets of German missions under the management of a statutory trust while continuing their charitable activities. The trust consisted of the governor, missionaries from the Anglican Church Missionary Society, and the London Missionary Society, with the Anglican bishop as its chair. One trustee, E. J. Barnett of the Anglican Church, later suggested transferring most Basel Mission operations (except for three churches in the New Territories) to the Anglican Church Missionary Society, while the London Missionary Society would manage Basel churches in the New Territories. All financial matters of the German missions were handled by the trust, which directed the revenue toward maintaining the missions‚Äô work in Hong Kong and their charitable activities.

  1. Mission to Seamen Incorporation Ordinance, 1919 (Davies 2017)

In June 1919, the Legislative Council passed the Mission to Seamen in Hong Kong Incorporation Ordinance. The work of the Mission to Seamen dated back to 1863, when the Anglican Church established a Sailors‚Äô Home in Sai Wan to cater to foreign sailors transiting through Hong Kong. In 1872, St. Peter‚Äôs Church was opened as an English-language church for sailors. The Mission to Seamen began working in Hong Kong in 1884 and collaborated with the Sailors‚Äô Home and St. Peter‚Äôs Church. In 1910, it opened a dormitory on Johnston Road in Wan Chai. By 1930, the Mission to Seamen merged with the Sailors‚Äô Home to form The Sailors‚Äô Home and Mission to Seafarers.

  1. Cheung Chau (Residence) Ordinance, 1919 (Hong Kong Government 1919a, 1919b)

In August 1919, the Legislative Council passed the Cheung Chau (Residence) Ordinance, which stipulated that no one could reside in the southern part of Cheung Chau Island (the hills) without the governor‚Äôs approval in council. The Attorney-General, when introducing the bill, explained that the land was reserved primarily for British and American missionaries. He added that several properties on the hilltop were already owned by missionaries. Chinese legislator Lau Chu-pak expressed disappointment that missionaries preaching equality and fraternity would make such a request. Ho Fuk, the brother of Ho Tung, also criticized the ordinance as racially discriminatory. In response, the Attorney-General reiterated that the ordinance had no racial considerations but aimed to provide missionaries with a suitable vacation location that could receive British protection, unlike other areas in southern China. Despite opposition from Lau and Ho, the ordinance was passed.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

During this decade, there was no social hostility or actions targeting Christianity among the general public.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

The Xinhai Revolution, World War I, the May Fourth Movement, and Hong Kong

(Carroll 2005: 81-83, 86-87; 2007; Liu Shuyong 2016: 139-149)

The Xinhai Revolution of 1911 successfully overthrew the Qing Dynasty, establishing the first republican government in Chinese history: the Republic of China. However, the revolution‚Äôs aftermath was fraught with challenges. Yuan Shikai seized control of the revolution‚Äôs achievements and even attempted to restore the monarchy, leading Sun Yat-sen to initiate the ‚ÄúSecond Revolution.‚Äù After Yuan‚Äôs death in 1916, China descended into a period of warlord fragmentation.

In Hong Kong, the colonial government grew concerned about revolutionary activities spreading from China, fearing they could threaten colonial rule. Colonial Secretary Frederick Lugard met with Chinese community leaders, allowing celebrations of the revolution on the condition that they notify the governor and refrain from overstepping boundaries, particularly by barring representatives of the Guangzhou Nationalist Government from entering Hong Kong. In 1913, the governor enacted the Education Ordinance, requiring all private schools to register with the government to prevent them from becoming hubs of anti-British propaganda. This was followed in 1914 by the Seditious Publications Ordinance, targeting materials that could destabilize Hong Kong.

Economically, Guangdong and Hong Kong had long been interdependent, with Chinese currency circulating freely in Hong Kong. However, the instability in Guangdong following the revolution caused the depreciation of Chinese currency. Concerned about its impact on Hong Kong, the government banned the circulation of Chinese currency in April 1912 and persuaded transportation companies, including ferries and trams, to stop accepting Chinese money by November. This angered the Chinese community, who saw the ban as an insult to the newly established Republic of China. The currency shortage also caused inconvenience for locals, leading to a boycott of tram services by Chinese residents. The boycott ended in early February 1913 after mediation by local Chinese merchants (Carroll).

The revolution also triggered an influx of refugees into Hong Kong. The Chinese population in Hong Kong rose from 438,000 in May 1911 to 500,000 by 1914. With the fall of the Qing Dynasty, Hong Kong became a refuge for former Qing loyalists such as Chen Botao, Zhu Ruzhen, and Lai Ji-xi. These intellectuals established forums to teach classical Chinese scholarship, with Lai founding the Xuehai Academy in 1923, turning Hong Kong into a center for preserving traditional Chinese studies. Later, the University of Hong Kong established a Chinese department with a curriculum centered on traditional Chinese scholarship (Leung Wing-sang et al., 2023).

The outbreak of World War I in 1914 also impacted Hong Kong. Although the war did not directly affect the city, the value of the Hong Kong dollar depreciated by 50%, and prices soared. Rice supplies were severely affected as shipments were diverted to Europe to alleviate food shortages. This prompted a significant outflow of Chinese residents, with 60,000 to 100,000 fleeing to Guangdong. The war also had profound effects on the German community in Hong Kong, as Germany became an enemy state. Many Germans were repatriated, expelled, or detained.

When the war officially ended in July 1919, Hong Kong joined global celebrations of peace. However, the Treaty of Versailles, which transferred Germany‚Äôs privileges in China to Japan, sparked the May Fourth Movement in China. Anti-Japanese sentiment spread across the country, reaching Hong Kong as well. Since the Anglo-Japanese Alliance, many Japanese had moved to Hong Kong, and during the war, Japanese merchants flooded the Hong Kong market with goods. By this time, the Japanese community in Hong Kong had surpassed European communities in size, fueling anti-Japanese sentiment.

Hong Kong students organized protests to support the patriotic actions of Beijing students, while local merchants launched a boycott of Japanese goods, encouraging residents to buy Chinese products. The city‚Äôs four major Chinese-owned department stores issued statements committing to selling only domestic goods like Suzhou and Hangzhou silks, refusing to trade in ‚Äúenemy goods.‚Äù The movement prompted the Chinese General Chamber of Commerce to intervene, and government officials, such as the Secretary for Chinese Affairs, instructed Chinese newspapers to avoid inciting anti-Japanese nationalism or using terms like ‚Äúimperialism.‚Äù The police also actively intervened in and monitored anti-Japanese demonstrations.

Interestingly, the New Culture Movement associated with the May Fourth era had limited impact on Hong Kong. This was due to the colonial government‚Äôs emphasis on traditional Chinese scholarship, which remained a significant focus of the local educational and intellectual scene.
