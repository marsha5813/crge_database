Hong Kong: Agency (Pre-1900)

Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

  1. John S. Burdon (Chen Ruiwen 2918; Zhong Renli 1951: 12-16)

John S. Burdon (1826‚Äì1907), bishop of the Diocese of Victoria of the Anglican Church, was the third bishop of this diocese. Born in Scotland in 1826, he was accepted as a missionary by the Church Missionary Society in 1850 and sent to Shanghai in 1853. In 1862, he served as an instructor at the Tongwen Guan in Beijing and returned to England in 1864 to report on his work. He returned to Beijing in 1865 and participated in Bible translation efforts with other missionaries, publishing a Mandarin version in 1872. He also translated the Book of Common Prayer into Chinese and supported translating ‚ÄúGod‚Äù as ‚ÄúTianzhu‚Äù (Â§©‰∏ª).

In 1874, he was consecrated as a bishop in England and appointed as Bishop of Victoria by the Archbishop of Canterbury under a Royal Letter of Warrant. He was the first bishop of the Diocese of Victoria not appointed through Letters Patent. This change in appointment method led to debates within the Hong Kong Anglican Church regarding the authority of a bishop appointed by the church rather than the state. The intervention of the Archbishop of Canterbury and the Colonial Secretary ultimately confirmed that his episcopal authority was no different from that of his predecessors.

At that time, the Diocese of Victoria covered parts of China (north of the 28th parallel, which was under the Diocese of North China) and Japan (which became an independent diocese in 1883). During his tenure, Bishop Burdon frequently visited Fujian and Beihai in Guangxi. In Hong Kong, he devoted himself to reorganizing St. Paul‚Äôs College, emphasizing its role in theological education to train Chinese clergy. Kwong Yat-sau, the first Chinese priest in the Hong Kong Anglican Church, graduated from St. Paul‚Äôs College and was ordained by Bishop Burdon in 1884.

Bishop Burdon also attempted to introduce the translation of ‚ÄúTianzhu‚Äù in Hong Kong. An inscription on the outer wall of the Hong Kong Anglican Church Bishop‚Äôs House reading ‚ÄúHonor Tianzhu, Love Your Neighbor as Yourself‚Äù is believed to be associated with him. However, the use of this term for ‚ÄúGod‚Äù was not widely accepted among Protestant missionaries, where ‚ÄúShangdi‚Äù (‰∏äÂ∏ù) and ‚ÄúShen‚Äù (Á•û) remained the mainstream translations. He also explored the idea of incorporating Chinese elements into the Eucharist, suggesting the use of rice cakes and tea instead of bread and wine, but this proposal was also not adopted.

In 1897, at the age of 70, Bishop Burdon retired. A year after leaving Hong Kong, he returned to Beihai and officially retired to England in 1900. He passed away in 1907 at the age of 81. Over his lifetime, he dedicated 44 years to missionary work, serving as Bishop of Victoria for 24 years.

  1. Thomas W. Pearce (Li Zhigang 2014a: 12-15; Liu Yuesheng 1996: 336-340; Ye Chenming 2014)

Thomas W. Pearce (1854‚Äì1938), an English pastor, was ordained by the London Missionary Society (LMS) in 1879 and arrived in Hong Kong in November of the same year. He initially served in Guangzhou and studied Chinese. In 1893, he was transferred to Hong Kong to assist Rev. John Chalmers, the superintendent of the Hong Kong Missionary District, and served as secretary of the Missionary Committee for South China. After Rev. Chalmers passed away in 1899, Pearce took charge of the LMS‚Äôs affairs in Hong Kong. He passed away in England in 1938 at the age of 84.

Rev. Pearce made significant contributions to the development of the LMS in Hong Kong. As early as 1889 in Guangzhou, he represented the LMS in negotiating the Agreement with the Chinese Church with the Daoji Church in Hong Kong, defining the relationship between the mission society and the self-governing church. This agreement held historical importance in delineating church-mission relations, especially in resolving disputes.

Later, as the Daoji Church congregation grew, its premises on Hollywood Road became insufficient, prompting discussions on constructing a new church. At the same time, the LMS considered rebuilding the Alice Memorial Hospital. Pearce proposed using the mission‚Äôs land on Bonham Road for the new church and hospital. The new church was completed in 1926 and named Hop Yat Church, acknowledging fundraising contributions by Chinese Presbyterians from Australia.

Rev. Pearce placed great importance on education, believing it aided evangelism. He advocated for church school teachers to be selected from among believers to strengthen church-school ties. After the University of Hong Kong was established in 1911 and Morrison Hall was completed, Pearce served as its warden. Between 1917 and 1919, he also acted as the principal of Ying Wa College.

In 1895, Pearce initiated prison visitations in Hong Kong to share the Gospel with prisoners, with Daoji Church actively participating in this ministry.

In 1898, Pearce launched missionary work in Macau, a Portuguese colony with a strong Catholic influence. He urged the LMS to negotiate with the Portuguese government to allow Chinese Christians to establish churches and carry out missionary activities in Macau. After years of effort, Chi Dao Church was established in 1906.

In 1904, the LMS, Daoji Church, and Union Chapel co-founded the New Territories Missionary Society, with Pearce serving as the council chairman. In 1915, the Hong Kong Christian Council was established, and Pearce, as the advisor of Daoji Church, was elected chairman for its first two terms.

  3. Wong Yuk Cho (Li Zhigang 2014b: 76-80; Liu Yuesheng 1996: 312-315; Ye Chenming 2014; Liu Shaolin 2003: 153-154)

Wong Yuk Cho (1843‚Äì1902), courtesy name Bing Yiu, was born in Dongguan, Guangdong, in 1843. His father, Wong Yuen Sum, joined Karl F. G√ºtzlaff‚Äôs Fu Han Society and later became a missionary with the Rhenish Mission in Hong Kong. Wong Yuk Cho attended the Rhenish Mission School in his youth and graduated from the Rhenish Theological Seminary in 1866. After graduation, he preached in Dongguan and Humen. In 1873, he came to Hong Kong for medical treatment and assisted the Berlin Foundling House. In 1884, he was ordained as a pastor.

In the 1880s, Chinese believers in the London Missionary Society (LMS) advocated for establishing self-governed churches and actively sought Chinese pastors. In 1885, Wong accepted an invitation from the LMS to become the pastor of its Chinese self-governed church. He led Daoji Church after its establishment in 1888, serving there until his death in 1902. Between 1885 and 1902, Wong made significant contributions to the development of the LMS‚Äôs Chinese self-governed churches.

As Daoji Church was adjacent to Alice Memorial Hospital, both under the LMS, Wong actively promoted hospital evangelism. He also advocated for vocational evangelism, establishing various projects to assist believers in need. These included a plantation on Lantau Island in 1891, a rattan goods factory in Yau Ma Tei in 1893, and a lime and shipyard on Peng Chau in 1894. In 1893, he founded the Yee Sang Industrial Mission to support sick believers. Although these initiatives had limited success, his efforts to integrate charity, industry, and evangelism were pioneering.

Wong also engaged in social issues. In 1877, he joined other pastors and believers in Hong Kong to participate in the anti-opium movement, establishing the ‚ÄúSociety for the Suppression of Opium.‚Äù He contributed articles against opium in the Global Times. In 1890, he wrote to the German church about the plight of blind girls in China, leading to the establishment of the Home for the Blind in Hong Kong with support from German Christian women.

In 1894, during a plague outbreak in Hong Kong, Wong raised concerns about sanitation in the Chinese community and petitioned the Governor for the construction of public baths. After the First Sino-Japanese War, he published Compilation of the Sino-Japanese War of 1894‚Äì95, advocating against a peace treaty with Japan. In 1896, he published Phonetic Script, proposing script reform to promote literacy. During the Hundred Days‚Äô Reform in 1898, he wrote a ten-thousand-word petition criticizing China‚Äôs shortcomings. Following the Boxer Rebellion in 1900, he wrote to Li Hongzhang, urging an end to anti-Christian disputes.

Wong‚Äôs involvement in national affairs brought him into discussions with Sun Yat-sen during the latter‚Äôs studies in Hong Kong. Sun frequently attended Daoji Church and discussed national issues with Wong, who supported the revolutionary activities of the Xingzhonghui in Hong Kong.

Wong Yuk Cho passed away in 1903 due to typhoid fever.

  1. Leong On Tong (Xing Fuzeng 2014: 156-159; Zhou Yifu 1975: 5-6)

Leong On Tong (1827‚Äì1913), a native of Xinhui, Guangdong, was born in 1827. In 1857, he moved to Australia to work in gold mining. In 1864, he converted to Christianity in Vaughan and joined the Methodist Church in Castlemaine, near Melbourne. He began preaching in 1866 and was ordained as a pastor in 1876, serving at the Little Bourke Street Church in Melbourne.

After retiring in 1885, Leong moved to Hong Kong. At that time, Chinese Methodists in Wellington Street had established Hui Shi Li Gospel Hall, but the South China District lacked pastors or missionaries and could only provide a monthly subsidy of 15 dollars. Seeing the need, Leong stayed in Hong Kong and became the first Chinese pastor of the Methodist Church in Hong Kong. Over ten years, more than 30 people were baptized under his leadership.

Due to financial constraints, Leong initially received a monthly salary of 20 dollars, which was reduced to a travel allowance of 10 dollars after two years. After 1896, he declined any payment altogether. Despite limited resources, Leong was devoted to church affairs. One major challenge was the lack of a suitable and permanent church site, which hindered the church‚Äôs development. In 1906, with donations from returning overseas Chinese and additional contributions from members, a building was purchased on Aberdeen Street and converted into a church. Within seven years, the number of baptized members grew to 150.

Leong On Tong passed away in April 1913 in Hong Kong at the age of 86. Over 28 years, he laid a solid foundation for the Methodist Church among Chinese believers in Hong Kong despite resource limitations.

  1. Charles R. Hager (Liu Yuesheng 1996: 322-327; Huang Yuhuo 2011: 326-349; Zhang Chengzhen n.d.)

Charles R. Hager (1851‚Äì1917), a pastor, was born in Switzerland in 1851. In 1858, he immigrated to the United States with his family. After graduating from the Pacific Theological Seminary in 1882, he was ordained as a pastor by the Congregational Church and commissioned by the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions to serve in Hong Kong.

The Congregational Church first sent missionaries to Hong Kong in 1843, but by 1845, they shifted their focus to Guangzhou. Due to limited success, the mission in Guangdong ended in 1867, and resources were redirected to Fujian and northern China. In the 1880s, Congregational missionaries returned to Hong Kong at the request and support of Chinese Christians in San Francisco who wanted to bring the Gospel to their hometowns in Guangdong.

After arriving in Hong Kong in March 1883, Hager rented a house on Bridges Street in Central, which served as a base for missionary work in inland Guangdong and a hostel for Chinese Christians traveling between the U.S. and Guangdong. The three-story building housed missionary quarters, a guesthouse, and the ‚ÄúAmerican Chinese Bookstore,‚Äù where Chinese children were taught English.

In May 1884, Hager baptized Sun Yat-sen, who was then a student at the Central School in Hong Kong. Using Hong Kong as his base, Hager studied Chinese and conducted missionary tours in the Siyi region, establishing mission stations in Xin Ning, Mu Ping, Guang Hai, and Hui An. In 1891, health issues forced him to temporarily return to the U.S., where he received medical training. He returned to China in 1894 with his new wife.

After his wife passed away in Guangzhou in 1895, Hager returned to Hong Kong, where he rented a house for a chapel. As the congregation grew, funds were raised to purchase land on Ladder Street, and a church was built in 1901, named ‚ÄúChinese Self-Supporting Church at Ladder Street.‚Äù

In 1910, health problems compelled Hager to retire from missionary work and return to the U.S. He passed away in California in July 1917 at the age of 66.

Hager‚Äôs 27 years of missionary service in Hong Kong and Guangdong included the establishment of over 40 churches and 29 schools, making him a pioneer of Congregational missions in Hong Kong and South China.

Christian or Pro-Christian Organizations

Christian Population in Hong Kong (Xing Fuzeng 2004: 4; Hong Kong Government 1899a, 1899b; Ying 2009: 67)

Between the establishment of Hong Kong as a colony and 1899, the development of Christianity in the colony did not achieve satisfactory results. According to the Hong Kong Blue Book and its Ecclesiastical Return, from 1844 to 1899, attendance at non-Chinese churches consistently outnumbered attendance at Chinese churches. For instance, in 1899, four major non-Chinese churches (St. John‚Äôs Cathedral, Garrison Church, St. Peter‚Äôs Church, and Union Church) had a combined attendance of 1,312, compared to 1,155 attendees at eleven Chinese churches (Anglican, London Missionary Society, Basel Mission, and Berlin Missionary Society). However, in the same year, the number of baptisms in Chinese churches (172) slightly exceeded that in non-Chinese churches (97).

In 1899, Hong Kong‚Äôs population (excluding the New Territories leased in 1898) was 259,312, of which 15,822 were non-Chinese and 243,490 were Chinese. Thus, Chinese Christians accounted for approximately 0.4% of the Chinese population. Compared to 1871, when only 396 Chinese Christians attended church services (0.34% of the Chinese population of 115,444), the growth was minimal. Christianity‚Äôs development in Hong Kong did not benefit significantly from its status as a British colony. Similarly, the growth of Christianity in China during this period was not exceptional. In 1873, there were 9,715 Chinese Christians who partook in communion, increasing to 80,682 by 1898. The growth rate of Christianity in mainland China during the 1870s to 1890s surpassed that in Hong Kong.

  1. Union Chapel, 1844 (Huang Wenjiang 2006: 89-113; Liu Yuesheng 1996: 155-156)

Union Chapel, established in 1844, was the first English-speaking church in Hong Kong, founded by the London Missionary Society (LMS) missionary James Legge. The name ‚ÄúUnion‚Äù signified inclusivity among believers of different backgrounds, while ‚ÄúChapel‚Äù rather than ‚ÄúChurch‚Äù reflected its non-conformist stance. Notably, the church also hosted afternoon services for Chinese attendees during its early years.

Following the colonial government‚Äôs decision to establish the City of Victoria in Central, Legge chose a site at the intersection of Hollywood Road and Peel Street, where the Western and Chinese communities converged. Due to a prominent stone pillar at its entrance, it was nicknamed ‚ÄúBig Stone Pillar Chapel.‚Äù In 1865, the church moved to Staunton Street, closer to the Taipingshan Chinese community. After Legge‚Äôs departure in 1867 and the diminishing tension between Anglican and non-conformist groups, the church was renamed Union Church.

In 1865, an agreement between the LMS and Union Chapel granted the former ultimate authority over the latter, including property transactions and pastor appointments. However, with its financial independence, Union Chapel managed to self-sustain and assumed full independence in 1872.

As the Chinese congregation grew, calls for a self-governed Chinese church intensified, leading to the establishment of Daoji Church in 1885, after which the Chinese congregation relocated. In 1890, Union Church moved to Kennedy Road in the Mid-Levels, near the Western residential area. The church continued to mature, establishing a Ladies‚Äô Committee (1893), a Christian Endeavour Society (1894), and a literary society (1902). In 1901, the government authorized Union Chapel to issue legally recognized marriage certificates. In 1904, it partnered with Daoji Church to establish the Hongkong & New Territory Evangelization Society.

  1. St. John‚Äôs Cathedral, 1849 (Zhong Renli 1951: 2-9; Wolfendale 2013)

In 1843, Vincent J. Stanton arrived in Hong Kong as the first Colonial Chaplain, tasked with ministering to British residents and initiating the construction of St. John‚Äôs Cathedral. In 1849, the Diocese of Victoria was established, and George Smith was appointed its first bishop. The diocese‚Äôs jurisdiction was extensive, covering all of China and Japan. During his tenure (1849‚Äì1865), Bishop Smith conducted nine visitations to regions including Shanghai, Ningbo, Xiamen, Guangzhou, Macau, Japan, India, Australia, and Southeast Asia.

After Hong Kong‚Äôs establishment, land transactions granted limited usage rights, with all land classified as Crown Land. However, St. John‚Äôs Cathedral was granted a freehold estate by the British Crown, exempting it from land taxes. The colonial government also covered two-thirds of the cathedral‚Äôs construction costs. Its location, adjacent to Government Hill and the Governor‚Äôs House, symbolized the close ties between the colonial government and the Anglican Church.

In 1850, St. Paul‚Äôs Theological College was established to train Chinese missionaries, primarily for missions in China rather than Hong Kong. Although the Anglican Church officially entered Hong Kong with Stanton‚Äôs arrival in 1843, its primary focus was on British residents. While Hong Kong served as the headquarters for the Diocese of Victoria, Bishop Smith prioritized mission bases in mainland China over Hong Kong. It wasn‚Äôt until the 1860s that local outreach to Chinese residents began, resulting in the establishment of Chinese Anglican churches.

  1. Methodist Garrison Church, 1893 (Wen Guowei 1992: 10-17)

Since Hong Kong‚Äôs early colonial days, many British soldiers stationed in the city were members of the Methodist Church, holding ‚Äúclass meetings‚Äù in military camps and attending Sunday services at Union Chapel. In 1885, Rev. C. Wenyon, the second chairperson of the South China District, wrote to the British Colonial Office, requesting land to build a chapel for military personnel and expatriates. Frederick Stewart, the under-secretary of the Colonial Office, approved the request in 1886.

The district‚Äôs annual meeting subsequently requested additional clergy to oversee both expatriate and Chinese congregations, as well as military personnel. At the time, Hong Kong‚Äôs garrison included 1,250 soldiers, 65 of whom were Methodists. In 1888, Rev. J. A. Turner became the first missionary assigned to Hong Kong specifically to minister to the military. Although financial difficulties led to proposals to cease military ministry in 1890, Rev. Wenyon insisted on its continuation.

In 1892, the Hong Kong government granted land for a garrison chapel, and the Garrison Church was inaugurated in May 1893 on Queen‚Äôs Road East, Wan Chai. Military ministry became a formal part of the South China District‚Äôs work, though financial support was not sought from the district. At its opening, the chapel had 22 members, with 380 Methodists among the troops.

In 1896, Rev. W. Musson observed the growing number of bars in Hong Kong and proposed creating a wholesome recreational space for soldiers. He wrote to the War Office, requesting the establishment of a Sailors‚Äô and Soldiers‚Äô Home. In 1897, the government granted land on Arsenal Street for this purpose, and the home opened in 1901.

  4. St. Stephen‚Äôs Church, 1865 (Chen Ruiwen 2023: 7-38; Liu Shaolin 2003: 164-165; Xing Fuzeng 2004: 22-23)

The Anglican Church in Hong Kong initially had no plans to establish Chinese churches. In a report written in 1844, before assuming his position as the first Bishop of Victoria Diocese, George Smith did not recommend Anglican missionary work on Hong Kong Island. One reason was the complex and transient nature of the Chinese population on the island. He also noted that some missionaries from other societies had already left Hong Kong. However, by 1862, with a significant influx of Chinese merchants fleeing the Taiping Rebellion, Smith acknowledged Hong Kong‚Äôs value as a missionary hub in a letter to the society headquarters.

In April 1862, T. Stringer arrived in Hong Kong as the first missionary formally sent by the Church Missionary Society (CMS). Anglican missionary A. B. Hutchinson marked 1862 as the beginning of Anglican Chinese evangelical work in Hong Kong. In December 1863, Bishop Smith ordained Lo Sam-yuen as a catechist, making him the first Chinese cleric in the Hong Kong Anglican Church. In 1864, the government granted land on Hollywood Road near the Chinese community in Taipingshan District for a church. However, in 1865, Stringer resigned to become the chaplain at the British Consulate in Guangzhou, leaving his work in Hong Kong to C. F. Warren. In June 1865, St. Stephen‚Äôs Church, the first Chinese Anglican church, was officially opened.

In 1884, Kwong Yat-sau was ordained as the first Chinese pastor of the Hong Kong Anglican Church. He succeeded the aging Lo Sam-yuen and managed the affairs of St. Stephen‚Äôs Church. Kwong regularly traveled to mission stations on the Kowloon Peninsula to preach.

In the 1880s, government road expansion plans required the relocation of St. Stephen‚Äôs Church. A new church was constructed on Pokfulam Road and was inaugurated in 1888 by Bishop Burdon.

  1. To Tsai Church, 1886 (Wang Zhixin 1986: 10-16)

As early as 1844, the London Missionary Society (LMS) established the first Chinese church in Hong Kong, Lower Bazaar Chapel (also known as True God Chapel), in the Lower Bazaar area of Central. The church was led by Ho Fuk Tong, the LMS‚Äôs first Chinese pastor. Lower Bazaar was a key commercial district for Chinese residents in early colonial Hong Kong. Simultaneously, some Chinese Christians gathered at Ying Wa College for services until the college closed in 1856. Taipingshan Chapel (later moved to Lantern Island, Causeway Bay) and Wan Chai Chapel were also established in 1862.

Due to the undesirable environment of the commercial Lower Bazaar, many Chinese Christians preferred the more spacious Union Chapel, attending its afternoon Chinese services. By 1866, the Chinese congregation at Union Chapel had grown to 120 attendees. However, governance at Union Chapel was dominated by Western members, leading to tension as the Chinese congregation expanded. Chinese believers increasingly felt they were merely ‚Äúborrowing‚Äù Union Chapel‚Äôs facilities. This strengthened their resolve for self-governance and the establishment of an independent church.

In 1885, Wong Yuk Cho accepted an invitation from the LMS to serve as the pastor of its Chinese self-governed church. With funding from the LMS and donations from Chinese believers, land was purchased at the intersection of Hollywood Road and Aberdeen Street. Alice Memorial Hospital and To Tsai Church were completed in 1886 and 1887, respectively. To Tsai Church became Hong Kong‚Äôs first self-governed Chinese church. Under Wong‚Äôs leadership, To Tsai Church initiated various ministries and collaborated with the LMS and Union Chapel to promote missionary work in the New Territories, becoming a mature self-governed Chinese church.

  1. Basel Mission, 1852 (Schrader 2008; Liu Yuesheng 1996: 39-43; Tong Wing See 2012: 15-22)

In 1847, Basel Missionaries Rudolf Lechler and Theodore Hamberg arrived in Hong Kong and joined Karl G√ºtzlaff‚Äôs Fu Han Society. They each learned local dialects‚ÄîTeochew and Hakka, respectively‚Äîusing Hong Kong as a base for missions in Guangdong. In 1851, they left the Fu Han Society and focused on establishing Basel Mission operations. Lechler began learning Hakka, laying the foundation for the mission‚Äôs focus on the Hakka population.

The Basel Mission established its first Hakka-speaking church in Sheung Wan, Hong Kong Island, in 1851, followed by another in Sai Ying Pun in 1852. While still based in Hong Kong, the mission concentrated on outreach to Hakka communities in Guangdong. In 1854, Hamberg passed away, and Lechler became the mission‚Äôs leader. Following tensions between Britain and China, Lechler returned to Hong Kong in 1856 and took a furlough in 1859. In 1860, he returned to Hong Kong, acquiring land in Sai Ying Pun to construct a mission house and a girls‚Äô charity school.

Due to an influx of Hakka refugees from the Taiping Rebellion, many settled in Sai Ying Pun. In 1867, the mission completed a church known as Basel Church. In 1878, Lee Ching Ko became the first Chinese pastor ordained by the mission.

From the 1860s onward, Basel Mission established additional churches in Hong Kong, including those in Shau Kei Wan (1862), To Kwa Wan (1890), Wong Nai Chung (1895), and Sham Shui Po (1898), all serving Hakka-speaking congregations. Lechler managed both Hong Kong and Guangdong Hakka missions until his retirement in 1899. He passed away in 1908 and, along with Hamberg, is considered a pioneer of Hakka evangelism.

  1. China Congregational Church, 1883 (Liu Yuesheng 1996: 46-52)

The American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions (ABCFM) oversaw the Congregational Church‚Äôs missions in China. In 1843, the ABCFM relocated its headquarters to Hong Kong and operated a school with six students. In 1845, the mission moved to Guangzhou. In 1883, responding to a request from Chinese Congregationalists in San Francisco, Charles R. Hager was sent to Guangdong, with Hong Kong as his base. Hager arrived in March 1883 and began missionary work in the Siyi region, establishing schools and evangelizing.

With assistance from local leaders, including Wen Qingxi and Song Zhi Rong, Hager established a Chinese day school and held English night classes. On Sundays, he preached at the school, baptizing students such as Song Yuk Lam, Sun Yat-sen, and Chen Zhong Gui. After renting a house for services, the church was initially called ‚ÄúUnion Church,‚Äù reflecting its purpose of uniting overseas and local Chinese Christians.

In 1899, funds were raised to purchase land on Ladder Street, and a church was constructed in 1901, named ‚ÄúChina Congregational Church at Ladder Street.‚Äù In 1912, the church was formally registered with the Hong Kong government as the China Congregational Church.

In addition to Ladder Street Church, branches were established in Foshan, Taishan, Zhongshan, and Gaoyao. In 1919, the ABCFM joined the Guangdong Chinese Christian Church, renaming the church ‚ÄúCongregational Chapel.‚Äù

Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

  1. The Anti-Opium Movement (1877) (Huang Zhiqi 2004: 38-45; Ye Chenming 2014: 303-304)

After the Opium Wars, opium flooded the Chinese market, causing widespread social harm. Many missionaries in China advocated for anti-opium campaigns, including F. S. Turner of the London Missionary Society (LMS), who served in Hong Kong and Guangdong. After returning to England in 1870, Turner became deeply involved in the anti-opium movement. In 1874, he joined the Anglo-Oriental Society for the Suppression of the Opium Trade as its Executive Secretary. Turner also coordinated with Chinese Christians, leading to the establishment of the Canton Anti-Opium Society at the Canton Pok Tsai Hospital in 1875, calling on believers to join the cause.

In Hong Kong, Lutheran pastor Wong Yuk Cho was particularly active in the anti-opium movement. Together with To Tsai Church‚Äôs Au Fung Chi, he co-founded the Hong Kong Anti-Opium Society, supporting counterparts in Guangzhou. He also published articles promoting the cause in The Global Times.

In 1877, over a dozen Hong Kong notables petitioned Zuo Zongtang, China‚Äôs special envoy to Britain, urging him to highlight the severity of the opium problem in Britain. They emphasized the role of British merchants in the opium trade as a key issue. The petition had 25 signatories, including prominent figures such as Wen Qingxi (LMS), Wong Tzu-chuen (Wong Tao, LMS), Wong Yuk Cho (Lutheran), and others from the Anglican Church, Basel Mission, and LMS. Zuo Zongtang responded positively.

The anti-opium movement in Hong Kong demonstrated the concern and activism of local Chinese pastors and believers regarding China‚Äôs opium problem.

  1. Chinese Christian Cemeteries (Xing Fuzeng 2012: 11-13; 78-98)

Since Hong Kong‚Äôs establishment, the government set up a Protestant Cemetery in Happy Valley in 1845, but it was designated exclusively for European and American burials. In the 1850s, a cemetery was also established for Chinese, but conflicts arose between the Buddhist and Taoist burial rituals and Christian beliefs, creating challenges for burying Chinese Christians.

In 1858, following advocacy by missionaries from the LMS, Anglican Church, and Basel Mission, the first Chinese Christian cemetery was established in the western district of Hong Kong Island (Sheung Wan and Taipingshan). In the 1880s, due to urban development, the government decided to reclaim the land and in 1882 granted a 43-acre plot in Pokfulam specifically for Chinese Christian burials. Missionaries from the three aforementioned societies served as trustees, but the cemetery‚Äôs management was entrusted to Chinese pastors and representatives. In 1909, the land was officially leased by the government, and after the formation of the Hong Kong Chinese Christian Churches Union in 1915, the cemetery came under its management.

As Christianity expanded to the Kowloon Peninsula, Chinese pastors such as Kwong Yat-sau petitioned the government in 1903, noting the lack of burial grounds for Kowloon Christians. With the support of Bishop Joseph Hoare of the Diocese of Victoria, the government granted a 10-acre plot in Kowloon City in 1904 for a Chinese Christian cemetery. Over time, this cemetery expanded, becoming the second major burial site for Chinese Christians in Hong Kong.

Both the Hong Kong and Kowloon Chinese Christian cemeteries feature stone tablets erected in 1920 inscribed with ‚ÄúCemetery Rules.‚Äù These rules strictly prohibited traditional Chinese customs, such as burning incense or paper offerings, using compasses to determine grave orientation, performing kowtows at graves, or offering sacrifices. These prohibitions reflected the tension between traditional Chinese culture and Christian practices in burial rituals.

Key Theological Issues

  1. The Term Question (Li Shichang 2008: 1-20; Xing Fuzeng 1995: 98-107)

Since Christianity was introduced to China, missionaries debated how to translate the Hebrew word Elohim and the Greek Theos in the Bible. Two main schools of thought emerged: one advocated for the term ‚ÄúÁ•û‚Äù (Shen), while the other preferred ‚Äú‰∏äÂ∏ù‚Äù (Shangdi). From 1877 to 1878, a debate known as the ‚ÄúTerm Question‚Äù unfolded, with significant contributions from Chinese Christians, including participants from Hong Kong.

The debate was sparked by an article, On the Term Question, by Shanghai missionary Huang Pin-san, published in The Chinese Recorder. J. S. Roberts, an American Presbyterian missionary and editor of the publication, responded with his own commentary, inviting readers to share their opinions. Over 60 articles were subsequently published, most written by Chinese pastors or believers. Among the contributors were Hong Kong believers Ho Yuk-chuen (LMS) and Kwong Yat-sau (LMS).

Ho Yuk-chuen, a Confucian scholar who converted to Christianity at 51 and was baptized by James Legge in 1857, was a prominent participant. In his article Harmonizing the Way of Heaven, Ho argued that the Christian ‚ÄúWay of Shangdi‚Äù aligns with the Confucian ‚ÄúWay of Shangdi‚Äù found in the Six Classics. Ho‚Äôs views drew criticism from Roberts, who asserted that Christianity represented the ‚ÄúWay of Heaven,‚Äù distinct from Confucianism‚Äôs ‚ÄúWay of Humanity.‚Äù Ho countered in a follow-up article, insisting that the two were compatible and accusing Roberts of obstructing the Gospel‚Äôs spread in China with his rigid views.

Kwong Yat-sau, who worked as a missionary after converting in Sydney, wrote An Examination of the Term Question. He emphasized that ‚ÄúÂ∏ù‚Äù (Di) in Confucian texts means ‚Äúruler‚Äù or ‚Äúhonored one,‚Äù making ‚Äú‰∏äÂ∏ù‚Äù an appropriate translation for Elohim and Theos. He argued that even in China, a land of idol worship, there existed an understanding of the Heavenly Father, making ‚Äú‰∏äÂ∏ù‚Äù a fitting choice.

Others involved in the debate included Wong Yuk Cho and Wong Chin Yu, sons of Wong Yuen Sum, though they were then serving in Guangdong and began their work in Hong Kong churches later in the 1880s. All these figures supported the use of ‚Äú‰∏äÂ∏ù,‚Äù though their arguments reflected different influences, with Ho‚Äôs rooted in Confucian scholarship.

  1. Can Christians Practice Ancestral Worship? (Xing Fuzeng 1995: 107-117; Xing Fuzeng & Leung Ka-lun 1997)

The issue of ancestral worship has been a persistent challenge in the interaction between Christianity and Chinese culture. This debate occurred among both missionaries and Chinese believers in the Ming-Qing Catholic missions and the late Qing Protestant missions. Even in British-ruled Hong Kong during the 19th century, the question of whether Christians could practice ancestral worship was raised.

In 1883, Guangdong Confucian scholar Luo Funan published An Inquiry into Correcting Misunderstandings in The Chinese Recorder. Luo expressed admiration for Christianity but refrained from converting due to its prohibition of ancestral worship. He argued that ancestral worship represented respect for parents, adherence to laws, and the preservation of propriety, rather than idolatry or superstition. This article sparked widespread discussion, leading to the compilation of A Collection of Clarifications in 1884 by the Presbyterian missionary Du Dingyu, featuring responses to Luo‚Äôs arguments.

Hong Kong Christians also joined the debate. Ho Yuk-chuen, despite his Confucian background and efforts to reconcile Confucianism with Christianity, condemned contemporary ancestral rites as corrupted by Buddhist and Taoist practices. Wong Yuk Cho, from the LMS, distinguished between the ‚Äúoriginal meaning‚Äù of ancestral rites‚Äîan expression of filial piety‚Äîand their ‚Äúcorrupted meaning,‚Äù which involved idolatrous practices. Wong argued that true worship lies in heartfelt remembrance rather than physical rituals.

Both Ho and Wong acknowledged the Confucian ideals behind ancestral worship but viewed contemporary practices as deviations. They emphasized that Christians, while rejecting ritualistic worship, upheld filial piety. Nevertheless, the mainstream missionary stance in the late Qing period remained firmly against ancestral worship, making it a highly contentious issue.

A Study of the Holy Path‚Äôs Arrival in the East was the first systematic historical account of Christianity‚Äôs spread to China, written by Wong Yuen Sum, an early Rhenish Mission evangelist. Wong completed the book in 1899, with its preface dated May 1899.
