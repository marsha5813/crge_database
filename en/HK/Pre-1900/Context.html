Hong Kong: Context (Pre-1900)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Background:

In 1841, Hong Kong Island was occupied by Britain and permanently ceded to Britain the following year under the Sino-British Treaty of Nanking. It became a Crown Colony under British rule. In 1860, the Treaty of Peking expanded the colony to include the Kowloon Peninsula. By 1898, Britain had secured a 99-year lease over the New Territories north of the Kowloon Peninsula. These acquisitions established Hong Kong‚Äôs territorial boundaries.

Following the establishment of Hong Kong as a British colony, Christianity (Protestantism) began its missionary work in the emerging colony. For many Western missionary societies, Hong Kong was viewed as a strategic base and logistical hub for missions in China (Xing Fuzeng 2004: 12-14). Early missionaries focused primarily on South China‚Äôs interior and did not initially plan to establish churches in Hong Kong. Examples include the Basel Missionary Society, the Barmen Missionary Society, and the Wesleyan Methodist Missionary Society. Some missions that began work in Hong Kong‚Äîsuch as the American Baptist Mission (1844), the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions (1845), and the American Baptist Mission (1859)‚Äîsubsequently relocated to Guangzhou or Shantou during the mid-19th century (Xing Fuzeng 2004: 14-21). The London Missionary Society (LMS) was the only society to maintain a consistent presence in Hong Kong.

Meanwhile, the Church of England‚Äôs establishment in Hong Kong was closely tied to the colonial administration. The Diocese of Victoria was founded through the efforts of the Colonial Chaplain. The first two bishops were appointed by Letters Patent issued by the British Crown, rather than by the Church itself. Early Anglican work in Hong Kong primarily served the British expatriate community. St. John‚Äôs Cathedral, completed in 1849, was an English-speaking church catering to colonial officials, the British military, and expatriates (Endacott & She 1949: 1-33).

As the colony developed and Chinese immigration from South China increased, Protestant missionary societies began to establish Chinese churches in Hong Kong. By the 1860s, the Anglican Church initiated its first Chinese ministries. By 1890, which marked the colony‚Äôs Golden Jubilee (1891), Christianity had laid a solid foundation in Hong Kong. The so-called ‚ÄúSeven Major Societies‚Äù‚Äîthe LMS, Anglican Church, Basel Mission, Southern Baptist Mission, Wesleyan Methodist Mission, American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions, and Rhenish Mission‚Äîhad firmly established themselves (Liu Shaolin 2018: 35). Additionally, the 1898 territorial expansion brought new opportunities for Christian development.

Religious Government Regulations:

The relationship between the Church of England and the colonial government was particularly close. In March 1847, the Legislative Council passed the first ordinance related to religion, approving the construction of St. John‚Äôs Cathedral (An Ordinance to Provide for a Church in Victoria in the Island of Hongkong, 1847). The Diocese of Victoria, established in 1849 with St. John‚Äôs Cathedral as its seat, was led by bishops and chaplains regarded as part of the governmental structure (Ecclesiastical Establishment). The bishop also served as the ex-officio chair of the Education Committee.

However, from the 1870s onward, the Anglican Church in Hong Kong gradually moved toward disestablishment. The third Bishop of Victoria was appointed by the Archbishop of Canterbury (rather than by Letters Patent), and from 1891, the colonial chaplain position was no longer part of the government structure (Endacott & She 1949: 47-56). Nevertheless, the Anglican Church and its bishops retained their unique status and influence in the colony.

As a British colony, Hong Kong also adopted the tradition of church weddings. The Marriage Ordinance of 1875 designated eight venues for legal marriages, all of which were Christian or Catholic churches. By 1890, the number of designated venues increased to ten, with nearly half being Chinese churches (including those of the Anglican Church, LMS, Basel Mission, and Methodist Mission) (Government Notification 1890). In 1899, two more Chinese churches (Anglican and Congregational) were added to the list (Government Notification 1899). This development reflected the growth of local Chinese churches.

Christian holidays also became public holidays in the colony. The Holidays Ordinance of 1875 designated Good Friday and Christmas as public holidays, while Easter Monday and Whit Monday were recognized as bank holidays.

In the 1890s, several religious-related ordinances were enacted by the Legislative Council, including:

  • St. John‚Äôs Cathedral Ordinances (1892, 1899): These established the cathedral‚Äôs corporate status, defined its rights to Anglican worship, and allowed military personnel to use the church. The ordinances also provided for government funding for church operations and clergy to offer pastoral services in prisons and government hospitals. The law also penalized disruptions of worship services or harassment of congregants.

  • London Missionary Society Incorporation Ordinance (1891): This granted corporate status to the LMS, allowing it to buy and sell land in the colony and conduct other transactions through a senior missionary as its representative. The appointment of senior missionaries was to be published in the government gazette.

  • Basel Evangelical Missionary Society Incorporation Ordinance (1896): Similar to the LMS ordinance, this established the Basel Mission as a corporate entity in Hong Kong, represented by its president or an authorized agent.

The drive for corporate status by religious organizations was closely linked to land ownership and property transactions. In the early years, missionary societies purchased land under the names of individual missionaries acting as trustees. In 1883, the LMS faced legal complications when attempting to sell a property, as the trustee had left Hong Kong and was no longer affiliated with the society. Chief Justice Sir George Philippo ruled that the LMS was not a corporate entity and therefore could not petition the court. This ruling prompted many foreign religious organizations in Hong Kong to seek incorporation. The Catholic Church and the Rhenish Mission were among the first to achieve this in 1885 and 1887, respectively (Summerfield 2014: 366-373).

The ordinances of 1892 and 1899 for St. John‚Äôs Cathedral further solidified its status as a corporate entity and clarified its organizational structure, granting it the legal right to conduct Anglican worship and oversee its property and operations.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

Background:

Since the establishment of Hong Kong as a British colony, the spread of Christianity on Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula did not provoke strong anti-Christian sentiments in society, nor did it lead to the type of conflicts between traditional elites and Christian missions (commonly known as missionary incidents) seen in mainland China. This can be attributed to Hong Kong‚Äôs unique colonial context.

At the time of Britain‚Äôs occupation, Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula had populations of only around 7,000 and 5,000 residents, respectively. Subsequently, large numbers of immigrants from South China migrated to the colony. These Chinese immigrants, driven by economic or political reasons, were, to some extent, voluntary participants in accepting colonial governance (Lau Siu-kai 1996: 173). Additionally, Chinese individuals who collaborated with the British gained entry into the colony‚Äôs elite class (Carroll 2005). As a result, Hong Kong did not develop a tradition of anti-colonial resistance, and anti-Christian movements rooted in anti-colonial or anti-imperialist sentiments never emerged in its history.

The colony‚Äôs development on Hong Kong Island was primarily concentrated in Victoria City. According to the 1881 census, out of Hong Kong‚Äôs population of 118,000, 100,000 resided in Victoria City, with an urban-to-rural population ratio of 86:14 (Yu Shengwu & Lau Chun-kuan 1994: 342). Within this context, the growth of Christianity was largely centered in Victoria City. Consequently, urban Christianity faced little opposition from traditional rural religions and customs, which often created tension and conflict in other regions.

However, after the New Territories became part of the colony in 1899, and as Christianity expanded into New Territories villages during the 20th century, such conflicts began to emerge on the historical stage.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

In 1841, Hong Kong‚Äôs establishment as a British colony was undoubtedly a result of the First Opium War.

Initially, Hong Kong‚Äôs primary value lay in serving as a foothold and logistical base for accessing China. Although it became a British colony, free movement between Hong Kong and mainland China allowed for frequent population exchanges. The colony‚Äôs population growth largely relied on immigration from South China, providing vital labor for the colony. During the 1850s, numerous Chinese merchants fled to Hong Kong to escape the Taiping Rebellion, transforming the colony from a frontier outpost into an important trade center. The territorial expansion of 1860 was driven by tensions in Sino-foreign relations and Britain‚Äôs ambitions to strengthen its influence in China. Additionally, Hong Kong became a hub for Chinese laborers destined for North America and Australia, and the arrival of capital-rich Chinese expatriates further facilitated the emergence of a transnational trade network centered on Hong Kong (Carroll 2007: 28-31, 72-73).

The post-1860 era saw further developments in Hong Kong‚Äôs role as a trade hub.

After China‚Äôs defeat in the Second Opium War, more ports were opened to foreign trade. The 1869 opening of the Suez Canal connected Europe and Asia by sea, enhancing Hong Kong‚Äôs significance as a transshipment hub. By the 1860s and 1880s, Hong Kong had regular shipping routes to Singapore, San Francisco, Manila, Japan, Taiwan, and Australia. The China Merchants Steam Navigation Company also established a branch in Hong Kong, cementing the colony‚Äôs role as a center for inland, coastal, and international maritime transportation (Yu Shengwu & Lau Chun-kuan 1994: 271-275).

The 1898 expansion marked a pivotal moment in Hong Kong‚Äôs territorial growth.

To strengthen the colony‚Äôs defense, Britain sought to acquire more land north of the Kowloon Peninsula to serve as a buffer zone between the colony and the Qing Empire. After China‚Äôs defeat in the First Sino-Japanese War in 1895, the Qing government faced significant pressure as foreign powers scrambled to carve out spheres of influence and lease strategic ports. In 1898, the Convention for the Extension of Hong Kong Territory was signed, granting Britain a 99-year lease of rural land between the Shenzhen River and the northern Kowloon Peninsula, along with 230 islands. The newly leased New Territories covered approximately 365 square miles‚Äîten times the size of Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula combined‚Äîand included over 430 villages with a population of 100,000.

The New Territories posed unique challenges for British rule.

Unlike the relatively peaceful occupations of Hong Kong Island and the Kowloon Peninsula, Britain faced strong resistance from local elites in the New Territories. This culminated in the ‚ÄúSix-Day War‚Äù from April 14 to 19, 1899, during which over 500 villagers were killed. To pacify the locals, the British government decided to preserve traditional practices, allowing the indigenous social customs and traditions to continue under colonial rule (Carroll 2007: 67-70).

James S. Lockhart, the colonial Under-Secretary, described the New Territories as ‚Äúthe Great Difference‚Äù in his 1898 survey report (Hayes 2006). The colonial administration adopted a governance approach for the New Territories that starkly differed from the more urbanized Hong Kong Island and Kowloon Peninsula.

Throughout the 19th century, the Chinese population played a key role in Hong Kong‚Äôs development.

Chinese collaboration with the colonial government significantly influenced the colony‚Äôs historical trajectory. However, working with the British did not equate to a lack of concern for China. Many local elites, including Chinese Christian pastors and believers, maintained an active interest in the late Qing reform movement, developing a uniquely Hong Kong-centered nationalism (Carroll 2005). Simultaneously, traditional Chinese culture persisted and adapted within the colony, underscoring Hong Kong‚Äôs role as a crossroads for East and West. This dynamic laid the foundation for Hong Kong‚Äôs identity as a hub of cultural exchange (Luo Xianglin 1961).
