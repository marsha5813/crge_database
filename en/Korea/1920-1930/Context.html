Korea: Context (1920-1930)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

The policy trend of this era aligns with the flow of Taisho (Â§ßÊ≠£) Democracy. This period saw a relaxation of thought control through religion in Japan due to the influx of liberal democratic ideas. The Japanese government‚Äôs efforts to control public thought through religion and to maximize the social role of religion led to the proposal of the Religious Bill (ÂÆóÊïéÊ≥ïÊ°à) in 1898. However, it was shelved due to opposition from the religious community, only to resurface in the late 1920s in connection with the Great Kanto Earthquake (1923) and the global economic depression.

Concerned that the implementation of this Religious Bill in Japan would inevitably affect Korea, the Korean Christian community, led by Pastor Han Kyung-jik (Ïèü general Assembly of Presbyterian Church in Kore, ÊúùÈÆÆËÄ∂ËòáÊïéÈï∑ËÄÅÊúÉ), submitted a petition to the Government-General of Korea opposing the enactment of the bill (1926). A vigorous opposition movement also developed in Japan. Nevertheless, Japan‚Äôs efforts to control public thought were not absent. In 1923, the ‚ÄúImperial Rescript on the Promotion of National Spirit‚Äù and, in April 1925, the ‚ÄúPeace Preservation Law‚Äù were enacted to strengthen control over public thought in Japan. In Korea, following the March 1st Movement, missionaries and Korean church leaders were wary of religion becoming a tool for thought control.

Christian private schools were the core of secret societies, organizing and spreading the independence movement across the nation. Influenced by the principle of national self-determination, the harsh suppression of Korea‚Äôs independence movement at the Paris Peace Conference, and negative views on colonial rule, there was a shift in religious and private school policies. This marked the beginning of the so-called ‚ÄòCultural Rule‚Äô era.

Ironically, perhaps due to the relaxation policy, this was also the period when the most pro-Japanese collaborators emerged and advanced into society.

In 1919, the union missionary conference submitted a petition to the Governor-General with the following six requests:

First, regarding the promotion of missionary work, the following modifications are requested from the Government-General:

Relax the control over churches and missionaries.

Ensure that officials of the Government-General do not discriminate against Christianity and Christians.

Encourage the Government-General to uphold the rights of petition and appeal.

Second, regarding educational activities, the following modifications are requested from the Government-General:

Allow Bible teaching and religious ceremonies to be included in the curriculum of private schools operated by Christian principles.

Abolish restrictions on the use of the Korean language.

Provide more freedom than currently given in the management of private schools and eliminate unnecessary interference by government officials.

Recognize the freedom of conscience for students.

Provide Koreans with equal educational opportunities as Japanese and grant more freedom in the selection of textbooks, as well as lift restrictions on teaching Korean and world history.

Grant the same privileges to graduates of private schools approved by the Government-General as those given to graduates of corresponding public schools.

Cease excessive property demands on private schools.

Third, regarding healthcare activities, request changes in the Government-General‚Äôs stance:

Delegate the detailed management of private hospitals to the hospital staff and avoid interference by the Government-General.

Fourth, regarding religion and literature:

Abolish censorship of Christian-related books.

Allow Christian newspapers, magazines, and other publications to cover not only church-related matters but also broader religious literature, removing the restrictions of the 1911 ‚ÄúPublication Ordinance.‚Äù

Ensure that local authorities do not interfere with the sale of Bibles, pamphlets, and other Christian literature by peddlers and businesses.

Fifth, regarding property ownership and financial matters:

Allow Korean churches and missionary groups to own and register property in their names by granting them corporate status.

Relax the current strict regulations on obtaining permits for collecting donations for hospitals, schools, and charitable activities.

Draw attention to the destruction of churches and other properties by officials of the Government-General without compensation to this day.

Sixth, regarding moral improvements:

Oppose the laws regarding the establishment of licensed quarters.

Demand amendments to laws affecting the production, manufacture, and sale of opium and morphine.

Revise the laws on the sale of alcohol and restore traditional local options, allowing communities to prohibit the establishment of bars according to their wishes.

Apply the age restrictions for minors on smoking to Korean minors as well.

Enact laws to restrict child labor and improve conditions for workers in factories and mines.

Ensure that Christian inmates in prison are not forced to work on Sundays or engage in tasks against their conscience.

Grant privileges that aid the religious and moral improvement of inmates.

In March 1920, the ‚ÄòPrivate School Regulations‚Äô were revised, and the previous provision (Article 6-2), which prohibited the addition of courses beyond those prescribed in the regulations for ordinary, higher ordinary, girls‚Äô higher ordinary, vocational, and specialized schools, was deleted. This allowed missionaries to teach the Bible as a subject, a proposal they had made in 1919.

The Propagation Regulations were also revised in April 1920. The amount of required information to be reported was minimized, and penalties were reduced. However, the newly established authority of the Governor-General to suspends or prohibit the use of religious facilities‚Äô indicated that, despite the appearance of relaxation, control was actually strengthened.

A key issue during this period was the incorporation Ë≤°ÂúòÊ≥ï‰∫∫ of religious organizations. This was very important because, in Korea, once religious organizations obtained corporate status, they could manage and maintain their basic assets. The Catholic Church was the first to achieve incorporation, unlike the Protestant Church, which did not openly express opposition to the Governor-General of Korea. The Catholic Church also continuously worked within its community to protect its assets. Protestant denominations founded their own incorporated foundations as below. While this could be seen as a softening of Christian policy through dialogue with missionaries, it can also be viewed as placing church and school assets under the control of the Governor-General of Korea. Although ‚ÄòCultural Rule‚Äô was promoted, ostensibly relaxing control over religious education and missionary activities, the legal restrictions on church assets and the freedom of assembly were actually tightened.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

As previously explained in the agency questionnaire, while the 1920s saw the emergence of Korean Christian movements and conflicts arising from the influx of communism, it is difficult to find any widespread atmosphere of rejecting Christianity in society as a whole.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

The 1920s were a critical period for the Korean independence movement, particularly for the Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea (established in 1919 in Shanghai), which sought international recognition and support for Korean independence from Japanese colonial rule. The Provisional Government‚Äôs diplomatic efforts focused on forging ties with key global powers, particularly China, the United States, and the United Kingdom, although these efforts met with varying degrees of success.

The Provisional Government established its base in Shanghai, a city that offered a degree of political autonomy under the Chinese Republic, and it benefited from the relatively tolerant stance of the Chinese government toward Korean exiles. Sun Yat-sen, the leader of the Kuomintang (Chinese Nationalist Party), offered moral support to the Korean independence cause. However, China‚Äôs own internal struggles, including the warlord era and the rise of the Communist Party, limited its ability to offer significant material support.

The Provisional Government made considerable efforts to engage with the United States, largely through the Korean National Association and Korean-Americans who lobbied the U.S. government for recognition of Korean independence. Syngman Rhee, one of the key figures of the Provisional Government, sought to garner U.S. support by appealing to the principles of democracy and self-determination espoused by President Woodrow Wilson. However, the U.S. government, prioritizing its relations with Japan and focusing on post-World War I reconstruction, was reluctant to antagonize Japan and did not officially recognize the Provisional Government.

The United Kingdom, like the United States, had significant geopolitical interests in East Asia and was generally supportive of Japan‚Äôs role in the region following World War I. The British government, therefore, was cautious in its approach to the Korean independence movement and did not extend formal recognition to the Provisional Government. British policy during this time was characterized by a desire to maintain stability and its own colonial interests rather than support independence movements in Asia.

The Provisional Government of Korea had a mixed relationship with the Soviet Union. While some factions within the Korean independence movement were sympathetic to communist ideas, others were wary of aligning too closely with the Soviets due to ideological differences and fears of Soviet domination. The Soviet Union, for its part, offered some support to Korean independence fighters, particularly those who were aligned with communist ideals, but this support was inconsistent and often influenced by the Soviets‚Äô shifting priorities in East Asia.

During the 1920s, the Soviet Union provided refuge to some Korean independence activists and allowed them to organize and train within its borders. This included support for armed resistance groups operating in the Russian Far East and Manchuria. However, the relationship was often strained, as the Soviet Union‚Äôs primary focus was on consolidating its own power and spreading communism, rather than supporting Korean nationalism per se.

Despite the lack of formal recognition from these major powers, the diplomatic efforts of the Provisional Government in the 1920s laid the groundwork for future international advocacy. The Provisional Government‚Äôs persistent appeals to global powers helped keep the issue of Korean independence alive in international forums, even if tangible support was limited during this period.
