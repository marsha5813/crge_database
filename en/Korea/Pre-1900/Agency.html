Lee Su Jung was born into a family of Confucian scholars in the Yangban class. He mainly learned Chinese classics but had knowledge about Roman Catholicism. On his way to Japan with Park Yonghyo, he brought the Evidence of Christianity by Martin, the Commentary on the Gospel of Mark, and the Nevius Catechism by Nevius (So, 1993). His uncle was a martyred Roman Catholic that had a considerable knowledge about Christianity. Thus, he had knowledge of both Confucianism and the Bible before he met Tsuda Shen. Lee was impressed by conversations with Tsuda Shen about Christianity and started studying the Bible. One day he had a dream about God‚Äôs vision of the Bible in Joseon that made him devote himself to the study of the Bible. When he returned to Japan without official obligations, Lee was requested to translate the Bible into Korean by Henry Loomis (1839-1920), the Japan agent of the American Bible Society (ABS). Lee then translated the five books of the Chinese New Testament -the Four Gospels and the Book of Acts-into Chinese with Korean language suffixes (added to aid the reading of Chinese texts), the Hyun-To-Han-Han-Shin-Yak-Sung-Gyung (Êá∏ÂêêÊº¢ÈüìÊñ∞Á¥ÑËÅñÊõ∏) in 1883. It is noteworthy that while the United Presbyterian Church Versions by Ross and McIntyre with Seo Sang-ryun were distributed under the auspice of the British Bible societies in northern Korean and among Koreans in Manchuria, the Su Jung Lee Versions were provided under the auspice of the ABS in the Southern Korea. A number of Korean students were studying in secondary schools in Tokyo. Lee began to preach to these Korean students and the resident foreign missionaries came to teach the Bible. This service grew to the regular worship services and became the first Korean Protestant Church in Japan in 1884. Later, some of the baptized students went to the U.S. for further study and others became high officials in the government in Joseon and Daehanminguk. (3) Calling for Western missionaries to Joseon. This is the first Korean modernist-nationalist organization and the Independent is its public intellectual forum. Whereas Seo Sang-ryun and Lee Su Jung translated the Bible in Korean and founded churches, Seo Jae-pil used Christianity as a philosophy to reform political, economic, and social structures in Joseon through educating ordinary people. His dream for this reform failed through the Gapshin Coup due to intervention by the Qing military and a lack of ordinary Joseon people‚Äôs support in 1884. He fled to the United States and his family was wiped out by King Gojong and conservative government officials. He converted to the Christian faith while learning English at the local YMCA. Seo attended high school and college with assistance by William Hollenback, a Christian philanthropist, and he became the first Korean to obtain U.S. citizenship and adapted his name, Philip Jaisohn in 1890. He gained a medical degree in 1892. 10 years after the failure of the Gapshin Coup that forced King Gojong to implement reforms, Seo with other plotters were invited to return to Joseon to help reform the Joseon government. Until he was expelled by the Joseon government again in 1898, his arguments were in two folds. First, the government must protect the life and property of any person. Second, everyone has the equality and rights of a person which was granted by God (Choi, 2020). (1) Enlightenment movement using Doknip sinmun (the Indipendent, Áç®Á´ãÊñ∞ËÅû). When Jaisohn returned to Joseon in 1895, the King and his government were confronted with the height of political instability. After losing the war, the Qing dynasty ceded its power over the Korean peninsula to the conqueror, Japan. Russia attempted to take the power which the Qing dynasty lost. King Gojong lost his control and visions for his government and people suffered under the tyranny of officials. Jaisohn created Doknip sinmun (1896, The Independent) and published various messages to enlighten people against the incumbent government. Doknip simun published in both Korean (Hangul) and English three times a week. Isabella Bishop who was travelling around Joseon described values of The Independent ‚Äúin creating a public opinion which shall sit in judgement on regal and official misdeeds. The Hangul was named as Eun mun (Ë´∫Êñá) meaning low-class letters. Using only Hangul allowed anyone to read this newspaper regardless of their social class. This strategy was attacked by high-class people, but the editorial kept Hangul because it was easy for everyone; everyone could be literate and therefore national pride and ultimately the independence of Korea would come out‚Äù (The Independent, April 7, 1896). Soon after The Independence was created, Jaisohn initiated the ‚ÄúIndependence Association‚Äù and then continued to be the guiding force of this association without making his role known in either its organization or its operations.
