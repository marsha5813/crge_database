Korea: Context (1900-1910)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

  1. Summary of regulations on religion in this decade

In November 1905, through the ‚ÄúSecond Japan-Korea Agreement,‚Äù Japan deprived Korea of its diplomatic rights and began preparing for full-scale colonial rule. The first religious-related regulation announced was the ‚ÄúRegulations on Religious Proclamation‚Äù (Governor-General Ordinance No. 45), issued in November 1906. Until the ‚ÄúProselytizing Rules‚Äù were implemented in 1915, the ‚ÄúRegulations on Religious Proclamation‚Äù were used for ten years to appease, control, or suppress Korean religions in accordance with the colonial rule situation. Below is the original text of this regulation and its full English translation.1

Rules Regarding the Proclamation of Religion

Article 1: When a denomination or sect belonging to Shinto, Buddhism, or other religions within the Empire wishes to engage in missionary activities, the person in charge or an equivalent authority must select a representative in Korea, attach a resume, and submit the following information through the supervising consul to obtain the approval of the Resident-General.

  1. Method of propagation

  2. Method of supervision of the propagator

Article 2: Except in the case described in the previous article, when an imperial subject wishes to engage in the proclamation of religion, they must prepare the required information regarding the name of the religion and the method of propagation, attach a resume, and obtain the approval of the Resident-General through the supervising consul.

Article 3: When intending to establish a temple, hall, synagogue, preaching place, or lecture hall for religious purposes, the manager of the denomination or sect or the propagator mentioned in the previous article must provide the following information and obtain the approval of the supervising consul in the location where the establishment is planned.

  1. Name and location

  2. Name of the religion

  3. Method of management and maintenance

Article 4: When the manager of a denomination or sect, the propagator mentioned in Article 2, or any imperial subject wishes to take charge of managing a Korean temple, they must attach the necessary documents and obtain approval from the Resident-General through the supervising consul in the location of the temple.

Article 5: If there is a desire to change the matters approved in the previous articles, re-approval must be obtained.

Article 6: The manager of a denomination or sect or the propagator mentioned in Article 2 must report the names and qualifications of the propagators to the supervising consul, and the same applies when there are changes to these propagators.

The importance of this regulation lies in two key aspects: 1) categorizing religions within the legal system, and 2) systematizing the registration procedures for Japanese religious organizations, using these as tools for assimilating Korean religions. This regulation was informed by the Japanese government‚Äôs experience in developing and implementing religious laws following the Meiji Restoration.

First, this regulation categorized religions into Shintoism (Sect-Shinto), Buddhism, and other religions. This categorization was based on Â§™ÊîøÂÆòÂ∏ÉÈÅî No. 19 (1884) and Article 28 of the Constitution of the Empire of Japan (Êó•Êú¨Â∏ùÂõΩÊÜ≤Ê≥ï; 1889). Â§™ÊîøÂÆòÂ∏ÉÈÅî No. 19 (1884) recognized Shintoism, Buddhism, and Christianity as official religions, while other religions, including folk and new religions, were classified as pseudo-religions. Confucianism and State Shintoism were recognized as non-religions. Specifically, Confucianism was employed as a moral and ethical philosophy for educating people. Article 28 of the Constitution of the Empire of Japan defined the realm of religious practice as subject to social regulations in Japan, confining religion within the social norms that should not transcend the national ideology of State Shintoism. This concept was transferred to the religious policies in colonial Korea and often conflicted with the missionaries there.

The importance of this regulation lies in two key aspects: 1) categorizing religions within the legal system, and 2) systematizing the registration procedures for Japanese religious organizations, using these as tools for assimilating Korean religions. This regulation was informed by the Japanese government‚Äôs experience in developing and implementing religious laws following the Meiji Restoration.

First, this regulation categorized religions into Shintoism (Sect-Shinto), Buddhism, and other religions. This categorization was based on Â§™ÊîøÂÆòÂ∏ÉÈÅî No. 19 (1884) and Article 28 of the Constitution of the Empire of Japan (Êó•Êú¨Â∏ùÂõΩÊÜ≤Ê≥ï; 1889). Â§™ÊîøÂÆòÂ∏ÉÈÅî No. 19 (1884) recognized Shintoism, Buddhism, and Christianity as official religions, while other religions, including folk and new religions, were classified as pseudo-religions. Confucianism and State Shintoism were recognized as non-religions. Specifically, Confucianism was employed as a moral and ethical philosophy for educating people. Article 28 of the Constitution of the Empire of Japan defined the realm of religious practice as subject to social regulations in Japan, confining religion within the social norms that should not transcend the national ideology of State Shintoism. This concept was transferred to the religious policies in colonial Korea and often conflicted with the missionaries there.

However, about half of the Shintoism followers were Koreans, and more than half of the Japanese Buddhism followers were Koreans, indicating that Japanese religions had begun their missionary work in Korea successfully. Other laws and regulations also had a significant impact on religions in Korea. For instance, the Japanese Resident-General of Korea implemented the Security Regulation in 1906 as part of broader efforts to tighten control over the peninsula. These regulations were designed to suppress any activities or movements that could threaten the stability of Japanese rule. They included measures to monitor and restrict the activities of Korean citizens, particularly those involved in political, religious, or social organizations that were viewed as potential sources of resistance. This regulation empowered Japanese authorities to arrest, detain, and punish individuals deemed to be engaging in subversive activities, thereby further consolidating Japan‚Äôs control over Korea.6

The Newspaper Law of 1907, followed by the Gwangmu Newspaper Law and the Revised Newspaper Law of 1908, was enacted to tighten control over the press in Korea. These laws were designed to restrict freedom of speech and prevent the spread of anti-Japanese sentiment through newspapers and other publications. The 1907 law was unable to effectively control foreign publishers, such as the Protestant Yeseugyo Sinbo and the Catholic Gyeonghyang Sinmun, as well as the general newspaper Daehan Maeil Sinbo. However, the revised laws of 1908 empowered the Minister of Home Affairs to ban the sale, distribution, and confiscation of newspapers published abroad or by foreigners within Korea if they were deemed to disrupt public order or corrupt public morals. Consequently, these newspapers faced censorship and significant limitations in publishing religious messages that could encourage Christian involvement in the independence movement.7

Response to Japanese‚Äô attempt to control Church and its schools in Korea: (1900-1910)

Protestant churches in Korea experienced rapid growth with relatively little resistance from the indigenous population and other religions. Gojong and Queen Min provided protection to missionaries, allowing them to establish connections with missionary organizations in their home countries. This support was evident even during events like the Donghak Rebellion, the Sino-Japan War, and the Russo-Japan War when indigenous people recognized that missionaries and their institutions, including churches, schools, and hospitals, represented a new force for the betterment of their country and families. Notably, the middle and lower social classes, as well as merchants, actively embraced Christianity. The Great Revival and the Million Soul Movement further fueled the expansion of the Protestant church.

Moreover, the Protestant church in Korea took a leading role in modernized education, with missionaries eagerly establishing schools. However, from Japan‚Äôs perspective, this development wasn‚Äôt entirely favorable because individual missionaries and mission denominations often competed with each other, creating a self-sustaining system that wielded significant social influence. When looking at the School Ordinance of 1906 (Teacher‚Äôs School Ordinance, High School Ordinance, Foreign Language School Ordinance, Common School Ordinance), it becomes evident that Japan‚Äôs educational policy in Korea at that time aimed to educate and legitimize the colonial regime through public schools, with the long-term goal of stability in mind.

To gradually address the integration of Christian schools into Japan‚Äôs new regime, early versions of the Private School Ordinance were discussed through negotiations with missionaries. According to Samuel Moffet‚Äôs report in 1908, the General Council of Protestant Evangelical Missions in Korea had multiple meetings and negotiations with the Japanese government starting in 1907.11 They sought the understanding of British and American diplomats and held discussions with Commissioner Zennei, the educational authorities, and Mr. Sammons. The committee provided information about the scale and nature of their educational activities and requested that government education regulations be formulated in consideration of their educational activities. They also requested recognition for their schools and graduates on par with government-run schools, provided that their schools reached or exceeded the standards set for government-run schools.

Furthermore, the committee made it explicitly clear that Christianity should be taught in all of their schools. They received very courteous treatment, and the meetings were seen as promising a harmonious relationship of mutual understanding between government authorities and missionary organizations in education.

Japan had promised religious freedom but requested that Christian schools adhere to the same standards as public schools. In response, the missionaries stated,

  1. Regarding the curriculum of our schools, we intend to maintain the same standards as public schools. Of course, Christianity will be taught as the foundation of ethics and religion.

  2. We request that the government consider treating our schools and their graduates on par with public schools and their graduates. We will ensure that our schools are not deemed inferior to public schools.

  3. To bring our schools in line with government regulations and policies, we wish to obtain information about the regulations and curricula of public schools. We are willing to provide all necessary information about our schools and their policies to the government.12

First, this regulation categorized religions into Shintoism (Sect-Shinto), Buddhism, and other religions. However, about half of the Shintoism followers were Koreans, and more than half of the Japanese Buddhism followers were Koreans, indicating that Japanese religions had begun their missionary work in Korea successfully. These Japanese religious institutions and workers were utilized to integrate Korea‚Äôs religions into the Japanese Empire as a means of assimilation.

Other laws and regulations also had a significant impact on religions in Korea. For instance, the Japanese Resident-General of Korea implemented the Security Regulation in 1906 as part of broader efforts to tighten control over the peninsula. These regulations were designed to suppress any activities or movements that could threaten the stability of Japanese rule. They included measures to monitor and restrict the activities of Korean citizens, particularly those involved in political, religious, or social organizations that were viewed as potential sources of resistance. This regulation empowered Japanese authorities to arrest, detain, and punish individuals deemed to be engaging in subversive activities, thereby further consolidating Japan‚Äôs control over Korea.6

The Newspaper Law of 1907, followed by the Gwangmu Newspaper Law and the Revised Newspaper Law of 1908, was enacted to tighten control over the press in Korea. These laws were designed to restrict freedom of speech and prevent the spread of anti-Japanese sentiment through newspapers and other publications. The 1907 law was unable to effectively control foreign publishers, such as the Protestant Yeseugyo Sinbo and the Catholic Gyeonghyang Sinmun, as well as the general newspaper Daehan Maeil Sinbo. However, the revised laws of 1908 empowered the Minister of Home Affairs to ban the sale, distribution, and confiscation of newspapers published abroad or by foreigners within Korea if they were deemed to disrupt public order or corrupt public morals. Consequently, these newspapers faced censorship and significant limitations in publishing religious messages that could encourage Christian involvement in the independence movement.7

Response to Japanese‚Äô attempt to control Church and its schools in Korea: (1900-1910)

Protestant churches in Korea experienced rapid growth with relatively little resistance from the indigenous population and other religions. Gojong and Queen Min provided protection to missionaries, allowing them to establish connections with missionary organizations in their home countries. This support was evident even during events like the Donghak Rebellion, the Sino-Japan War, and the Russo-Japan War when indigenous people recognized that missionaries and their institutions, including churches, schools, and hospitals, represented a new force for the betterment of their country and families. Notably, the middle and lower social classes, as well as merchants, actively embraced Christianity. The Great Revival and the Million Soul Movement further fueled the expansion of the Protestant church.

Moreover, the Protestant church in Korea took a leading role in modernized education, with missionaries eagerly establishing schools. However, from Japan‚Äôs perspective, this development wasn‚Äôt entirely favorable because individual missionaries and mission denominations often competed with each other, creating a self-sustaining system that wielded significant social influence. From 1905 to 1910, a number of missionaries doubled and the number of Christians in 1900 was 12,000, and by 1905, it had grown to 26,057. In contrast to the strong desire for independence among Korean Christians, missionaries received instructions from their respective home churches to keep their distance from Korean politics. In 1901, the Korean Protestant Church adopted a policy of non-interference in politics. The colonial government, taking a cue from the Christian control laws that had been promulgated in Japan in 1899, issued several regulations, such as Governor-General‚Äôs Decree No. 45 in 1906, titled ‚ÄòRegulations on Missionary Activities,‚Äô and Governor-General‚Äôs Ordinance No. 62 in 1908, regarding private schools. The ‚ÄòRegulations on Missionary Activities‚Äô aimed to restrict the spread of foreign missionaries into the Korean interior and among the common people while encouraging the missionary activities of Japanese missionaries.

Rather than the ‚ÄòRegulations on Missionary Activities,‚Äô it was the ‚ÄòPrivate School Ordinance‚Äô that directly exerted control over foreign missionaries before the start of complete colonial rule in 1910. The colonial government recognized the importance of the school system, but its effects would not be fully seen for at least a decade, so Japan hastened to reorganize the education system. Moreover, the Christian community‚Äôs schools were perceived as bases for independence movements. When looking at the School Ordinance of 1906 (Teacher‚Äôs School Ordinance, High School Ordinance, Foreign Language School Ordinance, Common School Ordinance), it becomes evident that Japan‚Äôs educational policy in Korea at that time aimed to educate and legitimize the colonial regime through public schools, with the long-term goal of stability in mind.

To gradually address the integration of Christian schools into Japan‚Äôs new regime, early versions of the Private School Ordinance were discussed through negotiations with missionaries. According to Samuel Moffet‚Äôs report in 1908, the General Council of Protestant Evangelical Missions in Korea had multiple meetings and negotiations with the Japanese government starting in 1907.11 They sought the understanding of British and American diplomats and held discussions with Commissioner Zennei, the educational authorities, and Mr. Sammons. The committee provided information about the scale and nature of their educational activities and requested that government education regulations be formulated in consideration of their educational activities. They also requested recognition for their schools and graduates on par with government-run schools, provided that their schools reached or exceeded the standards set for government-run schools.

Furthermore, the committee made it explicitly clear that Christianity should be taught in all of their schools. They received very courteous treatment, and the meetings were seen as promising a harmonious relationship of mutual understanding between government authorities and missionary organizations in education.

Japan had promised religious freedom but requested that Christian schools adhere to the same standards as public schools. In response, the missionaries stated,

  1. Regarding the curriculum of our schools, we intend to maintain the same standards as public schools. Of course, Christianity will be taught as the foundation of ethics and religion.

  2. We request that the government consider treating our schools and their graduates on par with public schools and their graduates. We will ensure that our schools are not deemed inferior to public schools.

  3. To bring our schools in line with government regulations and policies, we wish to obtain information about the regulations and curricula of public schools. We are willing to provide all necessary information about our schools and their policies to the government.12

Comparing the Korean Private School Ordinance with the Japanese counterpart from 1899 (Table 3), we can observe several differences in its provisions:

  1. Addition of Articles 2 and 4: Articles 2 and 4 were added to the Korean Private School Ordinance. However, the expressions ‚Äúthose with improper conduct‚Äù and ‚Äúthose who disrupt social order‚Äù are ambiguous and leave room for subjective judgment by the Japanese government, making it possible for them to interpret these terms as they see fit.

  2. Supervisory Authority: The entity responsible for supervision differs between Japan and Korea. In Japan, local officials oversee private schools, whereas in Korea, the Minister of Education directly assumes this role. Additionally, the Minister of Education in Korea has the power to review and approve textbooks and exercises command over local officials. Moreover, the authority to prohibit classes is unique to Korea.

  3. Annual Reporting Requirement: Article 12 stipulates that schools must provide an annual report on their status. Notably, even private schools in Korea are subject to textbook inspections and approvals, while Japanese private schools do not face such interference. This provision regarding textbooks is significant as, in Christian schools, the Bible served as a textbook, making it possible for authorities to potentially ban Bible education at any time.

  4. Stringent Conditions for Approval: The conditions for approval and the qualifications for school principals in Korea are more restrictive and specific compared to Japan. This specificity may have been intended to limit the granting of approvals to individuals not favored by the Japanese government.

These differences highlight the Japanese government‚Äôs intent to exert greater control and influence over education in Korea compared to Japan, particularly in the context of Christian schools and the potential regulation of Bible education.
