Unlike the Methodist Church, the Presbyterian Church, which strongly opposed shrine worship, could not withstand the pressure, and 17 out of 23 synods nationwide permitted shrine worship. The Suncheon Synod, to which he belonged, also allowed it in April 1938. In response, missionaries of the Southern Presbyterian Church withdrew from the presbytery. Pastor Kim Eung-gyu, who served at Aeyangwon Church, also resigned in opposition to shrine worship. Son Yang Won began the anti-shrine worship movement in 1938. He explained to his congregation, colleagues, and leprosy patients under his care that shrine worship was contrary to Christian doctrine and refused to participate in the movement. Once discovered, he was barred from evangelizing in the Busan area and was assigned to Aeyangwon Church in 1939. The Government-General of Korea was forcing churches to not only participate in shrine worship but also cooperate with the war effort.   1. Chu, Ki-chol Ï£ºÍ∏∞Ï≤† Êú±Âü∫Âæπ

  1. Birth/Death: 1897/11/25 (Gyeongsangnam-do Woongcheon-gun) ~ 1944/04/21 (Pyongyang)

  2. Education: As his name suggests, he was born into a devout Christian family, and he followed Christianity faithfully. He attended Osan School, a Christian school located in Pyeonganbuk-do during middle school, where he was baptized. In 1915, he enrolled in the Department of Economics at Chosun Christian University with the belief that he needed to create industries to help the destitute and hungry people. However, he had to discontinue his studies due to a vision problem. In 1922, he entered Chosun Presbyterian Theological Seminary and graduated while working as a missionary. Following this, he served at Busan Choryang Church and later took on a pivotal role in Korean Christianity at Pyongyang Sanjeonghyeon Church.

  3. Contribution: He was a Presbyterian minister, a prominent anti-Japanese independence activist who led the March 1st Movement and the refusal to worship at shrines, and a martyr. He is one of the most respected figures in Korean Christianity, alongside Son Yang-won.

  4. Pastor Chu, Ki-choll‚Äôs shrine refusal movement sheds light on how most Korean church associations under the Chosun Governor-General‚Äôs rule approved shrine worship and collaborated with Japan‚Äôs suppression of Korean Christianity. Pyongyang was often referred to as the Jerusalem of the East and a focal point of the Korean Christian revival, with the Sanjeonghyeon Church, where he served as a minister, at its center. In 1936, he took up his post at Sanjeonghyeon Church, and in July 1938, he was arrested for opposing shrine worship. On September 7, 1938, the church council allowed shrine worship in his absence. Despite constant police surveillance during worship services, he and most of the Sanjeonghyeon Church congregation strongly opposed shrine worship.

The Chosun Governor-General‚Äôs office, through the church council, prevented Pastor Chu Ki-chol from preaching and detained him again. Missionary Charles F. Bernheisel, the founder of Sanjeonghyeon Church, preached in his place. They tried to coerce Bernheisel through two elders who complied with shrine worship into delivering sermons related to shrine worship opposition, but the missionary and most congregants refused to comply. In 1939, the church council recommended the resignation of Pastor Chu Ki-chol. In 1940, Sanjeonghyeon Church was closed.

The content of Berheisel‚Äôs letter reads:

[Easter] On Sunday afternoon at 4 o‚Äôclock, one of the elders, accompanied by a police officer, appeared at Sanjeonghyeon Church, secured the keys to the church, and locked all church doors, posting a warning notice (‚ÄúDue to unfavorable circumstances, Sanjeonghyeon Church gatherings will be temporarily suspended‚Äù) under the orders of the church council of the Chosun Governor-General‚Äôs office. The police, through Pyongyang Council, claimed no responsibility for all their actions. Indeed, the police and their subordinates, the seven church council members, demolished the church and plunged it into turmoil. However, the congregants refused to comply with their attempts to force them into submission. Their testimony, borne out of a passionate desire for freedom of truth and faith, was openly and unequivocally expressed. Instead of compromising their loyal faith in God, they chose to close their newly built beautiful church building. (Park, 2016, p. 157)

In Pyongyang, the church council, along with the police, forcibly entered the house where Pastor Chu Ki-chol‚Äôs mother and children were staying while he and his wife were imprisoned and drove them out. Since then, Pastor Chu‚Äôs family had to live by taking shelter in barns.

In October 1940, the Methodist Church resolved that ‚Äúforeigners shall not hold leadership positions or representative positions in churches and institutions,‚Äù and the Presbyterian Church made the same decision on November 10 (Park, p. 161). In Pyongyang Council as well, missionaries decided to withdraw from all activities, and in 1941, the Bernheisel couple concluded their 41 years of missionary work in Korea and returned to the United States. Subsequently, Pastor Chu Ki-chol endured severe torture in prison and was martyred on April 21, 1944, in Pyongyang prison. In his final letter sent to his wife, he wrote:

‚ÄúMy dear, it seems that my body can no longer endure. I am sorry that I couldn‚Äôt take care of you due to the burden the Lord has entrusted to me. Please take care of my mother in my place. His concerns for the Korean church at that time apply equally to today‚Äôs Korean church. On the other hand, Japan claims that he died of illness, but according to testimonies of those who were imprisoned with him, it appears to have been a homicide due to forced drug administration.

  3. Contribution: Ham was a judicial officer, politician, pastor in Presbyterian church, and independent movement activist. Although there is ongoing debate about his stance in Rhee, Syngman‚Äôs dictatorial government, he rallied scattered Christians and churches that were demolished by the Japanization policy in the early 1940s. From 1945 to 1950, the political situation in Korean peninsula was in chaos. All three political leaders, Gu Ki, Gyu-sik Kim, and Syngman Rhee were Christians and they contended that new nation must be established on Christian principles. Ham represented the conservative party based on Christianity and put his effort to re-building Christian culture and church.

Ham resigned his pastor position at Yeondong church in 1941 due to activation of the Japanization policy. He secluded himself in a rural town until the independence in 1945. He worked to restore the collapsed Protestantism in Korea and strived to create a unified church. However, the South Convention of Joseon Council of Churches, where he worked, failed to differentiate itself from the united Church of Japan with Korean Presbyterian Church, which had been formed by integrating denominations towards the end of the Japanese occupation. Various Protestant denominations focused more on re-establishing their own churches rather than creating a unified church. After being appointed as the vice-president of the Presbyterian Church of Korea Southern Assembly, organized in June 1946 in South Korea, he led the reconstruction of the Presbyterian Church.

The Christian reconstruction movement was also important to politicians at the time. Among Gu Kim, Gyu-sik Kim, and Syngman Rhee, Syngman Rhee, who had a weaker domestic political base, participated most actively in the reconstruction of Christianity. Ham actively supported Syngman Rhee, arguing that a new state based on Christian principles should be built. Syngman Rhee was also a leader of the March 1st movement and needed Ham, who was a leader of the Christian community in Gyeonggi-do, to rally Christian forces. In December 1945, Ham formed The Independent Central Council of Religion with other religious groups, including Catholicism, Buddhism, Confucianism, Cheondoism, and Daesoonjinrihoe, to demand swift independence and oppose trusteeship. In December 1948, the UN recognized only the government established in South Korea and acknowledged its legitimacy. As division became an irreversible reality, a tripartite cooperation movement was promoted. Christians took the lead in this because the three leaders shared Christianity in common. At this time, Ham Tae-sik acted as a negotiator for the Christian tripartite cooperation promotion, encompassing both Catholic and Protestant denominations. Of course, the cooperation movement was not successful. He also established a Rural Gospel School to lead the reconstruction and evangelization of the rural areas devastated by Japan‚Äôs militarization.

  1. Yoo, Jae-gi Ïú†Ïû¨Í∏∞ ÂäâËºâÂ•á

  1. Birth and Death: 1905/05/19 (Gyeongsangbuk-do Yeongju-si) ~ 1949/07/14 (Seoul)

  3. Contribution: The Governor-General of Korea considered his Rural Research Society a secret society for independence movement and arrested him in July 1938. After his release in 1941, he took on the role of chairman of the Gyeongbuk Presbytery of the Korean Presbyterian Church for National Mobilization, actively encouraging participation in the Pacific War and promoting Shinto shrine worship, engaging in pro-Japanese activities. In 1995, he received a national decoration for his achievements in the rural enlightenment movement, but it was revoked following investigations into his pro-Japanese activities from 1940 to 1945. This article aims to highlight his renewed efforts in the rural movement after the liberation in 1945.

The most serious problems after liberation were land and food issues. 80 percent of the population were small farmers, due to colonial landownership and Japanese monopoly capital dominating rural areas. Agricultural reform involved abolishing colonial landownership, eliminating remnants of Japanese rule, and reforming the land system to enable self-sufficiency for farmers. However, post-liberation inflation and resistance from colonial landowners kept the rural areas impoverished.

Various national founding movements also occurred in the Christian community after liberation. In early September 1945, the Christian Social Democratic Party was formed in North Korea, centered around pastors Yoon Ha-young and Han Kyung-chik. The party was less a modern socialist party with socialist ideology and more aimed at establishing a democratic government and social reform based on Christian principles. However, its activities were hindered when the Soviet army imposed martial law and arrested its members.

In South Korea, Pastor Kim Jae-joon founded the Sunlin Brotherhood. The Brotherhood, mainly composed of Christian students from Eunjin Middle School in Yongjeong, Gando, where Kim Jae-joon taught, aimed at mission, medical, educational, nurturing, rural service, and democratic activism based on Christian faith. Kim Jae-joon proposed Christian national founding principles in his lecture ‚ÄúChristian Ideals for Nation Building: The Ultimate Ideal and Reality of State Formation‚Äù, advocating for a government that ensures freedom of faith, worship, press, assembly, publication, and individual conscience. His vision for nation-building was a Christian social democracy, neither capitalist nor communist, overcoming their extremes.

Meanwhile, Bae Min-soo led an anti-communist national founding movement. He considered the post-World War II era as a new phase of conflict between democracy and communism. To him, Christianity was the strongest force and bastion defending democracy against communism. The ideological confrontation in national construction was simplified by the right-wing camp into a struggle between democracy and communism, and freedom versus dictatorship. The Christian camp strongly supported the right-wing‚Äôs national construction ideology and logic by equating the conflict between democracy and communism with that between Christianity and communism.

Yoo Jae-gi‚Äôs nation-building movement was closely related to Pastor Kim Jae-joon‚Äôs movement. Politically influenced by the Christian social democratic nation-building theory, he carried out his movement through the Jesus Village movement, economically. In 1946, he organized the Heungguk Brotherhood with previously mentioned Ham Tae-young. They designated rural cultural movement areas called Jesus Villages, dispatched evangelists, and established rural gospel schools to train rural leaders. As a result, the Rural Gospel School produced 448 graduates and thousands of auditors as Jesus Village Crusaders over 14 sessions. Additionally, they achieved the formation of about 300 cooperatives centered around village leaders from the Rural Gospel School.

  1. Kim, Jae-joon ÍπÄÏû¨Ï§Ä ÈáëÂú®‰øä

  1. Birth and Death: 1901/09/26 (Hamgyeongbuk-do, Gyeongheung) ~ 1987/01/27 (Seoul)

  2. Education: He grew up learning the Four Books and Three Classics of Confucianism and Eastern classics from his Confucian scholar father and gained a reputation as a writer. In 1920, leaving behind a newly married family and a stable income from a financial cooperative secretary position, he moved to Seoul. In Seoul, he attended liberal arts, English, and current affairs courses at the YMCA. He also frequented various libraries in Seoul, where he delved into modern books and was introduced to Christianity.

In 1921, he was deeply moved and decided to convert to Christianity after attending a revival meeting held by Pastor Kim Ik-du at Seungdong Church in Seoul. Three years later, in 1924, he was baptized by Pastor Kim Young-goo and studied theology at Aoyama Theological School in Tokyo from 1926 to 1928, and then at Princeton and Westminster Theological Seminary in the United States from 1928 to 1932. After being ordained by the East Manchuria Presbytery in 1937, he served as a pastor. In 1940, due to the demands for Shinto shrine worship by the Governor-General of Korea, which led missionaries to voluntarily close the Pyeongyang Theological Seminary (now, Presbyterian University and Theological Seminary, Chongshin University), he founded the Chosun Theological School (now, Hanshin University) in Seoul. He also contributed to the formation of the Presbyterian Church in the Republic of Korea, a progressive denomination in South Korea, and founded the Kyungdong Church, one of the representative churches of the Presbyterian Church of Korea. However, conservative pastors famous in the church community for their doctrinal and political conservatism, such as Kim Jang-Hwan and Han Kyung-Chik, attacked Kim Jae-joon as a heretic.

  1. Contribution: The founding principles of New Country, based on Christian spirit, were advocated by Kim, Jae-joon in the post-liberation era. His ideological movement at this time contrasts with the real political participation of many Protestants. Immediately after liberation, the Korean church became assimilated into the ‚ÄòChristian nation-building‚Äô under the banner of Syngman Rhee, Kim Kyu-sik, and Kim Gu, thereby becoming a strong support base for right-wing politics and the establishment of a liberal democratic system. In contrast, Kim Jae-joon‚Äôs non-political ideological movement relativized the political ideologies and theories of the time as ‚Äòproducts of the era‚Äô and in this extension, argued for a ‚ÄòChristian nation-building‚Äô based on religious and moral foundations. Jae-joon‚Äôs Christian nation-building theory, based on religious and moral foundations, is also in stark contrast to Han Kyung-chik‚Äôs nation-building theory which was grounded on the notion that democracy flourishes only in the field of Christian culture and was focused on the confrontation with communism and the victory of ‚Äòdemocracy (American-style liberal democracy).‚Äô

On August 19, 1945, at a meeting of the Chosun Theological School, Kim Jae-joon lectured on ‚ÄúChristian Ideals for Nation Building.‚Äù He viewed the highest ideal of nation-building as the realization of God‚Äôs kingdom on Earth. God‚Äôs kingdom is not heaven in the afterlife, but ‚Äúcomes when God‚Äôs will descends into all aspects of human life, and the influence of the Holy Spirit dominates every part of life.‚Äù Moreover, God‚Äôs kingdom is not limited to a specific individual but comes to all humanity and the entire universe. Thus, in God‚Äôs kingdom, the people of the world are ‚Äúorganically‚Äù united.

Kim Jae-joon proposed a concrete sequence for forming the new nation. First, a political committee should be formed, followed by drafting a constitution for democratic politics, and then convening a broad national assembly to decide and enact it, thereby establishing a legislative body. Next, the head of government (president?) should be elected by the people‚Äôs vote. Afterward, the elected head of government would form a cabinet, completing the establishment of the government. However, he saw this journey of representative democratic nation-building as very new and unfamiliar in Korean history, and considering the low level of public awareness, it was a path that had to be tread carefully step by step.

As a Reformed Church theologian, Kim Jae-joon recognized the land as ‚ÄúGod‚Äôs garden and property.‚Äù To develop this land, the authorities established by God should be ‚Äúshepherds‚Äù caring for God‚Äôs flock. He argued that politics should aim to create a society of shared joy among the people, not policies benefiting a privileged class or minority but those for all citizens.

Regarding the purge of pro-Japanese collaborators, he proposed a general amnesty, arguing that their hearts supported the independence movement, but they were forced into pro-Japanese actions due to external pressure. Thus, Kim Jae-joon claimed that nation-building, with everyone participating ‚Äì even those with a pro-Japanese past who were forgiven ‚Äì should be determined by the will of the people. Decisions about the state system, constitution, and selection methods for government leaders should all follow the will of the people. For this, freedoms (press, assembly, thought, publication, etc.) must be guaranteed, opportunities must be equally available to all citizens, and after discussion and debate, the majority opinion must be followed. Harmony and cooperation between the whole nation and individual citizens should be achieved. Through this, a government of the people, by the people, and for the people, as stated by U.S. President Lincoln, will be formed. Finally, Kim Jae-joon once again hoped for the construction of a society where every citizen is respected as an individual and the sanctity of life is guaranteed.

To explain the difference with Han Kyung-chik, Kim Jae-joon viewed the kingdom of God ‚Äòon earth as it is in heaven‚Äô as the ideal for nation-building. He hoped that God would establish stewards (‚Äúofficials‚Äù) and through them fulfill His will. God‚Äôs stewards, from Kim Jae-joon‚Äôs perspective, should be shepherds of shared joy. These shepherds should execute public authority according to the power given by God, and the law of this authority lies in realizing justice.

  1. Han, Kyung-chik ÌïúÍ≤ΩÏßÅ ÈüìÊôØËÅ∑

  1. Birth and Death: 1902/12/29 (Pyongannam-do, Pyongwon-gun) ~ 2000/04/19 (Seoul)

  2. Education: He enrolled in Jin Kwang Elementary School, established by missionary Samuel Moffett (Korean name: Mapo Samyeol), and graduated in 1916 when he married his spouse Kim Chan-bin. After graduating from Osan School, he majored in natural sciences at Soongsil University, where he also received teachings from Lee Seung-hoon and Cho Man-sik. This connection later led him to serve as the president of the Cho Man-sik Memorial Business Association.

Upon graduating from Soongsil University, he went to the United States and studied humanities at Emporia College and theology at Princeton Theological Seminary. However, he was diagnosed with tuberculosis upon graduating from Princeton Theological Seminary and underwent treatment for two and a half years. Later, he was invited by Cho Man-sik to teach English and Bible studies at Pyongyang Sungin Commercial School and concurrently lectured at Soongsil University. In 1933, he was called to serve as the minister of Sinuiju Second Church and received his ordination from the Uisun Presbytery in 1934. However, in 1942, the Japanese authorities forcibly removed him from his position as the chair of the church council.

  1. Contribution: After liberation, Han Kyung-Chik believed that unlike during the Japanese colonial period, Christianity could now be freely propagated. He perceived that while politics should not interfere with religion, religion could have a significant impact on politics.

After resigning from Sinuiju Second Church, he served as the director of Borinwon, an orphanage, and organized a self-governing committee to maintain public order at the request of the Japanese governor of North Pyongan Province, before the arrival of Soviet troops. The committee was chaired by Lee Yoo-phil, who had extensive experience in the independence movement and was in Shanghai, with Yoon Ha-young and Han Kyung-chik as vice-chairs. At that time, there were rumors about the Soviet army entering North Korea as it was known that the U.S. army would be stationed in South Korea. Since Sinuiju was the provincial capital of North Pyongan and a region strong in Christianity, pastors were active as local leaders. However, after the Soviet army entered Sinuiju and organized the Communist Party, Han Kyung-chik and others formed the Social Democratic Party in response. When the Soviet army hindered the activities of the Social Democratic Party, and it became difficult to continue activities in North Korea, Yoon Ha-young and Han Kyung-chik came down to South Korea in 1945. There, he taught students at Chosun Sikha School alongside Kim Jae-joon, previously mentioned, and also worked as an interpreter for the U.S. military.

He focused on two ministries: helping people who came down from the North to adapt to life in South Korea, and striving to establish the nation-building of South Korea on a different ideological foundation than North Korea. He acquired the former headquarters and dormitory of Tenrikyo, a Japanese religious organization in Seoul‚Äôs Jung-gu district, and established Bethany Church, helping about 20 people who had come from the North. The church later changed its name to Youngnak Church, becoming a gathering place for people from the North. By the end of 1946, there were 962 adults and 476 youths, totaling 1,438 members. He also established an orphanage and was listed as the board chairman of numerous school corporations related to people who came down from the North, including Youngnak High School, Daekwang High School, Soongsil Middle and High School, Sungui Girls‚Äô Middle and High School, Boseong Girls‚Äô Middle and High School, and Soongsil University. His sermons emphasized living as a ‚Äòsojourner‚Äô, trying to find the identity of those who came down from the North within Christianity.

He advocated for the construction of a democratic nation. Han Kyung-chik saw the social disorder and political chaos of the time as a testbed for our nation to test its ability to become free citizens after gaining independence on August 15th. He observed that after liberation, society faced significant trials amid socio-political instability and confusion, failing to respect laws, disregard police, evade taxes, and neglect civic duties, having been suddenly thrust into freedom after a period of oppressive Japanese colonial rule. By the end of 1946, his sermons drew a parallel between liberation and the biblical exodus, viewing the Korean people as living in a wilderness-like state.

Han Kyung-chik, in a sermon titled ‚ÄúChristianity and Politics,‚Äù proclaimed that the church must actively participate in the construction of the new nation. Historically, the relationship between church and state can be seen in the Roman Empire church after Emperor Constantine, where the state dominated the church in a state church system, and in modern Germany‚Äôs Protestant church. Conversely, in the United States, there is a separation of church and state. Since Christianity in Korea originated from the United States, a church system based on separation of church and state was established. However, Han Kyung-chik emphasized that while Christians are members of the church, they are also citizens of the state and of the kingdom of heaven, and therefore should participate in secular politics. He saw Korea‚Äôs (South Korea‚Äôs) biggest task in 1946 as establishing a ‚Äúfully independent state,‚Äù which meant political freedom. This longing for political freedom was born from the harsh experiences under Japanese colonial rule, where he realized that without political freedom, no other freedom, including religious freedom, can be guaranteed. He recalled the Korean Presbyterian Church‚Äôs submission to the Japanese-imposed Shinto shrine worship, noting that political freedom is necessary for religious freedom.

He considered a ‚Äúmaterialist dictatorship state‚Äù as a form of government that directly confronts Christian political ideals and should be avoided. He witnessed and suffered from the internal conflicts caused by ideological conflicts in South Korean society at the time. In his sermon, he stated: ‚ÄúWe must face the reality of Korea. Everything is in a state of chaos now. The fundamental reason is the confusion of ideas. Whether it‚Äôs theism or atheism, democracy or dictatorship, Christian socialism or communism, construction or destruction, civilization or barbarism, young students, workers, farmers, small citizens, and the general public are wavering. Which is the true path for us? In this reality, our believers must engage in a thorough ideological church movement.‚Äù Han Kyung-chik criticized atheistic materialism of leftist ideology in at least four sermons in 1946 and 1947. He recognized that while leftist ideology, advocating for the economic equality and class liberation of workers and peasants, could align with Christian faith on the surface, the methods and means used by ‚Äúorthodox (real) communism‚Äù were ‚Äúdirectly contrary to Christian thought.‚Äù

Furthermore, Han Kyung-chik, proposing Christian socialism or social democracy in response to materialistic communism, emphasized that the new nation to be built in Korea (South Korea) must be a ‚Äúdemocratic state.‚Äù The spiritual foundation of this democratic state should be Christianity, standing against Communist dictatorship and totalitarianism. He proclaimed this in December 1947 during the second anniversary sermon at Youngnak Church.

Differing from Kim Jae-joon, Han Kyung-chik‚Äôs construction of a democratic nation did not involve the kingdom of God. Instead, he argued that the church should lay the spiritual foundation for nation-building. Commonalities in their nation-building ideals include the realization of God‚Äôs sovereignty in justice in the world, filled with selfishness and greed since Adam‚Äôs fall. Therefore, the stewards (government and officials) appointed by God must execute public authority to punish evil-doing humans. This execution of justice is primarily to punish human wrongdoing, ensuring the freedom and equality bestowed by God on all humans. Governments (stewards) must prevent and punish human crimes.

Im Hui-gook (2017) explains that their views are almost identical to the social ethics of Ulrich Zwingli, a 16th-century Swiss Reformation leader and first-generation Reformed Church reformer. Zwingli, too, based his understanding of human nature on God‚Äôs justice. However, while Zwingli closely collaborated with the Zurich council for reform, Kim Jae-joon and Han Kyung-chik participated in real politics through sermons and lectures, based on the American principle of separation of church and state. Particularly, Han Kyung-chik, drawing parallels with the Israelites‚Äô preparation for nation-building at Mount Sinai after the Exodus, proclaimed from the pulpit that the church must lay the spiritual foundation for nation-building in the current reality of not yet having achieved a fully independent state after the liberation on August 15th. Following this, in 1948, a democratic state with a constitution, as suggested by Kim Jae-joon and Han Kyung-chik, was established with the founding of the government of the Republic of Korea. The founding followed the steps outlined by Kim Jae-joon: drafting the constitution, forming the National Assembly, electing the highest leader of the government (President), and forming the cabinet.
