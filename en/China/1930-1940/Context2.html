China: Context II (1930-1940)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

1)Summary of regulations on religion in this decade (500-1,000 words for each regulation).

The targets of religious control in the Japanese occupation areas in China were quite complex, as they involved Japanese religious organizations advancing from Japan, foreign religious organizations from Western countries, and local Chinese religious organizations. While these were distinguished, they were also interrelated. Moreover, the religious policies under the military occupation were often not strict laws but military orders or guidelines.

As the occupation progressed, puppet governments were established in the occupied areas, eventually leading to the creation of the Wang Jingwei regime. Although religious laws from the Republic of China era were maintained, the actual control was in the hands of the Japanese military, making the Wang Jingwei regime‚Äôs religious regulations largely symbolic. This part will explore the various policies and regulations that defined Japanese military religious control.

First, the management and control of Japanese religious organizations advancing into China were important tasks for the Japanese military. In North China, the Special Affairs Department of the Japanese North China Area Army struggled with controlling the expansion of various Japanese religious groups. The military‚Äôs intention was not to promote missionary activities by these organizations but to use their charity and social work for pacification purposes. Consequently, the North China Area Army requested that the Ministry of Education, which is responsible for religious administration in Japan, control the expansion of religious groups. The Ministry responded by issuing ‚ÄúThe Directive Concerning Religious Activities in China (ÂØæÊîØÂ∏ÉÊïô„Å´Èñ¢„Åô„Çã‰ª∂)‚Äù(Document 1) , stipulating that any expansion required the ‚Äúpermission‚Äù and the ‚Äúapproval‚Äù of the military. Although this directive was formally issued by the Ministry of Education, it was effectively drafted under the military‚Äôs leadership.

Second, the management and control of Western religious organizations, particularly Christian groups and missionaries, became crucial. Following the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War in 1937, Japanese air raids and looting caused significant damage to missionary buildings and assets, leading to frequent protests against Japan‚Äôs Ministry of Foreign Affairs, especially through the U.S. Embassy. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs was particularly concerned with mitigating the deterioration of Western public opinion towards Japan due to bombings of Christian-related facilities and, in cooperation with the Army, planned and implemented measures to influence missionaries to correct the negative opinions about Japan.

Third, the control and management of local Chinese religious organizations were also essential. In particular, the Japanese military sought to promote negotiations and cooperation with Chinese Buddhist forces, aiming to mobilize them for pacification and security efforts. However, in the case of Chinese Christian forces, the presence of Western missionaries was a hindrance, preventing the Japanese military from directly intervening in Chinese Christianity before the Pacific War. Instead, they had to first deal with the Western missionaries.

The first religious guideline, ‚ÄúGuidelines for Religious Organizations in North China,‚Äù is divided into three sections: ‚ÄúGuiding Principles,‚Äù ‚ÄúGuiding Methods,‚Äù and ‚ÄúImplementation Methods.‚Äù Each section outlines the response strategies for Chinese, Japanese, and third-country religious organizations. Purpose:

To deploy missionaries for the pacification of the local populace and to contribute to cultural operations directed toward China.

Method:

To let missionaries select law-abiding residents, providing them with special protection under military‚Äôs permission, and to utilize them in maintaining local security, labor, and pacification efforts.

In view of the local circumstances, for the time being, religious proselytism shall be secondary.

First: Guiding Principles

The primary objective is to permit the freedom of religious propagation as long as it does not obstruct the construction of the new East Asian order. Efforts shall be made to implement self-renewal and control measures, thereby ensuring that religious activities contribute fundamentally to the establishment of the new East Asian order.

Second: Guiding Methods

  1. The focus of guiding religious organizations in North China primarily lies in constructive work under the influence of Japanese Buddhism, which will serve as the guiding spirit for Chinese Buddhism. Secondarily, edification work should be placed under the influence of third-country Protestantism and Catholicism.

  1. The guidance for third-country religious organizations should focus on their central governing bodies. The leadership must be made to adapt to the new situation in East Asia. Furthermore, measures should be taken to ensure that pastors affiliated with these organizations correctly understand their mission and the inevitable trends of world historical development. The objective is to normalize their activities and, as much as possible, to invite Japanese pastors, thereby encouraging the mass of believers to cooperate in the construction of the new East Asian order.

  2. Should any religious organization deliberately aid the enemy or obstruct the construction of the new East Asian order, decisive measures will be taken. However, in dealing with third-country religious organizations, careful consideration should be given to international relations.

First: Guiding Principles

The primary objective is to permit the freedom of religious propagation as long as it does not obstruct the construction of the new East Asian order. Additionally, efforts shall be made to implement self-renewal and control measures, ensuring that these religious activities become a permanent foundation for the spirit of the new East Asian order.

Second: Guiding Methods

The guidance for third-country religious organizations should focus on their central governing bodies. The leadership must be made to adapt to the new situation in East Asia. Furthermore, measures should be taken to ensure that all pastors and evangelists within these organizations correctly understand the current situation, normalize their activities, and, as much as possible, encourage the involvement of Japanese pastors. This will promote the cooperation of believers and members in the construction of the new East Asian order.

If any third-country religious organization deliberately aids the enemy or obstructs the construction of the new East Asian order, the reasons must be clearly established, and decisive measures will be taken. However, in implementing such measures, careful consideration should be given to international relations.

Third: Implementation Methods

The guidance of Christianity shall be implemented as follows:

‚Ä¶

To normalize the Christian churches‚Äô recognition of the current situation, efforts will be made to strengthen the ties with Japanese pastors in a subtle yet effective manner. Communication with Japanese pastors should be intensified, aiming for harmonious coordination through mutual exchange of preaching and, progressively, the appointment of permanent pastors.

First: Policy

The policy for enemy-affiliated churches is to eliminate the influence of British and American elements, and eradicate their ecclesiastical authority from North China, gradually transfer it to the Chinese side. To achieve this, efforts will be made to nurture and guide Christian doctrines and faith, without suppressing them, in order to stabilize public sentiment and support anti-communism.

Second: Guiding Methods

  1. Enemy-affiliated Protestant churches and organizations (such as the YMCA) will be eradicated their ecclesiastical authority and unified into a single entity, limited to those that cooperate with our side, and form the ‚ÄúChinese United Church of Christ in North China‚Äù (provisional name) to establish an independent Chinese Christianity. Churches and organizations that refuse to cooperate with our side will be suppressed and closed.

  2. In consideration of international relations, particularly with the Vatican, enemy-affiliated and third-party affiliated Catholic churches (Roman Catholicism) will not be interfered with, as long as they are not hostile.

  3. As for enemy-affiliated missionaries, they will not be suppressed as long as they are not hostile. However, they will be guided to cease voluntary missionary work, and efforts will be made to ensure that they voluntarily transfer their church properties and other assets to the Chinese side.

In the North China region under Japanese occupation, although no cases were recorded of individuals being arrested or imprisoned for resisting Japan‚Äôs religious policies, there was an instance that came close to such a situation. This involved Wang Mingdao (1900-1991), a preacher from Beijing‚Äôs independent church, the Christian Tabernacle, who resisted the Japanese religious policy of church unification, which sought to sever ties with Western missionary organizations and dissolve denominational distinctions. (Matsutani,Y≈çsuke :2020, 164-167)

Wang Mingdao, an independent preacher who established the Christian Tabernacle in the 1920s without affiliating with any denomination, edited and published a personal magazine, Spiritual Food Quarterly, for 30 years. He later became one of the most famous figures in Chinese Christian history due to his refusal to join the Three-Self Patriotic Movement (Three-Self Church) after the founding of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, which led to his long-term imprisonment.

Wang also resisted pressure during the Japanese occupation to join the Chinese United Church of Christ in North China (CUCCNC). During the period of establishing the NCCUC, Japanese authorities, through officers of the North China Liaison Department of the Board of Asia Development, Chinese secretaries of the YMCA who belonged to the North China Christian Union Promotion Association (ËèØÂåóÂü∫Áù£ÊïôÈÄ£Âêà‰øÉÈÄ≤‰ºö), and later a Japanese pastor who became an advisor to the united church, sought to persuade Wang to comply.

Wang Mingdao struggled with whether to join the association and the united church, recalling after the war:

It was against God‚Äôs will to participate in this Babylonian-style ‚ÄòNorth China Christian Union Promotion Association,‚Äô but not joining would certainly invite Japanese interference, leading to the closure of the church, and I personally would not escape danger. ‚Ä¶ If the pastor is arrested, the flock will scatter. ‚Ä¶ To avoid these tragic consequences, I once thought that surrendering and joining the ‚ÄòNorth China Christian Union Promotion Association‚Äô might be the only option. (Wang:1945, 15-16)

From this, it is clear that Wang feared both personal arrest and harm to his church and congregation. However, he ultimately solidified his resolve to refuse membership for the following reasons:

I have always insisted that the church must not merge with secular powers. ‚Ä¶God‚Äôs servants must not submit to unbelievers‚Ä¶faithful churches must not unite with churches controlled by unbelievers. ‚Ä¶ and true workers of God cannot cooperate with false prophets and false teachers. ‚Ä¶If I were to join the ‚ÄúNorth China Christian Union Promotion Association,‚Äù I would be overturning the testimony of faith I had preached for nearly 20 years across the country. If that happened, how many believers would stumble because of me, and how greatly would God‚Äôs name be dishonored because of me? I could not do such a thing, nor could I bear it. I do not have the courage to betray the Lord I have served for over 20 years, nor will I become a disciple of Judas. (Wang:1945, 16)

The Guidelines for the Reorganization and Management of Enemy-affiliated Churches in North China, formulated by the North China Area Army Headquarters in February 1942, stipulated in its ‚ÄúGuiding Methods‚Äù that ‚Äúchurches and organizations that refuse to cooperate with our side will be suppressed and closed.‚Äù Furthermore, the ‚ÄúImplementation Methods‚Äù stated that churches and organizations that did not join the NCCUC would be considered hostile and would be suppressed and closed. Thus, by refusing to join the united church, Wang Mingdao was prepared for the possibility of arrest and the closure of his church.

However, in the end, Wang avoided both arrest and the closure of his church until the end of the war. This may have been due to the policy outlined in the Guidelines, which stated, ‚ÄúAs for third-party affiliated and existing self-supporting Chinese churches, efforts will be made to incorporate them as members of this association.‚Äù Here, ‚Äúthird-party affiliated‚Äù churches referred to churches from neutral countries like Sweden and Switzerland, or from Japan‚Äôs allies such as Germany and Italy, distinguishing them from enemy-affiliated churches like those from the U.S. and Britain. The term ‚Äúself-supporting Chinese churches‚Äù referred to churches like Wang Mingdao‚Äôs Christian Tabernacle, which were not affiliated with Western missionary organizations.

Since these churches were not classified as enemy-affiliated, Japanese authorities likely attempted to persuade self-supporting churches to join the union, but had no legitimate reason to arrest individuals or close churches if they refused. Moreover, the Japanese military was more concerned with organizations connected to enemy nations, such as the U.S. and Britain, rather than Christianity itself. In that sense, the self-supporting churches, lacking such affiliations, were probably deemed harmless and thus left alone.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

In 1873, with the lifting of the prohibition on Christianity, overt political persecution against the Christiniaty ceased. In 1912, with the government‚Äôs recognition of Christianity during the ‚ÄúThree Religions Conference‚Äù (San-ky≈ç Kaid≈ç), which brought together representatives from Sect Shint≈ç, Buddhism, and Christianity, Christianity received a form of official endorsement. (Isomae:2014, 136) However, in society, perceptions of Christianity as a heretical or foreign religion persisted strongly. From the 1920s to the 1930s, incidents such as the Mino Mission Incident‚Äîwhere children from the independent denomination Mino Mission refused to participate in local Shint≈ç shrine visits‚Äîand the refusal of Christian students from Sophia University to visit Yasukuni Shrine led to increased public criticism of Christianity, particularly in the media.

In response to this situation, the Christian community engaged in various debates at first. However, with the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War in 1937 and the worsening relations between Japan and Western nations, suspicion arose regarding Christianity‚Äôs foreign connections. The Salvation Army, headquartered in London, became a particular target, with several of its senior members being arrested on suspicion of espionage in 1940. Although no conclusive evidence was found, and they were eventually released, this incident heightened public awareness and suspicion of Christianity as a foreign influence.

Another notable event during this period was when Tadao Yanaihara, a professor at the University of Tokyo and a Christian belonging to the Non-Church Movement, wrote a critical essay titled ‚ÄúThe Ideal of the Nation‚Äù in Ch≈´≈ç K≈çron magazine, denouncing the war. The essay was quickly banned, and Yanaihara faced severe criticism from within the university, ultimately being forced to resign. (Matsutani,Y≈çsuke :2020, 362-383, Ogawara:2014, 145-146) After this, public criticisms of the war were no longer heard from Christian circles..

In light of the above, Christianity in Japanese society was increasingly seen as unpatriotic or even unnationalistic due to its refusal to participate in Shint≈ç shrine visits, its foreign connections, and its perceived lack of cooperation with the war effort. To counter this criticism and integrate Christianity into Japanese society, Christian churches began to actively engage in shrine visits, emphasize the ‚ÄúJapanese-ness‚Äù of Christianity, and support the war effort.

First, regarding shrine visits, a prominent example is Mitsuru Tomita, a pastor of the Church of Christ in Japan (Nihon Kirisuto Ky≈çkai), chairman of the National Christian Council in Japan (NCCJ), and later president of the United Church of Christ in Japan (UCCJ). In 1938, Tomita addressed pastors in colonial Korea, asserting that Shint≈çism was merely a national ceremony that citizens should participate in, and that it did not require the abandonment of Christianity. In January 1942, after the establishment of the UCCJ Tomita visited the Ise Shrine to report the founding of the UCCJ. (Ogawara:2014,174)

Second, in response to criticism of Christianity as a foreign religion, arguments emphasizing the Japanese-ness of Christianity became prominent. This movement, known as ‚ÄúJapanized Christianity‚Äù (Nihonteki Kirisuto-ky≈ç), involved various pastors, theologians, and lay leaders, each with differing points of emphasis. However, they shared a common discourse: Japan was a unique nation centered on the Emperor, and Christianity should contribute to the development of the Imperial State. Another argument posited that Japan‚Äôs traditions and culture were superior to those of the West, and that by ‚ÄúJapanizing‚Äù Christianity, it could be purified from the distorted forms it had taken in the West. While these claims were intended to indigenize Christianity and fend off criticism of it as a foreign religion, the result was a syncretic distortion of Christianity, moving it far from its original essence. This is evidenced by the fact that many advocates of ‚ÄúJapanized Christianity‚Äù ceased promoting such views after the war. (Yamaguchi:2016, 11-37)

Third, in terms of support for the war, in July 1937, the NCCJ issued a statement immediately after the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War, expressing support for the government‚Äôs war efforts. They later collaborated in various activities such as organizing morale-boosting visits to military personnel and assisting with relief efforts. After the outbreak of the Pacific War, Christians further cooperated by raising funds for military aircraft, collecting iron and copper, and supporting religious outreach efforts on the southern front. (Ogawara:2014, 139-144, 165-180)

The church union movement in Japan, which later influenced the church union in China, can also be said to have been fundamentally driven by these three elements.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

3.Geopolitical dynamics and International Relationships

1)Summarize diplomatic relationships with its neighboring Asian-Pacific countries, such as a war, colonization, territorial disputes, military alliance, economic coalition, etc. No need to describe relationships with all neighboring countries, only those that are pertinent to Christianity.

After the Treaty of Shimonoseki that ended the First Sino-Japanese War in 1895, Japan annexed Taiwan as a colony and established the Governor-General‚Äôs Office, ruling the island for approximately fifty years. Initially, resistance movements against Japanese rule were observed, but the Japanese authorities gradually stabilized public security, developed infrastructure, and promoted Japanese language education as part of an intensified assimilation policy. Notably, from the 1930s onwards, Japan strengthened its ‚ÄúImperialization Policy,‚Äù banning the use of Taiwanese and indigenous languages, promoting cultural policies, and enforcing religious practices such as shrine visits to further the Japanese cultural agenda. (Komagome:2015)

Regarding Christianity, Japanese churches, particularly Protestant congregations, were established for the increasing number of Japanese immigrants, resulting in the construction of approximately forty Japanese churches during this period. Additionally, missionary work among indigenous peoples was attempted. Western missionaries from the Canadian Presbyterian Church and the English Presbyterian Church, for instance, were allowed to continue their work in Taiwan under Japanese rule and the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan could maintain its independence. Moreover, there was an increase in Taiwanese seminarians and pastors who studied in Japan. (Kain≈ç:1984, 34-54)

However, with the strengthening of the Imperialization Policy in the 1930s, compulsory shrine visits were imposed on Christians, placing significant pressure on Christian schools, particularly those operated by the Presbyterian Church. In response, the Presbyterian Church sought affiliation with the National Christian Council in Japan (NCCJ) and considered merging with the Church of Christ in Japan, both of which shared Presbyterian traditions; these efforts were unsuccessful. After the establishment of the United Church of Christ in Japan in 1941, the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan expressed a desire to join, but this was not realized. Instead, in 1944, the Taiwan United Church of Christ in Japan (TUCCJ, Êó•Êú¨Âü∫Áù£ÊïôÂè∞ÊπæÊïôÂõ£, Nihon Kirisutoky≈ç Taiwan Ky≈çdan) was formed through a merger of the local Japanese churches and the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan , which was later dissolved in October 1945, following Japan‚Äôs surrender. (Kain≈ç:1984, 85-109)

During this period, Taiwan was treated as an extension of Japan‚Äôs territory under colonial rule, but it was legally and administratively distinct from the Japanese mainland, with its governance falling under the Governor-General‚Äôs Office. Consequently, laws such as the Religious Organizations Act were not directly applied in Taiwan, preventing the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan from joining the UCCJ. This distinction led to the formation of a separate entity, the TUCCJ.

In 1910, Japan annexed Korea through the Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty, and the Korean Peninsula was under the control of the Governor-General‚Äôs Office for approximately thirty-five years. During the early years of rule, Japan enforced a strict military regime, suppressing independence movements and limiting cultural and press freedoms. The March 1st Movement in 1919 emerged as a significant resistance movement, which was ultimately suppressed. Subsequently, Japan transitioned from militaristic governance to cultural governance, though Japanese language education was strengthened, and Korean culture was suppressed. Japan viewed Korea as an essential industrial base and developed infrastructure, including railroads and mines. From the late 1930s, Japan intensified its Imperialization Policy, mandating the use of Japanese, enforcing shrine worship, and compelling Koreans to adopt Japanese names. (Komagome:2006, 59-90, Caprio:2009, 111-170)

As in Taiwan, numerous Japanese churches were established throughout Korea, and foreign missionary activities were allowed to continue. However, due to the strict suppression of anti-Japanese movements, missionaries were generally hesitant to support Korean Christians who engaged in anti-Japanese activities. Therefore, despite the presence of Christians in the March 1st Movement, it cannot be concluded that Korean Christianity, as a whole, resisted Japanese imperialism. (Matsutani, Motokazu:2020)

During the late 1930s and early 1940s, discussions arose among various Christian denominations in Korea regarding unification, but differing stances among denominations hindered smooth progress. The Korea United Church of Christ in Japan (KUCCJ, Êó•Êú¨Âü∫Áù£ÊïôÊúùÈÆÆÊïôÂõ£, Nihon Kirisutoky≈ç Ch≈çsen Ky≈çdan) was eventually established in July 1945, shortly before the end of the war. As in Taiwan, Korea‚Äôs legal and administrative framework was distinct from that of the Japanese mainland, preventing the direct application of the Religious Organizations Act and resulting in the formation of a separate united church. Following Japan‚Äôs colonial withdrawal, the KUCCJ dissolved, and most of denominations were reestablished. (Suh:2009, 269-282)

Japan occupied northeastern China following the Manchurian Incident in 1931 and established the puppet state of Manchukuo in 1932, with Puyi, the last emperor of the Qing dynasty, as its nominal leader. However, real power lay with the Japanese Kwantung Army. Louise Young points out that Japan‚Äôs rule over Manchuria can be characterized by three key elements: military conquest, economic development, and mass migration. (Young:1998, 4) Regarding the mass migration, Japan sent waves of immigrants from its rural areas as part of the ‚ÄúManchurian-Mongolian Development Group‚Äù policy, resulting in approximately 270,000 settlers by 1945. Many Japanese businesses also invested in Manchuria, developing its abundant natural resources, such as iron ore and coal, and building railways, roads, and ports. Although Manchuria was not a colony, Japanese influence was imposed through Japanese language education and the construction of Shint≈ç shrines. Nonetheless, Japan promoted the ideal of ethnic harmony, exemplified by the establishment of the Kenkoku University (Âª∫ÂõΩÂ§ßÂ≠¶), where Manchurians, Mongolians, Japanese, Koreans, Chinese, and Russians studied together. (Yamane:2003, Miyazawa:1997)

Christianity had a presence in Manchuria prior to 1931 due to a substantial Japanese population, and Japanese churches increased after the occupation. While foreign missionaries were allowed to continue their work, mounting pressure in the late 1930s compelled Christian schools to participate in Confucian temple visits. Missionary societies struggled to respond. With the start of the Pacific War, the influence of foreign missionary organizations waned, leading to the establishment of the Manchurian Christian Church in 1942. (Watanaba:2019, 79-90)

After the Sino-Japanese War broke out in July 1937, the Japanese government, under Prime Minister Fumimaro Konoe, sought German Ambassador Oscar Trautmann‚Äôs mediation to negotiate peace with Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs Nationalist government. However, this effort failed, and in January 1938, Japan declared in the Konoe Statement that it would no longer negotiate with the Nationalist government. Subsequently, Japan initiated peace negotiations with Wang Jingwei, who desired peace with Japan. Japan established puppet regimes in Japanese-controlled North and Central China‚Äîthe Provisional Government of the Republic of China (1937‚Äì1940) and the Reformed Government of the Republic of China (1938‚Äì1940)‚Äîeventually merging them into the Nanjing National Government under Wang Jingwei in March 1940. (Kobayashi and Hayashi:2005, Jing:2013)

During this period, China experienced a dual government, with Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs Chongqing-based Nationalist government and Wang Jingwei‚Äôs Nanjing-based Nationalist government. Japan and its Axis allies, Germany and Italy, supported Wang‚Äôs government, while Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs government received backing from the Allied Powers, including the United States. Following the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War, some missionaries and Christian schools relocated to areas under Chiang‚Äôs control, such as Chongqing, though many remained in areas controlled by Japan until the Pacific War in 1941. Nationwide Christian denominations, such as the Church of Christ in China, were divided along territorial lines, and the Manchurian region had already been separated as early as 1932.

Between the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War and the start of the Pacific War, Japan and the United States had not yet entered into armed conflict, and the Japanese government sought to avoid further deterioration of relations with the U.S. Following the onset of the Sino-Japanese War, economic sanctions against Japan by member states of the League of Nations commenced, leading the U.S., which had previously refrained from interfering with Japan‚Äôs overseas expansion, to increase its pressure on Japan. This culminated in the U.S. decision to freeze Japanese assets and impose a comprehensive oil embargo after Japan‚Äôs occupation of French Indochina from 1940 to 1941. Within both the Japanese government and the military, there were varied debates regarding war with the U.S. Ultimately, however, the war began in December 1941 with the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor.

As for Christianity, in April 1941, a peace delegation from Japanese Protestant churches was dispatched to the U.S., where it held the Japanese-American Christian Conference with American Christians in Riverside, California. The two parties issued a joint statement promoting peace; however, this had no substantial influence in preventing the outbreak of war.

Before the Pacific War, American missionaries were permitted to remain in Japanese-occupied regions of China. As American missionary properties were increasingly damaged or looted due to bombings in Japanese-occupied areas, the U.S. Embassy in China lodged fierce protests with the Japanese government. The Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs, concerned that these complaints and reports of atrocities by the Japanese military were aggravating American public opinion against Japan, undertook measures to improve this perception by coordinating with the Japanese Army. After the Pacific War began, most American missionaries in Japan were forced to return to the U.S., and those under Japanese occupation were either repatriated to America or interned in concentration camps. (Matsutani,Y≈çsuke :2020, 45-58)

Japan, as a countermeasure to the Soviet Union, had already concluded the Anti-Comintern Pact with Germany in 1936, expanding it in the following year to include Italy. After the start of the Sino-Japanese War, Japan‚Äôs confrontations with Britain and the U.S. deepened due to their support for the Republic of China. Japan subsequently strengthened the Anti-Comintern Pact with Germany and Italy, establishing the Tripartite Pact in September 1940 to form the Axis Powers and check against British and American influence.

During the Pacific War, American and British missionaries, citizens of countries now enemy states of Japan, were either repatriated or interned, while German and Italian citizens, though monitored, were not subjected to such strict controls. German and Italian missionaries within Japanese-occupied Chinese territories were similarly not interned or forcibly repatriated.

In 1942, during the Pacific War, Japan established diplomatic relations with the Vatican, becoming the first Asian nation to open an embassy there. (Duan:2009, 222-230, Blet:1999,127-131) While research on diplomatic documents between Japan and the Vatican has only just begun and remains incomplete, it is known that Japan attempted to leverage the Vatican for negotiations regarding the end of the war. Given the alliance with Italy and Japan‚Äôs strengthening ties with the Vatican, Catholic churches and missionaries in Japanese-occupied areas, including American Catholics, were monitored but not subjected to forced repatriation or internment.

During the period from the Sino-Japanese War through the Pacific War, Switzerland, Sweden, and Ireland remained neutral. As such, citizens of these nations were permitted to stay within Japanese-occupied areas in China, much like German and Italian citizens from Japan‚Äôs allied nations. Concerning Christianity, even if a missionary organization or denomination had affiliations with the U.S., individual missionaries holding neutral citizenship were monitored but not forcibly repatriated or interned. (Matsutani, Yosuke: 2020, 226-227)

The establishment of State Shint≈ç, which effectively positioned Shint≈ç and the Emperor‚Äôs Shint≈ç as quasi-state religions, had been promoted since the Imperial Rescript on Education was issued in 1890. However, with the start of the Sino-Japanese War in 1937, patriotism and nationalism were further intensified under a nationwide mobilization framework. Anti-war reporting was heavily restricted, and media and publications increasingly aligned themselves with pro-war stances.

Once the Sino-Japanese War began, reports glorifying the heroism of Japanese soldiers became more frequent, and major victories, such as the fall of Nanjing, were celebrated with large public events. For example, in Tokyo, 400,000 people participated in a lantern parade to commemorate the conquest of Nanjing. Conversely, anti-war commentary was severely censored, as illustrated by the case of Tadao Yanaihara of Tokyo Imperial University, whose article titled ‚ÄúThe Ideal of the Nation,‚Äù which contained anti-war sentiments, was promptly banned.

Public rituals reflected the entrenched ideology that Shint≈ç was non-religious, as shrine visits and reverences toward the Imperial Palace became mandated and routine. Skya describes and discusses the Shint≈çism of that time as ‚ÄúRadical Shint≈ç Ultranationanlism.‚Äù (Skya: 2009, 229-296) Japan‚Äôs religious community was also integrated into this nationalism surge, supporting the war effort not only by contributing physical resources, such as metal donations but also by adapting their religious doctrines to praise Imperial Japan and the emperor. Japanese Christianity, aiming to avoid the criticism of being an unpatriotic foreign religion, gradually conformed to this nationalism by organizing donation drives for military aircraft and incorporating patriotic rituals before worship services. (Ogawara:2014, 105-206)
