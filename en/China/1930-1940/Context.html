Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

In July 1930, the Ministry of Education issued an order to the education departments and bureaus of all provinces and cities, strictly prohibiting church-run school libraries from displaying or promoting religious books and images, ‚Äúso as to prevent the numbing of the youth.‚Äù This directive was based on Official Letter No. 1468 from the Central Committee of the Kuomintang (KMT), which cited a report from the Propaganda Department of the Jiangsu Provincial Party Affairs Reorganization Committee. The report, submitted by the Wuxian County Party Affairs Reorganization Committee, stated that church-run school libraries in various areas were ‚Äúdisplaying and promoting religious books and periodicals, numbing the minds of young people.‚Äù It was requested that the matter be forwarded to the central authorities and that the National Government instruct the Ministry of Education to ‚Äústrictly prohibit such activities in order to prevent future harm.‚Äù

The ban stated that imperialism was using cultural propaganda as a tool for invasion, ‚Äúconfusing the minds of young people and implementing a poisonous plan to destroy the nation and extinguish the race.‚Äù As the Chinese people became aware of this malicious scheme, anti-imperialist cultural resistance movements emerged throughout the country. However, recently, church-run school libraries were ‚Äúagain being used as places for students to conduct academic research, while covertly displaying all kinds of religious books, periodicals, and images as a form of invisible propaganda. In contrast, books promoting party ideology were extremely scarce.‚Äù Such a situation, described as ‚Äúputting the cart before the horse,‚Äù was deemed ‚Äúa phenomenon wholly inappropriate in schools under party governance.‚Äù

Therefore, at its 24th meeting, the committee resolved to submit the matter to the central authorities for strict prohibition. Following this, all church-run schools were required to ‚Äúcompletely prohibit the display or hanging of any materials promoting religion. As for religious books, periodicals, and magazines, except in universities and senior high schools where they are directly related to elective courses or have philosophical reference value, they may be displayed in limited quantities. All others must be strictly prohibited.‚Äù

In February 1931, the Kuomintang (KMT) Central Department of Mass Training submitted a proposal to the Central Executive Committee, suggesting measures for guiding Christian organizations. The Central Training Department pointed out that Christian groups (such as churches and YMCAs) were established throughout the country and that ‚Äúin the past, they often used religious propaganda as a form of cultural invasion, causing significant harm. Although the KMT recognizes freedom of religion, Christian organizations should be given appropriate guidance by the party and subjected to strict government oversight, so as to use them to promote the party‚Äôs doctrines, correct their errors, and guard against cultural imperialism.‚Äù To that end, a set of guidance regulations was drafted:

All Christian organizations established within China‚Äîsuch as churches and YMCAs‚Äîshall be guided in accordance with these regulations.

All local Christian organizations must be guided by the party and supervised by the government.

Christian organizations are prohibited from spreading propaganda that violates the Three Principles of the People or from engaging in profit-making activities.

Within four months of the promulgation of these regulations, each Christian organization must submit its charter and staff resumes to the local senior party office for registration. If there are branches, these must also be registered.

Only after registration with the party may Christian organizations apply to the local government for formal approval. Branches must also be filed.

Except for regular meetings, any large gatherings held by Christian organizations must be reported in advance to the local senior party office, which may dispatch personnel to guide the event. Notification must also be sent to the relevant government office, which may send supervisors.

Any schools, hospitals, or other institutions run by Christian organizations must comply with the relevant laws and regulations.

Local senior party offices may investigate the activities and operations of Christian organizations when necessary.

If any Christian organization violates these regulations, the government shall take legal action against it.

These regulations shall take effect upon resolution by the Central Executive Committee of the Kuomintang.

In June 1931, Chen Lifu, Secretary General of the Secretariat of the KMT Central Executive Committee, wrote to the Central Department of Mass Training, stating that the 131st regular meeting of the Central Committee had reviewed and amended the regulations. The revised version was renamed ‚ÄúRegulations for Guiding Foreign Missionary Organizations‚Äù, and included the following changes:

All missionary organizations established by foreigners in China, such as churches, shall be guided in accordance with these regulations.

All local foreign missionary organizations must be guided by the party and supervised by the government.

They are prohibited from promoting propaganda that violates the Three Principles of the People.

The headquarters of each foreign missionary organization must, within four months of the promulgation of these regulations, submit its charter and staff resumes, along with information about its local branches, to the central party office for registration.

After registering with the central party office, foreign missionary organizations must then report to the local government for filing.

Aside from regular meetings, foreign missionary organizations must allow local senior party officials to attend any large gatherings.

If foreign missionary organizations operate schools, hospitals, or other institutions, they must comply with the relevant laws governing those activities.

Violations of these regulations by foreign missionary organizations will be dealt with legally by the government.

These regulations shall take effect upon resolution by the Central Executive Committee of the Kuomintang.

  2. Provisional Constitution of the Period of Political Tutelage of the Republic of China, 1931 (Zhang Su, 1933: 197‚Äì198)

The Provisional Constitution of the Period of Political Tutelage of the Republic of China was the highest legal code enacted during the Kuomintang‚Äôs tutelage period prior to the implementation of the constitutional government. It was adopted by the National Assembly on May 12, 1931, and comprised 8 chapters and 89 articles. Article 11, under Chapter 2, ‚ÄúThe Rights and Duties of the People,‚Äù stipulated: ‚ÄúThe people shall have freedom of religious belief.‚Äù

However, during the National Assembly‚Äôs deliberations on the constitutional draft in Nanjing, some delegates argued that ‚Äúreligion is a tool of imperialists used to numb the youth, and thus must be restricted,‚Äù and proposed amending the clause guaranteeing freedom of religion. This view received considerable support. In response, Zhang Zhijiang firmly stated that freedom of religious belief ‚Äúcannot,‚Äù ‚Äúshould not,‚Äù and ‚Äúneed not‚Äù be restricted. Ultimately, Zhang‚Äôs position received the majority support of the review committee.

  2. Outline of the Constitution of the Chinese Soviet Republic, 1931 (He Husheng, 2004: 49‚Äì50; Wang Jianlang & Huang Kewu, 2021: 939)

In November 1931, the Chinese Communist Party issued the Key Principles of the Constitution to the Central Soviet Area, with Article 13 stating: ‚ÄúGuarantee the toiling masses the practical freedom of religious belief and the freedom to carry out anti-religious propaganda.‚Äù Soon after, the Outline of the Constitution of the Chinese Soviet Republic was passed by the Soviet Congress, formally establishing the religious policy within Soviet-controlled regions. Article 13 declared: ‚ÄúThe Chinese Soviet government aims to guarantee the working people and peasants the practical freedom of religious belief, strictly implementing the principle of separation of church and state. All religions shall receive no protection or funding from the Soviet state. All Soviet citizens shall have the freedom to engage in anti-religious propaganda. Churches aligned with imperialism shall be allowed to exist only if they comply with Soviet laws.‚Äù

In December, the Central Decision on the Tasks and Current Work of Red Trade Unions in Soviet Areas further specified: ‚ÄúMonks, Daoist priests, pastors, and other religious leaders, even if working in the Soviet areas, are not allowed to join trade union organizations. Those who have already joined must be removed.‚Äù

  2. The Chinese Communist Party‚Äôs Work with Christianity under the Anti-Japanese United Front (CCP Central Committee 1991: 18; Liu Qun 1936: 146‚Äì147; Liu Shaoqi 1981: 63‚Äì64; Luo Fu 1991: 693‚Äì698, 715‚Äì718)

In April 1936, the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) issued the Declaration of the Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party on the Establishment of an Anti-Japanese People‚Äôs Front by All Parties and Groups Nationwide (April 25, 1936). This declaration extended an invitation to groups including the National YMCA of China, emphasizing that ‚Äúregardless of the differences in our respective positions and beliefs, or the past conflicts and struggles among us,‚Äù a ‚ÄúPeople‚Äôs Anti-Japanese United Front‚Äù could be formed on the common basis of ‚Äúresisting Japan and saving the nation.‚Äù

In September 1936, the left-wing journal Current World Semi-Monthly published an article by CCP member Liu Qun titled The New Awakening and New Path for Christian Youth. Liu observed that students from Yenching University had actively joined the national salvation movement in Beiping, which had helped dispel former misunderstandings about Christianity. He thus called on more Christian youth to ‚Äúre-evaluate‚Äù their faith and, guided by the Christian principles of ‚Äújustice, equality, and fraternity,‚Äù to take an active role in supporting the movement to save the nation. He specifically highlighted the potential of the YMCA and church-run schools to unite youth from various factions to take on shared responsibilities in the cause of national salvation.

This demonstrates that under the overarching framework of establishing the Anti-Japanese National United Front, the CCP abandoned its earlier antagonism toward Christianity and instead worked to unite with Christian groups. At the same time, however, the CCP actively planned clandestine operations within Christian circles. In 1937, Liu Shaoqi issued directives concerning the Party‚Äôs work in ‚Äúwhite areas‚Äù (territories under Kuomintang control), in which he discussed the strategic use of ‚Äúgray organizations‚Äù: ‚ÄúWe must also make use of various gray organizations to organize the masses, and we should join existing legal organizations to carry out our work. Our comrades working in these gray legal organizations must earnestly fight for the direct interests of the masses to win their support and gain leadership positions. However, we must not transform these organizations into so-called ‚Äòred organizations‚Äô; outwardly, they should maintain their gray and legal appearance.‚Äù Christianity was clearly considered one of these ‚Äúgray‚Äù organizations.

From September to November the following year, the CCP convened its Sixth Plenary Session of the Sixth Central Committee. In a report on the Anti-Japanese National United Front and Party organization, Zhang Wentian (Luo Fu) emphasized the need to develop, expand, and consolidate youth organizations across regions that were ‚Äúrelatively progressive but not communist.‚Äù At the same time, he instructed Party members to participate in all public activities as ‚Äúactive participants in the anti-Japanese cause or as ordinary citizens,‚Äù without revealing their Party membership or organizational ties, thereby conducting sustained and secretive work, including the systematic recruitment of Party members and the establishment of Party branches. Chen Yun, then head of the Central Organization Department and secretary of the Youth Committee, presented a report on youth issues during the meeting. He clearly stated that in the rear areas, the CCP must carry out ‚Äúconcealed‚Äù work through the YMCA.

In July 1937, the Nationalist Government‚Äôs Ministries of Foreign Affairs and the Interior, in consultation with the Ministry of Judicial Administration, revised the standard contract for land leased in perpetuity to foreign churches and submitted it for approval by the Executive Yuan. This revision was prompted by the inconsistencies among various local contracts, highlighting the need for standardization.   2. Ministry of Military Affairs‚Äô Decision on Whether Church Evangelists May Be Exempted from Military Service (Li Chuanbin 2011: 290‚Äì291)

In 1938, American missionary M.V. Allworden of the China Inland Mission in Duchang County, Jiangxi Province, wrote to the county magistrate after an evangelist from the mission was ‚Äúcompelled to serve in the military.‚Äù She requested that all staff of the mission be exempted from conscription. The county magistrate responded that there was no legal basis for granting exemption to religious personnel and that such exemptions could lead to further problems. He therefore referred the matter to the Jiangxi provincial government for a decision.

The Jiangxi provincial authorities cited Article 27 of the Military Service Act Implementation Regulations, which permitted registered church school teachers to be exempted. However, they noted: ‚ÄúAs for church staff such as evangelists, preachers, and other church-affiliated social service workers (e.g., YMCA staff), there is no clear regulation from the central government regarding their exemption or deferment from service. Requiring them to serve may hinder religious work and potentially violate treaty provisions guaranteeing religious freedom, which could lead to international disputes. On the other hand, granting exemption or deferment would lack legal grounds under central government law.‚Äù The provincial government thus requested a ruling from the Ministry of Military Affairs.

The Ministry responded that teachers in registered church schools could indeed be exempted under Article 27 of the Military Service Act Implementation Regulations, but that ‚Äúchurch personnel who are not involved in official public service cannot be granted deferment.‚Äù

‚ÄúThe border region government should support the organization of mass groups and guarantee the people‚Äôs freedoms of assembly, association, speech, press, and religion.‚Äù

‚ÄúWithout harming the sovereignty or principles of the border region, the government shall protect the activities of all individuals sympathetic to China‚Äôs war of resistance ‚Äî including citizens, industrialists, and religious adherents ‚Äî in the fields of production, business, and cultural affairs.‚Äù

  2. The Assassination of Liu Zhan‚Äôen, President of Hujiang University (1938) (Li Yading, n.d.)

Liu Zhan‚Äôen was the first Chinese president of Hujiang University. After assuming office in 1928, he introduced the slogan: ‚ÄúMake Hujiang University more Chinese, more Christian.‚Äù Following the Mukden Incident (1931), he actively called for resistance against Japan and national salvation, inviting figures such as Feng Yuxiang, Tao Xingzhi, and Li Gongpu to speak at the university. In 1935, he joined 283 intellectuals from Shanghai‚Äôs cultural sector in issuing the Shanghai Cultural Community‚Äôs Declaration for National Salvation, demanding that the Nanjing government resist Japan and grant the people freedoms of speech and association.

After the outbreak of the War of Resistance in 1937, Hujiang University, along with Soochow University and St. John‚Äôs University, formed a united church university. Liu was elected chairman of the Shanghai United Committee for National Salvation. After the Japanese army captured Nanjing, he led the establishment of the International Friendship Society, representing the Chinese government in liaising with foreign diplomats stationed in Shanghai, and spreading awareness of China‚Äôs anti-Japanese stance to foreign governments and citizens.

During Japan‚Äôs invasion of China in the 1930s, the international community generally reacted with condemnation but limited action.
