Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

  1. Chia Yu Ming (Jia Yuming), 1880-1964 (Wang Delong 2017; Zha Shijie 1983b: 113-120; Guo Weilian 2002: 55-60)

Chia Yu Ming, courtesy name Dexin, self-styled as Xingwu, was from ChangLe County, Shandong, and was a third-generation Christian, baptized from a young age. He studied at the Dengzhou Literary Society, founded by American Presbyterian missionary Calvin Mateer. During his studies, he resolved to dedicate his life to preaching. After graduating from the Literary Society in 1901, he graduated from the Theological Seminary in 1904 and worked as a preacher in Anqiu, Weifang.

Chia was ordained as a pastor in 1907 and transferred to serve the Presbyterian Church in Yizhou, Shandong, in 1908. During this time, he actively participated in the church unification movement, including the Shandong Christian Federation and the National Presbyterian Alliance. In 1916, he became a professor at Jinling Theological Seminary, which was co-sponsored by the Southern Presbyterian Church, Northern Presbyterian Church, Disciples of Christ, Methodist Episcopal Church (South), and Methodist Episcopal Church (North), reflecting Chia‚Äôs commitment to unity. Later, when the Presbyterian Church discussed joining the Chinese Christian Church, Chia was involved. While in Nanjing, he founded the ‚ÄúLingguang Daily,‚Äù promoting the motto ‚ÄúUphold the truth, develop the spiritual life and knowledge of believers.‚Äù

However, as the conflict between old and new theology emerged, the unification movement faced challenges. Fundamentalist individuals, aiming to maintain the purity of their faith, refused to cooperate with modernists and even withdrew from united or cooperative organizations. At that time, a conservative faction of Presbyterians refused to participate in the Chinese Christian Church, of which Chia was a part. In 1922, he criticized the new theology for undermining unity and called on steadfast believers to be a bulwark of truth. That same year, Chia left Jinling Theological Seminary, accepting an invitation from Watson M. Hayes to teach at the North China Theological Seminary in Teng County, Shandong, reflecting his stance.

In 1929, members of the North China Presbyterian Assembly initiated the formation of the ‚ÄúLeague of Christian Churches,‚Äù and a conference was held in December of that year, collectively deciding not to join the Chinese Christian Church formed by the Presbyterians, Congregationalists, and London Missionary Society, and through the establishment of the League of Christian Churches.   1. Fan Tianxiang

Fan Tianxiang (ËåÉÂ§©Á••, Bliss M. Wiant, 1895-1975) was born in Clark County, Ohio, USA. His father, William Allen Wiant, served as the senior pastor of the Central Methodist Church in Springfield, Ohio, from 1911 to 1917.
