From 1845 to 1848, Legge returned to England for health reasons and to give lectures. After his return to Hong Kong in 1848 until his next return to England in 1873, he began translating Confucian classics into English. During his multiple visits to Paris, Legge engaged in sinological exchanges with the renowned French sinologist Stanislas A. James Hudson Taylor (Êà¥Âæ∑Áîü, 1832-1905), born in Yorkshire, England, had a desire for overseas missionary work from a young age. His interest in missionary work in China was sparked by reading the writings of missionary W. H. Medhurst, which led him to decide to self-study medicine as a means to provide medical missions. In 1853, the Chinese Evangelization Society accepted his application, and he arrived in Shanghai in March 1854 to learn Chinese. In 1860, due to health reasons, he returned to England for treatment. During this time, he obtained membership in the Royal College of Surgeons and a midwifery qualification. Meanwhile, he completed a hymnal in Ningbo dialect and translated and revised the Romanized version of the Ningbo New Testament. By the end of 1864, Taylor completed ‚ÄúChina: Its Spiritual Need and Claims,‚Äù pointing out that the number of British missionaries in China had dropped from 115 to 91, all concentrated in five coastal cities, with no one working in the inland regions of China. In 1865, he founded the China Inland Mission.

In May 1866, the Taylor family, along with four children and 16 missionaries, set off for China, arriving in Shanghai in September.

Between 1868 and 1871, Taylor carried out missionary work in the areas around Zhenjiang and Yangzhou. In 1868, due to rumors targeting a baby-care center, the Yangzhou Inland Mission faced a violent attack from the local populace, known historically as the ‚ÄúYangzhou Church Incident.‚ÄùAfter the conflict, Hudson Taylor established a policy for the China Inland Mission to avoid seeking compensation in order to prevent exacerbating tensions between the Christian and local communities. In 1871, his three young children and his beloved wife Maria died one after another. He returned to England in 1871 and married another female missionary in November. In November 1872, he returned to China. Between 1865 and 1875, the Inland Mission had established bases in Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Anhui, and Jiangxi.

In 1874, Taylor returned to England for health reasons and re-entered China in September 1876. From then on, the Inland Mission penetrated further into the inland provinces, even establishing churches in Yunnan. In 1879, while recuperating in Yantai, Shandong, he purchased land to establish the ‚ÄúZhi Fu School‚Äù to provide education for children of Inland Mission missionaries. In March 1883, he returned to England and called upon 76 people to come to China for missionary work. By 1885, the Inland Mission had entered nine inland provinces. In June 1886, Taylor gathered his colleagues in Shanghai to pray for the purchase of land to establish the Inland Mission headquarters. In February 1890, the headquarters was completed. In January 1887, he returned to England and called for 102 young people to go to China for missionary work. Later, he traveled to the United States to establish a North American branch of the Inland Mission, which by 1893 had dispatched 39 missionaries to China. Subsequently, the Inland Mission also recruited missionaries from Northern Europe and Australia. By 1895, the China Inland Mission had a total of 604 missionaries, along with 417 Chinese coworkers.

In 1895, Taylor appointed the young William Cooper as assistant director and transferred all affairs to him the following year before returning to England to continue sharing the vision in various places. He returned to China in January 1898 and traveled to Australia in September 1899. In April 1900, he led missionary gatherings in New York and Boston. In June 1900, Taylor and his wife went to Switzerland for recuperation. Shortly thereafter, they heard reports of the Boxer Rebellion and learned that over a hundred missionaries and their families from the Inland Mission had been martyred, which greatly saddened him.

In January 1901, he appointed Dixon E. Hoste as acting director. In November 1902, he resigned as general director, and Hoste took over as the general director of the China Inland Mission in January 1903. In February 1905, Taylor returned to China and arrived in Shanghai in April, later visiting coworkers in Hunan. On June 1, he arrived in Changsha, where he passed away two days later.

Hudson Taylor‚Äôs son, James II, his grandson, James III, and his great-grandson, James IV, all dedicated themselves to missionary work among the Chinese.

  1. Medical Missionary Society in China, 1838 (Choa 1990; Cohen 1978: 574-576; Tiedemann 2010: 436-445; Èõ∑Á••È∫ü 2024: 78-79; ÊùéÂÇ≥Êñå 2003: 34-38)

The Medical Missionary Society in China was jointly founded in February 1838 by Peter Parker (American Board), Thomas Colledge (British East India Company), and Elijah Bridgeman (American Board) in Guangzhou. This marked the establishment of the first organization in modern China focused on medical missions, representing the formal introduction of Western medicine into China and the beginning of the integration of the Christian missionary movement with modern medicine. The concept of medical missions aimed to heal the body in order to save the soul; on one hand, by healing, it sought to eliminate the Chinese people‚Äôs xenophobia and thereby win their respect, facilitating the smooth spreading of the gospel. On the other hand, it aimed to break superstitions and ignorance through scientific principles, thereby changing the worldview of the Chinese people and removing obstacles to the spread of the gospel.

In 1845, the Medical Missionary Society split into two factions, with some members establishing the Hong Kong Medical Missionary Society.

Christianity introduced Western medicine into China, yet in the 19th century, modern medicine in China was primarily represented by Western missionaries, especially those focused on curative medicine. Among these, surgical procedures gained prominence due to their ability to yield dramatic results quickly. The immediate effectiveness of surgical treatments attracted patients, demonstrating the superiority of Western medicine and thus establishing the missionaries‚Äô reputation. Similarly, ophthalmic surgeries and tooth extractions also had remarkable effects.

However, for medical missionaries trained in Eastern practices, there existed a tension between their dual roles of healing patients and engaging in missionary work. Although patients benefited from the medical services provided by missionaries, the conversion rate to Christianity was not significant. Additionally, various anti-missionary rumors circulated in the 19th century, targeting the medical work of missionaries.

In 1887, an International Medical Conference was scheduled to be held in Washington, D.C., where several medical missionaries in China planned to attend. Since the conference required participants to be members of an official medical society, John G. Kerr of the Guangzhou Boji Hospital and Henry Williams Boone, the president of the Shanghai Tongren Hospital, suggested reorganizing the existing Medical Missionary Society. Thus, in 1886, the Medical Missionary Association of China was established in Shanghai, with Kerr as president and Boone as vice president. This was the first national association of medical missionaries in China, with purposes that included: (1) promoting Western medicine in China and enhancing communication among medical missionaries; (2) advancing the development of missionary and medical work; and (3) fostering unity among members to facilitate the advancement of Western medical practices in China. Therefore, while the association had a religious foundation, it placed a significant emphasis on improving medical standards in China.They further required that medical missionaries coming to China must have recognized formal medical training. Early members were primarily foreign or expatriate doctors, and it wasn‚Äôt until 1903 that local Chinese physicians began to be gradually admitted into the society. They translated medical texts, standardized Chinese medical terminologies, published medical journals, promoted medical education, and worked on public health education, significantly contributing to the modernization of medicine in China.

In 1890, the missionary conference also discussed issues related to medical missions. There were traditional views emphasizing medical work as a means of evangelism, but some explicitly pointed out the undeniable contradictions and tensions between medical work and missionary activities. The medical work in 19th-century missionary endeavors continued to develop. Between 1887 and 1890, there were 46 medical missionaries who came to China. By the end of the 19th century, sizable church hospitals had been established across various regions, and efforts to prioritize medical education began, training Chinese medical workers.

In 1925, the name of the Medical Missionary Society was changed to the China Medical Association in English. In 1932, the China Medical Association merged with the National Medical Association to form the Chinese Medical Association.

  1. Establishment of the American and British Bible Societies in China (È∫•ÈáëËèØ 2010: 10-19)

The British and Foreign Bible Society was established in London, England, in 1804 as an ecumenical organization aimed at promoting the dissemination of the Bible. With the development of overseas missionary movements, there was also a desire to support Bible translation work. As early as 1812, the society provided funding for Robert Morrison‚Äôs translation of the ‚ÄúHoly Scriptures,‚Äù marking the beginning of its work in China. Subsequently, various other Chinese translations were also funded by the society. In 1863, the British and Foreign Bible Society formally established a branch in China, with Alexander Wylie serving as its first manager.

With the assistance of the British and Foreign Bible Society, the first Bible society in America, the Philadelphia Bible Society, was established in 1808. Following this, several Bible societies in other American cities received funding from the British and Foreign Bible Society. However, these societies primarily focused on their local areas, and the call for a national Bible society began to gain attention. In 1816, the American Bible Society was established in New York. In 1832, the American Bible Society first allocated funds to assist the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions‚Äô missionary Elijah Bridgeman in distributing Bibles in China, marking the beginning of its work in China. In 1875, the American Bible Society established a branch in China.

In 1809, the National Bible Society of Scotland was formed, and the British and Foreign Bible Society ceased participation in Scottish missionary work. In 1863, the Scottish Bible Society appointed Alexander Williamson as its first manager in China, and in 1877 they established their branch in China. Thus, the publishing and distribution of Bibles in China entered an era characterized by competition among the three societies.

In terms of the number of Chinese Bibles distributed, the British and Foreign Bible Society averaged 180,000 copies per year from 1879 to 1889. The American Bible Society reported print runs exceeding 250,000 copies in 1887. The Scottish Bible Society distributed approximately 200,000 copies per year from 1884 to 1894.

The China Inland Mission (CIM) was established in 1865 by British missionary James Hudson Taylor, with the goal of spreading the Gospel to the inland areas of China that had yet to be evangelized.

After the opening of China in the mid-19th century, missionary activities were concentrated in coastal regions. He proposed a strategy that involved penetrating into these inland areas for missionary work. The China Inland Mission operated under the belief of ‚Äúfaith missions,‚Äù emphasizing ‚Äúcomplete reliance on faith,‚Äù and firmly believed that God would provide.The China Inland Mission operated as an interdenominational organization, attracting missionaries from the UK, Europe, North America, and Australia. The China Inland Mission gradually expanded into inland provinces such as Zhejiang, Anhui, and Hunan. After the establishment of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in 1949, the China Inland Mission remained until it withdrew from China in 1951, shifting its focus to Southeast Asia and other regions.   1. The Chinese Recorder and Missionary Journal (Èô∂È£õ‰∫û 2007: 243-262; Rawlinson 1990)

The Chinese Recorder and Missionary Journal was the longest-running English publication during the tumultuous years of 19th-century China, in circulation from 1868 until its cessation in 1941 due to the outbreak of the Pacific War. This publication was founded as a platform for missionaries in China to exchange information and discuss missionary work, facilitating mutual understanding of Chinese culture and collaborative efforts. Initially launched in 1867 as the Missionary Recorder by Lucian N. Wheeler, a Methodist missionary in Fuzhou, with funding from the Methodist Church and the North American Presbyterian Church, it only lasted nine months due to Wheeler‚Äôs reassignment. However, due to requests for continued publication, the journal was revived in May 1878 as The Chinese Recorder and Missionary Journal, with S. L. Baldwin, a Methodist missionary, as its editor. In May 1869, Justus Doolittle from the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions joined the team. The journal ran into financial difficulties again in 1872 and ceased publication. In January 1874, it transitioned to a bimonthly publication distributed by the Shanghai Methodist Publishing House (later changing to a monthly publication in March 1885), with A. Wylie from the British and Foreign Bible Society serving as editor. After Wylie returned to Britain in 1878, Baldwin again took on the editorial role. Subsequently, editors included missionary doctors such as Happer A. Patton, L. H. Gulick, and G. F. Fitch, among others. Frank J. Rawlinson from the American Board served as assistant editor starting in 1912 and later became the longest-serving editor until his death in August 1937 during a Japanese attack on Shanghai. He was succeeded by Frank R. Millican from the American Presbyterian Church. The publication ceased in December 1941 after the onset of the Pacific War, making The Chinese Recorder the longest-running missionary publication in China.

As a missionary publication, The Chinese Recorder preserved a wealth of information about the missionary movement, particularly reflecting the various challenges faced by missionaries in China and showcasing their diverse voices.

The formation of the gentry class was closely linked to the imperial examination system, through which some entered government service (‚Äúlearning to improve the self‚Äù). Even those who could not gain entry into the narrow gateways of government still held significant influence in local affairs and education due to their knowledge and literacy skills. Locally, the gentry reinforced their social standing by engaging in public works (such as building schools, constructing dikes, and erecting temples), thereby playing leading roles in local governance.

In his book ‚ÄúThe Causes of Anti-Christian Sentiment Among Chinese Officials and Gentry (1860-1874),‚Äù Paul A. Cohen observes that while the participants in conflicts between the populace and faith (religious incidents) appeared to be ordinary citizens, the actual instigators and planners were often local gentry and intellectuals. When the populace expelled, attacked, or killed missionaries and believers, or destroyed or burned churches and the homes of congregants, it was largely under the influence or incitement of these gentry. The popular anti-Christian pamphlets and posts that stirred hatred and anger among the general populace were also penned by them. It can be said that the gentry class, including intellectuals (scholarly officials), was the main force behind anti-Christian sentiment in late Qing. Cohen also noted that many local officials, due to their responsibilities, had to enforce protective measures for missionaries and found themselves in opposition to the gentry, even though internally they similarly harbored anti-Christian sentiments.

Why did the Qing gentry oppose Christianity? Cohen noted that the primary reasons were mutual misunderstandings and various other factors. Another reason for the anti-Christian sentiment among Chinese people was that the missionary work in China was perceived to carry an invasive quality, particularly because protective clauses for missionaries were included in unequal treaties, which inevitably caused resentment. Specific issues derived from this invasive quality included disputes over church properties, the special status of missionaries, and excessive behaviors by missionaries, which evoked significant dissatisfaction among the populace. Furthermore, misunderstandings arising from the differences between Chinese social customs and Western practices also contributed to the conflicts between different faiths. Ultimately, the missionaries, by exercising extraterritoriality and protective clauses, created a new class of congregants distinct from ordinary citizens, challenging the dignity and interests of the Chinese gentry.

Su Ping, in her book ‚ÄúRumors and Modern Religious Incidents,‚Äù points out that rumors were a significant tool for anti-Christian activities during the Qing dynasty, and that the creators and disseminators of these anti-Christian rumors were often the gentry and officials. Under the instigation of these rumors, the general populace directly participated in the various anti-Christian conflicts.
