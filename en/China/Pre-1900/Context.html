After the 1870 Tianjin Massacre, the Zongli Yamen (Office for the Management of the Affairs of All Foreign Countries) issued, in February of the following year, a document titled ‚ÄúEight Articles of the Missionary Cases Regulations‚Äù. These were also communicated to the foreign ministries of Britain, France, Germany, the United States, Russia, Spain, Austria, Italy, Denmark, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Sweden, urging them to supervise their respective churches to strictly implement the regulations. The articles reflect the Qing government‚Äôs understanding and handling of missionary affairs at the time:

1.Proposed abolishment of foreign-run foundling homes, because many missionary incidents arose from rumors‚Äîsuch as gouging out eyes and cutting out hearts‚Äîlinked to these institutions, which caused public fear. If such homes were to be established, they should accept only children of converts who had no guardians, and must report to and be registered with local authorities. Non-Christian infants were to be entrusted to Chinese provincial and local officials, who would assign local gentry to adopt them, so that ‚ÄúChinese and foreigners may each do good according to their own customs, thereby avoiding suspicion.‚Äù

2.Chinese women were strictly prohibited from entering churches, and foreign nuns were not allowed to proselytize in China, citing traditional Chinese values of chastity, propriety, and the separation of men and women.

3.Missionaries were forbidden from interfering in lawsuits. Chinese Christian believers were to be treated the same as ordinary civilians in all legal matters. 4.No further indemnity claims were to be made under the pretext of missionary incidents.

5.Missionary activities were restricted to designated provinces as stated in their permits, and they were forbidden from secretly entering other provinces.

6.Caution was advised in accepting converts. Before admitting someone to the church, their background should be carefully investigated for any history of crime or misconduct. Their information should also be reported and recorded in the local registry for verification purposes.

7.When interacting with local officials, missionaries were to follow Chinese protocol, refrain from inappropriate use of official seals or authority, and behave with decorum and respect.

8.Missionaries were not allowed to unilaterally demand the return of church properties to avoid provoking conflict. If land or buildings needed to be purchased or leased, an application must be submitted to the local officials for investigation. If the site involved issues of geomancy (fengshui), it had to be resolved. Even with official approval, only when there was unanimous consent from the local community and no dissent, could the contract be finalized, clearly stating that the property belonged to the Chinese Christian community. It could not be falsely claimed or transferred to others, nor should missionaries trust deceitful locals who might manipulate property transactions in secret.

The reactions of various countries to the Zongli Yamen‚Äôs regulations were largely oppositional in nature.

‚Ä¢Russia stated that China ought to be ‚Äútolerant of all religions‚Äù, yet the regulations seemed to have ‚Äúa tone of suppression and harshness.‚Äù

‚Ä¢The German government reaffirmed that, under international law, it had the duty to protect the lives, property, and rights of German missionaries in China.

‚Ä¢The British side remarked that there were no British-run foundling homes in China, so it would not engage in detailed argument on that issue. ‚Ä¢Regarding the rejection of criminals and wrongdoers as converts, the British side countered by questioning why Chinese authorities failed to arrest and punish such individuals in the first place. Moreover, it claimed this proposal contradicted the missionary‚Äôs role to encourage moral reform.

In August 1895, in response to recent missionary incidents in Sichuan and Fujian, the Qing court issued a special imperial edict, stating:

‚ÄúAll generals, governors, and provincial administrators in the various provinces are to strictly instruct their subordinates: wherever there are churches, they must be vigorously protected. In September, another edict was issued in reference to the church incident in the provincial capital of Sichuan, stating:

‚ÄúAll of this was due to local officials who failed to properly guide and instruct the people, allowing the situation to escalate. In October, as missionary incidents once again erupted in Sichuan and Fujian, yet another edict was issued:

‚ÄúThere are many churches throughout the provinces, and it is the duty of local officials to constantly educate and guide the common people, and to strictly prevent even minor grievances or petty quarrels from suddenly escalating into conflicts. The edict further warned that if local officials continued to be negligent and allowed disturbances to occur, they would be severely punished without exception.

  2. Regulations on the Reception of Missionaries by Local Officials (1899)

(Wang Tieya 1982: pp. 862‚Äì863; Tao Feiya & Li Qiang 2016; Li Yumin 2010: p. 213)

To reduce the pressure on the central Qing government caused by frequent missionary incidents (ÊïôÊ°à), in March 1899 the Qing court promulgated the ‚ÄúFive Articles on the Reception of Bishops and Missionaries by Local Officials.‚Äù These regulations stipulated that missionaries were to be received by government officials of corresponding rank, based on the missionaries‚Äô ecclesiastical hierarchy.

The regulations were initiated to some extent by the Zongli Yamen and were not intended to bestow honorary titles on missionaries, but rather to require local officials to receive and handle missionaries according to a fixed protocol. The goal was to resolve missionary cases locally and prevent them from escalating to the national level, which often drew in foreign diplomatic pressure.

In cases where disputes arose between Christians and non-Christians, local officials were to handle them impartially, while missionaries were not to interfere or shield anyone, so as to promote peaceful coexistence between Christians and non-Christians.

However, in 1908, the Qing government abolished these regulations, citing widespread abuses by missionaries, who often overstepped their authority and acted as if they were high-ranking local officials, leading to numerous problems.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

  1. Anti-Christian Thought and Conflicts Between People and Missionaries (Missionary Cases)

(Xing Fuzeng, 2006: 241‚Äì257)

Why did the spread of Christianity in modern China provoke such intense resistance from the Chinese people? This question has long drawn significant interest from both Chinese and foreign scholars, giving rise to diverse debates and interpretations.

Regarding the causes of the missionary cases (ÊïôÊ°à), the mainstream explanation in early mainland Chinese scholarship approached the issue by viewing Christianity as a tool of imperialist aggression against China. In other words, the essence of conflicts between the people and missionaries was seen as a thoroughly anti-foreign religious struggle.

According to the Chinese Communist revolutionary historical perspective, modern Chinese history is a history of imperialist invasion, with Western missionaries portrayed as the vanguard of imperialist aggression. The missionary enterprise, along with Chinese churches, was viewed as a mechanism of imperialist expansion into China.

Thus, in this view, the history of modern Chinese Christianity, like that of modern China more broadly, is defined by a central thread of invasion and resistance. The Chinese people‚Äôs opposition to foreign religions is therefore portrayed as a natural and justified expression of anti-imperialist struggle‚Äîa cause that was both legitimate and righteous.

Therefore, as a non-orthodox belief system, Christianity inevitably encountered opposition from orthodox forces during its introduction and spread in China.

Cohen identifies the anti-Christian tradition that confronted Catholicism in the Ming and Qing periods and Protestant Christianity in the late Qing as a continuation and development of this anti-heterodoxy tradition.

According to L√º, the primary causes of conflict can be categorized into three main areas:

3.Officials and gentry sought to protect their dignity and interests.

‚Ä¢The behavior and attitudes of missionaries, and

Since the early modern era, China upheld a Sinocentric worldview, positioning itself as the center of global order through the tributary system. Entering the 19th century, Britain became increasingly determined to change the unequal terms of Sino-British trade. In 1834, the British Parliament abolished the East India Company‚Äôs monopoly on China trade and established the office of Superintendent of Trade in China.
