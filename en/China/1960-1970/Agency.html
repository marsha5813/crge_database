Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

  1. Zhang Wenxin (Zhang Wenxin 1969; China Group n.d.)

He paid particular attention to the development of the Three-Self Movement of Chinese Christianity and regarded the participation of Christians in the movement as a form of ‚Äúpragmatic compromise‚Äù (modus vivendi). The following year, he published an English translated collection of documents on the Three-Self Movement, ‚ÄúDocuments of the Three-Self Movement: Sources Materials for the Study of the Protestant Church in Communist China,‚Äù which became an indispensable English historical resource for studying the Three-Self Movement.

Zhang Wenxin (Francis P. Jones, 1890-1975), a missionary of the Methodist Church, was born in Dodgeville, Wisconsin, USA. In 1907, he entered Platteville Teachers‚Äô Training College. In 1911, he studied at Northwestern University and Garrett Evangelical Theological Seminary, continuing his studies at the University of Chicago after graduation. In 1915, Zhang came to Fukien, China, for missionary work. During his vacation from 1929 to 1930, he studied at Union Theological Seminary in New York, focusing on critical commentary on the original text of the Gospel of John, receiving his Master of Divinity degree.

After returning to China in the same year, Zhang taught in the English Department at Jinling University in Nanjing and participated in the hymn editorial committee, which included Fan Tianxiang (Bliss Wiant), co-editing ‚ÄúPraise to the Lord.‚Äù In the spring of 1937, he was appointed to teach New Testament studies at Jinling Theological Seminary in Nanjing. During the Second Sino-Japanese War, Jinling Theological Seminary moved to the Shanghai concession, and Zhang returned to Shanghai. In 1940, he went to Chengdu, Sichuan, to plan for the Chengdu branch of Jinling Theological Seminary, while also starting to organize the translation and editorial work of ‚ÄúChristian Classics.‚Äù From 1944 to 1946, he further developed plans in the United States. In 1946, Zhang returned to Nanjing and experienced the ‚Äúliberation‚Äù of Nanjing in April 1949, but finally left China in December 1951 and returned to the United States.   1. Lin Naimei (Lin Naimei n.d.)

Lin Naimei (Lin Naimei, 1928-2006) was the founder of the first family church in Wenzhou in 1960. Born into a Christian family, she participated in the youth group while working in a coal brick factory in 1954 and abandoned her faith. In April 1959, she was injured by an explosion while working in the kiln and, after resting at home for a month, experienced a rebirth, weeping and confessing her sins. From then on, she began evangelizing in the factory, which led to her expulsion from the Party. In October, she received a revelation in a vision to preach the gospel for the Lord. In December, she saw the five large characters ‚ÄúJehovah is Holy‚Äù in a vision. By the end of 1960, she began holding gatherings in her home and faced persecution during this period.

In 1969, she participated in the preparation for the establishment of the ‚ÄúWenzhou Christian Church General Association,‚Äù which was officially founded in 1970 with Lin as the person in charge. From 1971 to 1980, a total of 1,759 meeting points were established in the Wenzhou area, and spiritual retreats for believers were held two to four times a year in various locations.In 1980, Lin hoped to master the Three-Self movement by participating in it, which ultimately led to the first split within the Wenzhou family church.

Lin passed away on March 27, 2006.

  1. Huang Peiyong (Ding Guangxun 1998: 486-487; Shen Derong 2000a: 63-64; Li Shoubau 1962: 23-24)

Huang Peiyong passed away on December 8, 1962.

Huang Peiyong (Hwang Pei Yung, 1917-1962) was from Fuzhou, Fujian, and had a Methodist background. He studied at Fuzhou Yinghua Middle School and Shanghai Hujiang University. He served as the chairman of the Shanghai United Church (1940). After graduating from the sociology department of Hujiang University in 1941, he joined the YMCA, assisting Jiang Wenhan in student work. During the Anti-Japanese War, he relocated with the YMCA and taught at Chongqing Theological Seminary after the war. After 1949, he worked in the Student Department of the National China YMCA and was ordained as a pastor by the Methodist Church in 1953, also serving as an associate pastor at Shanghai Mool Church.

Huang Peiyong was a key figure actively involved in the Three-Self Movement. He was the initiator of the Three-Self Declaration and served as the secretary of the Three-Self Reform Movement‚Äôs training class and a member of the study committee. As early as January 1950, he published an article in ‚ÄúTianfeng‚Äù titled ‚ÄúStarting from Understanding to Engage Thoughts‚ÄîTalking about Christianity and Marxism with Youth Friends,‚Äù opposing the attempt to reconcile Christianity with dialectical materialism or historical materialism, arguing that one must never ‚Äúforce a fit,‚Äù otherwise it would result in something that was ‚Äúneither materialism nor Christianity.‚Äù Huang also pointed out that the most urgent task for Christians today is to stand in their positions under the overall goal of opposing imperialism, feudalism, and bureaucratic capitalism, cooperating with the Party and the government to serve the people and build a new China.   1. Zhao Fusan (Wu Zongsu 2017: 5-7)

Zhao Fusan (Zhao Fusan, 1926-2015) was born into a Christian family in Shanghai. His mother, Wang Shuyi, was the tenth daughter of Pastor Wang Yuchu. His father, Zhao Shike, had leftist thoughts. Zhao entered the economics department of St. John‚Äôs University in Shanghai in 1943 and graduated in 1946. While studying at St. John‚Äôs University, he joined the Communist Party and became an underground member. In 1947, he served as an officer in the Student Department of the Beijing YMCA while also being the executive officer of the Student Relief Association, engaging in the Christian student movement and student relief work. In 1949, he served as an officer in the Beijing Christian Alliance and was the deputy secretary-general of the Beijing YMCA. In 1950, he participated in the Three-Self Movement, being one of the initiators of the Three-Self Declaration, playing a crucial role in promoting the Three-Self Movement in Beijing.

In 1953, he was ordained as a deacon by the Chinese Anglican Church (and was ordained as a pastor the following year), serving as the registrar at Yenching Union Theological Seminary. He later became the general secretary of the North China Diocese of the Chinese Anglican Church and the vice-chairman of the Beijing Three-Self Association. In 1964, when the Institute of World Religions was established under the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Zhao was hired as the director of the Christian Research Department. During the Cultural Revolution, he was subjected to scrutiny for ten years. In 1977, when the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences was established, he was appointed as a researcher at the Institute of World Religions. In 1980, he became the deputy secretary of the Party Committee of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences. In 1981, he served as the vice-chairman of the Beijing Three-Self Association. In 1985, he became the vice-president of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences and was also the vice-chairman of the third and fourth sessions of the Christian Three-Self Patriotic Movement Committee. After the ‚ÄúJune Fourth Incident‚Äù in 1989, he publicly condemned the Chinese government in Paris and subsequently refused to return to China, moving to the United States. In June 1990, he was stripped of his positions, including that of a member of the Standing Committee of the National People‚Äôs Congress and of the Foreign Affairs Committee of the National People‚Äôs Congress.In 1979, under the pen name ‚ÄúYang Zhen,‚Äù he published ‚ÄúAn Outline of Christian History‚Äù (Beijing: Sanlian), organizing the history of Christianity from a materialist historical perspective. After moving to the United States, he translated ‚ÄúA History of European Culture‚Äù (P. J. A. N. Rietbergen), ‚ÄúA History of European Thought‚Äù (Friedrick Heer), and ‚ÄúA Brief History of Chinese Philosophy‚Äù (Feng Youlan); he authored ‚ÄúHistorical Pages: Essays on Western Cultural History‚Äù (2009) and ‚ÄúReflections‚Äù (2011).

He passed away in the United States on July 15, 2015.

  1. Cao Shengjie (Cao Shengjie 2016; Wu Zongsu 2011: 581)

She was a representative of the Shanghai People‚Äôs Congress from 1988 to 1995 and served as a member of the National Committee of the Chinese People‚Äôs Political Consultative Conference from 1995 to 2003.
