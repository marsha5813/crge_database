China: Context (1960-1970)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

  1. Adjustment Period Religious Policies 1960-1962 (Zhu Yueli 1994: 151-152; Zhou Enlai 1984: 445; Li Weihan 1987: 437-439; Xu Bing 1988: 401-402)

In February 1960, the Sixth National Religious Work Conference was held in Tianjin. At the meeting, Zhang Zhiyi summarized and reiterated the characteristics of the ‚Äúfive natures‚Äù of religion, and provided important instructions on how to work with the religious population and correctly understand and implement the policy of religious freedom.

In April 1962, Zhou Enlai delivered a speech at the Third Session of the Third National Committee of the Chinese People‚Äôs Political Consultative Conference. He acknowledged that ‚Äúthere are some shortcomings and mistakes in this work,‚Äù specifically that ‚Äúthe issue of religious belief is often seen too simplistically, requiring everyone to conform to the worldview of Communist Party members under Marxism-Leninism, which is impossible.‚Äù He pointed out that patriotic religious figures still hold religious beliefs, and this does not hinder the expansion and unity of the overall people‚Äôs democratic united front, nor does it impede the socialist construction of our motherland.

From April to May 1962, the Twelfth National United Front Work Conference was convened, presided over by Xu Bing (First Deputy Minister, also known as Xing Xiping), who raised the slogan ‚ÄúAdjust relationships and strengthen unity.‚Äù Central United Front Minister Li Weihan did not attend this meeting and specially wrote a report for Xu Bing‚Äôs summary. Regarding the religious issue, Li Weihan reflected that members of the religious community (mainly referring to ‚Äúreligious professionals‚Äù) hoped to ‚Äúremove the label of the exploiting class.‚Äù Li reiterated that the religious community ‚Äúhas not been a single class or tier in the past and present, but rather is connected to different classes,‚Äù including imperialist lackeys, feudal ruling classes, the bourgeoisie, and the working people. However, since 1949, significant changes have taken place in the religious community after twelve years of effort. ‚ÄúIt can be affirmed that the majority of the religious community, now mainly from the middle and upper classes, are generally patriotic, and there are more or less progressive figures as the backbone.‚Äù Li Weihan candidly stated, ‚ÄúThere are still many contradictions between the religious community and us, reflecting their resistance to democratic reform and socialist transformation,‚Äù but the contradictions involved mainly remain internal contradictions among the people. Li further pointed out that while the CCP‚Äôs religious work has achieved great results, ‚Äúthere are many shortcomings, mistakes, and problems,‚Äù primarily due to the fact that ‚Äúit has leaned too far to the left, gone overboard, and fought excessively.‚Äù His conclusion was that, while helping all levels of religious figures to continue self-education and self-transformation, and simultaneously maintaining vigilance against counter-revolutionaries, it is necessary to ‚Äúresolutely correct our shortcomings and mistakes in our work, uphold the party‚Äôs policy of religious freedom, restore the proper religious activities of the masses, and open up temples and meeting halls that need to be opened.‚Äù

In his summary report, Xu Bing pointed out that ‚Äúsome unwelcomed events have occurred concerning relationships, including with the religious community, which require our adjustment.‚Äù He used the religious community as an example: ‚ÄúIn relation to the religious community, we fail to recognize the mass nature of religion and subjectively and one-sidedly think that religion serves the exploiting class.‚Äù He called for ‚Äúproactively adjusting relationships in the future to strengthen unity, resolve knotty issues, and stabilize people‚Äôs minds.‚Äù He suggested that non-Party members, intellectuals, bourgeois elements, ethnic minorities, and religious leaders, ‚Äúas long as they stand on a patriotic stance, are all part of the masses,‚Äù which constitutes ‚Äúinternal contradictions among the people,‚Äù and that we must ‚Äúgo among them to understand their thoughts, problems, and opinions, and engage in sincere discussions,‚Äù analyze and organize their opinions, and submit suggestions for Party committee approval, ‚Äúthen go back to the masses.‚Äù Xu repeatedly emphasized the need to correct the ‚Äúleftist‚Äù issues, saying, ‚ÄúIn the past, they were afraid of the right but not of the ‚Äòleft;‚Äô in fact, both the left and right come from the same source, the left pushes the masses to the enemy, while the right sends the masses to the enemy.‚Äù In recent years, ‚Äúthere have been many leftist problems in united front work,‚Äù and ‚Äútoday‚Äôs main emphasis is to counter the ‚Äòleft‚Äô and re-educate cadres.‚Äù

  1. Class Struggle-Oriented Religious Policies 1962-1965 (Chi Nai 1998: 121-124; He Husheng 2004: 112)

From November 1962 to January 1963, the Seventh National Religious Work Conference was held. This conference completely lacked the spirit of ‚Äúunity‚Äù and ‚Äúadjusting relationships‚Äù emphasized in the national united front work conference, instead raising high the banner of ‚Äúclass struggle‚Äù put forward at the Eighth Plenary Session of the Eighth Central Committee of the Communist Party of China. The conference believed that since 1962, the class struggle in the religious sphere has been very intense, with some reactionaries wrapped in religious garb brazenly attacking the Party, and landlords and rich peasants utilizing religion for restoration, with quite a number of religious figures attempting by all means to expand religious influence and power.The conference determined to carry out class education focused on anti-imperialism, patriotism, and law-abiding themes among religious figures; to strengthen class education and scientific cultural knowledge education for the religious population; and to resolutely combat counter-revolutionaries, bad elements, and rightists who hide behind religion and oppose the Party and socialism.

In June 1963, the State Council Religious Affairs Bureau issued a document proposing that class education focused on anti-imperialism, patriotism, and law-abiding conduct (the ‚ÄúThree Principles‚Äù) be conducted among religious figures, organizing religious leaders to expose the crimes of imperialism and domestic reactionary classes using religion. In August, the State Council Religious Affairs Bureau convened over 550 representatives from national religious groups and religious figures from Beijing, stating that issues should be recognized from the perspective of class struggle and ‚Äúuncovering the lid on class struggle.‚Äù

At the same time, during the nationwide socialist education movement in rural areas, religious affairs departments conducted surveys in villages with significant religious populations, where many religious figures were criticized, and religious activities were restricted.

Between August and September 1964, the State Council convened a national symposium for believers, emphasizing: ‚ÄúReligion is the opiate of the people; the essence of the religious issue is a class issue, a task exploited by imperialism and domestic reactionary classes; religion is a heavy burden on the heads of the faithful, a spiritual shackle that binds the masses, and a serious obstacle to the struggle for production; systematically weakening religion is the fundamental task in our entire transitional period regarding religion, with the aim of its eventual disappearance.‚Äù

In June 1965, Public Security Minister Xie Fuzhi stated at a national conference that Catholic and Christian churches were ‚Äúthe landlord party,‚Äù ‚Äúa special team of imperialism.‚Äù He advocated addressing rural socialist education movement issues by ‚Äústarting from class struggle, mobilizing the masses thoroughly, to more completely resolve these issues.‚Äù Later, the central authorities disseminated this report nationwide, agreeing to leverage the ‚ÄúFour Cleans‚Äù campaign to ‚Äúpull out these imperialist nails, aiming to dismantle them in rural areas and rendering them ‚Äònominally existent‚Äô in cities.‚Äù

  1. Cultural Revolution‚Äôs Religious Elimination Policy (He Husheng 2004: 114; Duan Dezhi 2013: 117; Chi Nai 1998: 126-127)

As the elimination of religion became a policy goal of the CCP, the constitutional clause regarding ‚Äúfreedom of religious belief‚Äù was shelved. The United Front Department became the first target of liquidation. In August 1966, the Eighth Plenary Session of the Eighth Central Committee of the CCP was convened, where Chen Boda and Jiang Qing pointed out: ‚ÄúAttack the United Front Department,‚Äù branding the Central United Front Department as the ‚Äúheadquarters of revisionism,‚Äù with Minister Xu Bing facing criticism. By the end of November, mass organizations were successively established, and the United Front Department was suspended from work. In July 1968, the Central Committee dispatched Liu Youfa and Zhao Jingxuan as military representatives to oversee the work of the United Front Department. In April 1969, except for those participating in central special project reviews, all staff of the United Front Department were assigned to labor at the Five-Seven Cadre School.

Simultaneously, the religious work departments were labeled by the rebels as ‚Äúimplementing the line of surrenderism and revisionism,‚Äù acting as ‚Äúa refuge and protective umbrella for ‚Äòmonsters and demons.‚Äô‚Äù Religious work cadres were accused of being the ‚Äúloyal children of the landlord class and bourgeoisie,‚Äù while religion was viewed as ‚Äúthe Four Olds,‚Äù with the faithful regarded as ‚Äúmonsters and demons‚Äù and ‚Äútargets of oppression.‚Äù Revolutionary rebels within various local United Front and religious departments rising up to criticize religious workers denounced them for ‚Äúdesperately maintaining reactionary religious forces, trying every means to protect the religious elite.‚Äù In May 1975, the Religious Affairs Bureau was officially abolished.

  1. Wang Chunyi Counter-Revolutionary Case 1962 (Wang Chunyi 1993; Feng Xiaowei n.d.; Wang Zhongxiao 2019)

Wang Chunyi (1913-2006) was arrested on July 2, 1962, and in November 1963, was sentenced to ten years in prison for ‚Äúactively engaging in counter-revolutionary activities under the guise of religion,‚Äù and for involving other believers in participating in ‚Äúillegal ‚Äòfamily meetings,‚Äô‚Äù where they openly attacked and vilified our Party and government policies, cursing our Party and national leaders under the pretext of prayer.

Wang Chunyi was born in Liyang, Jiangsu, and began preaching in 1936, serving at the Baptist Church of True Faith. After the War of Resistance Against Japan, she started refugee assisting work and evangelized near Hangzhou. In 1938, she moved to Hong Kong and returned to Shanghai to work with the Baptist Church in 1940. In 1942, she assisted Zhao Shiguang in establishing the ‚ÄúWorld Food Church Evangelism Association.‚Äù In 1946, she opened the ‚ÄúMorning Star Orphanage‚Äù and established a primary school. In 1947, she went to Canada for theological study and returned to Shanghai the following year to continue serving orphans.

After the establishment of the CCP, the Morning Star Orphanage merged with the Bethany Orphanage in 1952. From 1952 to 1957, she established a preaching site on Emei Road.After the joint worship in 1958, the preaching site was closed. She began personal evangelism and translated Andrew Murray‚Äôs ‚ÄúThe Life of Prayer.‚Äù She organized family prayer meetings, and her translation work was reported to the government by Jiang Mengguang, a pastor from the original Shanghai Shouzhentang (which was closed after the joint worship), resulting in Wang‚Äôs arrest in July 1962. In November 1963, she was sentenced to 10 years in prison and imprisoned in Shanghai Tilanqiao Prison.

Along with Wang Chunyi, those arrested included Qian Pingzhou, who was responsible for proofreading ‚ÄúThe Life of Prayer‚Äù (sentenced to 7 years), and Wang Zhongxiao, who transcribed the work (sentenced to 8 years). Qian Pingzhou (1927-2007) and Wang Zhongxiao (1933-2022) were students at the Shanghai Chinese Spirituality College from 1953 to 1954. Wang graduated in 1957, and Qian graduated in 1958. On New Year‚Äôs Day 1962, Qian, Wang, and Wang Chunyi stayed up all night praying for church revival at Wang Hongli, and later were accused of ‚Äúattempting to restore capitalism.‚Äù Both Qian and Wang were vindicated in 1980.

In prison, Wang Chunyi was assigned the number ‚Äú339.‚Äù Despite facing various pressures to undergo reformation in the prison chapel, enduring group criticism sessions and even physical abuse, she remained steadfast in her faith. In March 1973, after serving her sentence, she was sent to the Maple Hill branch of the Baiyalin Farm in Anhui for labor reformation, where she worked for six years. On June 8, 1979, she was released and returned home to Shanghai. In July, she submitted a petition to the court. In 1980, she began writing her memoirs titled ‚ÄúHow Wonderful.‚Äù In October, the court in Hongkou District of Shanghai declared her innocent and vindicated her. She continued to lead meetings, drawing people to the Lord.

After moving to the United States in 1986 at the age of 73, Wang remained enthusiastic about preaching, serving in a Chinese church in Southern California and at the Bread of Life Church until her retirement in 2003. She passed away on March 24, 2006, at the age of 93.

  1. Beijing Expels Eight Foreign Nuns (People‚Äôs Daily 1966; SCMP 1966a, 1966b, 1966c, 1966d, 1966e)

On September 1, 1966, the People‚Äôs Daily reported: ‚ÄúThe Beijing People‚Äôs Committee accepted the demands of the capital‚Äôs Red Guards and the broad revolutionary masses, announcing on August 26 the dissolution of the Franciscan Missionaries of Mary and taking over the Sacred Heart Convent School to safeguard our nation‚Äôs security and the interests of the people. On August 28, the Beijing Public Security Bureau announced an expulsion order, expelling eight foreign nuns, including Sister Fedorowicz, who engaged in counter-revolutionary activities. Accompanied by our public security personnel and Red Guards, they were expelled from the country on August 31.

The Franciscan Missionaries of Mary was established in 1915 amid imperialist aggression against the Chinese people, following directives from the Roman Generalate. Before liberation, this order colluded with the domestic and foreign reactionary ruling classes, working hand in glove to oppose our people and undermine the liberation of the Chinese people. After liberation, this order, under the command of the Roman Generalate, disguised itself under religious pretenses while using education as a cover, and maintained secret collusion with some counter-revolutionaries in the Catholic Church in locations like Beijing, Hebei, Shanxi, Inner Mongolia, and Harbin, gathering intelligence for our country, distributing reactionary documents, creating and spreading rumors, inciting conspiracies among counter-revolutionaries, and conducting a series of espionage activities that severely endangered our national sovereignty.

During this unprecedented, vigorous Cultural Revolution, armed with Mao Zedong Thought, the young Red Guards rallied to uphold the great red banner of Mao Zedong Thought, sweeping away imperialist and revisionist reactionary forces with overwhelming momentum. They would no longer allow counter-revolutionary nuns serving imperialism to commit misdeeds on our territory. On August 24, Red Guards from more than ten schools, including Beijing Anti-Japanese Military School No. 1 (originally No. 21 Middle School), Beijing No. 12 Girls‚Äô High School, Beijing Hongqi School, and Beijing Anti-Japanese Military School No. 2 (formerly No. 1 Girls‚Äô School) rose to rebel against the counter-revolutionary nun order. This revolutionary action immediately garnered the most resolute and enthusiastic support from the broad masses of workers, farmers, soldiers, revolutionary cadres, and revolutionary intellectuals.

On August 26, the Beijing People‚Äôs Committee accepted the firm demands of the young Red Guards and the broad revolutionary masses, announcing at a mass meeting the dissolution of the Franciscan Missionaries of Mary and the takeover of the Sacred Heart School, which was met with enthusiastic support from the Red Guards and the broader revolutionary masses. Representatives of the Red Guards spoke at the meeting, angrily exposing the crimes committed by this order and firmly supporting the decision of the city‚Äôs people‚Äôs committee.On the evening of August 28, the Beijing Public Security Bureau announced at a mass meeting the expulsion of Sister Fedorowicz and seven other foreign nuns engaged in counter-revolutionary activities, executing it immediately. That night, the crowd gathered inside and outside the convent, with impassioned expressions. Many representatives from the Red Guards and revolutionary masses, as well as victims who had suffered from the oppression and abuse of the reactionary nun order, rushed to speak at the meeting, denouncing the crimes committed by the order and expressing enthusiastic support for the expulsion order announced by the Beijing Public Security Bureau. The attendees chanted: ‚ÄúDown with U.S. imperialism!‚Äù ‚ÄúCounter-revolutionary foreign nuns, get out!‚Äù ‚ÄúLong live the dictatorship of the proletariat!‚Äù ‚ÄúLong live the Communist Party of China!‚Äù ‚ÄúLong live Chairman Mao! Ten thousand years!‚Äù

According to a September 1 report from the Hong Kong South China Morning Post, the eight nuns entered Hong Kong through the Lo Wu border on August 31, 1966. They had been escorted by Red Guards from Beijing to Guangzhou by train and stated they had been prohibited from sleep for eight days. Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

  1. Sino-Soviet Split (Radchenko 2010: 349-372; Robinson 1991: 254-275)

The Sino-Soviet split of the 1960s was a significant event in international relations during the Cold War, driven both by ideological differences and geopolitical conflicts of interest.

The ideological divide was the main root of the deterioration in Sino-Soviet relations. In the 1950s, after the Soviet Union established communist regimes in Eastern Europe, it sought to expand its model to other nations, while China adopted a socialist system after the establishment of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in 1949. However, as China‚Äôs economic and political situation evolved, Mao Zedong became dissatisfied with the Soviet policy of ‚Äúpeaceful coexistence‚Äù and Khrushchev‚Äôs ‚Äúde-Stalinization,‚Äù believing that the Soviet Union had deviated from the revolutionary spirit of Marxism. The Soviet Union‚Äôs unilateral withdrawal of technical experts from China in 1960 became the immediate spark that broke relations between the two countries. Throughout the 1960s, the split escalated into an open ideological struggle, with both sides vying for leadership in the international communist movement; China criticized the Soviet Union as ‚Äúrevisionist,‚Äù while the Soviet Union accused China of ‚Äúleftist adventurism.‚Äù

Additionally, border conflicts between China and the Soviet Union shifted the confrontation from ideological to military. The Sino-Soviet border stretches for thousands of kilometers, and historical boundary issues led to the 1969 conflict over Zhenbao Island. Although this armed conflict was limited in scale, it highlighted the hostility between the two sides. Subsequently, China reinforced its national defense and sought to improve relations with the United States to counterbalance the Soviet Union.

From a geopolitical perspective, the Soviet Union aimed to maintain its dominance in East Asia, while China sought an independent diplomatic avenue. By the end of the 1960s, China initiated the ‚ÄúThird Line Construction‚Äù program in response to potential threats from the Soviet Union amidst economic difficulties. On the other hand, the Soviet Union strengthened ties with countries like Mongolia and North Korea in an attempt to isolate China.

  1. Sino-American Relations (Pollack 1991: 402-426)

Sino-American relations in the 1960s were characterized by hostility and were an important component of the Cold War framework. Due to ideological differences, geopolitical confrontations, and conflicts of interest in Asia, there was virtually no formal diplomatic exchange between China and the United States during this period. However, subtle adjustments gradually emerged due to changes in the international situation, laying the groundwork for improved relations in the 1970s.

In the early 1960s, Sino-American relations were extremely tense. After the establishment of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in 1949, the United States refused to recognize the new China and supported the Republic of China government in Taiwan in maintaining China‚Äôs seat in the United Nations. During the Korean War in the 1950s, direct military confrontations deepened mutual hostility. Throughout the 1960s, the United States continued its policy of isolating China, imposing a comprehensive trade embargo and blocking other nations from establishing diplomatic relations with China.

By the mid-1960s, the Vietnam War became a significant battlefield for Sino-American confrontation. China supported the communist government in North Vietnam by providing military supplies and economic assistance, while the United States backed the government of South Vietnam and sent a large number of troops to intervene in the war. The Vietnam War not only continued the ideological confrontation between China and the United States but also reflected the intensifying competition for influence in Asia. During the 1960s, the U.S. military presence in Taiwan was seen by China as a significant symbol of an infringement on national sovereignty, further deepening the adversarial stance between the two sides.

Beginning in 1966, the Cultural Revolution made China‚Äôs internal and external policies more radical. This strategic consideration created the conditions for a thaw in relations between the two sides in the early 1970s.Sino-American relations in the 1960s were characterized by confrontation and isolation, yet were also profoundly influenced by changes in the international situation. Although both sides failed to achieve direct contact, geopolitical pressures, particularly the Sino-Soviet split, set the stage for an improvement in Sino-American relations in the later years of the Cold War. The intertwining of hostility and adjustments during this period constituted a significant turning point in the development of Sino-American relations.
