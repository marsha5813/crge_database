China: Context (1980-1990)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Regulations Related to Religion

  1. Report on Implementing the Property Policy of Religious Organizations and Related Issues 1980 (Compiled by the Comprehensive Research Team of the Central Document Research Office of the CPC, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 23-26)

On July 16, 1980, the State Council approved the Report on Implementing the Property Policy of Religious Organizations and Related Issues submitted by the State Administration for Religious Affairs, the State Construction Commission, and other units, proposing solutions to the property issues of religious organizations: (1) All property rights of religious organization buildings should be returned to the religious organizations; for those that cannot be returned, compensation should be provided. Whether the rented part can be reclaimed by the religious organization for their own operation may be decided based on local conditions; (2) The rental fees that were suspended since the Cultural Revolution should be settled according to national regulations. After deducting maintenance costs, property tax, and management fees from the rents collected, any excess will be refunded, and any deficit will not be compensated; (3) Churches, temples, and their affiliated buildings occupied during the Cultural Revolution, which need to continue to be open for internal and external work, should be returned for use by the respective religious organizations. If the religious organizations do not need them for their own use, the occupying units or individuals should pay rent from the date of occupation; for those that have been reconstructed or demolished, compensation should be provided; (4) Deposits of various religious organizations frozen during the Cultural Revolution should be refunded, and those misappropriated by other units should be repaid.

  1. Request Report on Resisting Religious Infiltration by Foreign Churches 1980 (Hubei Provincial Bureau of Ethnic and Religious Affairs 1988: 10-19)

In February 1980, the State Administration for Religious Affairs, the Ministry of Public Security, and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs submitted the Request Report on Resisting Religious Infiltration by Foreign Churches to the Central Committee of the CPC and the State Council. The so-called ‚Äúforeign churches‚Äù refer to the Roman Catholic Church and overseas Christian groups that, taking advantage of China‚Äôs reform and opening up, have ‚Äúplanned and organized‚Äù religious infiltration into our country, which involves: (1) Gathering intelligence and conveying the intentions of the Vatican and foreign churches; (2) Conducting missionary activities to expand religious influence; (3) Using money and goods as bait for bribery; (4) Activities by foreign churches abroad that have not ceased, such as gospel broadcasting and the supply of Bibles and religious materials.

The report points out that the ‚Äúinfiltration of foreign reactionary religious forces‚Äù also has a ‚Äúdomestic soil suitable for foreign infiltration‚Äù: (1) China still has a significant social foundation for the existence and development of religion; (2) During the time of Lin Biao and the Gang of Four, many people were persecuted and sought religious solace due to mental trauma; (3) The religious policy and united front policy have not been fully implemented, resulting in dissatisfaction among the religious community and leaving gaps.

Therefore, the report proposes five measures: (1) Effectively implement the spirit of the central documents related to religious work, enforce religious policies, and quickly address the issues faced by the religious community in their religious life; cultivate a team of active members within the religious community and strengthen the united front work with religious personnel; (2) Conduct political and ideological education for religious personnel and the religious community; (3) Further improve the work of various churches, including restoring sound patriotic organizations for Catholicism and Christianity, assisting them in playing their roles, and establishing administrative bodies for Catholicism and Christianity to manage church affairs independently, with the patriotic organizations operating under ‚Äúone organization, two names‚Äù; restoring previous religious schools to train personnel with certain religious knowledge and a patriotic political stance, assisting religious organizations or church administrative bodies to publish necessary scriptures as soon as possible; (4) Quickly resolve the self-supporting issues of churches, such as releasing the unpaid rents during the Cultural Revolution in installments, but government assistance should not be excessive to avoid forming ‚Äúgovernment-operated churches‚Äù; (5) Strengthen the approval and management of church personnel entering the country, and for those confirmed to have missions to China, do not issue entry visas, and when hiring foreign experts, enhance scrutiny to prevent church personnel from infiltrating.

  1. Document No. 19 1982 (Compiled by the Comprehensive Research Team of the Central Document Research Office of the CPC, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 53-73; MacInnis 1989: 8-26)

In March 1982, the Central Committee of the CPC issued the Basic Views and Policies on Religious Issues in Our Socialist Period (Document No. 19), which became the first important religious policy document during the reform and opening up period. The document includes 12 chapters: (1) Explaining why, during the socialist period in China, religion has not disappeared as expected according to Marxism, but rather has shown growth, and concluding that religion will ‚Äúlong exist‚Äù in socialist China. At the same time, it reiterates that ‚Äúthe idea that religion will quickly disappear with the establishment of the socialist system and a certain level of economic and cultural development is unrealistic. The notion that religion can be eradicated at once through administrative orders or other administrative means is even further from the basic views of Marxism on the religious issue; it is completely wrong and harmful.‚Äù

  1. It mentions that China is a country with multiple religions, with the number of believers being over ten million for Islam, over three million for Catholicism, and three million for Christianity. Buddhism (including Lamaism) is almost universally practiced among the Tibetan, Mongolian, and Dai ethnic groups, while Buddhism and Daoism also have a certain influence among the Han people. Since the founding of the CPC, ‚Äúafter profound changes in the social and economic systems and significant reforms in the religious system,‚Äù the religious issues in China ‚Äúhave mainly become internal contradictions among the people,‚Äù but ‚Äúare still influenced by certain class struggles and complex international factors.‚Äù Therefore, they cannot be taken lightly, nor should they be left unattended.

  1. It reviews the significant achievements in religious work since the founding of the CPC, noting that since 1957, mistakes of a ‚Äúleftist‚Äù nature appeared in religious work, worsening further in the 1960s. The Cultural Revolution completely negated the correct policies regarding religious issues, prohibiting believers from engaging in normal religious life and treating patriotic religious individuals as ‚Äútargets of suppression,‚Äù while producing a large number of wrongful cases in the religious community. Current religious work must oppose ‚Äúleftist‚Äù errors, but also pay attention to preventing and overcoming the tendency of laissez-faire attitudes. (4) It reaffirms that ‚Äúrespecting and protecting the freedom of religious belief is the party‚Äôs basic policy on religious issues.‚Äù At the same time, it emphasizes the guarantee of ‚Äúpeople‚Äôs freedom not to believe in religion.‚Äù ‚ÄúIt is absolutely forbidden for religion to interfere in state administration, judicial matters, school education, and social public education; it is absolutely forbidden to compel anyone, especially minors under eighteen, to join a religion, become a monk, or attend temple schools; it is absolutely forbidden to restore the abolished feudal privileges of religion and the systems of religious oppression and exploitation; it is absolutely forbidden to use religion to oppose the party‚Äôs leadership and the socialist system, undermining national unity and the solidarity of various ethnic groups within the country.‚Äù It calls for all believers and non-believers to unite in striving to ‚Äúbuild a modern socialist strong country.‚Äù

After establishing the above principled issues, Document No. 19 gives directives on different aspects of religious work: (5) To strive for, unite, and educate religious personnel; (6) To reasonably arrange places for religious activities, specifically mentioning that ‚Äúthe gathering of Christians for religious activities at home should generally not be allowed, but should not be forcibly stopped; rather, it should be handled through patriotic religious personnel to persuade the believers and make appropriate arrangements,‚Äù while also emphasizing that ‚Äúall places for religious activities fall under the administrative leadership of the government‚Äôs religious affairs departments and are managed by religious organizations and religious professionals‚Äù; (7) To fully leverage the role of patriotic religious organizations; (8) To cultivate and educate a younger generation of patriotic religious professionals in a planned manner, continuously raising awareness of patriotism and socialism, striving to improve cultural levels and religious knowledge, and faithfully implementing the party‚Äôs religious policies; (9) Communist Party members must not believe in religion and must not participate in religious activities; (10) To persist in combating all illegal criminal activities and counter-revolutionary destructive activities hidden under the guise of religion, as well as various superstitious activities that do not belong to the scope of religion and endanger national interests and people‚Äôs lives and property; (11) To develop friendly relations in religious aspects on the basis of independence and self-reliance, maintaining high vigilance and closely monitoring the establishment of underground churches and other illegal organizations by foreign religious hostile forces in our country, as well as preventing espionage activities disguised under religious cover; (12) To strengthen party leadership and improve and enhance the government‚Äôs institutions responsible for religious affairs.

Finally, the document reaffirms the vision of the natural dissolution of religion, but emphasizes the gradual elimination of the social roots and cognitive foundations that allow religion to exist, stating that ‚Äúonly through a long historical period and the joint struggle of several generations, including both vast believers and non-believers, can this be achieved.‚Äù ‚ÄúAt that time, the Chinese people will completely free themselves from any state of poverty, ignorance, and spiritual emptiness on this land, creating a bright world of highly developed material and spiritual civilization, standing at the forefront of humanity. By then, the vast majority of citizens in our country will consciously adopt a scientific attitude towards the world and life, and will no longer need to seek spiritual solace from the illusory world of gods. This is the era described by Marx and Engels where all social life is under the conscious and planned control of humans, free from the domination of all alien forces, and also the era described by Comrade Mao Zedong where people consciously transform themselves and the world. Only in such an era will the various reflections of religion in the real world finally disappear. Our entire party must work tirelessly from generation to generation to achieve this glorious prospect.

  1. Constitution of the People‚Äôs Republic of China 1982 (Compiled by the Comprehensive Research Team of the Central Document Research Office of the CPC, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 77; Legislative Affairs Office of the Standing Committee of the National People‚Äôs Congress 1996: 158-159; MacInnis 1989: 34-35; Ying 2006: 348-349)

On December 4, 1982, the fifth session of the fifth National People‚Äôs Congress voted to adopt the Constitution of the People‚Äôs Republic of China (1982), which is the fourth constitution since the founding of the CPC. Article 36 stipulates:

The citizens of the People‚Äôs Republic of China enjoy freedom of religious belief.

No state organ, social group, or individual may coerce citizens to believe in religion or to not believe in religion, nor may they discriminate against citizens who believe in religion or those who do not.

The state protects normal religious activities. No one may use religion to disrupt social order, harm citizens‚Äô health, or interfere with the national education system.

Religious organizations and religious affairs are not subject to foreign control.

The above provisions reveal two attitudes of the party-state towards religion. The first is based on safeguarding the right to religious belief freedom, which is reflected in the first and second clauses of this article. Compared to the ‚Äú75 Constitution‚Äù and the ‚Äú78 Constitution,‚Äù the ‚Äú82 Constitution‚Äù does not add the stipulation of ‚Äúthe freedom to not believe in religion or to promote atheism‚Äù while affirming the freedom to believe in religion. Instead, it addresses past extreme ‚Äúleftist‚Äù policies by stating that citizens who believe in religion should not be discriminated against. As Li Weihan, who had long engaged in united front work, stated in 1961, ‚ÄúProviding freedom to both believers and non-believers effectively protects people‚Äôs freedom to not believe, promotes a retreat from religious belief rather than promoting the belief itself.‚Äù It can be said that the ‚Äú82 Constitution‚Äù reaffirms that ‚Äúcitizens of the People‚Äôs Republic of China enjoy freedom of religious belief,‚Äù discarding the previous expression of ‚Äúthe freedom to not believe in religion or to promote atheism,‚Äù which represents significant progress. During the process of amending the constitution, some opinions suggested retaining the provision of the ‚Äú75 Constitution,‚Äù which stated that ‚Äúcitizens have the freedom to believe in religion and to not believe in religion as well as to promote atheism.‚Äù However, other opinions argued that making such a provision would raise doubts and dissatisfaction among many religious believers, and the constitutional amendment committee ultimately adopted the latter opinion. To eliminate the issue of one-sided interpretation of this clause, making it understandable that the freedom of religious belief includes both the aspects of not being coerced into belief and not believing, the committee added the provision of the second clause as a supplement to the first.

The second attitude delineates the boundaries of religious belief freedom by setting restrictive regulations, which is reflected in clauses three to five of the article. Freedom of religious belief is not an absolute freedom; its granting and enjoyment must be predicated on not violating certain bottom lines. What constitutes ‚Äúnormal‚Äù? What constitutes ‚Äúabnormal‚Äù? Perhaps the fourth and fifth sentences can be viewed as negative annotations to ‚Äúnormal.‚Äù As long as religious activities do not ‚Äúdisrupt social order, harm citizens‚Äô health, or interfere with the national education system,‚Äù and are ‚Äúnot subject to foreign control,‚Äù they can be understood as ‚Äúnormal‚Äù and thus protected by the state.

In the draft, a provision was retained stating that ‚Äúno one may use religion to conduct counter-revolutionary activities,‚Äù but ultimately, the constitutional amendment committee accepted the opinions of the religious community and decided to remove it.

  1. Central Committee of the Communist Party of China‚Äôs Instructions on Correctly Addressing Religious Issues in the Campaign to Eliminate Spiritual Pollution 1983 (Compiled by the Comprehensive Research Team of the Central Document Research Office of the CPC, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 101-104)

In 1983, conservative factions within the CPC launched the ‚ÄúCampaign to Eliminate Spiritual Pollution‚Äù in response to the ‚Äúalienation‚Äù phenomena in the literary and artistic circles and ideological spheres since the reform and opening up.

On December 31, the Central Committee issued instructions on how to correctly address religious issues in the campaign to eliminate spiritual pollution, reaffirming: (1) Protecting freedom of religious belief and normal religious activities is the party‚Äôs fundamental policy on religious issues, which is also explicitly stated in the constitution. ‚ÄúReligion and spiritual pollution are two separate matters‚Äù that must be distinguished; (2) One cannot oppose the implementation of the party‚Äôs religious policies against the elimination of spiritual pollution; (3) Distinguishing normal religious activities from superstitious activities that do not belong to the realm of religion and that endanger national interests and the lives and property of the people; (4) It is necessary to distinguish normal religious activities from ‚Äúillegal religious activities‚Äù that exceed the constitutional, legal, and party policy boundaries; (6) No one should promote atheism in religious venues or incite debates on theism or atheism among believers. Religious organizations are also not permitted to proselytize or promote theism outside of religious activity venues. Additionally, one should not use literary works and publications, broadcasting, or television ‚Äúto carry out propaganda to expand religious influence.‚Äù

  1. Investigation Report on Implementing the Party‚Äôs Religious Policies and Related Issues 1985 (Compiled by the Comprehensive Research Team of the Central Document Research Office of the CPC, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 136-145)

On December 29, 1985, the General Office of the Central Committee of the CPC and the General Office of the State Council forwarded the Central Investigation Team‚Äôs Investigation Report on Implementing the Party‚Äôs Religious Policies and Related Issues. The report mainly investigated the key temples and monasteries of Buddhism and Daoism in Han regions. It noted that ‚Äúamong the key temples and monasteries nationwide, about half are managed well or relatively well,‚Äù due to strong core personnel among the monks and initial democratic management systems. In addition to normal religious activities, some also engage in production, service industries, and other social welfare projects, contributing to society. However, more than half of the temples are poorly managed or not adequately maintained, mainly due to issues stemming from the handover, such as conflicts between the monastic personnel and workers, their families, and the internal problems regarding personal conduct.

  1. Religious Legislation in China (Xing Fuzeng 2006: 7-14; Ying 2006: 351-353)

In the 1982 ‚ÄúDocument No. 19,‚Äù it was stated that practical and feasible religious regulations should be formulated. However, the religious community actively sought, and even earnestly hoped for, a comprehensive Religious Law. In June 1989, based on the opinions of relevant central leaders, the State Administration for Religious Affairs decided to shift the focus from drafting a Religious Law to drafting individual administrative regulations concerning religious affairs.

The difficulties encountered in formulating the Religious Law were attributed to substantial differences in opinion among relevant parties on the legislative purpose, definitions of religion, and other principle issues, which made it impossible to reach a consensus. Regarding the purpose of legislation, the religious community demanded that the Religious Law focus on a ‚Äúrights-based‚Äù approach to ensure effective legal protection for believers‚Äô freedom of religion, assembly, and association. It simultaneously aimed to further establish the autonomy of religious organizations in personnel, finance, economics, and operations. In contrast, the Chinese government framed its approach based on ‚Äúobligations,‚Äù seeing the management of religious affairs as the fundamental spirit of legislation. The political and religious sides held differing opinions on the legislative purpose. The Chinese government‚Äôs concern was that framing the purpose of legislation as the protection of religious freedom would create a more lenient social environment for the development of religion, thus promoting the rapid growth of certain religions.

Another emerging issue in the process of religious legislation in China was how to define religion. There were numerous differing views in academic circles regarding what constituted religion, and thus, the legislative process primarily focused on the issue of what constituted a ‚Äúlegitimate‚Äù religion. When the State Administration for Religious Affairs drafted the Religious Law in the late 1980s, the regulations team held discussions with relevant departments and religious groups, where the ‚Äúissue of legitimate religions‚Äù was raised. A representative from Buddhism suggested that the Religious Law only needs to define which religions are legally recognized, without addressing what qualifies as religion. This approach could consider defining religion as ‚Äúthe religions referred to in this Law are the Buddhism, Daoism, Islam, Catholicism, Christianity, and other religious entities based on common religious beliefs with corresponding doctrines, regulations, rituals, and organizations recognized in the People‚Äôs Republic of China‚Äù or ‚Äúthe religions referred to in this Law are the Buddhism, Daoism, Islam, Catholicism, Christianity, and other religious entities registered in accordance with this Law.‚Äù At that time, the regulations team proposed three possible criteria for defining legitimate religions: First, to include Eastern Orthodoxy alongside the existing five major religions (Buddhism, Daoism, Islam, Catholicism, Christianity); second, to include folk religions and certain minority religions in addition to these six; and third, not to specify which religions are included but consider the five major religions, folk religions, and minority religions while preventing heretical sects from taking advantage of the situation. The core of the issue was not merely whether specific religions were legitimate, but whether the CPC was willing to fully open (or relax restrictions on) the religious market in China, allowing various different religions to spread and develop. Furthermore, while ‚Äúopening‚Äù the religious market, how to prevent reactionary sects from rising again and causing negative consequences for national security and social order was also a focus of concern for the relevant authorities. The CPC recognized that various uncertainties might arise from opening the religious market, including an increase in the number of religious believers, the potential for religious disputes and conflicts, and how to regulate and manage various forms of religions. These changes would undoubtedly pose significant challenges to the current religious management work, leading to the decision to suspend the drafting of the Religious Law.
