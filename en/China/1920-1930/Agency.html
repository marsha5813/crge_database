The Anti-Christian Movement can be divided into two phases. The first phase erupted in April 1922 in response to the World Student Christian Federation‚Äôs annual meeting held in Beijing. In 1924, the Shanghai ‚ÄúAnti-Christian Federation‚Äù was reorganized, marking the beginning of the second phase of the Anti-Christian Movement. The ‚ÄúDeclaration of the First National Congress of the Kuomintang‚Äù clearly pointed out that imperialism and warlords were the roots of China‚Äôs turmoil, stating that only by opposing imperialism could China achieve national freedom and independence. The ‚ÄúAnti-Christian Federation‚Äù became an important platform for the Kuomintang-Communist cooperation against imperialism, and both parties used their respective propaganda publications (such as the Kuomintang‚Äôs ‚ÄúRepublic Daily‚Äù and the Communist Party‚Äôs ‚ÄúChinese Youth,‚Äù ‚ÄúThe Guide‚Äù) to publish numerous articles accusing Christianity of being a tool of imperialism in China.   1. Educational Rights Movement, 1922-1927 (Yang Tianhong 1994: 241-276; Yang Cuihua 1984: 235-289; Lutz 1988: 91-159)

The Educational Rights Movement was an important aspect of the Anti-Christian Movement and ultimately implemented through government regulations. Its primary focus targeted the educational enterprises established by Christianity in China.

According to the ‚ÄúChristian Education in China: The Report of the China Education Commission of 1921-1922‚Äù published in 1922, there were 6,890 church-run primary and secondary schools across the country, with a total of 199,604 students. If various types of Christian educational institutions (such as Bible schools, seminaries, special education, orphanages, nurse training, etc.) are included, there were a total of 7,382 Christian educational institutions, serving 214,254 students. At that time, the total number of students at all levels nationwide was 5,700,000. The number of students enrolled in Christian educational institutions accounted for only 3.76% of the total student population. However, in higher education, students attending Christian universities (2,017) constituted 7.9% of students in national universities (25,373) (China Education Commission 1922: 371-374).

After the establishment of the Republic, the issue of Christian-run education began to attract the attention of Chinese intellectuals. Many educators (such as Cai Yuanpei) advocated for the separation of religion from education, especially criticizing the religious courses and activities in church schools. The proselytization to minors was particularly condemned by educational scholars. Amid the wave of nationalism in the 1920s, students, driven by patriotic feelings, participated in student movements, and church schools were no exception. However, the missionaries leading the church schools took a high-handed approach to prohibit such activities, creating conflicts and opposition between students and the school authorities. During this period, some students began to protest against ‚Äúenslavement‚Äù education. Consequently, church schools found themselves at odds with Chinese nationalism.

In 1924, the number of missionaries in China reached a historical peak of 7,663. However, during 1927, nearly 3,000 missionaries left China (including those on leave in 1928 and some who returned early in 1929), resulting in a sharp decline in the number of missionaries to 3,113. The primary reason for the withdrawal of missionaries was the instability of the political situation in China. In July 1926, Chiang Kai-shek was appointed commander of the National Revolutionary Army and issued the mobilization order for the Northern Expedition, launching an offensive from Guangdong. Within six months, he defeated the two major warlords, Wu Peifu and Sun Chuanfang, expanding the National Government‚Äôs control from Guangdong to the provinces of Hunan, Hubei, Jiangxi, and Fujian. In January 1927, the Northern Expedition Army forcibly took over the British concessions in Hankou and Jiujiang, prompting foreign nationals across various locations along the Yangtze River to retreat to Shanghai. In March, Shanghai and Nanjing fell into the hands of the revolutionary army. After entering Nanjing, on March 24, radicals attacked British, American, and Japanese consulates and foreign churches, schools, hospitals, and shops, resulting in the tragic deaths of some missionaries in what became known as the ‚ÄúNanjing Incident.‚Äù

In provinces reached by the Northern Expedition Army, missionaries retreated to nearby provinces to observe the situation. Following the recovery of the British concession in Hankou, more missionaries withdrew. Simultaneously, various regions experienced anti-Christian mass movements. For instance, in Christmas 1926, Hunan hosted an ‚ÄúAnti-Christian Week,‚Äù featuring marches and speeches, with attacks on churches, and missionaries being paraded and bound in public, along with churches, church schools, hospitals, and youth associations being occupied.
