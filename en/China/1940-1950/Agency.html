Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

  1. Zhao Shiguang

Zhao Shiguang (Timothy Dzao, 1908-1973), originally named Zhao Yuanchang, was born in Yuyao, Zhejiang Province, but grew up in a non-Christian family in Shanghai. He received his education at church schools and joined Sunday School in 1921 under the guidance of his cousin. In December 1924, Zhao was baptized at the Methodist Church‚Äôs Moulton Chapel in Shanghai, adopting the name Shiguang. In 1925, he participated in a spiritual retreat led by Yu Cidu in Shanghai and later attended a revival meeting conducted by the British pastor Wilkes Paget, where he resolved to dedicate himself to preaching. After graduating from high school in 1926, he continued his studies at the Shanghai Evangelical Church‚Äôs Shoucheng Theological School while also teaching at a nearby elementary school. He began working at Shoucheng Church in July 1928.

From 1930 onwards, Zhang led revival meetings in Hangzhou, leading to spiritual awakenings. In 1932, he was ordained as a pastor by Baptist pastor Evans. In 1935 and 1939, he served as a speaker at the Guangdong-Hong Kong Spiritual Conferences, leading Bible studies and revival sermons.

Between 1936 and 1941, Zhao accepted an invitation from the China Inland Mission‚Äôs R. A. Jeffray to preach in the Nanyang islands (Philippines, Borneo, Indonesia, Thailand, etc.). After returning to Shanghai, he founded the Bread of Life Church in January 1942, initially operating in the auditorium of Shanghai Joint Education School, and later establishing branches in Nanjing, Hangzhou, Suzhou, and other locations. He also launched the magazine ‚ÄúSpirit Food,‚Äù which included the publication of hymns. In 1945, he established the ‚ÄúBread of Life World Mission,‚Äù followed by the establishment of the Bread of Life Theological Institute and East China Theological College in Suzhou. The seminary advocated for a life of faith, rejecting fundraising and only accepting voluntary donations from believers. At the time, he observed approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees in Shanghai and sent staff to lead Bible studies in English for the Jewish community.

In March 1949, Zhao traveled to the U.S. to lead meetings and shared the needs of the Chinese church at a missionary convention. Returning through Hong Kong, he preached at the Guangdong-Hong Kong Spiritual Meetings. After leaving Hong Kong, he spent over a month in Taipei before returning to Shanghai. During his stay in Taipei, he decided to bring his family back to leave China. On October 1, they departed Shanghai, traveling first by sea to Taipei and then transferring to Hong Kong. In November, he hosted the first worship service of the Bread of Life Church in Hong Kong at the Kowloon Baptist Church. Starting January 1, 1950, he rented a theater to hold Sunday revival evangelistic meetings, with advertisements placed on the front pages of the China Daily, Sing Tao Daily, and Hong Kong Commercial Daily, promoting it with the theme ‚ÄúThe Great Event of 1950.‚Äù Attendance reached 1,200 that day. Subsequently, he utilized the Salvation Army and other churches to conduct a 30-day evangelistic series and held another 40-day series at Xun Guang School. In September 1951, after the completion of the new church building at 22 Gai Lin Road in Kowloon, they celebrated with another 100 days of evangelism. In 1958, he was consecrated as a bishop in the Bread of Life Church.

Starting in the second half of 1951, the Chinese church underwent an intensive movement of denunciation. During the denunciation conferences held at the Shanghai Bread of Life Church, Zhao Shiguang became a target of internal criticism, being labeled a tool of American imperialism and an enemy of the Chinese people. In July 1952, Zhao moved his family to Indonesia, while still serving as the president of the ‚ÄúBread of Life World Mission,‚Äù with the headquarters remaining in Hong Kong.

After leaving Hong Kong, he traveled to various places, including Java, Borneo, Bangka, Sumatra, Indonesia, Thailand, and Japan, leading evangelistic meetings and establishing branches of the Bread of Life Church. From February to May 1955, Zhao traveled to Europe and the United States, stopping in places like America, Sweden, the Netherlands, France, Switzerland, Italy, and Athens. From October to November, he also visited London, Germany, and Yugoslavia. By 1967, his evangelistic efforts had spread to Vietnam, the United States (Philadelphia, Los Angeles, San Francisco), France, India, Indonesia, the United Kingdom, Africa, the Philippines, Japan, and Taiwan.

In 1955, he was awarded an honorary Doctor of Laws degree from Taylor University in the United States. In 1965, he was elected president of the Fellowship of Asian Evangelicals.

Zhao Shiguang‚Äôs health began to decline in 1972, and he passed away on September 13, 1973, at the age of 65.

In January 1930, The Amethyst: A Journal of Christian Fellowship, Worship and Devotion, edited by Liu Tingfang, was first published. Its content primarily focused on spirituality, worship, and fellowship. Unlike many debate-oriented publications of the Republic era, The Amethyst emphasized the creation of prayers, hymns, and liturgies, representing a new approach to constructing an indigenous Chinese church.

In the editorial titled ‚ÄúA Conversation with Friends Meeting The Amethyst for the First Time,‚Äù the importance of ‚Äúpersonal devotion,‚Äù ‚Äúprivate spirituality,‚Äù and ‚Äúpublic worship‚Äù in Christian life was emphasized. The mission of The Amethyst was established as follows: (1) ‚Äúto center on spirituality, worship, and fellowship‚Äù; (2) ‚Äúto employ experimentation, discussion, and exchange as methods‚Äù; (3) the content was to include discussions on fellowship, preaching, reflections on spirituality, hymns, prayers (both personal and communal), liturgical practices, notes, book reviews, and miscellaneous writings. The publication was not affiliated with any specific organization but was the collaborative effort of Liu Tingfang, Yang Yinliu, Xie Jingsheng, Xu Dishan, and others dedicated to experimenting with spirituality, worship, and fellowship.

Several petitions for prayer related to the crisis were included, reflecting the urgent need for spiritual engagement during a turbulent time.

  1. Jiang Changchuan

Jiang Changchuan (Z. T. Kaung, 1884-1958) was elected as the first Chinese bishop of the Methodist Church in China in 1941. Born in Shanghai, he attended a Methodist school at the age of 14 and was instructed by a female missionary, Clara E. Steger, being baptized at 19. He became the first Christian in his family. After graduating from the Anglo-Chinese College in 1909, Jiang aspired to serve the church and studied theology at Soochow University, graduating in 1912 as its sole theology graduate.

Jiang was later appointed as an assistant pastor at Moulton Chapel in Shanghai and became a full pastor the following year. He served for three years at Haidao Church in Huzhou, Zhejiang, before moving to Suzhou and becoming district superintendent, and then moving to Shanghai‚Äôs Jinglin Church.

In December 1927, Soong Mei-ling married Chiang Kai-shek. Since Chiang was not yet a Christian, Jiang did not officiate their wedding. On October 23, 1930, Chiang Kai-shek was baptized by Jiang at his home, becoming a member of the Methodist Church.

Outside of his pastoral duties, Jiang was appointed to the board of his alma mater, Soochow University, in 1924, becoming one of the first Chinese board members. He served as chair of the board from 1928 to 1940 and again from 1945 to 1947.

Jiang attended the American Methodist Church conferences in the U.S. five times between 1919 and 1940. In 1926, he was awarded an honorary Doctor of Theology from Asbury University. He served as president of the Chinese Student Missionary Society from 1920 to 1930 and was the chair of the Suzhou and Zhejiang Annual Conference from 1934 to 1941, also holding roles such as president of the National Sunday School Association in 1937.

In 1939, the American Methodist Church, the Methodist Episcopal Church, and the Methodist Protestant Church united to form the Methodist Church (USA). In 1941, these three organizations convened an unification conference to establish the Methodist Church of China, and Jiang was elected as its first Chinese bishop, serving in Beiping to oversee church affairs in northern China.

After Beiping was occupied by the Japanese military, Jiang was appointed to lead the organization under the influence of Japanese authorities in October 1942. Despite internal pressure and being designated ‚Äúuncooperative‚Äù by the Japanese military, he maintained his duties while trying to keep a distance from the Japanese church authorities.

  1. The Life of Liu Tingfang

Liu Tingfang (Timothy T. Lew, 1891-1947) was a third-generation Christian born in Wenzhou, Zhejiang Province. His family had ties to the China Inland Mission. Despite facing numerous challenges during the war and political upheaval, Liu continued to champion the cause of the church and actively engage with social issues affecting the community. His contributions underscored a commitment to the integration of faith and social responsibility.

  1. North China Christian Union, 1942 (Xing Fuzeng 2006: 104-121; Song Jun 2011: 197-220; Brook 1996: 331-337)

On December 8, 1941, the Pacific War broke out, and Britain and America became belligerent nations against Japan, causing Christianity to be seen as a tool of Anglo-American influence. A large number of British and American missionaries in occupied areas were sent to concentration camps. The policy of the Japanese military towards Christianity in occupied areas was, on one hand, backed by coercive force, and on the other hand, it was a dual approach of ‚Äúappeasement‚Äù and ‚Äútransformation.‚ÄùIn relation to Christianity, there was a specific demand to establish an ‚Äúadaptive system,‚Äù which meant adopting Japan‚Äôs policy of transforming Christianity domestically, promoting a complete severance of ties between Christianity and Western denominations, and uniting various sects into the formation of a Christian alliance. Consequently, similar united Christian organizations were formed in various occupied areas. For instance, in 1942, the ‚ÄúManchurian Christian Church‚Äù was established by the united denominations of Christianity in Northeast China, ending the Manchurian Christian Alliance. In areas such as Shanghai and Hangzhou, the ‚ÄúEast China Christian Alliance‚Äù and the ‚ÄúSino-Japanese Christian Agreement Association‚Äù were formed, respectively. In Northwest China, the ‚ÄúMongolia and Xinjiang Christian Union‚Äù was created, while Shandong established the ‚ÄúShandong Chinese Christian Church,‚Äù and Hankou had the ‚ÄúCentral China Christian Federation.‚Äù

In North China, the ‚ÄúNorth China Christian United Promotion Association‚Äù was established in Beijing in April 1942. In September 1942, the Ministry of Internal Affairs of the North China Political Affairs Committee convened various Christian churches for a North China Christian seminar. The seminar resolved three main points: (1) to quickly organize a North China Chinese Christian Union; (2) to draft a Christian faith outline; and (3) to draft a Chinese Christian constitution. To facilitate the early establishment of the union, the Japanese side convened the second preparatory meeting of the new union from October 12 to 14. At the same time, the Ministry of Internal Affairs announced the implementation of the ‚ÄúTemporary Regulations for the Supervision of Christianity‚Äù and ‚ÄúTemporary Provisions for the Registration of Christian Missionaries.‚Äù The first article of the ‚ÄúRegulations‚Äù indicated that all Christianity and its affiliated enterprises in the jurisdiction of the North China Political Affairs Committee were to be supervised according to these regulations, and the second article explicitly stated that each denomination should agree to organize the North China Chinese Christian Union.

On October 15, the establishment ceremony of the North China Chinese Christian Union was held at Huairen Hall in Beijing, attended by more than twenty officials from the Sino-Japanese government, 83 representatives of pastoral staff from more than thirty churches across Hebei, Henan, Shandong, Shanxi provinces, and the cities of Beijing, Tianjin, and Qingdao, along with over 700 representatives of church members. Jiang Changchuan, a bishop of the Methodist Church of China, was elected as the head of the North China Christian Union, while Zhou Guanting, the secretary-general of the Beijing YMCA, was elected as the deputy head.

The North China Chinese Christian Union aimed to unify all denominations under one organization, with branch offices set up under the general assembly (Hebei Province, Shandong Province, Henan Province, Shanxi Province; with special branch offices in Beijing, Tianjin, and Qingdao), district associations (a total of 25 district associations), and congregations. The educational group of the union established three levels of theological colleges: Level A is represented by the North China Central Christian Institute (Beijing), Level B includes three local institutes (Beijing, Tianjin, Shandong Teng County), and Level C is the Christian Theological Hall. Staff at Level A theological colleges had to be appointed from Japanese religious organizations, particularly the Level A and B colleges, which had to hire Japanese pastors and teachers. The content of theological thought was also required to be transformed into Eastern cultural thought, in line with the ‚Äúpolitical ideology of the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere.‚Äù

The Japanese government actively utilized religion in occupied areas to serve the war of aggression against China. On December 8, 1942, marking the first anniversary of the ‚ÄúGreater East Asia War,‚Äù the North China Christian Union was instructed by the Ministry of Internal Affairs to hold various activities, resulting in resolutions: (1) to hold peace prayer gatherings in various places; (2) to hold public lectures discussing livelihood issues; (3) to post slogans; and (4) to present flowers to injured soldiers from friendly nations and their own country as a gesture of consolation. In October 1943, to commemorate the anniversary of the North China Christian Union, peace prayer gatherings were held throughout the month.

  1. Chungking Theological Seminary, 1944 (Xing Fuzeng 2001: 76-80)

In October 1943, the China Inland Mission held a meeting in Chongqing to outline the principles for their post-war work. Frank Houghton, the general director, emphasized that the mission must ‚Äúserve through the local Chinese churches,‚Äù where missionaries would contribute their gifts and expertise through local congregations, participating as ordinary members. The China Inland Mission aimed to develop into an auxiliary of the church, extending a ‚Äúhelping hand‚Äù while moving away from a ‚Äúcontrolling hand,‚Äù collaborating with Chinese colleagues to promote evangelistic efforts. To implement this goal, the establishment of a theological seminary with a Chinese president became a topic of discussion. Houghton strongly believed that institutions led by Westerners could no longer meet the needs of Chinese churches.

As early as the end of 1938, Houghton had repeatedly invited Chen Chonggui to assist in establishing a seminary for the China Inland Mission. After further discussions in January 1944, both parties decided to collaborate to found Chungking Theological Seminary, with Chen Chonggui as president. Houghton emphasized that while the China Inland Mission would have some involvement in the establishment process, Chungking Theological Seminary was not a ‚Äúmission project,‚Äù but developed on an independent basis.

In March 1944, Chen Chonggui and Houghton jointly issued a founding declaration. In September of the same year, the seminary temporarily borrowed the premises of the China Inland Mission‚Äôs Yibin Church as its site, officially moving to Chongqing in May 1946. The seminary emphasized ‚Äúliving by faith, not soliciting donations, not borrowing debts, only accepting contributions from those whom God has moved to give.‚Äù Through this, they aimed to demonstrate the existence of God and the power of prayer. Additionally, faculty and students, regardless of their backgrounds, were encouraged to love one another and diligently maintain the unity granted by the Holy Spirit, thereby proving the fellowship and cooperation among Christians. Chungking Theological Seminary championed faith-based contributions. While the China Inland Mission was a collaborator, it was not the sole provider of funding and did not bear the primary economic responsibility. Their greatest support came in the form of personnel, as all salaries for Western missionaries teaching at the seminary were fully paid by the China Inland Mission.

It can be said that Chungking Theological Seminary became one of the institutions in the history of Chinese theological education that achieved results in Sino-Western cooperation. After 1949, due to Chen Chonggui‚Äôs active participation in the Three-Self Movement, Chungking Theological Seminary became one of the few seminaries exempted from consolidation after the establishment of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, continuing to train pastors in the southwest region.   1. Chinese Inter Varsity Christian Fellowship (CIVCF), 1945 (Liang Jialin 2001: 4-11; Zhao Junying 1981: 18-22)

After the War of Resistance Against Japan, following the fall of various major cities, the Nationalist government relocated to the rear in Chongqing. In July 1945, the Evangelical Crusade was reorganized into the Chinese Evangelical Association. With support from foreign donations, Zhao Junying convened a summer spiritual retreat for Christian students from various Chinese universities, focusing on the Chongqing Spiritual Academy. Speakers at the event included Zhao himself, Jia Yuming, He Gengshi (Martin A. Hopkins), Zhang Jingyu, and Yin Renxian, notable figures from political and economic circles. Before the conclusion of the summer retreat on July 21, the ‚ÄúChinese Inter Varsity Christian Fellowship‚Äù (Fellowship) was established, with Zhao Junying serving as the general secretary. After the end of the War of Resistance Against Japan, as universities relocated back to their original cities, the Fellowship‚Äôs work expanded in various places. The post-war headquarters of the Fellowship was located in Nanjing, with Zhao continuing as the general secretary and David Adeney as the deputy. The Fellowship also established an advisory board, with Jia Yuming becoming the first chief advisor. In 1947, students‚Äô fellowships from various countries united to form the International Fellowship of Evangelical Students, holding the inaugural conference in Boston, USA, with Zhao Junying representing secondary schools.

In July 1947, the Fellowship convened the second National Christian Student Assembly in Nanjing, with speakers including Jia Yuming, Yang Shaotang, Zhao Zhaoying, and Frank Houghton. Over 500 students attended, and many made commitments during the gathering.

Undoubtedly, the establishment of the North China Christian Union, while beneficial to Japan‚Äôs attempts to control religion, also represented an avenue for church leaders and believers (especially those from Anglo-American missionary backgrounds) to maintain and practice their religious faith. For the vast majority of the populace living in the occupied areas, the realities they had to confront and accept were not only the Japanese occupation but also how to collaborate with the Japanese.   1. ‚ÄúNo One Has Ever Seen God,‚Äù 1943-1948 (Xing Fuzeng 2018: xxxii-xli)
