China: Context (1940-1950)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

  1. Policies Regarding the Withdrawal of Missionaries from Enemy Nations (Yang Weihua 2020: 91-93; Zhang Le 2020: 114-120)

On July 2, 1941, China severed diplomatic relations with Germany and Italy. The two countries‚Äô governments planned to evacuate their citizens from China, but most German and Italian missionaries insisted on remaining. In August, Foreign Minister Guo Taichi stated that German and Italian missionaries, as long as they focused on preaching and obeyed Chinese laws, could continue to reside in China. However, local military and political authorities were dissatisfied with the Foreign Ministry‚Äôs policy, demanding the expulsion of all such foreigners to prevent them from acting as spies.

On December 9, China declared war on Germany and Italy, making the remaining German and Italian missionaries enemy aliens. The Foreign Ministry ordered temporary protection and surveillance for citizens from the two nations, allowing them to remain in their original locations while monitoring communications. Nonetheless, there were growing calls to implement more stringent measures. To establish norms and unify perspectives, the Foreign Ministry consulted with various departments, initially deciding that missionaries should not be sheltered, but concentrated in designated churches under local official supervision to continue their pastoral work. However, the Military Commission emphasized that several cases of espionage disguised as missionary work had already been uncovered, rendering the justification for missionary activities inadequate. Nevertheless, the Foreign Ministry insisted on maintaining the status quo, as the Vatican and foreign church missionaries had significantly assisted China‚Äôs war efforts through propaganda and relief efforts. Furthermore, since Germany and Italy were relatively lenient toward their nationals in China, the concentrated supervision was viewed as sufficient to mitigate espionage concerns.

In January 1942, the Foreign Ministry promulgated the ‚ÄúRegulations on the Treatment of Enemy Nationals‚Äù and the ‚ÄúRegulations on the Concentration and Surveillance of Enemy National Clergy.‚Äù Article 13 of the regulations mandated that enemy clergy be concentrated in designated Catholic or Chinese-led Jesus churches, continuing their evangelistic work under the supervision of the relevant authorities. The regulations specified that clergy would be concentrated in designated churches for missionary work, using a cathedral as the primary location if it was a Catholic church. The Jesus churches would be directed by Chinese officials and were prohibited from installing radio and broadcasting equipment. Clergy were required to report to the designated church within five days of notification, and upon arrival, they had to report to local authorities and present their passports for registration, with any movements subject to approval by both internal and external authorities. Local authorities were responsible for issuing temporary passports, designating itineraries and routes, and notifying relevant authorities along the way for adequate supervision, dispatching police for transportation if necessary; the local authorities concentrated on cataloging information about clergy and maintaining strict surveillance, recording daily occurrences, and compiling monthly reports for internal and external review.

In June 1942, following diplomatic relations established between China and the Vatican, Chiang Kai-shek proposed three principles for addressing enemy clergy: (1) Those suspected of espionage would be treated as enemy aliens; (2) Those without espionage suspicion would be permitted to preach freely within designated areas but would not be allowed to enter military zones; (3) Church properties should be returned until proven to be hostile assets. On June 25, the Executive Yuan ordered the implementation of these three principles. However, Bishop Leo C. Ferrary, the papal representative stationed in Chongqing, considered this tantamount to repealing the previously centralized orders, informing all clergy. In July, the Executive Yuan indicated that designated areas would be defined by local authorities for each missionary‚Äôs free preaching, eliminating the need for centralized supervision. However, local provinces expressed difficulties in enforcement. Consequently, the Ministry of the Interior and the Foreign Ministry established seven additional guidelines for execution: (1) The designated preaching areas would serve as the specified free preaching zones, without requiring centralized supervision; (2) Those clergy already centralized, if requesting to return to their original locations, would not be permitted to do so if their original preaching sites were not within the designated preaching zones; (3) The handling of enemy aliens would continue as planned to facilitate a swift conclusion; (4) Those clergy seeking to leave the country would be prohibited under the pretext of military security; (5) Enemy female clergy would be allowed to continue residing in their original locations, except in military zones; (6) Any requests for changes to the handling of clergy must receive approval from both the Interior and Foreign Ministries; (7) Regarding judgments on enemy church properties, it was stipulated that foreign churches renting land and buildings should adhere to national regulations in their contracts.

In 1943, the Ministry of the Interior and the Foreign Ministry began refining relevant regulations, deciding to abolish the ‚ÄúRegulations on the Concentration and Surveillance of Enemy National Clergy‚Äù and incorporate them into the newly established ‚ÄúAmended Regulations on the Treatment of Enemy Nationals‚Äù and ‚ÄúImplementation Details for the Regulations on the Treatment of Enemy Nationals‚Äù (January 1944).

According to statistics, in 1939, there were 395 Catholic missionaries and 465 Protestant missionaries from Germany and Italy in China. In a 1942 investigation conducted by the Chinese Foreign Ministry, there were 389 German expatriates (including 150 Protestant missionaries) and 357 Italians.

  1. Policies for Missionaries from Enemy Nations (Yang Weihua 2020: 91-93; Zhang Le 2020: 114-120)

On July 2, 1941, China severed diplomatic relations with Germany and Italy. Nevertheless, the Foreign Ministry insisted on maintaining the status quo since the Vatican and foreign church missionaries had significantly assisted with wartime propaganda and relief efforts. In June 1942, after diplomatic relations were established with the Vatican, Chiang Kai-shek proposed three principles for dealing with enemy national clergy: (1) Suspected spies would be categorized as enemy aliens; (2) Those free from suspicion would be allowed to preach within designated areas but prohibited from entering military zones; (3) Church properties would be returned unless deemed hostile by the new regime.
