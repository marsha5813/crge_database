China: Context (1910-1920)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

  1. Religious Provisions in the Early Republic of China Constitution (Â≤ëÂæ∑ÂΩ∞ 1933)

The ‚ÄúProvisional Constitution of the Republic of China‚Äù was the first constitutional law enacted after the establishment of the Republic of China. It was passed by the Provisional Senate located in Nanjing on March 8, 1912, in the first year of the Republic. Article 5 stated: ‚ÄúThe people of the Republic of China shall be equal, with no discrimination based on race, class, or religion.‚Äù Article 6 included among the freedoms enjoyed by the people the ‚Äúfreedom of religion.‚Äù

To facilitate peace negotiations between the North and South and ensure the abdication of the Qing dynasty, Sun Yat-sen, in February 1912, relinquished the position of Provisional President to Yuan Shikai. In March, Yuan Shikai took office as Provisional President in Beijing, and both the House of Representatives and Senate began drafting a constitution, using the Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests at the Temple of Heaven as their drafting location. The ‚ÄúDraft Constitution of the Republic of China‚Äù (also known as the ‚ÄúTemple of Heaven Constitution Draft‚Äù) was completed in October. Article 4 stipulated that ‚Äúthe people of the Republic of China are equal before the law, with no distinctions based on race, class, or religion.‚Äù Article 11 stated: ‚ÄúThe people of the Republic of China have the freedom to practice their religion, which shall not be restricted except by law.‚Äù

In October 1913, after Yuan Shikai became President, he was dissatisfied with the limitations the ‚ÄúDraft Constitution of the Republic of China‚Äù imposed on presidential power. He therefore drafted a new constitution known as the ‚ÄúConstitution of the Republic of China‚Äù (also called the ‚ÄúYuan Constitution‚Äù), which was announced in April 1914. Article 4 of this document reiterated that ‚Äúthe people of the Republic of China are equal before the law, without distinction of race, class, or religion.‚Äù Article 5 further stated that among the freedoms enjoyed by the people, ‚Äúthe people have the freedom of religion within the confines of the law.‚Äù

After Yuan Shikai‚Äôs death in 1916, Li Yuanhong took office as President. However, the situation in China further deteriorated, descending into a state of warlord fragmentation. It was not until the establishment of the Nanjing National Government in 1927 that efforts to draft a new constitutional law resumed.

Social Hostility toward Christianity

  1. The Church-State Movement and Religious Freedom, 1913-1917 (ÈüìËèØ 2007; Âê≥ÂÆóÊÖà 1924: 38)

After the establishment of the Republic of China, while the Provisional Constitution included provisions advocating for ‚Äúreligious freedom,‚Äù it still faced significant challenges in the early years. Among the most critical challenges was the proposal to establish Confucianism as the state religion.

As early as the late Qing, Kang Youwei advocated for Confucianism to be recognized as the state religion, believing that its institution could save China. Following the establishment of the Republic, he remained steadfast in this belief. In 1913, Kang published an article in the journal ‚ÄúThe Unbearable‚Äù that reiterated the idea of ‚Äúdesignating Confucianism as the state religion.‚Äù Additionally, Chen Huanzhang, the initiator of the Shanghai Confucian Association, actively supported this notion.

Although Yuan Shikai expressed a willingness to maintain a policy of religious freedom upon taking office as President, he exhibited considerable support for the Confucian movement. For instance, in June 1913, he ordered the resumption of Confucius worship. In July of that year, a constitutional drafting committee was formed to prepare a constitution. During this time, the Confucian Association immediately petitioned Yuan to formalize Confucianism as the state religion while also allowing for religious freedom. In response to these developments, Christians in Beijing organized the Anti-Confucian Association and collaborated with representatives from Catholicism, Buddhism, Daoism, and Islam to advocate for religious freedom and oppose the establishment of Confucianism as the state religion, engaging in petition activities. When Congress debated a proposal to designate Confucianism as the state religion, it sparked a wide range of opinions. Support came from members of Yuan‚Äôs party and a few progressive legislators who suggested designating Confucianism as the basis of national moral education, while members of the Kuomintang voiced their opposition. After lengthy discussions, a temporary compromise was reached by October, with Article 19 of the draft constitution, known as the ‚ÄúTemple of Heaven Constitution,‚Äù emphasizing that ‚Äúnational education shall be based on the Way of Confucius.‚Äù This effectively concluded the Confucian movement in failure, but the provision laid the groundwork for later efforts advocating for religious freedom.

After Yuan Shikai‚Äôs death in 1916, Li Yuanhong assumed the presidency, and Congress reconvened. At that time, leaders of the Christian community in Beijing sought to ensure permanent religious freedom by uniting various faiths to petition for the repeal of Article 19 of the draft constitution. However, this initiative met with strong opposition from the Confucian Association, which sought the assistance of regional military leaders to rally support for the claim of Confucianism as the state religion.

In response to the resurgence of the church-state issue, the ‚ÄúReligious Freedom Association‚Äù was officially established in Beijing in November 1916, with Xu Qian elected as its president. The Association‚Äôs activities involved promoting its mission through speeches, distributing pamphlets advocating for religious freedom, issuing declarations, and various writings to garner public support. Additionally, they directly petitioned President Li Yuanhong twice, establishing connections with members of both houses of Congress to influence their decisions. At that time, Li expressed, ‚ÄúI consider the reasons for the Religious Freedom Association‚Äôs petitions to be adequate,‚Äù and promised to present their opinions to Congress. Meanwhile, the Christian community united with Catholics, Orthodox Christians, Muslims, and Buddhists to collaborate in their efforts for religious freedom.

On May 14, 1917, Congress reconvened a constitutional conference, where supporters and opponents of the state religion continued to clash, resulting in multiple inconclusive votes. Eventually, both sides agreed to eliminate the previously proposed wording, replacing ‚Äúnational education shall be based on the Way of Confucius‚Äù with ‚Äúthe people of the Republic of China have the freedom to revere Confucius and to practice their religion, which shall not be restricted by law.‚Äù On May 15, the Religious Freedom Association held a national representative conference at the Beijing YMCA, expressing satisfaction with the decision made by Congress, considering their goals achieved, and planned to hold a celebration on the 17th.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

  1. Political Situations After the Foundation of the Republic of China (ÈÉ≠Âª∑‰ª• 1983: 427-502; Young 1983: 208-255; Nathan 1983: 256-283; Sheridan 1983: 284-321)

After the Xinhai Revolution, which led to the fall of the Qing Dynasty, Sun Yat-sen assumed the role of Provisional President in January 1912. However, due to the inability of the revolutionary party to control military power, Yuan Shikai, leader of the Beiyang Army, became a key figure in the political landscape. After reaching an agreement with the revolutionaries, Yuan forced the abdication of the Qing and became the first official President of the Republic of China. Yet, Yuan‚Äôs political ambitions surpassed the ideals of a republic, as he quickly undermined the power of the parliament and political parties, concentrating authority in his own hands.

In 1913, Sun Yat-sen, leading the Kuomintang, initiated the ‚ÄúSecond Revolution‚Äù in an attempt to overthrow Yuan Shikai, but was defeated due to the power imbalance. Subsequently, Yuan dissolved the parliament and declared the restoration of the monarchy in 1915, proclaiming himself ‚ÄúEmperor of the Chinese Empire.‚Äù However, the restoration faced widespread opposition across the country, including from figures like Cai E and Li Liejun, who initiated the National Protection War against Yuan‚Äôs monarchy. In 1916, Yuan removed the monarchy amid both internal and external pressures and soon after passed away, thus ending his brief and autocratic rule.

Following Yuan‚Äôs death, the Beiyang government fractured into different factions, including the Zhi faction, Wan faction, and Feng faction, ushering in an era of warlordism. The Beiyang warlords struggled for control of both the central government and regional powers, leading to prolonged warfare and political instability: (1) The Zhi faction, led by Feng Guozhang and Cao Kun, controlled Zhili and northern China, advocating for the maintenance of the existing powers of the Beiyang government; (2) The Wan faction, led by Duan Qirui, controlled Anhui and the middle Yangtze River region, forming a competitive relationship with the Zhi faction; (3) The Feng faction, led by Zhang Zuolin, held power in Northeast China and gradually expanded its influence over northern China.

In 1917, Duan Qirui of the Wan faction dismissed Li Yuanhong and disbanded the parliament, provoking Sun Yat-sen to launch the National Protection Movement in Guangzhou, aiming to restore the Provisional Constitution. In 1920, Duan Qirui‚Äôs close ties with Japan upset the Zhi faction, leading to a war between the two factions for central authority. The Zhi faction ultimately defeated the Wan faction, causing Duan Qirui to resign, significantly weakening the Wan faction‚Äôs influence. Subsequently, conflicts erupted between the Zhi and Feng factions as they vied for control over the Beijing government. The Zhi faction emerged victorious, repressing the Feng faction, yet their underlying tensions remained unresolved. Although the Beiyang government nominally maintained central authority, warlord infighting became frequent, and regional governments increasingly embraced autonomy. Financial, military, and diplomatic powers became decentralized, leaving the central government nearly powerless.

During the Beiyang government era, China‚Äôs internal divisions provided opportunities for foreign powers to expand their influence. Japan emerged as one of the most impactful foreign powers during this time. Particularly during Yuan Shikai‚Äôs leadership, Japan forced China to make further concessions through the infamous ‚ÄúTwenty-One Demands.‚Äù Afterward, Japan continued to support the Wan faction while expanding its interests in China through economic control and military advisors, especially in Shandong and Manchuria. Britain primarily focused on its economic interests in China, notably in the Yangtze River Valley and the Shanghai concession, maintaining a flexible stance toward the Beiyang warlords without clear allegiance to any faction. The United States adhered to its ‚ÄúOpen Door‚Äù policy, attempting to curb Japanese expansion; however, focused on domestic issues after World War I, it exerted limited influence on the Beiyang government. In the wake of the October Revolution, Soviet Russia adopted a friendly diplomatic posture, claiming to abandon the unequal treaties of the Tsarist era and supporting revolutionary forces in China, such as the Kuomintang led by Sun Yat-sen, in an effort to expand its influence.
