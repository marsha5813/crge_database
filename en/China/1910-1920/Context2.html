Controversy on religious freedom and state religion

In 1912, Chen Huanzhang ÈôàÁÑïÁ´† took the lead to found the Confucian Society Â≠îÊïô‰ºö in Shanghai, in order to promote Confucianism and to save society. In 1913, Chen together with a few leading scholars, such as Liang Qichao and Yan Fu, to petition to the congress to make Confucianism the state religion. After a presidential decree on respect and promotion of Confucius, there was a hot debate in the constitution drafting committee, regarding the conflict between state religion and freedom of religion. It also arose protests from Chinese Christians, who struggled to defend the article on religious freedom in the Provisional Constitution of 1912. A Petition Committee for Religious Freedom and against State Religion Âåó‰∫¨‰ø°ÊïôËá™Áî±‰∏çÂÆöÂõΩÊïôËØ∑ÊÑøÂõ¢was founded in Beijing. They proposed to the Congress that state religion and religious freedom were in conflict from a legal perspective, and state religion was against the Republican principles with church-state separation in particular. The petition won supports from other religious groups, including the national associations of the Buddhists, the Daoists and the Muslims. They further organized as a united petition committee of five religions to the congress. However, President Yuan Shikai dissolved the congress, and continued to promote sacrifice to the Heaven and Confucius through political orders. In 1916, when Yuan‚Äôs emperor dream bankrupted and the congress was re-opened, the Society for Religious Freedom ‰ø°ÊïôËá™Áî±‰ºöwas founded as a permanent organization, with a veteran revolutionary Xu Qian ÂæêË∞¶ as the President, and Ma Xiangbo È©¨Áõ∏‰ºØand Cheng Jingyi ËØöÈùôÊÄ°as vice presidents to represent Catholics and Protestants respectively. The Protestant group was the most active under the leadership of Cheng Jingyi. They sent agents to local provinces on one side, and organized petition groups to Beijing on the other side. Correspondences, telegrams, and donations were publicized in regular newsletters. Four basic ideas were proposed by the Society: religious freedom was a guarantee to end religious conflicts and wars; religious freedom was benefactory to the harmonious relations of five races of the Republic; the Confucian teaching was not proper to equal education of five races; religious freedom was a basic right for citizens of the Republic. The conservatives, such as the intellectual Kang Youwei Â∫∑Êúâ‰∏∫ and the royalist general Zhang Xun Âº†Âãã, petitioned to the government to make Confucianism the state religion. And a committee to defend state religion was also established in Beijing. From September 1916 to April 1917, several rounds of discussion happened in the constitutional meeting. Finally, a moderate article was passed: ‚ÄúThe People of Republic of China have the freedom of respecting Confucius and believing religions, which should not be restricted without proper legal reason‚Äù (‰∏≠ÂçéÊ∞ëÂõΩ‰∫∫Ê∞ëÔºåÊúâÂ∞äÂ¥áÂ≠îÂ≠êÂèä‰ø°‰ª∞ÂÆóÊïô‰πãËá™Áî±ÔºåÈùû‰æùÊ≥ïÂæãÔºå‰∏çÂèóÈôêÂà∂). It was relatively a triumph for the Christians, and a big celebration was held after the article was passed. The item of state religion was totally deleted, even the moderate way of Confucianism as the moral basis of Chinese could not be kept. Freedom of religion became a constitutional principle through the whole Republican period, and possibly also influenced the Communist regime later. (Liu 2012)

Social Hostility toward Christianity

The Republican Revolution of 1911Ëæõ‰∫•Èù©ÂëΩbrought some interruptions on the missionary enterprise. In Henan province, a missionary and his wife were assailed by robbers. In Shandong province, a few Chinese Christians were killed or ill-treated by reactionary societies and by soldiers loyal to the ancient regime. The most acute disturbance happened in Shaanxi province by the Society of Elder BrothersÂì•ËÄÅ‰ºö. They attacked foreigners as well as Manchus. In October 1911, in the city of Xi‚Äôan, two missionaries and six children associated with the Scandinavian Alliance Mission were killed by a mob. (Latourette 2009: 607-608)

In 1913, robbers attacked a party of Canadian Methodist missionaries in Sichuan province. There was also a local persecution in a village in Hunan province. In 1912, there were reports about the destruction of mission property, the murder of Christians, and the occupation of a chape by troops, but these unfortunate incidents were due more to disorder than to anti-Christian sentiment. (Latourette 2009: 616)

In consistence with the last decade, independence and cooperation was the dominant response to social hostilities towards Christianity. As early as 1908, the Presbyterians in North China planned to do missionary work in the name of Church of Christ in China ‰∏≠ÂçéÂü∫Áù£Êïô‰ºö. After several years of preparation and discussion, the churches in Jinan, Yantai, and Qingdao finally united in 1924 to form the Church of Christ in China, Shandong province. In 1910, the Church of Christ in China was formed in Tianjin. And in 1912, the Church of Christ in China was established in Beijing. In 1916, these churches in Shandong, Tianjin and Beijing united to form the North China Federation of Churches ÂçéÂåóÂü∫Áù£Êïô‰ºöËÅîÂêà‰ºö. In 1919, the Church of Christ in China was also formed in south Fujian and Guangdong province. (Duan 2004: 119-128) Through the work of Cheng Jingyi under the auspice of China Continuation Committee, these churches would later unite in the National Council of Churches in 1922.

In early Republican period, there was also the controversy between Confucianists and Christians regarding the constitutional articles on state religion and religious freedom. The Christians went to defend their rights, together with other religious groups, and finally won the campaign. It became one of the successful stories of Chinese Christians struggling for their rights.

The New Culture and May-Fourth Movement ‰∫îÂõõÊñ∞ÊñáÂåñËøêÂä®, with the spirit of science and nationalism, did not challenge Christianity directly during this period. But it prepared intellectual resources for the anti-Christian movement in the 1920s.

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

The Republican Revolution of 1911 Ëæõ‰∫•Èù©ÂëΩhad some interruptions on the missionary work, but it proved to be temporary. The revolutionaries in general were friendly to missionaries and Chinese coverts. Some of the leaders were Christians and had favorable attitudes to Christianity. It was reported that a veteran revolutionary leader Huang Xing ÈªÑÂÖ¥ said Christianity contributed to the revolution more than any other single cause. The leader of the revolution and the founder the Republic, Dr. Sun Yat-sen Â≠ô‰∏≠Â±±, had been educated in missionary schools and were himself a Christian. At the founding of the Republic, he spoke publicly about Christianity. In particular, he promised religious freedom, which was confirmed in the Provisional Constitution. In the newly founded government of Wuchang, it was said that half of the young officers were Christians. And in the Provincial Government of Guangdong, sixty-five percent of the officials were Christians or had been educated in mission schools. In the cabinet of 1912, there were also three members as Christians. The later presidents, both Yuan Shikai Ë¢Å‰∏ñÂáØand Li Yuanhong ÈªéÂÖÉÊ¥™, also showed their hospitality to the visiting missionaries and mission organizations. During Sherwood Eddy‚Äôs campaign in 1914, the pavilion was erected in the Forbidden City, and among his audience many were officials of the Republican government. In 1913, the Cabinet set aside one day of prayer, and Christian worship services were held across the whole country and even abroad. (Latourette 2009: 607-612; Liang 2007)

The World War = 1 * ROMAN I from 1914 to 1918 curtailed the missionary enterprise in China to much extent. German Protestantism and Russian Orthodox Church suffered the most. Europe itself was impoverished, and the Western prestige in China was shaken. The ‚ÄúConcert of the Powers‚Äù during the Boxer Movement had been shattered. At the Paris Conference and the following Washington Conference, the Chinese representatives formally asked for the termination of extraterritoriality and all other special concessions. This was supported by the rise of Chinese nationalism, and the Renaissance or New Tide of cultural movement. (Latourette 2009: 687-694; Tao and Qiao, 2021)

However, the war did not stop the spread of Christianity in China. Actually, there was still increase in some fields. Between 1908-1909 and 1912-1913, the baptized community rose from 195,905 to 285,045, or about 11.5% per year. Between 1915 and 1917, it grew from 330,926 to 398,760, or at the rate of about 10% every year. (Latourette 2009; 750-751) The income of the missionary societies were exceptional examples. During the war period, the annual receipts of the Church Missionary Society averaged ¬£372,510, as against ¬£402,681 for the five years before the war and ¬£479,464 for the four years after the war. The annual income of the London Missionary Society during the war was ¬£222,144, as against ¬£186,673 for the five years before the war and ¬£337,014 for the five years after the war. The Baptist Missionary Society had an annual income during the war of ¬£116,621, as against ¬£93,648 for the five years before the war and ¬£199,862 for the five years after the war. The Wesleyan Methodist Missionary Society had an annual income of ¬£374,089 during the war, as against ¬£199,198 for the five years before the war and ¬£ 432,076 in six of the seven years after the war. (Latourette 2009; 765-766) The American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions had an income of $1,043,357 during 1910-1914, $1,486,331 during 1915-1919, and $1,887,358 during 1920-1924. The portion of Americans in the missionary enterprise also increased obviously. In 1905, about 41% of the Protestant missionaries were from Britain and 35% came from the United States. In 1922, the British declined to 19%, and the Americans grew to 51%. (Latourette 2009: 767-768)

The New Culture and May-Fourth Movement from 1915 to 1920s: The New Youth MagazineÊñ∞ÈùíÂπ¥founded by Chen Duxiu in 1915 became a dominant platform of spreading new ideas. When Cai Yuanpei Ëî°ÂÖÉÂüπ was appointed as the chancellor of Peking University, he led a reform characterized by freedom and openness, which attracted a large group of progressive intellectuals. In 1919, due to the unfair treatment to China at the Paris Conference, a large demonstration happened across the whole country. The New Culture Movement and the May-Fourth Movement merged to push forward a Chinese Renaissance supported by the rise of nationalism. (Chow 1980)
