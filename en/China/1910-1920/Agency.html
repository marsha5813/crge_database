China: Agency (1910-1920)

Christian or Pro-Christian Leaders

Yu Guozhen ‰øûÂõΩÊ°¢ (1852-1932) was a pioneer of Christian independence movement in early 20th-century China. He was born in Ningbo of Zhejiang province. After some traditional education, he went to the missionary schools founded by Presbyterians (USA). He got baptized in 1868, and was ordinated in 1888. After some preach work in Zhejiang and Anhui provinces, he became a pastor of Hongkou Presbyterian Church in Shanghai. Yu had strong concern about missionary cases especially after the traumatic Boxer Movement of 1900, and hoped to fix the identity paradox between a Christian and a Chinese. He thought an independent Chinese church was the proper way for solution. In 1903, he together with a few prominent Christians in Shanghai to form the Chinese Christian Union ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£Âæí‰ºö, a pioneering organization for independence, with Yu as the chairperson for some time. Due to its loose organization, Yu established the China Christian Independent Church ‰∏≠ÂõΩËÄ∂Á®£ÊïôËá™Á´ã‰ºö in 1905. He tried to get the official recognition of the new organization from the government. To reach the aim, he went to Beijing in 1909 to send lavishly printed and decorated New Testament to the royal court as tributes. There he got contacted with Cheng Jingyi ËØöÈùôÊÄ°and other prominent Christians in Beijing, and got involved in the petition for religious freedom as part of the constitutional campaign. Yu won big fame during the Republican Revolution of 1911. He helped found the Zhabei Merchant Association Èó∏ÂåóÂïÜÂõ¢ and acted as a vice chairman. The Association played a crucial role during the revolution in Shanghai. After the Revolution, Yu praised Sun Yat-sen Â≠ô‰∏≠Â±±for leading the ‚ÄúThird Revolution,‚Äù which was just after Moses bringing the Jews out of Egypt and the birth of Jesus Christ. Yu was also involved in the Boycott of American Goods in 1905, the Campaign of Protecting Railway Rights in 1907, and the Anti-Drug Movement in 1908. So, he was not just a pioneer in Christian independence, but also a public figure in revolution and social reform. In 1924, Yu was involved in the controversy with the Presbyterian church regarding the property right of Zhabei Church. He left the Presbyterian church after losing the case. Yu‚Äôs efforts really won legitimate status for the China Christian Independent Church. In 1920, the first national conference of the organization was held in Shanghai, with 189 congregations and 10,000 believers at the moment. Before his death, a new headquarter church was built in Shanghai, and there were 309 congregations across 20 provinces and Southeast Asia. Through the hardships during the Japanese invasion, the China Christian Independent Church continued to develop and later merged into the Three-Self Patriotic Movement. (Zhang 2011; Sun 2022)

He got educated at University of Toronto and Knox College, and was awarded the Doctor of Divinity. In 1907, he witnessed the Korean Revival. During his return through Manchuria, he was invited by the local congregations, and led the Manchurian revival. The revival transformed Goforth‚Äôs life and ministry. He won a big fame, but was also criticized by some for ‚Äúemotionalism.‚Äù Goforth had also been invited to preach in the troops of the Christian general Feng Yuxiang ÂÜØÁéâÁ••. The Goforthes worked actively in Manchuria into the 1930s until his eyesight failed in 1935. The Goforths co-authored Miracles Lives of China. And Rosalind, the gifted writer, penned the popular Goforth of China. (Goforth 1937; Bays 1998: 247)

Bernt Berntsen ËµâÂæ∑Êñ∞(1863-1933) was born in Norway and later emigrated to the United States. In 1904, he came to China to work as a non-denominational missionary in South Zhili. In 1906, Berntsen came across an early publication of The Apostolic Faith which described the Azusa Street Revival. He went to the Centennial Missionary Conference in Shanghai to seek people with similar experience, but with no avail. Thus, he made a trip to Los Angeles, where he received the Baptism of the Holy Spirit through speaking in tongues. Together with a few missionary friends, the Apostolic Faith Mission was founded. Berntsen came back to China in 1908, and started a new mission in Zhengding of Hebei province. In 1910, he visited Norway, Sweden and Denmark, and got in touch with the Pentecostal movement in Norway. Since 1912, Berntsen began a Chinese publication entitled Popular Gospel Truth ÈÄö‰º†Á¶èÈü≥ÁúüÁêÜÊä•, which promoted the baptism of the Holy Spirit. His mission in Zhengding continued to 1916. From 1914, Berntsen and many of the Apostolic Faith Mission workers in China became affiliated with the Assemblies of God. (Iap 2015: 91-106)

Christian or Pro-Christian Organizations

China Inland Mission ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂÜÖÂú∞‰ºö: The mission had the largest number of missionaries in China, most of whom working in the internal area. It suffered a lot during the Boxer Movement, but did not ask any indemnity. In 1903, the founder Hudson Taylor retired from the office of Director and transferred to D.E. Hoste, one of the famous ‚ÄúCambridge Seven.‚Äù Taylor died in 1905. But it did not stop the mission‚Äôs growth. Between 1901 and 1910, the mission and its associated partners opened 52 new stations, as against 78 in the preceding decade. All the provinces and most major cities had resident missionaries then. It also went further to explore the borderland areas. By 1904, the Mission had stations on the Tibetan border both in Gansu and Sichuan provinces. Beginning in 1905, itineration was initiated in Xinjiang. Moreover, many mission organizations chose to be associated with the China Inland Mission, which enriched the body a lot and became a distinctive characteristic of its organization. Some prominent examples include the Scandinavian China Missionary Alliance, the Norwegian Alliance Mission, the Swedish Holiness Union Mission in China, the Finnish Free Church Mission, the Alliance China Mission (Germany), and the German Women‚Äôs Missionary Union. The mission centers grew from 123 in 1895 to 521 in 1905, and to 1,100 in 1915. There were 604 missionaries in 1895, 825 in 1905, and 1,063 in 1915. The Chinese helpers grew from 417 in 1895 to 1,152 in 1905, and to 2,765 in 1915. Between 1895 and 1905, there were 11,452 new baptisms; between 1905 and 1915, it was 32,146. (Latourette 2009: 582-584)

Chinese Christian Union ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£Âæí‰ºö was founded in 1902 by some prominent Christians in Shanghai such as Gao Fengchi È´òÂá§Ê±†, the founder and manager of Commercial Press, and Song Yaoru ÂÆãËÄÄÂ¶Ç, the founding father of the well-known Song family during Republican China. A specific background was the missionary case in Chenzhou of Hunan province. And the aim of the organization was to dissolve the missionary cases through the founding of independent churches. The slogan of the organization highlighted the labels of ‚Äúthree-selves‚Äù: self-support, self-propagation, and self-governance. The organization was defined as a federation instead of a new church. And it was an interdenominational organization. Among its founders, there were believers from the background of the Methodist and the Presbyterian. There were many connections and communications between the Chinese Christian Union and the China Christian Independent Church, both in personnel and in ideas. In particular, Yu Guozhen ‰øûÂõΩÁ•Øhad been the chairman of both organizations. In 1910, there was a proposal for merge of the two organizations. But due to different regulations and Yu‚Äôs personal attitude toward the Chinese Christian Union, the merge plan got bankrupt. The Union had an organ, The Chinese Christian ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£ÂæíÊä•. It published a lot of articles on church independence, as well as Christian perspectives regarding public affairs. It had a loud voice in the campaign for religious freedom, and promoted Christian patriotism. At the beginning, the Union had branches in Beijing, Tianjin, Taiyuan, Xuzhou, Changsha, Ningbo, Hong Kong, etc. But due to financial issue and personnel change, its activities could not be continued, and the organ suddenly stopped publishing in 1915. Its member churches were merged into the Church of Christ in China later. Chinese Christian Union was the first organization to promote church independence in a national level. However, its main contribution was on propagation of ideas. Without enough support from the local churches and effective organization, it was destined to go bankrupt. Its sister organization, the China Christian Independent Church would have a larger, and more enduring influence. (Hu 2011; Wu 2014)

China Christian Independent Church‰∏≠ÂõΩËÄ∂Á®£‰ºöËá™Á´ã‰ºö, founded in 1905 by Yu Guozhen ‰øûÂõΩÊ°¢, is a representative example of Chinese Christian independent movement in early 20th century and has a legacy till the 1950s. Its headquarters was set in Shanghai. The original aim was to end missionary cases due to foreign intervention, and to highlight ‚Äúthe idea of loving country and loving religion and the spirit of self-independence and self-governance‚Äù (Áà±ÂõΩÁà±Êïô‰πãÊÄùÊÉ≥ÔºåËá™Á´ãËá™Ê≤ª‰πãÁ≤æÁ•û). It was defined as a federation of independent churches without strict organization and theological requirements. Its members came from various backgrounds, including China Inland Mission, London Missionary Society, the Presbyterians, and the Baptists. The churches in Pingyang county of Zhejiang province became one of the earlies members, which was followed by the Churches in Hubei and Shanxi provinces. In 1911, an organ was published with the title The Holy NewsÂú£Êä• and continued to 1951 with a few interruptions. In 1920, a national conference of the China Christian Independent Church was held in Shanghai, with a membership of 189 congregations and 10,000 believers. It developed quickly against the background of Chinese nationalism. By the end of 1924, there had been 310 congregations with 22,000 believers across the country, plus social enterprises such as primary schools and clinics. A second national conference was held in 1930, to celebrate the opening of the federation church in Shanghai, as well as the 25th anniversary of the organization and the 20th anniversary of its organ. Then it had 309 congregations covering 20 provinces as well as Southeast Asia. Respectively, there were 142 congregations in Zhejiang, 65 in Henan, 18 in Fujian, 15 in Jiangsu, 6 in Shanghai, 3 in Anhui, and most other provinces with just one. The Japanese attacks in 1932 and 1938 had destructive influences on the organization, including its newly opened headquarters church. In the 1940s, a former custom officer Xie Yongqin Ë∞¢Ê∞∏Èí¶ recovered the organization‚Äôs work. But there was also a competitive agency by a border member in Zhoukou of Henan province. In the 1950s, members of the China Christian Independent Church joined the Three-self Patriotic Movement, with Xie Yongqin as the Chairman of the Shanghai committee. With ‚Äúindependence‚Äù as the label, its original aim was to prevent foreign intervention. Later, its attitude became tolerant gradually, with a distinction between missionaries and mission societies. It joined the National Council of Churches in 1922, but had a controversy with the Presbyterian churches due to church property. Self-support was a big problem. Its early development owed to donation from rich members in Commercial Press or other firms. The national federation claimed an annual donation since 1920, but it proved hard to continue. In the 1940s, it had to accept relief funds from foreign resources. It also faced challenges in other fields. For example, according to a survey in 1946, there were only 7 pastors in 56 congregations, and only three of them had formal theological education. Among its members, 46% were peasants, 19% were workers, 17% were businessmen, and 9% were students. (Zhang 1998)

Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association (YMCA)‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£ÊïôÈùíÂπ¥‰ºö: The first YMCAs in China were organized in mission schools as early as 1885. In 1900, there were 47 Association in eight provinces, and a national convention was held in 1901 with 170 delegates, over 3/4 of them Chinese. The YMCA leadership was especially sensitive to the new conditions after 1900. John R. Mott in person played an important role during the process. The first city association was established in Shanghai in 1889. In 1901, there were also associations in Tianjin and Hong Kong. In 1906, graduates and students of Princeton University were sent to Beijing to inaugurate the association there. In 1908, the Association even sent two secretaries to Tokyo, including one Chinese Wang Zhengting ÁéãÊ≠£Âª∑. And partly due to the YMCA influence, an organization of Christian Chinese Students was founded in the United States. While the study of the Bible was emphasized by the Associations in America, the Chinese associations organized 2,732 Bible classes in 1911 alone. The YMCA made a phenomenal growth in China. In 1911, there were 93 student Associations. By 1912, fourteen cities had fully organized general Associations, and in seven more were in initial stages. The secretarial staff increased from a body of seven foreigners and sixteen Chinese in 1901 to 75 foreigners and 85 Chinese in 1912. From the very beginning, the YMCA insisted on Chinese majority in membership and in leadership. The Chinese officials and businessmen contributed most of the budget. No money was asked from abroad, except for the salaries of the foreign secretaries and the buildings and permanent equipment. It was said that President Yuan Shikai Ë¢Å‰∏ñÂáØhimself contributed to YMCA. The general convention of the Associations of China and Korea was held in Beijing in 1912. President Li Yuanhong ÈªéÂÖÉÊ¥™ held a warm reception of the delegates. The President, the Premier, and the Secretary of the National Assembly all gave welcome addresses. The secretaries of YMCA were asked to help prepare Chinese athletes for participation in the Far Eastern Olympic Games in Manila in 1913. (Latourette 2009: 584-591)

The Anti-Opium LeagueÁ¶ÅÁÉü‰ºö: It was an organization of the missionaries working in China. The idea was proposed in 1896, and the first meeting was held in 1897 in Shanghai. The basic objectives were: 1) to cooperate with the Society for the Suppression of the Opium Trade which was founded in Britain in 1874; 2) to collect information and statistics about opium in China; 3) to inform the Christian people of their original countries concerning the evils of opium and to enlist their prayers; 4) to agitate the anti-opium question among Chinese. In 1899, a survey was made through the missionary journal The Chinese Recorder, under the title Opinions of Over 100 Physicians on the Use of Opium in China, which provided general information of opium in China. (Lodwick 1996: 38-40) A few missionaries in China took a leading role in such kind of initiatives. For example, James Hudson Taylor, the founder of the China Inland Mission, addressed at the Centenary Conference of the Protestant Missions of the World in 1888 that ‚Äúthe opium traffic is doing more evil in China in a week than Missions are doing good in a year.‚Äù (Lodwick 1996: 50) The archdeacon of Church Missionary Society in China, Arthur E. Moule, published a book in 1892 and denounced the British for introducing opium to China. (Lodwick 1996: 54) In 1906, an imperial edict was issued prohibiting the use of the opium. In 1907, China and Britain made an agreement to reduce the export of Indian opium to China by one-tenth annually in ten years. The Anti-Opium League in 1908 sent out questionnaires to mission stations throughout China regarding the anti-opium movements in each area. In the centennial conference of 1907 in Shanghai, opium was singled out as a particular issue to be addressed. In 1909, the International Opium Commission ‰∏áÂõΩÁ¶ÅÁÉü‰ºöheld its first meeting in Shanghai, at which China‚Äôs situation was presented in detail. In 1911, the International Opium Conference was held in The Hague, which reached a protocol about restrictions on opium trade. (Lodwick 1996: 117-144) In the same year, China and Britain renewed the agreement that the opium trade would cease entirely by 1917 or even before that date if the Chinese could prove that all native cultivation had stopped. (Lodwick 1996: 166) It was said that the missionaries did not terminate the opium trade themselves, but they did contribute to the process through the Anti-Opium League and other channels.

Christian or Pro-Christian Events/Movements

China Centenary Missionary Conference of 1907: It was held in Shanghai from April 25 to May 8, 1907. In continuation of the missionary conferences in 1877 and 1890, it was originally planned for 1900. The Boxer Movement and other reasons made it postponed being a centenary celebration of the first Protestant missionary Robert Morrison‚Äôs coming to China in 1807. There were 1,170 delegates in total, representing 3,700 missionaries working in China then. Although the Chinese church was a main theme of the conference, fewer than ten Chinese could be identified among the participants. Twelve subjects were discussed around specific program committees. One key topic was ‚Äúcomity and federation,‚Äù to address the denominational divisions of missionaries working in China. A Christian Federation of China was proposed to handle such an issue. It also reflected a Protestant missionary consensus in China then. The missionaries not only cared about growth of converts. It was said to be 180,000 communicants at the time of conference, a big achievement of one-century‚Äôs mission work. They also expected to influence all strata of China‚Äôs society, with education as a main tool. So, the conference called for mission schools to be expanded and upgraded. Some of them even dreamed making China a Christian nation. But there were also debates regarding tensions between preaching and social services. Another important topic was about the unequal treaties. Two questions were discussed: whether the treaty protections should be maintained; and whether the Chinese converts should be put under protections. The committee chair Calvin Wilson Mateer from the American Presbyterian Mission (North) withheld the situation and expected possible political reforms to help solve the issue later. But it was opposed by Dixon Edward Hoste, the general director of China Inland Mission, and others. They thought the unequal treaties had hindered the spread of gospel and arose Chinese hate. The conference was characterized by an atmosphere of optimism. It manifested the achievements of the past century and prepared foundation for the future union movement. But some potential conflicts were also to be seen, such as the rise of Chinese nationalism and anti-imperialist spirit, and the controversy between liberalism and fundamentalism in the 1920s. (Centenary Conference Committee 1907; Yao 2008)

The Independent Church Movement Ëá™Á´ãËøêÂä® was a dominant response of Chinese Christians to the situation after the Boxer Movement. Conservatives and Pentecostals: After 1900, there were large groups of new mission societies rushing into China. A large part of them were from conservative, evangelical churches, with the ‚Äúfaith missionaries‚Äù in particular. This broke the consensus of the dominant missionary bodies in the 19th century, theologically represented by the Congregationalists and the Presbyterians. Anti-Christian Events/Movements

The Chenzhou Missionary Case in 1902 Ëæ∞Â∑ûÊïôÊ°à: Hunan was well known as one of the most anti-foreign and anti-Christian provinces. The China Inland Mission sent missionaries to Chenzhou in 1888, but were expelled by the local folks. In 1901, J.R. Buruce and R.H. Lewis came to build a gospel church and a hospital. A plague happened in 1902. The missionaries were accused of putting poison in water, and were beat to death by the local people. The British minister protested to the Qing court. Under pressure, ten more people were sentenced to death as murders. The local officials, including one prefectural magistrate, two country magistrates, and two military generals, were either dismissed or exiled. And two military generals had death sentence. The local government also paid to the British 80,000 taels for compensation, and a separate land was allocated to build church and other properties. A memorial stele was erected for this event. The locals took it as a humiliation, and it was bombed by the troops of the later communist general He Long Ë¥∫Èæô in 1924. As one of the few missionary cases after the Boxer Movement, the penalty was really heavy to the Hunan province. And it pushed Chinese Christians for independent churches, with the Chinese Christian Union ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£Âæí‰ºö as a typical example. (Wang 2008)

The Nanchang Missionary Case in 1906 ÂçóÊòåÊïôÊ°à: In February 1906, when meeting with the French priest Jean-Marie Lacruche, the Nanchang magistrate Jiang Shaotang Ê±üÁªçÊ£†was found throat-cut in the mission compound. A controversy rose whether Jiang did suicide or was murdered by the priest, without clear witness at the moment. Two days later, on a Sunday morning, 700 students together with local folks rushed to the mission compound for protest. The mission compound was set ablaze, with 9 foreigners being killed or fatally injured, including one couple and their nine-year daughter from the Plymouth Brethren. Some other missionaries, such as the American Methodist Episcopal Church and the China Inland Mission, survived the riots due to their distance from the Catholic compound or the instant coming of soldiers. Later, it was found that Magistrate Jiang went to the mission compound to fix two earlier missionary cases: the Chigang case of 1901 and the Tangpu case of 1904. The first actually happened between the Methodist and Catholic believers regarding a short ride on river. Mutual attacks and sacks happened which led to a few deaths and wounds. A British and a French consul arrived to fix the case. However, the local officials were involved due to personal relationship with some attests, and there was a controversy regarding attest and release. The second case happened because two Chinese lay Catholics attempted to open a chapel in a town market, and they were beaten and drown with their bodies never found. An insurrection happened against Catholics. It was reported by a British missionary that the leader of the outburst Gong Dong was a former deacon of the American Methodist Episcopal Church. Jiang who was then in charge of the issue, arrested Gong but promised no execution. But the Catholic mission was displeased with that. Lacruche happened to be the person who negotiated with Jiang. Jiang was hailed by the Chinese as a hero. But the provincial government had to make concessions and compensations due to the pressure of British and French ministers. In particular, the provincial treasurer was dismissed, and the provincial judge was demoted. Both were involved in the 1901 case to press Magistrate Jiang. The provincial governor was also dismissed as a punishment. Nine alleged offenders were executed, including Gong Dong, and thirty-three others were also sentenced to jails for various terms. But the Catholics who were involved in the Chigang sack were totally released. 33,000 taels were paid to the British for settlement. The French claimed more with a final number of 355,000 taels plus some land. It was said to be the largest missionary case after the Boxer Movement of 1900. The complicated factors involved shocked and confused the audience and attracted particular academic attention. (Young 1996; Ma 2008)

The prominent response from Christians was the independence church movement. For example, the Chenzhou missionary case Ëæ∞Â∑ûÊïôÊ°à became the direct cause leading to the foundation of the Chinese Christian Union ‰∏≠ÂõΩÂü∫Áù£Âæí‰ºö. (Wu 2014)
