Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Relevant Regulations on Religion

In December 1990, the National Religious Work Conference was held in Beijing. The conference discussed and passed the Circular on Some Problems Concerning the Further Improvement of Work on Religion (referred to as Document No. 6), which clarified the direction and tasks of religious work going forward. Document No. 6 affirmed that ‚Äúthe overall situation of religious work is good,‚Äù but also pointed out that ‚Äúforeign hostile forces have continuously taken advantage of religion to infiltrate and carry out destructive activities against us,‚Äù and indicated that there are ‚Äúa few hostile elements that are active, establishing illegal organizations to contest leadership in temples, monasteries, and churches,‚Äù as well as ‚Äúillegally running scripture schools, seminaries, and theological colleges to compete for the youth.‚Äù Therefore, it is essential to recognize the complexity and seriousness of religious issues and to pay great attention to religious work.

The document covers six key points: (1) to be conducive to ‚Äúuniting the vast masses of believers and to the stability of the nation and society,‚Äù comprehensive and correct implementation of the policy of freedom of religious belief must be executed; (2) religious affairs must be managed according to law; (3) fully leverage the role of patriotic religious groups; (4) resolutely combat criminal activities utilizing religion; (5) improve the organizational structure of religious work and strengthen the construction of religious work cadres; (6) enhance the leadership of the party in religious work.

Among them, the management of religious affairs according to law can be said to be the focus of Document No. 6. It mentions that religious activities should be included within the scope of laws, regulations, and policies, clearly stating that ‚Äúall places of religious activity must be registered according to law,‚Äù and firmly prohibiting the illegal preaching activities of ‚Äúself-appointed preachers.‚Äù At the same time, foreign religious groups and individuals are not allowed to interfere in China‚Äôs religious affairs, including establishing offices, building temples, monasteries, and churches, or conducting missionary activities. Projects with foreign cooperation ‚Äúmust not carry conditions related to missionary work, establishment of religious institutions, or building of temples, monasteries, and churches.‚Äù It also indicates that ‚Äúwe must accelerate religious legislation,‚Äù which involves administrative regulations concerning religious affairs and allows provinces, autonomous regions, and municipalities to formulate local religious administrative regulations. This initiated a new phase in the religious legislative work of China in the 1990s.

In April 1991, the State Council‚Äôs Bureau of Religious Affairs submitted a report titled ‚ÄúIdeas on the Religious Legislative System and Religious Legislative Projects During the ‚ÄòEighth Five-Year Plan‚Äô‚Äù and proposed four levels of legislative ideas: (1) laws promulgated by the National People‚Äôs Congress or its Standing Committee that adjust the relationship between religion and other aspects of society as a whole, ensure citizens‚Äô rights to freedom of religious belief, protect the legitimate rights and interests of religious organizations, and ensure the normalization of religious activities, i.e., to formulate a basic law concerning religion. At that time, three proposals were envisioned: (i) to formulate a Religious Law; (ii) to separately formulate laws regarding freedom of religious belief and religious organizations; (iii) to formulate general principles for religious laws. (2) Administrative regulations promulgated by the State Council adjusting issues common to various religions that can be resolved within government jurisdiction, such as management of religious venues, management of religious personnel, management of foreigners‚Äô religious activities in China, and management of religious audiovisual products. (3) Departmental regulations that adjust issues with a smaller scope, strong policy orientation, and fall within the realm of internal departmental management. (4) Local religious regulations or local government regulations formulated by local people‚Äôs congresses or governments based on local conditions. Additionally, the promotion and support for religious groups and temples to combine their characteristics to formulate internal management systems and regulations according to law (not part of legislation). From subsequent developments, it can be seen that, apart from the first proposal, the others have been implemented to varying degrees.

This is the second policy document concerning religious issues and work from the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China since Document No. 19 in 1982. The next similar central document was the Decision of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China on Strengthening Religious Work in 2002 (Document No. 3).

National Religious Administrative Regulations 1994

Administrative regulations are a general term for normative legal documents related to administrative management and administration of administrative matters promulgated by the highest national authority, the State Council. Their status is lower than that of the constitution and laws, but higher than that of general local regulations.

On January 31, 1994, Premier Li Peng signed State Council Orders No. 144 and 145, promulgating the Regulations on the Supervision of the Religious Activities of Foreigners in China (Order No. 144) and the Regulations Regarding the Management of Places of Religious Activities (Order No. 145).

Order No. 144 consists of a total of 13 articles and is the first regulation specifically addressing the religious activities of foreigners in China. Subsequently, the State Administration for Religious Affairs‚Äô Interpretation of Certain Provisions of the Regulations on the Supervision of the Religious Activities of Foreigners in China was issued in April 1994, followed by the Specific Rules for Implementing the Regulations on the Management of Religious Activities of Foreigners in China in September 2000 (revised in November 2010).

The term ‚Äúforeigners‚Äù as referred to in Order No. 144 also includes ‚ÄúChinese citizens who reside abroad‚Äù and residents of Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Macau (Article 11). There is a clause stating ‚Äúrespect for the religious beliefs of foreigners‚Äù (Article 2), which the authorities interpret as ‚Äúregardless of which religion they believe in, and whether this religion exists in China or not, the Chinese government shows respect.‚Äù At the same time, it stipulated that foreigners conducting religious activities in China ‚Äúmust not establish religious organizations, set up religious offices, establish places for religious activities, or run religious schools in China, nor develop believers among Chinese citizens, appoint religious personnel, and carry out other missionary activities‚Äù (Article 8). This reflects the principle of ‚Äúdifferentiated treatment‚Äù adopted by the Chinese government‚Äîon one hand, respecting the religious activities of foreigners of different faiths in China (excluding preaching to Chinese people and developing believers), and on the other hand, limiting the ‚Äúlegitimate religions‚Äù available for Chinese people to the ‚Äúfive major religions.‚Äù

Order No. 144 stipulates that ‚Äúforeigners may participate in religious activities at temples, monasteries, mosques, churches, and other religious venues in China. Invited by provincial, autonomous region, or direct-controlled municipality religious organizations, foreigners may preach and give sermons in Chinese religious venues‚Äù (Article 3). Additionally, religious activities ‚Äúwith participation from foreigners‚Äù can be held in locations approved by the religious affairs department of the county-level people‚Äôs government (Article 4). This refers to the ‚Äúdesignated religious venues‚Äù approved by the State Administration for Religious Affairs for foreigners in China. These designated venues are not only for the five major religions recognized by the Chinese government (for instance, the Beijing International Mandarin Church was approved for establishment on August 12, 2007) but also include religions outside the five major religions (such as Mormonism). In 2000, the State Administration for Religious Affairs stated in the Specific Rules for Implementing the Regulations on the Management of Religious Activities of Foreigners in China that ‚Äúforeign religious organizations and their members without corresponding legitimate Chinese religious organizations in China‚Äù may engage in interaction with relevant departments of the Chinese government or religious communities, with the consent of the provincial religious affairs department and subsequent approval from the national authorities (Article 9).

The issuance of Order No. 144 reflects the continuous increase in the number of foreigners working and studying in China as part of the reform and opening-up. Taking Shanghai as an example, official estimates show that the number of foreigners living in Shanghai increased from 3 in 1998.

Religious Activity Regulations and Management

In 2019, the number of foreign residents in Shanghai increased from 50,000 in 1998 to 180,000. Among the foreign individuals in China, there are many who practice various religions. In addition to the five major religions recognized by the Chinese government (Buddhism, Taoism, Islam, Christianity, and Catholicism), there are also various other religions (such as Judaism, Mormonism, Baha‚Äôi Faith, etc.). Meeting the religious activity needs of these foreigners has become a challenge for China‚Äôs religious work. It has been reported that consulates from countries such as the United States, Canada, South Korea, France, Italy, and Australia in Shanghai have repeatedly demanded that the Shanghai Municipal Government and relevant departments arrange for the religious lives of their citizens in Shanghai. Other foreigners and individuals from Hong Kong, Macau, Taiwan, and overseas have also continuously made new religious requests.

Order No. 145 consists of a total of 20 articles and outlines management measures for places of religious activities, introducing specific requirements and procedures for registration. Its promulgation was in accordance with the requirements of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China‚Äôs Document No. 6 (1991): ‚ÄúAll places of religious activities must be registered in accordance with the law (detailed methods will be specified separately).‚Äù

The purpose of the regulations is ‚Äúto protect normal religious activities, maintain the legitimate rights and interests of places of religious activities, and facilitate the management of places of religious activities‚Äù (Article 1). It stipulates that ‚Äútemples, monasteries, mosques, churches, and other designated places for conducting religious activities‚Äù ‚Äúmust register. Registration methods will be formulated by the State Council‚Äôs Bureau of Religious Affairs‚Äù (Article 2). Furthermore, ‚Äúno one may use places of religious activities to conduct activities that undermine national unity, ethnic solidarity, social stability, harm citizens‚Äô health, and interfere with the national education system,‚Äù and that ‚Äúthey are not subject to the control of foreign organizations and individuals‚Äù (Article 4). ‚ÄúThe religious affairs departments of the people‚Äôs governments at county level and above shall provide guidance and supervision on the implementation of these regulations‚Äù (Article 13), and the interpretation of these regulations falls under the responsibility of the State Administration for Religious Affairs (Article 19).

In April, the State Administration for Religious Affairs issued the Registration Procedures for Venues for Religious Activities. To establish a place for religious activities, the following conditions must be met: (1) a fixed location and name; (2) citizens who regularly participate in religious activities; (3) an organizational management structure composed of believing citizens; (4) religious personnel or individuals who meet the religious qualifications to host religious activities; (5) management regulations; (6) legal economic income (Article 2). Regarding registration procedures, the responsible person of the management organization of such a venue must submit an application for establishment registration to the religious affairs department of the people‚Äôs government at county level or above (Article 4). Those that meet the requirements will be registered, while those that are ‚Äúnot fully compliant‚Äù will be subject to ‚Äútemporary registration or deferred registration‚Äù based on circumstances, and those that do not meet the requirements ‚Äúwill not be registered‚Äù (Article 6).

In July 1996, the State Administration for Religious Affairs issued the Method for the Annual Inspection of Places of Religious Activity (21 articles), requiring registered places of religious activity to undergo annual inspections. The annual inspection content includes: (1) compliance with national laws, regulations, and policies; (2) the formulation and implementation of management regulations; (3) major religious activities and foreign-related activities; (4) major financial management and income and expenditure situation; (5) changes in registered items; (6) changes and management of affiliated enterprises, institutions, and existing real estate; (7) other relevant matters (Article 4). The management organization of religious activity venues must fill out the annual inspection form and submit it to the competent authority (Article 6), which will conduct the inspection and sign off on the findings (Article 7). If the annual inspection results are ‚Äúunsatisfactory,‚Äù they must be rectified within a specified time (Article 12). If the venue fails to accept the annual inspection or does not rectify within the specified time, the person in charge will be held accountable, and penalties will be applied to the venue, including warnings, cessation of activities, or cancellation of registration based on the severity of the situation. In particularly serious cases, it will be referred to the same-level people‚Äôs court for closure (Article 14). The venue management organization may apply for administrative reconsideration or file an administrative lawsuit against the administrative actions of the competent authority (Article 15).

In November 2004, Premier Wen Jiabao signed State Council Order No. 426, promulgating the Regulations on Religious Affairs, which officially came into effect on March 1, 2005. The drafting of the Regulations on Religious Affairs took six years and is the first comprehensive administrative regulation concerning religion in China, marking a new milestone in China‚Äôs religious legislative work.

Departmental Regulations of the State Council

  1. (ÂúãÂÆ∂ÂÆóÊïô‰∫ãÂãôÂ±ÄÊîøÁ≠ñÊ≥ïË¶èÂè∏2000: 8-22; Chinese Law and Government 2000a: 69-74)

Departmental regulations refer to various administrative normative legal documents published by ministries and commissions under the State Council based on laws and administrative regulations of the State Council, also known as ministerial regulations.

In the 1990s, the departmental regulations related to religious affairs included: (1) Rules for the Implementation of Registration and Administration of Religious Social Bodies, issued by the State Administration for Religious Affairs and the Ministry of Civil Affairs in May 1991; (2) Registration Procedures for Venues for Religious Activities, issued by the State Administration for Religious Affairs in April 1994; (3) Method for the Annual Inspection of Places of Religious Activity, issued by the State Administration for Religious Affairs in July 1996; (4) Method for Employing Foreign Specialists to Religious Schools, issued by the State Administration for Religious Affairs, the State Foreign Expert Bureau, and the Public Security Bureau in November 1998.

Among them, the Rules for the Implementation of Registration and Administration of Religious Social Bodies were formulated based on the administrative regulations Regulations on the Registration and Administration of Social Organizations, issued in October 1989. The relevant regulations stipulate that social organizations accept ‚Äúguidance from the relevant business management departments,‚Äù and all organizations must obtain ‚Äúapproval from the relevant business management departments‚Äù before applying for registration with the ‚Äúregistration management authority‚Äù (i.e., the Ministry of Civil Affairs at all levels). The ‚Äúbusiness management departments‚Äù and the ‚Äúregistration management authority‚Äù constitute the dual management system of the Chinese government over social organizations. For religious social organizations, the Bureau of Religious Affairs and the Ministry of Civil Affairs formulated corresponding registration management methods, applying the aforementioned dual management system to religious social organizations, meaning that religious organizations at all levels must first secure approval from the corresponding religious affairs departments (business management departments) before applying for registration with the Civil Affairs Department (registration management authority) (Articles 2 and 3). The establishment conditions include: (1) a group name, office address, and person in charge; (2) statutes that do not violate the constitution, laws, or regulations; (3) legal sources of income; (4) verifiable classics, doctrines, and regulations that conform to China‚Äôs existing religious history and do not contradict the organization‚Äôs statutes; (5) broad representation among organizing members (Article 4). Additionally, it stipulates: ‚ÄúNo identical or similar religious social organization may be established within the same administrative area‚Äù (Article 7). In other words, registered religious organizations have a special monopolistic position, excluding competition from other similar organizations.

The Registration Procedures for Venues for Religious Activities and the Method for the Annual Inspection of Places of Religious Activity were issued primarily based on the provisions of Order No. 145, Registration Procedures for Venues for Religious Activities.

As for the Method for Employing Foreign Specialists to Religious Schools, it was formulated based on Order No. 144, Regulations on the Supervision of the Religious Activities of Foreigners in China, and the Interim Provisions on the Work of Foreign Cultural and Educational Experts, consisting of a total of 8 chapters and 36 articles: (1) General Principles; (2) Employment Principles; (3) Application and Approval for Employment Qualifications; (4) Conditions for Hired Personnel; (5) Reporting and Approval of Employed Personnel; (6) Management of Teaching Work of Foreign Specialists; (7) Legal Responsibilities; and (8) Supplementary Provisions. The provisions primarily target religious schools, stipulating that the hiring of long-term or short-term foreign specialists must follow prescribed application and reporting processes.   1. White Paper: Freedom of Religious Belief in China, 1997 (ÂúãÂãôÈô¢Êñ∞ËÅûËæ¶ÂÖ¨ÂÆ§1997; Chinese Law and Government 2003b: 7-20)
