  1. Han Wenzao (Wang Rongwei 2006: 12-14; Shen De Rong 2006: 19-20; Zhao Zhi En 2006: 18-21; Amity Foundation 2006: 13-16)

Han Wenzao (ÈüìÊñáËóª, 1923-2006), born in Shanghai, attended the Huadong Public School run by the Shanghai Municipal Council. In 1940, he enrolled in the Civil Engineering Department of Zhejiang University and was baptized as a Christian the same year. After the Pacific War, he transferred to St. John‚Äôs University in Shanghai. After graduating in 1944, he served as a training officer in the Student Department of the Shanghai YMCA, and after a year of training, was assigned to work at the YMCA in Nanjing. In 1951, he became a member of the Christian Three-Self Reform Committee and also served as the General Secretary of the Nanjing Three-Self Reform Movement Promotion Association, later becoming the General Secretary and Chairman of the Nanjing Three-Self Patriotic Association, playing a key role in the Nanjing Three-Self Movement. Additionally, he served as the Vice Chairman and Secretary-General of the Jiangsu Christian Three-Self Patriotic Movement Committee and from 1961, was the Chairman of the Board of the Jinling Union Theological Seminary and the Office Director.

In 1980, he was elected as the Deputy Secretary-General of the National Three-Self and Deputy General Secretary of the National Christian Association during the Third National Christian Conference, as well as the Chairman of the Jiangsu Christian Three-Self Patriotic Movement Committee and President of the Jiangsu Christian Association. In 1991, he became the Vice President of the China Christian Association and Vice Chairman of the Jiangsu Political Consultative Conference. From 1997 to 2002, he succeeded the retired Bishop Ding Guangxun as President of the China Christian Association and was also a member of the Standing Committee of the National Political Consultative Conference and Vice Chairman of the Jiangsu Political Consultative Conference.

In 1985, Han Wenzao assisted Bishop Ding Guangxun in founding China‚Äôs first non-governmental organization initiated by Christians, the Amity Foundation, and served as Vice Chairman and Secretary-General for a long time, leading the Amity Foundation to actively introduce resources from overseas churches for charitable causes in China, such as education, healthcare, social welfare, and rural development. Later, Han Wenzao, along with Bishop Ding Guangxun, collaborated with the United Bible Societies to establish Amity Printing Co., Ltd., prioritizing the printing of Bibles for Chinese churches.

After the reform and opening-up, Han Wenzao was actively involved in and responsible for the foreign affairs work of the National Two Sessions of Christianity, adhering to the principle of independent management of churches and summarizing the six-character principle of ‚Äúlegal, open, and honest‚Äù for foreign affairs work. He worked hard to facilitate visits to the World Council of Churches by the National Two Sessions of Christianity in 1983 and 1989, and he helped the China Christian Association become a member of the World Council of Churches in 1991. Han defended the situation of religious freedom in China, citing the significant increase in the number of Chinese Christians since the end of the Cultural Revolution. He criticized the U.S. State Department‚Äôs 1999 International Religious Freedom Report for inconsistencies with facts regarding China and, during the visit of the Chinese religious leaders‚Äô delegation to the U.S. in 2000, he stated that foreign believers who come to China to preach without an invitation from the China Christian Association or provincial associations are violating Chinese law.

Han Wenzao actively participated in activities promoting world peace and served as the Vice Chairman and Secretary-General of the Chinese Religious Peace Committee, Executive Committee member of the Asian Religious Peace Conference, and Council member of the International Council of the World Religious Peace Conference, actively engaging in international activities that promote world peace, such as the Asian Religious Peace Conference and the United Nations Millennium Peace Conference for Religious and Spiritual Leaders.

He passed away on February 3, 2006, at the age of 83.
