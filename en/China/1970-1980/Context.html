China: Context (1970-1980)

Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Related Regulations on Religion

  1. Constitution of the People‚Äôs Republic of China 1975 (Xu Chongde 2003: 408-496; Li Weihan 1981: 569)

After the Ninth Party Congress held by the Communist Party of China in 1969, Mao Zedong proposed to amend the constitution, and in March 1970, a constitutional amendment group led by Kang Sheng was established. In July, the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China established the ‚ÄúConstitutional Amendment Drafting Committee,‚Äù chaired by Mao Zedong and vice-chaired by Lin Biao. In January 1975, the First Session of the Fourth National People‚Äôs Congress adopted the ‚ÄúConstitution of the People‚Äôs Republic of China‚Äù (the ‚Äò75 Constitution), which was born during the Cultural Revolution. The new constitution used ‚Äúcontinue to carry out class struggle‚Äù as its guiding ideology and reduced the scope of citizens‚Äô basic rights and freedoms.

The ‚Äô75 Constitution consists of 4 chapters, excluding the preface. In Chapter 3, ‚ÄúCitizens‚Äô Basic Rights and Duties,‚Äù there are only 4 clauses. Clause 28 states: ‚ÄúCitizens have the freedom of speech, communication, publication, assembly, association, procession, demonstration, strike, the freedom to believe in religion, the freedom not to believe in religion, and the freedom to advocate atheism.‚Äù

Li Weihan, then Minister of the United Front Work Department of the Central Committee, pointed out as early as 1961 that ‚Äúensuring that both believers and non-believers have freedom essentially protects the freedom of people to be non-believers; it promotes the retreat from religious beliefs rather than the promotion of religious beliefs.‚Äù

  1. Constitution of the People‚Äôs Republic of China 1978 (Xu Chongde 2003: 497-547)

After the Cultural Revolution ended, the Communist Party of China held the Eleventh Party Congress in August 1977, where Ye Jianying made a report on amending the constitution, and the Political Bureau of the Central Committee was responsible for leading the constitutional amendment. In February 1978, the Second Plenary Session of the Eleventh Central Committee passed the draft amendment, which was approved in March at the First Session of the Fifth National People‚Äôs Congress. The ‚Äô78 Constitution mainly revised the Cultural Revolution tone of the ‚Äô75 Constitution and restored some civil rights clauses from the ‚Äô54 Constitution. However, the ‚Äô78 Constitution continued to mention ‚ÄúMao Zedong Thought‚Äù in the text, representing only minor modifications to the ‚Äô75 Constitution.

The ‚Äô78 Constitution, excluding the preface, consists of 4 chapters. In Chapter 3, ‚ÄúCitizens‚Äô Basic Rights and Duties,‚Äù Clause 46 states: ‚ÄúCitizens have the freedom to believe in religion and the freedom not to believe in religion, and the freedom to advocate atheism.‚Äù Aside from restoring a standalone clause concerning religious belief, it essentially maintained the statement of the ‚Äô75 Constitution.

  1. Document No. 65 1978 (Zeng Chuanhui 2020; She He 2015: 526-527; Hubei Provincial Ethnic and Religious Affairs Bureau 1988: 4)

In July 1978, the United Front Work Department of the Central Committee held a seminar attended by heads of the United Front Work Departments from some provinces, municipalities, and autonomous regions. In October, the central government formulated a ‚ÄúReport on Two Urgent Policy Issues Needing Resolution in Current Religious Work‚Äù (Document No. 65) based on the opinions from this meeting. The two policy issues were:

  1. ‚ÄúTo earnestly and comprehensively implement the religious freedom policy stipulated in the constitution. Respect the lawful religious lives of believers and open a limited number of temples and churches,‚Äù with the goal of ‚Äúturning the public‚Äôs religious activities into open events,‚Äù while ‚Äúeliminating secret underground religious activities.‚Äù The report pointed out that Clause 46 of the ‚Äô78 Constitution is the principled basis for religious work, ‚Äúboth a powerful weapon in the struggle against religion and a legal guarantee for citizens‚Äô religious freedom.‚Äù It also indicated that fully implementing the religious freedom policy not only helps ‚Äúmobilize the vast number of believers in building socialism but is also the correct way to gradually weaken religious forces and influence. Fears that opening a limited number of temples and churches to bring religious activities out from underground will ‚Ä¶ expand religious influence are unfounded.‚Äù

  2. ‚ÄúStrictly distinguish between two types of contradictory nature and strengthen the management of religious activities,‚Äù reiterating the following stipulations: (1) Citizens aged 18 and older have the freedom to believe in religion, the freedom not to believe in religion, and the freedom to advocate atheism. No one may be forced to believe or not believe, nor may anyone be discriminated against for their religious beliefs or non-beliefs; (2) It is prohibited to instill religious thoughts in minors under 18 years of age, and minors are not allowed to participate in religious activities; (3) The government protects normal religious activities, and all religious activities must comply with government policies and laws, not interfere with administration, education, and marriage, nor disrupt production and social order, nor deceive or endanger personal health. Collective property may not be used for religious activities; (4) The abolished religious feudal privileges and oppressive systems may not be restored; (5) Any illegal or unlawful activities violating the above provisions should be stopped through education; (6) Communist Party members and Youth League members may not believe in religion; (7) Implement the policies of unity, education, and transformation among religious figures, addressing their political, work, and life difficulties.Select and cultivate politically reliable individuals with knowledge of religion to adapt to future work needs; (8) For class enemies conducting counter-revolutionary activities under the guise of religion, mobilize the masses to thoroughly expose and resolutely strike back, gradually weakening the influence and power of religious forces and consolidating the dictatorship of the proletariat.

  3. The Eighth National Religious Work Conference (Hubei Provincial Ethnic and Religious Affairs Bureau 1988: 2-9)

From December 1 to 11, 1978, the Eighth National Religious Work Conference was held in Beijing. It was the first national religious work conference after the end of the Cultural Revolution, with a 13-year gap since the last one in 1963. This conference mainly discussed the ‚ÄúNotice on the Central Committee‚Äôs Circulation of the United Front Work Department‚Äôs ‚ÄòReport on Two Urgent Policy Issues Needing Resolution in Current Religious Work‚Äô‚Äù (Document No. 65).

The conference acknowledged that during the Cultural Revolution, ‚Äúwhile religious activities seemed to have ceased on the surface, in reality, most turned underground, beyond our control, and imperialism and class enemies took the opportunity to incite and manipulate the dissatisfaction of believers, leading to rampant religious forces in certain areas, with some regions even experiencing chaos.‚Äù Therefore, it was essential to eliminate the influence of extreme leftist thought that sought to ‚Äúuse administrative orders and coercive measures to eradicate religion.‚Äù

The conference proposed that religious work should pay attention to: (1) Carefully handle religious issues, draw a clear line between ideological belief issues and political issues, and strictly distinguish and correctly address two different types of contradictions; (2) The thorough resolution of religious issues is a ‚Äúhistorically lengthy process,‚Äù and religious work must have ‚Äúlong-term plans‚Äù; (3) Religion involves the beliefs of millions of people and can only be addressed through persuasion and education, guiding positively, without coercion, suppression, or attacks; (4) Attention should be paid to the distinctions and connections between religious issues and ethnic issues; (5) The policy of religious freedom is the fundamental policy for resolving religious issues.

The conference provided opinions on restoring and improving religious work institutions, revitalizing various religious organizations, and issues related to religious properties: (1) It is recommended that the central government restore the institutions and staffing of the religious affairs office under the State Council, and corresponding religious work departments of provincial and municipal governments should also be restored; (2) The patriotic religious organizations of the five major religions should be revived, ‚Äúexcept that Taoism no longer has much influence domestically and abroad and need not be re-established.‚Äù Local religious groups should ‚Äúoperate under the guidance of local government religious work departments and have no affiliation with national organizations‚Äù; (3) Restoration of past religious undertaking funds (approximately 1.5 million yuan annually nationwide); (4) For religious property, those renting out their properties should handle it according to the pre-Cultural Revolution methods, unfreezing their bank deposits.

  1. United Front Work Department‚Äôs Report on Removing the ‚ÄúSurrenderism‚Äù Label from National United Front, Ethnic, and Religious Departments 1979 (General Research Team of the Central Committee‚Äôs Literature Research Office, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 1-4)

As early as 1964, Li Weihan, then Minister of the United Front Work Department, was criticized for adhering to a ‚Äúrevisionist line that is anti-Party, anti-central, and anti-Chairman Mao‚Äù regarding the united front, ethnic, and religious work. After the onset of the Cultural Revolution, the United Front Work Department became known as the ‚Äúheadquarters of revisionism,‚Äù and national united front and ethnic and religious work departments were labeled as ‚Äúimplementing a surrenderism line.‚Äù Other accusations included ‚Äúshelters for monsters and freaks,‚Äù ‚Äúa base for the restoration of capitalism,‚Äù ‚Äúagents of the bourgeoisie,‚Äù ‚Äúworthy sons and grandsons of the bourgeoisie,‚Äù and ‚Äúcounter-revolutionary revisionists.‚Äù

In February 1979, the United Front Work Department of the Central Committee published a report recommending the removal of the ‚Äúsurrenderism‚Äù label on national united front, ethnic, and religious work departments, reflecting on the united front work in ethnic and religious matters since the founding of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, stating that the accusation of ‚Äúimplementing surrenderism and a revisionist line‚Äù was entirely unfounded. The report further noted that since the ‚Äúoverthrow of the Gang of Four,‚Äù the central government had been rectifying past errors, but the ‚Äúpoison and influence‚Äù of Lin Biao and the ‚ÄúGang of Four‚Äù in united front as well as ethnic and religious work still existed severely. Many officials had yet to be fully liberated from the imposed ‚Äúsurrenderism‚Äù mental shackles. Therefore, removing the ‚Äúsurrenderism‚Äù label from national united front, ethnic, and religious work departments was an urgent and significant issue.

The report suggested to the Central Committee: (1) Communicate the report to the entire party; (2) Have Xinhua News Agency publish the news and have United Front Work Department Minister Ulanfu deliver a speech to the party and the public, with an announcement in the press; (3) Completely overturn all slanders attached to cadres at various levels of the united front system and non-party personnel concerning ‚Äúimplementing a surrenderism line,‚Äù thoroughly vindicate them, and destroy any conclusions related to their personal files; (4) When convening a national united front work conference, clarify the guiding tasks for united front work in the new era.In March 1979, the Central Committee officially removed the ‚Äúsurrenderism and revisionism line‚Äù label from national united front, ethnic, and religious work departments.

  1. Criminal Law of the People‚Äôs Republic of China 1979 (General Research Team of the Central Committee‚Äôs Literature Research Office, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 9)

In July 1979, the Second Session of the Fifth National People‚Äôs Congress passed the ‚ÄúCriminal Law of the People‚Äôs Republic of China.‚Äù Article 147 states: ‚ÄúState personnel who illegally deprive citizens of their legitimate religious freedom and infringe upon the customs and habits of ethnic minorities, in serious cases, shall be sentenced to imprisonment of up to two years or detention.‚Äù

  1. Party‚Äôs Religious Policy in the New Era 1979 (General Research Team of the Central Committee‚Äôs Literature Research Office, Policy and Regulations Department of the State Administration for Religious Affairs 1995: 12; United Front Work Department of the Central Committee 1988: 436-440, 461)

From August 15 to September 3, 1979, the 14th National United Front Work Conference was held in Beijing. This conference was held 16 years after the last national united front work conference in 1963 and marked the first united front work conference since the end of the Cultural Revolution, heralding the opening-up era.

On October 14, the Central Committee circulated the document from the national united front work conference titled ‚ÄúGuiding Tasks of the United Front in the New Historical Period‚Äù (from September 13), listing religious work as one of the ten united front tasks in the new period, reiterating ‚Äúcomprehensive implementation of the policy of religious freedom.‚Äù It acknowledged significant ‚Äúresistance‚Äù in this implementation and expressed that it was ‚Äúextremely detrimental to uniting hundreds of millions of believers,‚Äù providing ‚Äúopportunities for individuals within the country to conduct illegal religious activities and for international religious forces to infiltrate.‚Äù Therefore, it was necessary to promptly resolve issues related to places of religious activities, supplies, and the clergy conducting those activities, ‚Äúuniting the vast majority of believers and religious figures around the government to contribute to the Four Modernizations under the leadership of the Party.‚Äù It also pointed out the need to harness the positive role of patriotic religious organizations, strengthen the management of religious activities, and conduct ideological education work among believers and religious figures, as well as carry out ‚Äúatheistic propaganda education‚Äù among the general public.

Legal cases or events related to religious leaders or organizations:

  1. Li Tian‚Äôen Imprisoned for 7 Years 1972 (Ma Feibi 2001: 156-175)

In 1972, preacher Li Tian‚Äôen (also known as Li Mosheng), originally from Henan, learned about a revival in his hometown. After hearing that young people planned to dedicate themselves, he obtained police approval for a one-and-a-half-month leave to visit his hometown under the pretense of seeking traditional Chinese medicine. He was deeply moved by what he saw in Henan and led several gatherings. After returning to Shanghai, he couldn‚Äôt forget what he experienced in Henan and secretly decided to return two months later, preaching the gospel and leading gatherings in 13 counties. In June 1973, when Li returned to Shanghai, he was immediately arrested by the police on the charge of ‚Äúconducting religious activities in Henan, leading to religious fanaticism in the local area.‚Äù There were plans to send him back to Henan for execution, but due to his deteriorating health in custody, he was instead moved to a detention center for political prisoners in Shanghai. At the end of 1974, during his interrogation, he was asked to renounce his faith and so-called reactionary stance, but Li steadfastly testified for his faith. After being imprisoned for two years, he was brought to trial, and due to his continued refusal to renounce his beliefs, he was sent back to Henan in 1975. He was originally scheduled for execution before October, but due to a flood in Henan in August that caused severe devastation, the execution was rescheduled for the Spring Festival of 1976. However, Zhou Enlai passed away in early 1976, and his sentence was extended to October. Ultimately, due to the death of Mao Zedong and the fall of the Gang of Four, the officials handling his case were also arrested. Li Tian‚Äôen was released and returned to Shanghai in 1979.

  1. Pastor Wang Zhiming Martyrdom (Chan 2019: 142-150; Westminster Abbey n.d.; Huang Shuli 2022: 50-61)

On December 29, 1973, Miao pastor Wang Zhiming (Wang Zhiming, 1907-1973) was executed by the Chinese government.

Wang Zhiming, born in Wuding County, Yunnan, was a product of the China Inland Mission‚Äôs work among the Miao people. He attended the Sapushan Church Primary School from 1913 to 1924, after which he engaged in education and missionary work. In 1944, he served as the president of the Miao People‚Äôs Church of the Inland Mission based in Sapushan, overseeing Miao churches in five counties: Wuding, Luxian, Fumin, Lufeng, and Yuanmou‚Äîthe largest parish. In 1945, he translated hymns into the Miao language and compiled excerpts of the Old Testament in Miao. He was ordained as a pastor in 1951, becoming the first Miao pastor.

After the founding of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, starting in 1950, he participated in the signature campaign for the Three-Self Patriotic Movement in Christianity.However, during the land reform period in 1952, he refused to participate in denouncing landlords and missionaries, stating to his congregants: ‚ÄúMy hands have baptized many believers and must not be used to partake in evil.‚Äù In 1954, the Public Security Bureau briefly detained Wang on the charge of being a ‚Äúreligious spy,‚Äù but he was released after a few months. In 1955, the Wuding Public Security Bureau initiated criticism against Wang, sending him to a labor reform camp in Lufeng for six months. After his release, Wang was assigned a position at the county cultural center.

In 1956, a national delegation of ethnic minorities visited Beijing for the National Day celebration, and Wang, as a representative of the Miao, served as the vice-head of the delegation. After being received by Mao Zedong, he was subsequently appointed to the Political Consultative Conference of Chuxiong Prefecture. However, as the religious and ethnic policies began to shift further left in the 1960s, he was dismissed from all positions in 1964 and placed under close surveillance.

In 1966, after the Cultural Revolution broke out, Wang Zhiming was accused of being an ‚Äúimperialist spy and lackey,‚Äù and his home was raided. Nevertheless, Wang continued to secretly lead gatherings. On May 10, 1969, Wang was arrested along with his wife and son, charged with ‚Äúfive hats‚Äù: (1) imperialist lackey, unrepentant schemer, spiritual opiate for the people; (2) current counter-revolutionary; (3) consistent opposition to state religious policies; (4) landlord party; (5) having obstructed the Long March of the Red Army in the 1930s.

On December 28, 1973, the day before Wang Zhiming was executed, his son was permitted to visit him. Wang advised them: ‚ÄúYou should listen to those above, not to those below; you should work diligently and be productive, do not be like me who cannot work; you should pay attention to sanitation.‚Äù These three statements contained the teachings of the Bible: ‚ÄúSet your minds on things that are above, not on things that are on earth.‚Äù (Colossians 3:2); ‚ÄúTherefore, my beloved brothers, be steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that in the Lord your labor is not in vain.‚Äù (1 Corinthians 15:58); ‚ÄúTo be holy and blameless before our Father God.‚Äù (In the Miao language, ‚Äúclean‚Äù and ‚Äúholy‚Äù are the same word, with many scriptures regarding ‚Äúholiness,‚Äù such as 1 Thessalonians 3:13).

On December 29, Wang Zhiming was first taken to the football field of Wuding County Middle School for a public denunciation, where he was accused of betrayal by Long Fuhua, who claimed Wang was an imperialist lackey and part of a reactionary group. Long appealed to the revolutionary leaders present, stating his desire to represent the Miao people in requesting the People‚Äôs Republic of China government to eliminate Wang Zhiming, a harmful member of society. It was subsequently announced that he would be executed immediately. Before the execution, Wang was paraded through the streets, his mouth bound with wire to prevent him from speaking. He was taken to an abandoned airport for a public execution.

On December 1, 1980, Pastor Wang Zhiming was publicly vindicated, and a grave was constructed for him, with his family receiving 1,300 yuan in compensation. In 1981, a memorial monument was erected in front of his grave, inscribed with Revelation 14:13: ‚ÄúAnd I heard a voice from heaven saying, ‚ÄòWrite this: Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord from now on.‚Äô‚Äù

On July 9, 1998, Queen Elizabeth II announced a list of the top ten Christian martyrs of the 20th century, and Wang Zhiming was included. Westminster Abbey in the UK specially inscribed a set of statues outside commemorating these ten martyrs. On September 28, Rev. Dr. Anthony Harvey, sub-dean of Westminster, introduced Wang Zhiming during a sermon: ‚ÄúThere has never been a time in Christian history when someone, somewhere, has not died rather than compromise with the powers of oppression, tyranny and unbelief.‚Äù

Geopolitical Dynamics and International Relationships

  1. The People‚Äôs Republic of China and the United Nations (Ralph 1991: 840-842, 849-853; Chen Wenxian 2021: 13-34)

On October 25, 1971, the United Nations General Assembly adopted Resolution 2758: ‚ÄúThe Assembly, recalling the principles of the United Nations Charter, considering that the restoration of the lawful rights of the People‚Äôs Republic of China is essential for the maintenance of the purposes and principles of the United Nations and for the work that the United Nations must carry out in accordance with the Charter, recognizes that the representatives of the Government of the People‚Äôs Republic of China are the only legitimate representatives of China in the United Nations; the People‚Äôs Republic of China is one of the five permanent members of the Security Council, and decides to restore all rights of the People‚Äôs Republic of China and to recognize its representatives as the only legitimate representatives of China in the United Nations and to expel immediately the representatives of Chiang Kai-shek from the seats they illegitimately occupy in the United Nations and in all its affiliated agencies. The resolution replaced the ‚ÄúChina‚Äù seat in the United Nations with the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC), established in 1949, instead of the Republic of China (ROC). In fact, as early as November 18, 1949, after the establishment of the PRC on October 1, Foreign Minister Zhou Enlai sent a telegram to UN Secretary-General Trygve H. Lie and UN General Assembly President Carlos P. Romulo, protesting that the ‚Äúremaining reactionary Kuomintang government of China‚Äù represented the Chinese government in the United Nations, and requested the expulsion of the representatives sent by Chiang Kai-shek from their seats in the United Nations, to be replaced by representatives of the PRC government. At that time, the Soviet Union also echoed Zhou Enlai‚Äôs protest.

As the Korean War broke out in June 1950, U.S. President Harry A. Truman, worried about the expansion of communism in Asia, changed the policy towards Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs Kuomintang government in Taiwan, dispatching the Seventh Fleet to patrol the Taiwan Strait, thus continuing U.S. support for the ROC‚Äôs representation in the United Nations. In the 1950s, both the Soviet Union and India proposed in the UN General Assembly to include the issue of replacing the ROC with the PRC in the agenda, but it was rejected in the General Assembly due to the majority presence of the United States and its allies.

In the mid-1950s, U.S. President D. Wight Eisenhower and Secretary of State John F. Dulles envisioned the possibility of both the PRC and ROC having seats in the United Nations, but both Taipei and Beijing found it difficult to accept the ‚Äútwo Chinas‚Äù policy.

In one year in 1960, up to 17 third world countries (from Asia, Africa, and Latin America) joined the United Nations, making it even harder for the U.S. to support Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs government in retaining China‚Äôs seat in the UN. In 1961, President John F. Kennedy invoked Article 18 of the UN Charter, stating that the ‚ÄúChina representation issue‚Äù was a ‚Äúmatter of great importance‚Äù and had to be decided by a two-thirds majority of member states present and voting. At the same time, Kennedy hoped that the ROC would not oppose PRC‚Äôs entry into the United Nations. However, Chiang Kai-shek insisted on the stance of ‚Äúone cannot coexist with the other‚Äù and preferred to withdraw from the United Nations rather than compromise. From 1961 to 1969, the U.S. managed to maintain the ROC‚Äôs seat in the United Nations through ‚Äúimportant issue‚Äù proposals. However, by 1970, the votes in support of the PRC (51 votes) exceeded the opposing votes (49), but due to prior passage of the ‚Äúimportant issues‚Äù proposal, the ROC still retained its seat. In 1970, the number of member states opposing the classification of ‚ÄúChina representation‚Äù as an important issue had increased by 18 compared to ten years prior.

At this time, U.S. President Richard M. Nixon and his National Security Advisor Henry A. Kissinger aimed to start a strategic consideration for ‚Äúengagement with China to counter Russia‚Äù while also hoping to promote a ‚ÄúDual Representation‚Äù plan. However, due to Chiang Kai-shek‚Äôs insistence on ‚Äúone cannot coexist with the other‚Äù and ‚Äúbetter broken jade than intact tile,‚Äù it could not be implemented.

In October 1971, during a speech at the General Assembly, 45 countries spoke in favor of excluding the ROC or representatives of Chiang Kai-shek, while 22 countries opposed it, and 2 countries remained neutral. When the U.S. representative spoke, he argued that the ROC should continue to hold its United Nations seat.At that time, Albania and 16 other countries proposed to ‚Äúrestore the lawful rights of the People‚Äôs Republic of China‚Äù (including the PRC becoming one of the five permanent members of the Security Council and expelling the representatives of Chiang Kai-shek who unlawfully occupied the seats in the United Nations). However, the United States‚Äô proposal to vote on it as an ‚Äúimportant issue‚Äù was rejected (59 votes against, 55 votes in favor, and 15 abstentions), in other words, the issue of China‚Äôs representation was effectively settled. The U.S. hoped to separate the votes on restoring the PRC‚Äôs rights in the UN and the expulsion of Chiang‚Äôs representatives, but the result was 61 votes against, 51 votes in favor, and 16 countries abstaining, which was also rejected.

The then Foreign Minister of the ROC, Zhou Shukai, was permitted by the chair to speak, announcing that the ROC government decided to withdraw from participating in the current assembly‚Äôs proceedings, leading his delegation out of the assembly hall to hold a press conference to announce the ROC‚Äôs ‚Äúwithdrawal‚Äù statement from the United Nations that had been prepared in advance. In the end, the Albanian proposal was passed with 76 votes in favor, 35 votes against, and 17 abstentions.

Overall, the PRC‚Äôs admission to the United Nations reflected the power adjustments in the context of the Cold War confrontation between the U.S. and the Soviet Union, as well as the rise of newly independent countries in Asia, Africa, and Latin America forming a ‚ÄúThird World‚Äù coalition within the United Nations and their rising influence in the international system. Of course, this was also closely related to the CCP‚Äôs economic aid and military support to Third World countries. Regardless, the entry of the CCP into the United Nations in 1971 marked a significant elevation of China‚Äôs international status during the Cold War.

  1. U.S.-China Relations (Pollack 1991: 407-455; Carter 2005: 26-27)

In July 1971, U.S. Secretary of State Henry A. Kissinger made a secret visit to Beijing, meeting with Chinese Premier Zhou Enlai to pave the way for the normalization of U.S.-China relations. This visit symbolized the beginning of the end of hostilities between the two nations. Kissinger‚Äôs visit signified a strategic adjustment by the United States regarding China, with both sides recognizing that improved relations would help counter the Soviet threat. Additionally, after Kissinger‚Äôs visit, the U.S. announced its support for the lawful seat of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in the United Nations, ultimately facilitating the passage of Resolution 2758.

In February 1972, U.S. President Richard M. Nixon made a historic visit to China, marking a major breakthrough in U.S.-China relations. During the visit, Nixon met with Mao Zedong and initiated high-level dialogues with Zhou Enlai, resulting in a Joint Communiqu√© issued in Shanghai. The contents of the Shanghai Communiqu√© included: (1) the U.S. acknowledges that there is only one China and does not oppose Taiwan being a part of China, hoping for a peaceful resolution of the Taiwan issue in the future; (2) both sides agree to develop relations on the basis of equality and mutual benefit, respecting each other‚Äôs sovereignty and territorial integrity; (3) emphasizes common interests in opposing hegemonism. This visit not only symbolized the thawing of U.S.-China diplomacy but also laid the foundation for practical cooperation between the two countries.

On March 1, 1973, the U.S. established the United States Liaison Office (USLO) in Beijing. This was a transitional diplomatic institution set up between the two countries before they had formally established diplomatic relations, aimed at handling bilateral affairs and promoting the development of bilateral relations. That same year, China also set up the Liaison Office of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in the United States in Washington.

In 1974, Nixon resigned due to the Watergate scandal, and Gerald R. Ford, who succeeded him, continued his China policy, visiting China from December 1 to 5, 1975. However, considering that there remained significant domestic support for Taiwan in the U.S., no major breakthroughs were achieved in the establishment of formal diplomatic relations.

In 1977, after U.S. President James E. Carter took office, he actively promoted the process of establishing diplomatic relations with China. In December 1978, the two countries officially announced the establishment of diplomatic relations, and on January 1, 1979, formal diplomatic ties were established. This marked the beginning of a new phase in U.S.-China relations. The U.S. recognized the People‚Äôs Republic of China as the only legitimate government of China and severed diplomatic relations with Taiwan, while simultaneously maintaining informal relations with Taiwan through the Taiwan Relations Act.

From January 28 to February 5, 1979, Chinese Vice Premier Deng Xiaoping visited the United States, marking the first official visit by a Chinese leader to the U.S. after the establishment of diplomatic relations. Deng‚Äôs trip was a significant event in U.S.-China relations, consolidating the newly established diplomatic ties and launching comprehensive cooperation between the two countries in areas such as economy, technology, culture, and military.In China, the Third Plenary Session of the 11th Central Committee of the Communist Party was held in November 1978, establishing the path for reform and opening up. Deng Xiaoping appeared on the international stage with an image of openness and pragmatism (wearing a cowboy hat), enhancing China‚Äôs influence in the world and garnering greater global attention on the development of reform and opening up in China.

During Deng Xiaoping‚Äôs visit to the United States, Carter presented three demands regarding religion: first, religious freedom; second, the printing of Bibles; and third, allowing Western missionaries to return to China. Ultimately, Deng Xiaoping agreed to the first two demands but rejected the latter. This reflected the CCP‚Äôs adherence to its independent and self-managed church policy.

The development of U.S.-China relations in the 1970s marked a transition from hostility to contact and cooperation, signaling a significant shift in the Cold War backdrop. The two countries were able to break ideological constraints in the context of the Cold War and achieved a historic breakthrough through pragmatic strategic considerations.

  1. The Third Plenary Session of the 11th Central Committee of the Communist Party (Ding Xueliang 1994: 20-31; Chen Yongfa 2021: 905-918)

In November 1979, the Communist Party convened the Third Plenary Session of the 11th Central Committee and decided to shift the main focus of China‚Äôs work from ‚Äúclass struggle‚Äù to ‚Äúsocialist modernization construction,‚Äù establishing the basic direction for economic development and modernization. According to the session‚Äôs communiqu√©, the Communist Party had achieved significant accomplishments in the socialist modernization construction following the completion of socialist transformation, which, however, had been disrupted by Lin Biao and the ‚ÄúGang of Four.‚Äù Now, the focus of the entire party‚Äôs work would be redirected to achieving the modernization of agriculture, industry, national defense, and science and technology, with rural economic reform as the primary concern.

Additionally, Deng Xiaoping promoted an open-door policy. In 1979, he approved the introduction of foreign investment from Guangdong and Fujian provinces, as well as overseas Chinese and Hong Kong investment, and established the Shenzhen Special Economic Zone in 1980 to attract foreign businessmen to invest and set up factories under favorable conditions, thus introducing foreign capital and driving the national economic development.

The economic reforms and opening-up policies implemented by Deng Xiaoping aimed to liberate China from Mao Zedong‚Äôs era of ‚Äúrevolutionary messianism‚Äù and a ‚Äúpoliticized society,‚Äù returning to ordinary common sense focused on economic construction and the development of productivity. Deng Xiaoping understood that only by implementing economic reforms, developing productivity, and improving people‚Äôs lives‚Äîrather than repeating past ultra-leftist paths and the steel-like fixation on staged struggle‚Äîcould the Communist Party of China re-establish its legitimacy to govern.

In the context of moving away from the revolutionary atmosphere, reformists like Hu Yaobang advocated the idea that ‚Äúpractice is the sole criterion for testing truth‚Äù to demonstrate the errors of the Cultural Revolution, countering Hua Guofeng‚Äôs ‚Äúwhatever faction‚Äù (i.e., ‚ÄúWe steadfastly uphold all decisions made by Chairman Mao; we unwaveringly follow all of Chairman Mao‚Äôs instructions‚Äù), forming what is known as the ‚Äúideological liberation movement.‚Äù By the end of 1978, ‚ÄúBeijing Spring‚Äù emerged, where intellectuals discussed various topics, published underground materials, and demanded the addition of political democratization as the ‚Äúfifth modernization‚Äù alongside the four modernizations (Wei Jingsheng). For the Communist Party of China, this ideological liberation movement posed a threat to its leadership, as it perceived the discourse to have exceeded the limits of tolerance. Therefore, after consolidating his power position at the Third Plenary Session, Deng Xiaoping ordered the suppression of the Beijing democratic movement. Wei Jingsheng was arrested in March 1979 and sentenced to 15 years in prison for ‚Äúcounter-revolutionary activities.‚Äù

Against this background, in March 1979, Deng Xiaoping proposed the ‚ÄúFour Cardinal Principles,‚Äù stating that in order to achieve the four modernizations, it was essential to adhere to the socialist path, uphold the dictatorship of the proletariat, maintain the leadership of the Communist Party, and uphold Marxism-Leninism and Mao Zedong Thought. The so-called four insists essentially meant maintaining the regime and leadership of the Communist Party of China. He understood that only by adhering to the Four Cardinal Principles and politically defending against the right could the one-party rule of the Communist Party achieve long-term stability.

In 1979, the Communist Party set this non-leftist, non-rightist, and anti-leftist yet defensive of the right route, which became the basic framework of the ‚ÄúDeng Xiaoping Era‚Äù in the 1980s.

  1. Chinese Nationalism in the 1970s (Zhao 2004: 29-31; Mark 2012)

The 1970s saw the development of Chinese nationalism reflecting not only changes in domestic politics, economy, and society but also the influence of international circumstances. During this decade, there was a transition from revolutionary nationalism to pragmatic nationalism.

In the early 1970s, China was still in an era dominated by Mao Zedong Thought. As the ideological core of the People‚Äôs Republic of China, revolutionary nationalism emphasized anti-imperialism, anti-hegemony, and the defense of national sovereignty.During this period, China actively promoted its image as a leader of the Third World, supporting nationalist liberation movements in Asia, Africa, and Latin America. For example, China aided Vietnam in its struggle against American aggression and provided economic and technical support to African nations, demonstrating its firm stance against colonialism and hegemony.

With the thawing of U.S.-China relations, Chinese nationalism entered a new phase. In 1971, China successfully restored its seat in the United Nations and welcomed U.S. President Nixon‚Äôs visit in 1972. These diplomatic efforts not only strengthened China‚Äôs image as an independent nation but also infused nationalism with a new pragmatic tone.

Against the backdrop of Sino-American reconciliation, Chinese nationalism began to shift from a confrontational stance to a more pragmatic approach. National interests were placed in a more prominent position, and national pride was increasingly reflected in China‚Äôs enhanced international status. China harnessed the power of nationalism to unite its internal populace and prepare for the initial stages of reform and opening up, showcasing pragmatic characteristics distinct from traditional revolutionary nationalism.

In the 1970s, China also faced territorial and sovereignty disputes, such as the Diaoyu Islands issue with Japan, border conflicts with Vietnam, and border issues with India. These events fueled heightened nationalist sentiments and solidified public support for the central government. For instance, the victory in the 1974 naval battle in the Paracel Islands was viewed as a successful case of China defending its territorial sovereignty and became a significant source of national pride.

Regarding territorial issues, the Chinese government adopted a tough diplomatic and military stance, vigorously promoting the legitimacy of its sovereignty defense domestically. Nationalism was shaped as an important tool for cohesion, further strengthening the public‚Äôs sense of national identity. However, on the Diaoyu Islands issue, the Chinese Foreign Ministry reiterated its sovereignty through statements, but due to the normalization of diplomatic relations with Japan in 1972, the sovereignty dispute over the Diaoyu Islands was temporarily set aside for diplomatic needs.

Chinese nationalism in the 1970s showed distinct pragmatism. As the international landscape changed, China placed greater emphasis on achieving national interests in both foreign and domestic policies, rather than solely adhering to ideological struggles. For example, the reconciliation with the U.S. allowed China to break isolation within the Cold War framework and improve its developmental conditions. This pragmatism made nationalism not only a tool for internal cohesion but also an important strategy for China‚Äôs participation in international competition.
