  1. Shen Derong (Shen Xuebing 2017: 42-43; Zhu Jun 2001: 38-41; Yao Minquan 2012: 4-5; Cao Shengjie 2016: 98-99)

  1. Practice-led Pentecostalism (Kuo 2009: 171-188)

Gao Chenyang in ‚ÄúThe Cultural Revolution and the Emergence of Pentecostal-style Protestantism in China‚Äù points out that the Cultural Revolution‚Äôs eradication of religious activities led to a non-establishment form of faith, lacking religious authority and emphasizing supernatural experiences. He refers to this as ‚ÄúPractice-led Pentecostalism.‚Äù

Through interviews, Gao reveals how the Cultural Revolution restructured China‚Äôs religious landscape, particularly through individualisation, feminisation, and experiential forms of religion. The article notes that Christianity quickly adapted to the high-pressure policies of the Cultural Revolution, attracting followers, especially women, with its characteristics of supernaturalism (such as healing and exorcism) and individualistic practices. This form of religion not only met the needs of local communities but also resembled the cultural patterns of global Pentecostalism, including direct personal religious experiences and an emphasis on supernatural practices.

Gao particularly focuses on the role of women. He points out that the Cultural Revolution led to a decline in male participation in religious activities, while women, taking on household responsibilities and management of family misfortunes, became the leaders of religious activities. Many women chose to embrace Christianity during this period, viewing it as a ‚Äúclean, high God‚Äù religion capable of bringing inner peace and practical help.

Moreover, this form of Pentecostalism in Chinese Christianity has not fully transformed into institutionalized Pentecostal doctrine mainly due to its lack of a theological theoretical foundation regarding the importance of the Holy Spirit and spiritual experiences. He refers to this as ‚ÄúA Pentecostalism without Pentecostal Theology.‚Äù With the return of religious authority in the 1980s, this form of religion gradually faced challenges. Finally, Gao proposes using ‚ÄúPractice-led Pentecostalism‚Äù as an analytical tool to explore the evolution of Chinese Christianity in the context of historical and social changes. This reflects local adaptations within the globalization process, allowing for flexibility in operating within local laws, cultures, and needs, while also facing the risk of being reabsorbed by traditional religions or state authority. The article concludes that the religious reshaping during the Cultural Revolution not only shaped the trajectory of Christianity‚Äôs development in China but also provides important perspectives for understanding the interaction between globalization and local religions.
