(1) Church of England Trust Ordinance, 1930

In January 1930, the Legislative Council passed the Church of England Trust Ordinance. The ordinance established provisions for the incorporation of trustees specifically for the Church of England, including St. John‚Äôs Cathedral in Central and St. Andrew‚Äôs Church in Tsim Sha Tsui, as property trustees.

(2) Hop Yat Tong Church of Christ Hong Kong Incorporation Ordinance, 1931

In July 1931, the Legislative Council passed the Hop Yat Tong Church of Christ Hong Kong Incorporation Ordinance. The Hop Yat Tong Church, formerly known as To Tsai Church, was renamed after a new church building was completed in 1926.

(3) Chinese Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association Ordinance, 1932 (HK Legislative Council 1932a: 46-47)

In May 1932, the Legislative Council passed the Chinese Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association Ordinance. Established in 1901, the Hong Kong YMCA was divided into Chinese and Western sections in 1908, each with its own board of directors. The Chinese section became the Chinese YMCA of Hong Kong, while the Western section remained the Hong Kong YMCA. The land parcels (Inland Lot No. 1757, Inland Lot No. 2048) previously held by the International Committee of YMCA as trustee were transferred to the Chinese YMCA of Hong Kong, necessitating the latter‚Äôs incorporation to receive the properties.

(4) St. Stephen‚Äôs College Incorporation Ordinance, 1932 (HK Legislative Council 1932b: 47-48)

In May 1932, the Legislative Council passed the St. Stephen‚Äôs College Incorporation Ordinance, officially granting the college corporate status. St. Stephen‚Äôs College was originally founded in 1903 by the Church of England in Sai Ying Pun and relocated in 1928 to a former military site in Stanley.

(5) Kowloon Union Church Incorporation Ordinance, 1939

In September 1939, the Legislative Council passed the Kowloon Union Church Incorporation Ordinance, formally establishing its corporate status. Kowloon Union Church, the second English-speaking church in Kowloon, was founded in 1924. The government granted land on Jordan Road, where a new church building was completed in April 1931.

From the Great Depression to the War of Resistance Against Japan (Liu Shuyong 2016: 173-175; Carroll 2007: 112-119)

The global economic depression that began in 1929 significantly impacted Hong Kong‚Äôs import and export trade. In 1933, Hong Kong‚Äôs import trade value was HK$501 million, a decrease of 19.7% compared to 1932 and a drop of 32.1% compared to 1931. Export trade value in 1933 amounted to HK$403 million, down 14.6% from 1932 and 25.6% from 1931. By 1935, the situation further deteriorated, with the total annual import and export value in Hong Kong reaching HK$636 million, a 50.3% decline compared to the 1931 total of HK$1.28 billion.

Hong Kong‚Äôs trade relations with China also regressed. In 1933, the value of goods exported from China to Hong Kong was 125,067,438 yuan (Chinese currency), representing a decline of over 50% compared to 1930. Imports from Hong Kong to China were even more severely affected, dropping to just 48,512,295 yuan in 1933, an 85% decrease compared to 1930. This was not only due to the global economic crisis but also China‚Äôs declaration of tariff autonomy in 1928, which significantly increased import duties.

In 1932, members of the British Commonwealth signed the Ottawa Agreement in Canada, which allowed products manufactured within the British Empire to enjoy preferential tariffs. As a British colony, Hong Kong benefited from this policy, which stimulated the development of its export-oriented industries. By 1936, Hong Kong‚Äôs industries gradually emerged from the shadow of the global economic depression, with the total value of import and export trade increasing by 26.3% compared to 1935.

As Japan accelerated its invasion of China and launched a full-scale war in 1937, Hong Kong‚Äôs economic position became increasingly important. With key Chinese trading ports such as Shanghai, Nanjing, and Wuhan falling one after another, Hong Kong became the sole gateway for China‚Äôs foreign trade. In 1940, Hong Kong‚Äôs import value reached HK$753 million, a 66.6% increase from 1936, while export value rose to HK$622 million, a 77.2% increase.

Meanwhile, a large influx of Chinese refugees poured into Hong Kong. Between July 1937 and July 1938, the population of Hong Kong increased by nearly 250,000. In 1938 alone, 500,000 refugees arrived, many of whom were wealthy merchants from Shanghai and Guangdong, injecting vitality into Hong Kong‚Äôs commerce and industry. Banks such as the Bank of China and the Bank of Communications also relocated their headquarters to Hong Kong, making the colony the financial exchange center of China. While war ravaged China, Hong Kong remained a relatively peaceful refuge due to its status as a British colony.

To meet wartime demands, many factories in Hong Kong shifted to producing military supplies, including helmets, gas masks, uniforms, mess tins, canteens, and military-grade paint, driving industrial growth.

However, the British government sought to avoid conflict with Japan and declared Hong Kong neutral in September 1938. When the Chinese government requested that Hong Kong provide arms to support its resistance, the colonial government refused. Proposals by Chinese unofficial members of the Legislative Council to send relief funds to China were also denied. The Hong Kong Red Cross was prohibited from dispatching personnel to the war zones.

Despite this, Hong Kong played an important role in supporting the War of Resistance. The colonial government did not ban civilian efforts to support China‚Äôs resistance against Japan. Both the Nationalist and Communist parties used Hong Kong to transport supplies to China, with more than 50 tons of ammunition and supplies purchased overseas being transported via the Kowloon-Canton Railway to China each month before Guangzhou fell in 1938.

Civil organizations in Hong Kong actively supported the resistance. Numerous groups dedicated to aiding the war effort were established, such as the Hong Kong Students Relief Society, the Hong Kong Chinese General Chamber of Commerce Relief Fund Committee, the Chinese Women‚Äôs War Relief Committee, and the Hong Kong Chinese Women‚Äôs Association. On August 13, 1938, the first anniversary of the Battle of Shanghai, a fundraising campaign for South China refugees was launched, raising over HK$200,000 in donations through collection boxes set up by 12 banks, public districts, and overseas Chinese associations between August 11 and 19. In early August, vendors initiated a charity sale, eventually raising HK$1 million by September.

In addition to providing relief efforts in the rear, Hong Kong Chinese organized volunteer groups to return to China and join anti-Japanese activities. Refugee camps were also set up across Hong Kong to meet the basic needs of the large influx of refugees.

The ordinance established provisions for the incorporation of trustees specifically for the Church of England, including St. John‚Äôs Cathedral in Central and St. Andrew‚Äôs Church in Tsim Sha Tsui, as property trustees.

(2) Hop Yat Tong Church of Christ Hong Kong Incorporation Ordinance, 1931

In July 1931, the Legislative Council passed the Hop Yat Tong Church of Christ Hong Kong Incorporation Ordinance. The Hop Yat Tong Church, formerly known as To Tsai Church, was renamed after a new church building was completed in 1926.

(3) Chinese Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association Ordinance, 1932 (HK Legislative Council 1932a: 46-47)

In May 1932, the Legislative Council passed the Chinese Young Men‚Äôs Christian Association Ordinance. Established in 1901, the Hong Kong YMCA was divided into Chinese and Western sections in 1908, each with its own board of directors. The Chinese section became the Chinese YMCA of Hong Kong, while the Western section remained the Hong Kong YMCA. The land parcels (Inland Lot No. 1757, Inland Lot No. 2048) previously held by the International Committee of YMCA as trustee were transferred to the Chinese YMCA of Hong Kong, necessitating the latter‚Äôs incorporation to receive the properties.

(4) St. Stephen‚Äôs College Incorporation Ordinance, 1932 (HK Legislative Council 1932b: 47-48)

In May 1932, the Legislative Council passed the St. Stephen‚Äôs College Incorporation Ordinance, officially granting the college corporate status. St. Stephen‚Äôs College was originally founded in 1903 by the Church of England in Sai Ying Pun and relocated in 1928 to a former military site in Stanley.

(5) Kowloon Union Church Incorporation Ordinance, 1939

In September 1939, the Legislative Council passed the Kowloon Union Church Incorporation Ordinance, formally establishing its corporate status. Kowloon Union Church, the second English-speaking church in Kowloon, was founded in 1924. The government granted land on Jordan Road, where a new church building was completed in April 1931.

From the Great Depression to the War of Resistance Against Japan (Liu Shuyong 2016: 173-175; Carroll 2007: 112-119)

The global economic depression that began in 1929 significantly impacted Hong Kong‚Äôs import and export trade. In 1933, Hong Kong‚Äôs import trade value was HK$501 million, a decrease of 19.7% compared to 1932 and a drop of 32.1% compared to 1931. Export trade value in 1933 amounted to HK$403 million, down 14.6% from 1932 and 25.6% from 1931. By 1935, the situation further deteriorated, with the total annual import and export value in Hong Kong reaching HK$636 million, a 50.3% decline compared to the 1931 total of HK$1.28 billion.

Hong Kong‚Äôs trade relations with China also regressed. In 1933, the value of goods exported from China to Hong Kong was 125,067,438 yuan (Chinese currency), representing a decline of over 50% compared to 1930. Imports from Hong Kong to China were even more severely affected, dropping to just 48,512,295 yuan in 1933, an 85% decrease compared to 1930. This was not only due to the global economic crisis but also China‚Äôs declaration of tariff autonomy in 1928, which significantly increased import duties.

In 1932, members of the British Commonwealth signed the Ottawa Agreement in Canada, which allowed products manufactured within the British Empire to enjoy preferential tariffs. As a British colony, Hong Kong benefited from this policy, which stimulated the development of its export-oriented industries. By 1936, Hong Kong‚Äôs industries gradually emerged from the shadow of the global economic depression, with the total value of import and export trade increasing by 26.3% compared to 1935.

As Japan accelerated its invasion of China and launched a full-scale war in 1937, Hong Kong‚Äôs economic position became increasingly important. With key Chinese trading ports such as Shanghai, Nanjing, and Wuhan falling one after another, Hong Kong became the sole gateway for China‚Äôs foreign trade. In 1940, Hong Kong‚Äôs import value reached HK$753 million, a 66.6% increase from 1936, while export value rose to HK$622 million, a 77.2% increase.

Meanwhile, a large influx of Chinese refugees poured into Hong Kong. Between July 1937 and July 1938, the population of Hong Kong increased by nearly 250,000. In 1938 alone, 500,000 refugees arrived, many of whom were wealthy merchants from Shanghai and Guangdong, injecting vitality into Hong Kong‚Äôs commerce and industry. Banks such as the Bank of China and the Bank of Communications also relocated their headquarters to Hong Kong, making the colony the financial exchange center of China. While war ravaged China, Hong Kong remained a relatively peaceful refuge due to its status as a British colony.

To meet wartime demands, many factories in Hong Kong shifted to producing military supplies, including helmets, gas masks, uniforms, mess tins, canteens, and military-grade paint, driving industrial growth.

However, the British government sought to avoid conflict with Japan and declared Hong Kong neutral in September 1938. When the Chinese government requested that Hong Kong provide arms to support its resistance, the colonial government refused. Proposals by Chinese unofficial members of the Legislative Council to send relief funds to China were also denied. The Hong Kong Red Cross was prohibited from dispatching personnel to the war zones.

Despite this, Hong Kong played an important role in supporting the War of Resistance. The colonial government did not ban civilian efforts to support China‚Äôs resistance against Japan. Both the Nationalist and Communist parties used Hong Kong to transport supplies to China, with more than 50 tons of ammunition and supplies purchased overseas being transported via the Kowloon-Canton Railway to China each month before Guangzhou fell in 1938.

Civil organizations in Hong Kong actively supported the resistance. Numerous groups dedicated to aiding the war effort were established, such as the Hong Kong Students Relief Society, the Hong Kong Chinese General Chamber of Commerce Relief Fund Committee, the Chinese Women‚Äôs War Relief Committee, and the Hong Kong Chinese Women‚Äôs Association. On August 13, 1938, the first anniversary of the Battle of Shanghai, a fundraising campaign for South China refugees was launched, raising over HK$200,000 in donations through collection boxes set up by 12 banks, public districts, and overseas Chinese associations between August 11 and 19. In early August, vendors initiated a charity sale, eventually raising HK$1 million by September.

In addition to providing relief efforts in the rear, Hong Kong Chinese organized volunteer groups to return to China and join anti-Japanese activities. Refugee camps were also set up across Hong Kong to meet the basic needs of the large influx of refugees.

From the Great Depression to the War of Resistance Against Japan (Liu Shuyong 2016: 173-175; Carroll 2007: 112-119)

The global economic depression that began in 1929 significantly impacted Hong Kong‚Äôs import and export trade. In 1933, Hong Kong‚Äôs import trade value was HK$501 million, a decrease of 19.7% compared to 1932 and a drop of 32.1% compared to 1931. Export trade value in 1933 amounted to HK$403 million, down 14.6% from 1932 and 25.6% from 1931. By 1935, the situation further deteriorated, with the total annual import and export value in Hong Kong reaching HK$636 million, a 50.3% decline compared to the 1931 total of HK$1.28 billion.

Hong Kong‚Äôs trade relations with China also regressed. In 1933, the value of goods exported from China to Hong Kong was 125,067,438 yuan (Chinese currency), representing a decline of over 50% compared to 1930. Imports from Hong Kong to China were even more severely affected, dropping to just 48,512,295 yuan in 1933, an 85% decrease compared to 1930. This was not only due to the global economic crisis but also China‚Äôs declaration of tariff autonomy in 1928, which significantly increased import duties.

In 1932, members of the British Commonwealth signed the Ottawa Agreement in Canada, which allowed products manufactured within the British Empire to enjoy preferential tariffs. As a British colony, Hong Kong benefited from this policy, which stimulated the development of its export-oriented industries. By 1936, Hong Kong‚Äôs industries gradually emerged from the shadow of the global economic depression, with the total value of import and export trade increasing by 26.3% compared to 1935.

As Japan accelerated its invasion of China and launched a full-scale war in 1937, Hong Kong‚Äôs economic position became increasingly important. With key Chinese trading ports such as Shanghai, Nanjing, and Wuhan falling one after another, Hong Kong became the sole gateway for China‚Äôs foreign trade. In 1940, Hong Kong‚Äôs import value reached HK$753 million, a 66.6% increase from 1936, while export value rose to HK$622 million, a 77.2% increase.

Meanwhile, a large influx of Chinese refugees poured into Hong Kong. Between July 1937 and July 1938, the population of Hong Kong increased by nearly 250,000. In 1938 alone, 500,000 refugees arrived, many of whom were wealthy merchants from Shanghai and Guangdong, injecting vitality into Hong Kong‚Äôs commerce and industry. Banks such as the Bank of China and the Bank of Communications also relocated their headquarters to Hong Kong, making the colony the financial exchange center of China. While war ravaged China, Hong Kong remained a relatively peaceful refuge due to its status as a British colony.

To meet wartime demands, many factories in Hong Kong shifted to producing military supplies, including helmets, gas masks, uniforms, mess tins, canteens, and military-grade paint, driving industrial growth.

However, the British government sought to avoid conflict with Japan and declared Hong Kong neutral in September 1938. When the Chinese government requested that Hong Kong provide arms to support its resistance, the colonial government refused. Proposals by Chinese unofficial members of the Legislative Council to send relief funds to China were also denied. The Hong Kong Red Cross was prohibited from dispatching personnel to the war zones.

Despite this, Hong Kong played an important role in supporting the War of Resistance. The colonial government did not ban civilian efforts to support China‚Äôs resistance against Japan. Both the Nationalist and Communist parties used Hong Kong to transport supplies to China, with more than 50 tons of ammunition and supplies purchased overseas being transported via the Kowloon-Canton Railway to China each month before Guangzhou fell in 1938.

Civil organizations in Hong Kong actively supported the resistance. Numerous groups dedicated to aiding the war effort were established, such as the Hong Kong Students Relief Society, the Hong Kong Chinese General Chamber of Commerce Relief Fund Committee, the Chinese Women‚Äôs War Relief Committee, and the Hong Kong Chinese Women‚Äôs Association. From the Great Depression to the War of Resistance Against Japan (Liu Shuyong 2016: 173-175; Carroll 2007: 112-119)

The global economic depression that began in 1929 significantly impacted Hong Kong‚Äôs import and export trade. In 1933, Hong Kong‚Äôs import trade value was HK$501 million, a decrease of 19.7% compared to 1932 and a drop of 32.1% compared to 1931. Export trade value in 1933 amounted to HK$403 million, down 14.6% from 1932 and 25.6% from 1931. By 1935, the situation further deteriorated, with the total annual import and export value in Hong Kong reaching HK$636 million, a 50.3% decline compared to the 1931 total of HK$1.28 billion.

Hong Kong‚Äôs trade relations with China also regressed. In 1933, the value of goods exported from China to Hong Kong was 125,067,438 yuan (Chinese currency), representing a decline of over 50% compared to 1930. Imports from Hong Kong to China were even more severely affected, dropping to just 48,512,295 yuan in 1933, an 85% decrease compared to 1930. This was not only due to the global economic crisis but also China‚Äôs declaration of tariff autonomy in 1928, which significantly increased import duties.

In 1932, members of the British Commonwealth signed the Ottawa Agreement in Canada, which allowed products manufactured within the British Empire to enjoy preferential tariffs. As a British colony, Hong Kong benefited from this policy, which stimulated the development of its export-oriented industries. By 1936, Hong Kong‚Äôs industries gradually emerged from the shadow of the global economic depression, with the total value of import and export trade increasing by 26.3% compared to 1935.

As Japan accelerated its invasion of China and launched a full-scale war in 1937, Hong Kong‚Äôs economic position became increasingly important. With key Chinese trading ports such as Shanghai, Nanjing, and Wuhan falling one after another, Hong Kong became the sole gateway for China‚Äôs foreign trade. In 1940, Hong Kong‚Äôs import value reached HK$753 million, a 66.6% increase from 1936, while export value rose to HK$622 million, a 77.2% increase.

Meanwhile, a large influx of Chinese refugees poured into Hong Kong. Between July 1937 and July 1938, the population of Hong Kong increased by nearly 250,000. In 1938 alone, 500,000 refugees arrived, many of whom were wealthy merchants from Shanghai and Guangdong, injecting vitality into Hong Kong‚Äôs commerce and industry. Banks such as the Bank of China and the Bank of Communications also relocated their headquarters to Hong Kong, making the colony the financial exchange center of China. While war ravaged China, Hong Kong remained a relatively peaceful refuge due to its status as a British colony.

To meet wartime demands, many factories in Hong Kong shifted to producing military supplies, including helmets, gas masks, uniforms, mess tins, canteens, and military-grade paint, driving industrial growth.

However, the British government sought to avoid conflict with Japan and declared Hong Kong neutral in September 1938. When the Chinese government requested that Hong Kong provide arms to support its resistance, the colonial government refused. Proposals by Chinese unofficial members of the Legislative Council to send relief funds to China were also denied. The Hong Kong Red Cross was prohibited from dispatching personnel to the war zones.

Despite this, Hong Kong played an important role in supporting the War of Resistance. The colonial government did not ban civilian efforts to support China‚Äôs resistance against Japan. Both the Nationalist and Communist parties used Hong Kong to transport supplies to China, with more than 50 tons of ammunition and supplies purchased overseas being transported via the Kowloon-Canton Railway to China each month before Guangzhou fell in 1938.

Civil organizations in Hong Kong actively supported the resistance. Numerous groups dedicated to aiding the war effort were established, such as the Hong Kong Students Relief Society, the Hong Kong Chinese General Chamber of Commerce Relief Fund Committee, the Chinese Women‚Äôs War Relief Committee, and the Hong Kong Chinese Women‚Äôs Association. On August 13, 1938, the first anniversary of the Battle of Shanghai, a fundraising campaign for South China refugees was launched, raising over HK$200,000 in donations through collection boxes set up by 12 banks, public districts, and overseas Chinese associations between August 11 and 19. In early August, vendors initiated a charity sale, eventually raising HK$1 million by September.

In addition to providing relief efforts in the rear, Hong Kong Chinese organized volunteer groups to return to China and join anti-Japanese activities. Refugee camps were also set up across Hong Kong to meet the basic needs of the large influx of refugees.
