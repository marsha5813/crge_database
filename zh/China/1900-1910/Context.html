Governmental Regulations on Religion and Legal Cases

Imperial Edict Declaring War on the Eight Powers (June 21, 1900)

(Yang Dachun 2004: 42)

On June 21, 1900, the Qing court issued an imperial edict declaring war on the foreign powers. The edict refers to Qing policies on Christianity since the Daoguang and Xianfeng reigns, denounces the Western powers and Christian missions for their misconduct in China, and portrays the burning of churches as the act of righteous volunteers (Áæ©Âãá):

‚ÄúFor more than two hundred years, our dynasty has governed with profound benevolence and kindness. All foreigners who came to China were treated with generosity and goodwill by our ancestors. During the reigns of Daoguang and Xianfeng, we condescended to allow trade and, upon their request, permitted them to preach in our country, believing that their religion encouraged good deeds. Initially, they respected our authority and complied with our restrictions.

But over the past thirty years, taking advantage of our tolerance and kindness, they have grown increasingly overbearing‚Äîbullying our nation, seizing our territory, trampling on our people, and extorting our wealth. The more the court made concessions, the more arrogant and aggressive they became‚Äîoppressing the commoners at best, and insulting the sacred and divine at worst.

The resentment of our people has grown; every citizen harbors a grudge and longs to strike back. That is why the volunteer forces have burned churches and killed Christians. Yet the court was still unwilling to provoke conflict, and sought to protect the foreigners for fear of harming our own people. For this reason, we repeatedly issued edicts to prohibit violence, guard foreign legations, and show compassion to Christian converts.

Our previous edict‚Äîsaying that Boxers and Christians alike are our subjects‚Äîwas meant to resolve longstanding animosities between the people and the converts. The court has done everything in its power to accommodate foreigners, but they have not shown gratitude, and instead responded with threats and coercion. Just yesterday, Seymour delivered a formal notice demanding that we abandon the Dagu Forts and hand them over, or else they would take them by force.

Such aggressive rhetoric clearly aims to intimidate the capital region and provoke chaos. We have always conducted ourselves properly in diplomatic relations and never acted discourteously, yet those who claim to be ‚Äúcivilized nations‚Äù behave lawlessly, relying solely on military strength and advanced weaponry to push matters to this point.

I have reigned for nearly thirty years, treating the people as my own children, and they in turn revere me as the Son of Heaven. Empress Dowager Ci Sheng, who has revived the realm, has shown benevolence that penetrates to the bone and marrow. The spirits of our ancestors, the gods above, and the people‚Äîall are stirred with unprecedented loyalty and righteous anger.

Now, I weep as I report this to the ancestral altars. With solemn resolve, I rally the troops: better to fight boldly for honor than to survive in disgrace, leaving shame to future generations. Is it not better to launch a full campaign and decide this in one final battle?‚Äù
