The Migration of Chinese Churches to Taiwan

In 1949, as the Chinese Civil War turned unfavorable for the Kuomintang (KMT) government led by Chiang Kai-shek, the Republic of China (ROC) began retreating to Taiwan. On October 1, 1949, under the leadership of Mao Zedong, the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) declared victory and established the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC). In its first year of rule, the CCP‚Äôs atheist policies forced thousands of foreign missionaries to leave China.

Since the 19th century, various Christian missionary organizations from Europe and the United States had been active in China. With the political shift in 1949, many of these missions relocated to Taiwan. These included theBaptist Church, Methodist Church, Lutheran Church, Quaker Church, Christian Missionary Alliance, Assemblies of God, Episcopal Church, Holiness Church, Evangelical Fellowship, Church of the Nazarene, Zion Church, Christian and Missionary Alliance, Church of the Nazarene, Sabbath Church, Mennonite Church, Bread of Life Church, Lutheran Church, China Evangelistic Mission, and the Local Church (Âè¨ÊúÉ), among others. This migration contributed to Taiwan‚Äôs present-day landscape of diverse Christian denominations.

As these new religious groups arrived in Taiwan, they found a receptive environment. Many of the new immigrants who had followed the KMT to Taiwan were displaced from their homeland, experiencing uncertainty and seeking spiritual solace. The well-resourced missionaries and churches that had relocated from mainland China actively recruited members in densely populated areas and schools. Consequently, Christian churches began emerging rapidly across Taiwan.

Another significant factor in the expansion of Christianity in Taiwan during this period was the personal faith ofChiang Kai-shek and Soong Mei-ling, which held considerable influence in the authoritarian era. This relationship between the ruling elite and the church led to deep ties between the Christian community and the government, extending beyond political and military control. (Huang Kexian, 2021, 72-73)

Additionally, the denominations that relocated to Taiwan came from different missionary regions in China, resulting in variations within the same denominational traditions. This historical divergence continues to shape the landscape of Christianity in Taiwan today. Furthermore, the Christian groups that arrived in 1949 often held different political and theological positions from thePresbyterian Church in Taiwan, which had been established for over a century. These differences led to tensions that persist in Taiwan‚Äôs Christian community to this day.

Japanese Colonial Government

During Japan‚Äôs colonial rule over Taiwan, the Japanese government maintained a cautious attitude towards Christianity. However, since the Presbyterian Church largely cooperated with the authorities‚Äîfor example, by conducting worship services in Japanese as part of the imperialization policy‚ÄîChristianity was not eradicated as it had been during previous regime changes. By the late Japanese colonial period, tensions between the Presbyterian Church and the government increased due to the enforcement of the imperialization policy, which promoted reverence for Shintoism.

During the wartime period of the 1940s, contradictions deepened. As Japan‚Äôs anti-British and anti-American sentiment intensified, Western missionaries in Taiwan faced increasing difficulties. Eventually, due to the expansion of the war, they were forced to leave Taiwan. The colonial authorities demanded that Japanese individuals replace Western leaders in Christian medical and educational institutions. By the end of 1941, before Japan went to war with the United States, Scottish Presbyterian missionaries had withdrawn from Taiwan. As mentioned in Wan Ronghua‚Äôs account, the Japanese government coveted church school properties. To protect the school and ensure that the evangelical mission among students could continue, Wan Ronghua proactively appointed a Japanese principal and took a step back to serve as a teacher and chaplain. He was the last missionary to leave Tainan, departing on November 22, 1940.

At the same time, both the southern and northern branches of the Presbyterian Church in Taiwan completely severed ties with their foreign mother churches. In response to the situation, the southern church adopted the Three-Self Movement, while the northern church initiated the New Man Movement‚Äîboth serving as self-preservation strategies that ultimately enabled Taiwanese churches to transition into independently operated local churches. The Chinese Communist Party

The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) is the ruling party of the People‚Äôs Republic of China (PRC). It was founded in August 1920 during the Beiyang Government era of the Republic of China by figures such as Chen Duxiu and Li Dazhao. In 1922, the CCP joined the Communist International (Comintern), receiving guidance and support. Subsequently, the CCP collaborated with the Kuomintang (KMT) in the Northern Expedition, successfully dismantling the Beiyang warlords and their government. However, after the first KMT-CCP alliance collapsed in 1927, the two parties engaged in a decade-long military struggle.

Starting in the 1930s, a domestic faction led by Mao Zedong gained prominence within the CCP, allowing the party to gradually distance itself from Comintern influence. In 1931, the CCP established the‚ÄùChinese Soviet Republic‚Äù (‰∏≠ËèØËòáÁ∂≠ÂüÉÂÖ±ÂíåÂúã), marking the first communist regime in China, with the goal of overthrowing the KMT-led National Government.

During the Second Sino-Japanese War, the CCP and the KMT formed a second alliance, establishing a unified front against Japan. However, after Japan‚Äôs defeat in 1945, the CCP led the People‚Äôs Liberation Army in the Chinese Civil War, ultimately defeating the KMT and the Republic of China Armed Forces. The CCP gained control over mainland China and most coastal islands, leading to the founding of the People‚Äôs Republic of China in 1949, where it has remained the ruling party ever since.

As a Leninist party that embraces atheism, the CCP has maintained strict control over religious activities. Prior to the establishment of the PRC in 1949, many foreign missionaries voluntarily left China in anticipation of political upheaval. After 1949, foreign clergy were either expelled, followed the ROC government to Taiwan, or relocated to British Hong Kong or their home countries.

Although the PRC Constitution nominally guarantees freedom of religious belief, its implementation has been subject to scrutiny. Under the leadership of Zhou Enlai, then-Premier of the Central People‚Äôs Government, the CCP introduced the‚ÄùThree-Self Principles‚Äù‚Äîself-governance, self-support, and self-propagation‚Äîfor Christian organizations. Shinto Shrine Worship

After the Meiji Restoration, Japan promoted the nationalization of Shinto as a state religion, leading to the establishment of shrines throughout the country. These newly established shrines symbolized state power and served as a means to transmit national consciousness to local populations, with the ultimate goal of transforming society into loyal subjects of the emperor (Murakami Shigeyoshi, 1982, pp. 5-6). Shinto shrines became emblematic of Japanese imperialism, regarded as part of colonial governance through religion. As Japan expanded its colonial empire during World War II, shrines were established across its colonies, gradually integrating into the daily lives of colonial residents (Tsai Jin-tang, 1994, pp. 15-19). In newly developed territories such as Hokkaido and colonies like Taiwan and Korea, colonial subjects were often forced to participate in shrine customs and rituals during the war period.

The Japanese colonial government saw the swift ‚ÄúJapanization‚Äù of Taiwan as essential for governance, aiming to reshape Taiwanese identity into that of a nation centered on the emperor‚Äôs sovereignty. From 1895 to 1945, with the full support of the Japanese colonial administration, shrines dedicated to Shinto deities symbolizing absolute authority were constructed across towns and villages in Taiwan. By the end of the Naichi Ench≈ç (Inner Territory Extension) policy in Sh≈çwa 9 (1934), the government sought to achieve the goal of ‚Äúone shrine per town or village‚Äù (Taiwan Governor-General‚Äôs Bureau of Education and Social Affairs, 1943).

In 1900, the Japanese House of Representatives passed a resolution on the ‚ÄúProposal for the Construction of a Special Official Shrine in Taiwan.‚Äù Simultaneously, the Ministry of Home Affairs announced, in Notification No. 81, the decision to establish Taiwan Shrine (Tsai Jin-tang, 1994, pp. 15-19). This shrine was built to commemorate Prince Kitashirakawa Yoshihisa, who led the Imperial Guard Expeditionary Force that landed at Aodi on May 31, 1895. The force engaged in battles from Keelung to Tainan, where the prince eventually succumbed to illness. He was later honored as the ‚ÄúGuardian of Taiwan.‚Äù As the first official shrine in an overseas colony, Taiwan Shrine designated October 28‚Äîthe day of the prince‚Äôs passing‚Äîas its annual festival day. The Governor-General declared this day a public holiday across the island, during which an imperial envoy and the Governor-General, accompanied by government and military officials as well as local elites, would participate in worship ceremonies, demonstrating the shrine‚Äôs supreme political and symbolic significance (Tsai Jin-tang, 1994, p. 23).

In 1936, the Taiwan Governor-General‚Äôs Office launched the Kominka (imperialization) movement, introducing the ‚ÄúEthical Reform Movement,‚Äù which promoted reverence for gods and ancestors, the veneration of imperial ancestors, and the widespread adoption of the national language (Japanese) among Taiwanese people. As part of this initiative, Taiwanese were required to use Japanese in daily life and were encouraged to participate in shrine worship and the veneration of sacred Shinto amulets (Taima h≈çsai) (Tsai Jin-tang, 1991, pp. 69-72). By the final phase of the war, during the ‚ÄúImperial Citizens Service Movement,‚Äù efforts were intensified to fully instill Japanese imperial ideology, leading to the establishment of various patriotic service organizations (Hsu Chien-hsin, 1965, pp. 262-264).

During this period, Christian missionary schools‚Äîparticularly middle schools‚Äîfaced severe challenges. Schools such as Chang Jung High School and Tamkang High School struggled with the requirement of ‚Äúshrine worship,‚Äù which became a condition for government recognition. Initially, church leaders strongly resisted, but they were eventually forced to comply with the Japanese Ministry of Education‚Äôs argument that shrine worship was a ‚Äúsupra-religious‚Äù patriotic act. The Kominka movement posed significant dilemmas for missionary schools, not only altering curricula and school administration but also striking at the core of their religious identity through the demand for shrine worship (Lo Chi-ming, 2016, pp. 24-25).

The February 28 Incident

The February 28 Incident refers to the violent conflicts between the government and civilians that erupted across Taiwan from February 27 to May 16, 1947. The Taiwanese people demanded political reform, but the Nationalist Government of the Republic of China ultimately dispatched military forces to suppress the uprising with brutal force.

After World War II, the people of Taiwan eagerly anticipated the island‚Äôs reintegration into China. Some figures from the Presbyterian Church, such as Tsai Pei-huo and Chen Si-zhi, even composed songs to celebrate Taiwan‚Äôs ‚Äúliberation‚Äù (Chen Guan-zhou, 2002, p. 35). However, the incoming Chinese administration treated Taiwan as a new colonial possession rather than as a reclaimed territory, subjecting the island‚Äôs people to even greater suffering.

Many Taiwanese civil servants who had been employed under the Japanese administration were dismissed en masse, while those who remained were often subjected to discrimination and humiliation by their Chinese colleagues (Chang Yan-xian et al., 2006, pp. 13-42, 171-221). The lived experiences, language, and education that Taiwanese had acquired under Japanese rule were not recognized; instead, they were denigrated as remnants of Japanese imperialism (Lai Ze-han, 1994, pp. 3-27). Economically, Taiwan suffered from severe hyperinflation due to the Nationalist government‚Äôs planned economy policies. Rising unemployment, food shortages, and widespread economic hardship led to a series of social crises (Chang Yan-xian et al., 2006, pp. 13-42). Additionally, infighting among different factions of the Kuomintang (KMT) further weakened political and military discipline, leading to rampant corruption (Lai Ze-han, 1994, pp. 3-27). These accumulated grievances set the stage for the February 28 Incident, triggered by an event on February 27, 1947. On that day, Monopoly Bureau agents attempted to crack down on illegal cigarette sales near Tianma Teahouse in Taipei‚Äôs Taipingding district. During the raid, they severely injured a cigarette vendor, Lin Jiang-mai, and accidentally shot and killed a bystander, Chen Wen-xi. This incident quickly escalated, igniting island-wide unrest within days (Lai Ze-han, 1994, pp. 47-81).

In an attempt to mediate the crisis and address public grievances, representatives from various counties and cities, along with local gentry, formed the February 28 Incident Settlement Committee. They hoped to resolve the conflict through political negotiations and proposed political reforms (Lai Ze-han, 1994, pp. 47-81).

At first, Governor-General Chen Yi appeared to compromise, accepting the committee‚Äôs recommendations. However, he secretly regarded the uprising as an act of ‚Äúrebellion.‚Äù Soon after, the central government deployed troops to Taiwan to carry out large-scale purges. This military crackdown resulted in the mass arrest, execution, or disappearance of numerous Taiwanese, including many intellectuals and elites who had no direct involvement in the protests (Chang Yan-xian et al., 2006, pp. 43-76, 171-243, 449-489).

Among the victims were several prominent members of the Presbyterian Church, including Pastor Xiao Chao-jin (leader of the Three Principles of the People Youth League); Zhang Qi-lang, a renowned doctor from Fenglin (who was also a delegate to the Constitutional National Assembly, speaker of the Hualien County Council, and a physician), along with his two sons; Taipei City Councilor Xu Chun-qing; Dr. Lin Mao-sheng, acting dean of the College of Liberal Arts at National Taiwan University; Chen Neng-tong, principal of Tamkang High School; discipline director Huang A-tong; teacher Lu Yuan; chairman Xu Chun-qing; Kaohsiung City Councilors Xu Qiu-zong and Tu Guang-ming; lawyers and brothers Li Rui-han and Li Rui-feng; and Ruan Chao-ri, president of the Taiwan Shin Sheng Daily News, among many others (Li Xin-ren, 2011).
